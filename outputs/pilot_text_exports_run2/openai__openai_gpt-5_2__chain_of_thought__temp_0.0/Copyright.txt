Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is “moral squalor” and theft. He says it is like reaching into someone’s pocket and stealing their wallet. In an article for Index on Censorship, Pullman (the president of the Society of Authors) strongly defends copyright laws. He criticizes internet users who think it is OK to download music or books without paying.

Pullman writes that the technology is so impressive that people do not see the wrong thing they are doing. He says it is outrageous that someone can steal an artist’s work and not be punished. He repeats that it is theft, like stealing a wallet.

His article came after music industry leaders met British Prime Minister David Cameron at Downing Street. They talked about web piracy.

Pullman, who wrote the His Dark Materials trilogy, says authors and musicians often work for years in poverty and in little-known jobs. He says they work hard to make something that gives joy to audiences, but then their chance to earn a living is taken away. He ends with a simple idea: if we want to enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

Pullman is writing in the next issue of the group’s magazine in a dialogue with Cathy Casserly. She is the chief executive of Creative Commons. Creative Commons offers open content licences “that lets creators take copyright into their own hands”. Casserly says many things are wrong with copyright, which was made “in an analogue age”. She writes that, by default, copyright stops many ways for people to share, build on, and remix each other’s work. She says these ways were hard to imagine when the laws were created.

Casserly says artists need to think in new ways about how they share and make money from their work. She quotes science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees. The editor, Rachael Jolley, said existing copyright laws do not work in the digital age and could make consumers into criminals. She said new models are needed for how artists, writers, and musicians earn a living from their work.

The debate is very active, and illegal downloading is very large. Data collected by Ofcom (the independent regulator and competition authority for the UK communications industries) suggests that, between November 2012 and January 2013 in the UK, 280 million music tracks were pirated. Also pirated were 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files.

Ofcom said 18% of internet users aged over 12 admit they recently pirated content, and 9% say they fear getting caught. Pullman writes that it is still amazing how easily and quickly music can be gotten as MP3 downloads, especially to people who have been building up their iTunes list for some time.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron appointed the Conservative MP Mike Weatherley to be his adviser on the issue. A spokesman for the BPI, the record industry trade body, said Mike Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creative producers it is meant to protect. The spokesman said they hope Weatherley’s influence, and the prime minister’s support for copyright, will affect the approach of the UK’s intellectual property office.
