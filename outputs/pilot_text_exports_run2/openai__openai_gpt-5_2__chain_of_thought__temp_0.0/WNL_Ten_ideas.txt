From glow-in-the-dark trees to underground bike storage and solar-powered bins, here are some unusual ideas to make cities better places to live.

Living in a city has many good points, but living in a way that is good for the environment is getting harder. People in cities often deal with pollution, traffic, and fewer green spaces.

Here are five of the stranger ideas that inventors have tried to make cities more livable.

Pop-up parks
Some cities look like they are built more for cars than for people. A pop-up park is a simple idea: take an empty parking space, a little money, and one or two plants, and turn it into a small park. The PARK(ing) project began as an art experiment in San Francisco and then spread around the world. It has led to ideas like temporary city farms and ecology demonstrations. The movement has a day of action every September.

Subterranean storage
Not everyone in cities uses a car. Bikes are becoming more popular, but people need a safe place to keep them. A Tokyo engineering company called Giken designed an “eco-cycle, anti-seismic underground bicycle park.” It is a round storage space that is only seven metres wide. It goes deep underground and can hold 204 bikes. Owners get their bike back by pressing a button, and the automatic system brings it up in about 13 seconds.

Glow-in-the-dark trees
Many people think of Christmas lights when they imagine something glowing in the dark. Dutch designer-artist Daan Roosegaarde thinks of glowing plants. He created an experimental “bioluminescent” plant. The technology combines DNA from glowing marine bacteria with a plant’s chloroplast genome to make the plant glow like a jellyfish. Tests are happening to make a large-scale version. Roosegaarde hopes it could one day replace normal street lights.

Footfall harvesting
In West Ham in east London, many commuters and shoppers walk over an elevated walkway near the underground station every day. They might notice the surface feels a little springy, but not realize why. The rubber surface bends about five millimetres, and this helps power the streetlights above. The walkway has smart tiles that capture energy from footsteps and turn it into electricity. The UK company Pavegen made this system. It has also installed a similar system at Heathrow Airport in London and in other places.

Supertrees
In Singapore, Gardens by the Bay has man-made trees called “supertrees.” These steel-framed trees are up to 50 metres tall. Flowers and ferns grow on them, and their metal tops help absorb and spread out heat. They also collect rainwater and help provide air ventilation for two “climate-controlled biomes” (large conservatories) below. Eleven of the 18 trees also have solar panels on their “branches.”
