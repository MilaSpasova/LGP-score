This invention could be as useful as Trevor Baylis’s wind-up radio in the pre-internet 1990s. It is a cheap light that gets free power from gravity. It could help stop people using dangerous kerosene-fuelled lamps in Africa and India.

A British designer named Patrick Hunt tried to get money for his invention from banks or venture capital firms. But he had a problem. He said getting funding was slow and complicated. The idea was unproven, and no one wanted to take the risk.

So he tried crowdfunding on a US website called Indiegogo, which had recently opened in the UK. In five days, he reached his goal and raised £36,200. His campaign was very popular at the end of 2012. In 40 days, he had raised £400,000.

The LED light uses a dynamo. The dynamo is powered when a 10kg bag of rocks slowly drops down. The weight is attached to the light and lifted to about 2m high. As it slowly falls to the ground, it makes enough power for half an hour of light.

Hunt is getting ready to make the lights in China. He will test the market again by sending 1,000 lights to Africa before full mass production. He hopes to make millions of units.

Hunt is part of a new group of entrepreneurs who use the fast-growing crowdfunding industry to raise money. Another new site is InvestingZone. It matches wealthy people with start-up entrepreneurs.

Indiegogo does not give people shares. Instead, it lets users offer “perks” for different donation levels. People who helped fund Hunt’s light could feel good about helping poorer people, and they also got their own light.

Danae Ringelmann, a co-founder of Indiegogo, says the “gravity light” is a great example of how crowdfunding can work. She says it can quickly test an entrepreneur’s idea. She said finance can be fast, efficient, and meritocratic because it is not about knowing the right people at a bank or a venture capital firm. It is about showing your customers and fans that your idea is good, so they support it and fund it.

She also says even ideas that do not get funding are still worth testing. Then you can find out sooner if an idea is not good, and learn faster.

Ringelmann is based in the US. She started her career as a Wall Street analyst. In 2008, she quit and used her skills to help friends in the arts raise money.

At first, the site focused on films. It launched at the Sundance Film Festival that year.

Five years later, the site was raising about $2m a week for new businesses that were starting or growing. In December 2012, it started euro and sterling services to grow on this side of the Atlantic. It says Britain is its third biggest market. International activity is up 41% since December.

There are many competitors, like Kickstarter, Seedrs, and Funding Circle. But Ringelmann says Indiegogo is different because anyone can start a campaign. No project is considered too wacky.

Indiegogo charges a 4% fee for successful campaigns. If a campaign does not reach its target, users can choose to refund all the money to contributors for free, or keep all the money raised and pay a 9% fee.

A British woman named Lauren Pears raised £100,000 on the site to open a “cat café” in London. It is called Lady Dinah’s Cat Emporium. It has not opened yet, but it is described as a place where people can “come in from the cold to a comfortable wingback chair, a hot cup of tea, a book and a cat”.

Ringelmann said some campaigns are rejected by venture capitalists. They might say, “Great idea, but we do not know if the market wants it; it could be a gadget that no one cares about.”

She said entrepreneurs can run an Indiegogo campaign, even before launching the project. The campaign itself can be proof that people want it. Then venture capitalists may believe there is a market.

Ringelmann says this lets you test your market, pricing, and features. You can find new ways to make money and get important feedback.

Because of her Wall Street background and helping 100,000 businesses and services raise money, Ringelmann has shaped her advice for new entrepreneurs.

She says ideas are common. What matters is how well you carry out the idea. She says if you are afraid someone will steal your idea and do it better and faster, then you are not the right person to do it. She says you need confidence to move fast and learn.

Ringelmann says moving into Europe is part of a dream to make finance open to more people. Indiegogo also made a deal with a web transaction provider. This will let people pay through local card services like Maestro in the UK and Carte Bleue in France, as well as PayPal. More than 7,000 finance campaigns are live on the site.

Crowdfunding has grown as another choice besides banks. But it does not interest all big-money investors. Many of them want a stake in a promising business. That could start to change in the UK with the launch of InvestingZone.
