A thick layer of bird droppings covers a gold-painted railing at one of Britain’s most expensive homes. Pigeon skeletons lie among broken mirrors, and water runs through broken ceiling edges. This is The Towers, a £30m palace on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London. Its terrible state has been kept secret until now.

The Towers is one of ten mansions in the middle of The Bishops Avenue. This street is at the centre of London’s fast-rising property market. These homes have been almost empty since they were bought about 25 years ago. It is believed they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. Their Greek-style columns are breaking. Their swimming pools with mosaic tiles are full of rubble. Nature has taken over so much that owls have moved in.

This empty, broken scene is seen along the avenue. Lloyds Bank says The Bishops Avenue is the second most expensive street in Britain. At the same time, more people are struggling to buy a home in London as house prices rise 11.2% a year. Yet 16 mansions on the most expensive part of the street are empty. Many are behind locked gates. Their windows have steel bars, and their grounds are overgrown and watched by guard dogs.

Across the street is another ruined mansion worth £18m, with broken windows and anti-climb paint on the walls. Another mansion has metal bars on the windows and has been sold for £20m.

Even so, prices keep rising. Dryades, a mansion that was owned until recently by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007 and is believed to be worth about £30m today. Heath Lodge, where fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou was murdered in 1984 by a silver bullet, is worth £13m today. It was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

For people who struggle to keep a home in one of the world’s most expensive cities, these empty houses can be painful to see. A security guard on the avenue said it was upsetting to watch so much space—tens of thousands of square feet, enough for dozens of people—falling apart.

Rich royals with oil money from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia were among the first to come to this curved road near Hampstead Heath. Iranians came here after the fall of the shah. Now, Chinese buyers are following Russians and Kazakhs. They have spent millions to get an address that estate agents say is as famous as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions were for sale at £65m and £38m. They offered Italian marble, lifts with leather padding, and luxury panic rooms.

But on the grounds of the empty mansions, stone fountains are breaking and lawns have turned into muddy wetlands. Inside one house, water drips through a huge crystal chandelier onto a thick carpet that is rotting under parts of a collapsed ceiling. Moss grows through broken bricks. Mirror tiles are scattered across a bathroom. The swimming pool has about a foot of dirty salty water, and flowers grow through the tiles. Wooden slats are pushing out from the sauna.

The most dramatic ruin is The Towers. It is a large mansion set among acres of hornbeams, oaks, and limes. Pigeons fill its tall halls. Water pours through three storeys, turning the walls bright green with algae, and then drops into a huge, empty basement swimming pool. Unopened wooden crates marked “bullet-proof glass” show the security worries of the earlier owners.

Today, very few people live on The Bishops Avenue all the time. A security guard outside one mansion said the owners were not there. Another guard, outside Royal Mansion, would not say if anyone was home. Staff at another mansion warned the Guardian about the guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare mogul with personal wealth of £45m, said he is one of the few who live there permanently.

“It’s not a neighbourly place, where you can chat over the fence,” he said. “To be honest, I have never seen what my neighbours look like. Next door, a Saudi princess spent £35m on a new house and I’ve never seen her. There are about three houses that are lived in 24/7 and half of the properties are occupied three to six months a year. The other half, who knows if they come or not?”

These broken, very expensive homes show a property culture where the world’s richest people treat British homes as investments. One Hyde Park, a block of flats in Knightsbridge, is another example. More than half its flats are registered with the council as empty or as second homes.

Still, people on the avenue talk about building £5m apartments instead of £50m mansions, to bring people back.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, plans to rebuild one of the most valuable sites on the avenue. He wants to make 20 apartments with a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

“If you build a big house and try and sell for £30m to £40m, it won’t sell,” he said. “Locals won’t buy and so you have to bring in overseas buyers.”

But using the empty properties to help solve the housing crisis still seems far away. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some of the ruined homes could become affordable housing. He said the land price would be too high.

“Very wealthy people own property there,” he said. “Sometimes they live in them and sometimes they don’t.”
