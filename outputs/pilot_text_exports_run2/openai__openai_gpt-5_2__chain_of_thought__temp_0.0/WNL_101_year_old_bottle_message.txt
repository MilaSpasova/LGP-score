Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8th April, 2014, she talked about a very unusual moment. She received a message in a bottle, 101 years after her grandfather threw it into the Baltic Sea. People think it may be the world’s oldest message in a bottle. A museum in Germany gave it to her and is now showing it.

Erdmann, 62, said it was very surprising. She said a man came to her door and told her he had post from her grandfather. He said a message in a bottle had been found, and the name on the card was her grandfather’s. The visitor was a family-history researcher. He found her in Berlin after the letter was given to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg, a northern port city.

The bottle was a brown beer bottle. It had been in the water for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it in his catch while fishing in the Baltic Sea near the northern city of Kiel. Holger von Neuhoff, a museum worker in charge of ocean and science, said it was the oldest bottled message he had seen. He said the museum has older documents without bottles, but this bottle and document together are the oldest right now. He said it is in extremely good condition.

Researchers believe Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913. He did it while hiking with a group that enjoyed nature. He was 20 years old then.

Most of the postcard could not be read. But the Berlin address on the front could be read. The card also politely asked the finder to send the note to his home address.

Erdmann said her grandfather also put two stamps from that time in the bottle, so the finder would not have to pay. She said he did not think it would take 101 years.

She said the message made her feel emotional. She did not know her grandfather because he died at age 54, six years before she was born.

She said she knew very little about him, but learned that he was a writer. She said he was open-minded, believed in freedom, and thought people should respect each other. She said he did a lot for young people and later traveled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful because she could see where her family roots came from.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and traveling around the world. She also called herself open-minded. She said what her grandfather taught his two daughters was taught to her by her mother, and she has passed it on to her sons.

Even though she was happy to get the message, she said she hopes people will not throw bottles with messages into the sea today. She said the sea is already full of bottles and rubbish, so no more should be thrown in.

The message and bottle will be shown at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts will try to read the rest of the text. It is not clear what will happen to the bottle later, but Erdmann hopes it will stay at the museum.

She said they want to add some photos with the bottle, so visitors can see the young man who threw it into the water.
