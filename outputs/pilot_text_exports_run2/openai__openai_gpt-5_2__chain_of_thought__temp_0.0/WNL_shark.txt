Opposition to Western Australia’s shark cull has grown. Thousands of people went to beaches across Australia to ask the state’s premier to stop the policy. RSPCA Australia and Virgin Atlantic owner Richard Branson also spoke against it.

The policy involves catching and killing sharks longer than three metres. It began after what the state government called an “unprecedented” number of shark attacks on Western Australia’s coast. A 35-year-old surfer was killed in November 2013. He was the sixth person to die from a shark attack in two years.

But the Shark Attack File says that across all of Australia, there has been an average of one shark-related death each year for the last 50 years.

Kate Faehrmann, a board member at the Sea Shepherd Conservation Society, spoke at a protest in Perth. She said the policy will not work. She said drumlines are used to catch sharks, but they can kill many animals. She said they can kill sharks of any size, and also dolphins, turtles, and other animals. She said this is why the community does not want the cull.

Thousands of people protested at Perth’s Cottesloe Beach and Sydney’s Manly Beach. Hundreds also protested at Glenelg in south-west Adelaide, and at beaches in Victoria and Queensland. Faehrmann said the protests showed many Australians want sharks protected. She said that even with fear from movies like Jaws, people are coming out in large numbers to say the ocean belongs to sharks too and they should not be killed.

Anthony Joyce, a surfer who once had his foot caught in a shark’s mouth, said the number of sharks being killed will not make a difference.

The state government has refused to give a running total of how many sharks have been killed. There have been reports that sharks smaller than three metres were released after being caught on drumlines. Drumlines are floating drums anchored to the sea bed, with bait hanging on hooks under them.

Conservationists say there is no evidence the cull will reduce shark attacks on humans. They say no earlier cull used only drumlines. Researchers at the University of Western Australia say the recent rise in shark attacks in the state may be linked to Western Australia having the fastest-growing population in Australia, not to more sharks.

Richard Peirce, chairman of the UK conservation charity the Shark Trust, said the cull would not work and could even bring more predators closer to the coast. He said it is sad that the government did not try other options and chose a plan that is ineffective and makes things worse. He said drumlines are not selective. He warned that even if they are checked during the day, leaving them in overnight could attract other predators, drawn by sharks and other animals that are hooked and injured.

Worldwide in 2012, there were 80 unprovoked shark attacks, and seven were fatal. In comparison, humans kill nearly 100m sharks each year. RSPCA Australia said it believes the cull is not justified. It said there is no evidence that more attacks are caused by more sharks. Instead, it said the increase fits with changes in population and human behaviour, meaning more people are in the water.

Richard Branson told Fairfax Radio the policy was backfiring. He said Western Australia Premier Colin Barnett may have thought it would help tourism, but he believes it will do the opposite. He said it makes people think there is a big problem when there is not, and it may stop people from visiting Perth and the surrounding countryside. He said it will only make people worry for no good reason.
