Kenton Cool usually likes to talk, but now he can hardly speak. After working hard high up in the mountains, his voice sounds rough and hoarse. He is now in Kathmandu, the capital of Nepal. That morning, he flew down from Everest base camp.

Cool is thinking about a surprising set of climbs he finished the weekend before. Early on Saturday morning, he reached the top of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of three main summits in the Everest “horseshoe.” These peaks surround an icy valley called the Western Cwm.

Later that same day, he climbed to the top of Everest. He reached the summit in complete darkness early on Sunday. Then he and his climbing partner went on to the top of Lhotse on Monday morning. Lhotse was the third peak in this three-mountain challenge.

“It was a snatched opportunity,” he says. “For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. That doesn’t take away the physical achievement of what I did. I’ve set the bar at a certain level. But whoever comes along next will move the bar further and do it without ropes or bottled oxygen.”

Sixty years after Everest was first climbed, many reports look back to Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. They remember an earlier time, before today’s commercial climbing and damage to the environment. I ask Cool to look ahead and imagine what top climbers might do 60 years from now.

“I hate to think,” he says. He mentions the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. In April, Steck fled the mountain after what Cool calls “an altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck planned a hard trip: climb Everest’s west ridge (first climbed in 1963), go down to the South Col, and then immediately climb Lhotse by a new route. He planned to do it all without fixed ropes. “Ueli had been training like a machine,” Cool says. “He’s a climber in a class all his own. He’s technically brilliant but he had also taken his physical condition to an astronomic level. It would have been amazing to see what he could have done.”

What tourism will look like is another question. One sign is a helicopter rescue by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro’s angry words helped start the conflict at Camp 2. Moro flew back to Everest on Tuesday in a high-powered helicopter. He rescued a very sick or injured climber at 7,800 metres.

This was the highest rescue ever done on Everest. It also shows how helicopter flights have risen very fast in recent years. By 2073, the mountain could even have a helipad on the South Col. Tourists might arrive there while breathing bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing how rescues can happen for both climbers and the many more trekkers who go only as far as base camp.

Mountain geographer and environmentalist Alton Byers says it is not clear if the Everest region can keep handling the fast growth of tourism. He says climate change and tourism together are putting new pressure on the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers in the Everest region are shrinking, and some are even disappearing. This is already having a big effect. “Everywhere you go, people are talking about how there’s less water. There’s less water for agriculture and less water for all the new lodges that are getting built.”

Byers says that in the Sherpa town of Namche Bazaar, workers are laying a new five-mile pipeline to bring water. It is needed because more tourists want showers and flush toilets. The local stream is now polluted with human waste. It also does not have enough water for a town that, in high season, is extremely crowded. “Every village is digging a pit just beyond the houses for garbage. Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world,” he says. Human waste at base camp is now handled well and taken away in plastic barrels. But Byers says the barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley, and it could leak into the region’s streams and rivers.

“These problems can be solved, but we need to get serious about it,” he says. “One climber can spend $85,000 climbing Everest. And that’s fine. But at some point we’re going to have to address these other priorities. For half a million dollars a year, you could solve most of them.”

Climate change is harder. Byers works with local conservation groups to find and plan for climate-change effects. They often look for new water sources or set up rainwater harvesting. Fast growth of glacial lakes is also a constant danger. These lakes could burst and flood the Sherpa homeland. “There’s going to come a time when people are going to have to get out of their way.”

Changing weather also affects tourism. More cloud cover during times that are usually clear is closing Lukla Airport more often. Lukla Airport is the main entry point to the Everest region. A new road for 4x4 vehicles is being built to Lukla to make sure tourists and their money can keep coming. But Byers worries that roads in Nepal are spreading too quickly and too cheaply. He says this can cause serious soil erosion and landslides.

“Everest is the icon everyone knows,” he says. “It’s the canary in the coalmine that everyone understands. It’s the perfect laboratory for figuring out how to address some of these problems, like the impacts of climate change and tourism.”
