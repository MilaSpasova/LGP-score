Brazil’s newest funk star, Anitta, has gained millions of fans. She brought the favela sound into popular music. But she is now in the middle of a debate about skin colour.

Anti-discrimination campaigners and writers say the music industry’s fastest rising star had to give up showing her blackness to succeed in a mostly white, middle-class market.

The argument grew after people shared “before and after” photos. The photos show that Anitta’s skin looks much lighter since she signed a deal with Warner.

In the first photo, from when she was not well known, she looked darker. In the second photo, a marketing picture after she became famous, she looks paler. People argue about why. Some mention skin-whitening products and cosmetic surgery, but others say it is more likely Photoshop changes. The difference has started the discussion again about whether you must look pale to get ahead in Brazil.

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote a post about what she called hidden discrimination in media and marketing. She said Anitta’s image change showed this. Arraes wrote that people do not want to accept they are racist, and they think Brazil is a “multiracial democracy,” but statistics show that is not true. She said the “whitening” shows a society that does not support diversity. She wrote that people connect white with rich, nice, successful, and good, and they see black as the opposite.

Anitta was born Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

She has now become very famous with an album and a big hit song, “Show das Poderosas.” It reached number one on the charts and got 52 million views on YouTube.

Many people love her as a pop idol with catchy songs and a strong message. Her supporters also present her as a bridge between the mostly black and mixed-race shanty towns on Rio’s hillsides and the richer and whiter neighbourhoods below.

She has made baile funk less extreme for a bigger audience. She reduced suggestive dancing, gangsta references, and explicit lyrics. But now some people ask if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract a richer group of buyers. Arraes said that if pop stars have curly hair, they may feel forced to straighten it. If they have a big nose, they may feel forced to get rhinoplasty. She said this can hurt self-esteem and create a “vicious cycle.”

This topic is sensitive in Brazil, a country with many mixed-race people. Brazil was one of the last big countries to ban slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. But race and ancestry matter less than skin colour. And even though Brazil aims to be a multiracial democracy, there is a clear link between skin tone and inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Up to 2011, black or mixed-race students also spent two years less in school on average.

The government says this gap is getting smaller because of quota systems for university places and other kinds of affirmative action. But the difference is still easy to see. Most business and government executives are white, while most low-level jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In Ipanema, Gávea, and other rich areas, you are much more likely to see black nannies pushing strollers with white toddlers than a white nanny pushing a black child.

Deciding who is what colour is complicated. In the 2010 census, people who called themselves white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million said black, two million said Asian, and 0.5% said indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology lecturer at the Federal University of Pernambuco, said Anitta won over the middle class by taking a rebellious sound and making it calmer and easier for everyone to accept.

He said this was not done by “racial whitening.” He said what changed was the social place where Anitta made her art: from the periphery to the centre.

Others say people are making too much of the colour issue. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who worked with Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot, but not her colour.

He said he did not believe it was whitening. He said it was more about how her team uses makeup, hairstylists, and clothing. He also said he did not think this happened because people pressured her. He said she always liked to show off, sing, and dance, and that it was natural for her. He said he believed this naturalness is why she is successful today.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador in north-eastern Brazil, said the bias was not in the public, but in music producers and media leaders. He said the public showed they care about music itself when they chose Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, as the recent winner of the talent show The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for comment. But in a recent interview, she said identity should be defined by the person.

She said an “all-powerful” woman does not need to be beautiful. She said a woman can be marvellous and powerful because of her attitude. She said she tries to show everyone that we can be who we want.
