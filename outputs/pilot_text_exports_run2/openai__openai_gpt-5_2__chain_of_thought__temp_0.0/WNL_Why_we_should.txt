James Hamblin is a senior editor at The Atlantic magazine. In his series “If Our Bodies Could Talk,” he tried a challenge: he stopped showering as much to see what happens when we clean our bodies too much. He took fewer showers, and when he did shower, he did not use shampoo or soap.

He found something many other people have found. When we use a lot of soap, body wash, and scrubbing tools, our skin works harder to get back to its normal balance. Showering can wash away the skin’s natural oil and bacteria. This can happen even more when we use hot water and cleaning products.

After a shower, you might feel tight and dry. That is because some of your skin’s natural moisture has been washed away. Also, your skin, like your gut, has millions of helpful bacteria. Frequent rubbing and scrubbing can destroy these bacteria. When bacteria grow back after being washed off, they can favor microbes that cause a bad smell. So showering too often may actually make you smell more. When you stop showering and using soap, your skin may go through an early adjustment time that can seem unpleasant. After that, the skin often returns to balance, oil production slows, and healthy bacteria grow again.

In the end, Hamblin agreed with other people who avoid soap and showers: the human body can work well on its own. It is not only about smell or looks. Dermatologists suggest that using less soap can improve acne and eczema. Taking fewer showers and using fewer cleaning products can also help the environment. The average shower lasts seven minutes and uses 65 litres of water. That is 65 litres of clean, drinkable water that we mix with soap and send down the drain each day, sometimes more than once.

Clean water is becoming harder to ignore as California has another summer of drought. It is becoming clear that clean water is one of the world’s most valuable resources, and it may soon be in short supply. If you also think about the environmental impact of all those body wash bottles, there are strong reasons to let your body be more natural.

If this idea makes you feel uncomfortable because you remember being close to people who do not shower much and still smell, stay calm. Many people who shower less still use deodorant if they need it. Everyone’s natural smell is different, and it can change with diet, hydration, and exercise. Also, washing your hands with soap is still recommended as an important way to reduce the spread of infectious diseases.

You do not need to stop all at once like Hamblin did. But between cleaning many times a day and never showering at all, there is a simple middle choice: reduce. Skip some showers, use less soap, and let the helpful bacteria grow a little.
