It was a warm summer evening. I decided to swim near Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland, where I moved in 2012. In the water, a woman was swimming with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty often swims with a group of people.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Since then, hundreds of people have swum with her, so she seems very friendly. She was even in an Irish tourist-board ad where a girl says she wants to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty’s stomach, and it looked safe and fun. About 20 tourists and local people were on the pier watching. Right after I got into the water, Dusty left the woman and suddenly acted in a wild way. Later I learned that she gets very protective of her space when she is with someone.

Her tail was moving fast. At first, I thought she was just showing off. Then I realized she might be angry. I tried to swim back to the pier to get out. But very quickly, Dusty slammed into me with her nose. It was very strong and painful, and she was moving very fast. I was thrown forward.

People on the pier stared at me in shock. Dusty stayed beside me, still hitting the water with her tail. That scared me most. I thought that if she hit me with her tail, I could sink.

I reached the pier, but I could not climb out because I was hurt. I felt deep fear. I shouted for help. A man reached down and pulled me onto the steps. Then another man came. He said he was an orthopaedic surgeon who specialized in injuries from the sea. He had been driving into Doolin when he saw what was happening.

I was very cold and very worried. I did not know how bad my injuries were, and I was most afraid of bleeding inside my body. He checked me and said he could not feel signs of that, but he thought I probably had broken bones. Later I learned I had six fractures in my spine, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in the hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for several weeks. I could not work for five months and had trouble moving, with stiffness and pain. Later I was diagnosed with post-traumatic stress. After coming close to death, I became anxious about everything and reacted in ways I never had before. I felt like people were looking at me the wrong way. Loud noises became hard for me. I also had memory loss.

Three months before the accident, I had opened a health-food shop. But I had to give it up because I could not work anymore. That year was the hardest of my life, but now it is behind me. I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building my own osteopathy practice. I understand patients better because I have been one.

I am thankful that I am healthy, and I really want to stop other people from getting hurt. Many people think dolphins are always safe and kind. Who expects a dolphin to attack a person? If you see a dangerous animal coming at you with its teeth showing, you feel afraid. But dolphins look like they are smiling.

I am not angry with Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her space, and she is a wild animal that can act without warning. People need to know that. Many people come to swim with her and do not understand how dangerous it can be. People said my injuries were among several that summer.

After the man pulled me out, Dusty swam away. Then she came back. She floated straight up and down next to me and looked at me. We looked into each other’s eyes, and I felt she was sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin, and the anger was gone. The people on the pier were amazed. After that moment with her, my terror ended. I felt at peace with her.
