Dan Sullivan was late to breakfast with the Guardian. The police had stopped him while he was riding his motorcycle.

Sullivan says he works too much. He misses dinner dates. He forgets to give gifts.

So, like many people in Silicon Valley, the 27-year-old venture capitalist (an investor in new companies) made a start-up to help: BetterBoyfriend.me. It sends girlfriends and wives a gift every month for about $70.

Sullivan works in finance. He has been testing the program quietly. He has signed up about 350 boyfriends. They bring in about $17,000 in revenue each month. He says most members are his Harvard and investor friends. They include venture capitalists, founders, and workers at companies like Apple, Google, and Facebook. Their girlfriends have been getting gifts from Sullivan for the last year.

Each month, members pick from a list of seven gifts, like chocolate, tea sets, and manicures. The package is sent to the boyfriend with no brand name and no label.

Sullivan tests ideas by buying Facebook ads to see how much interest they get. BetterBoyfriend.me became popular. Sullivan said, “BetterBoyfriend hit something. It’s a pain point everyone has.”

BetterBoyfriend.me was not Sullivan’s first idea. For a couple of days, he thought about sharing dogs. He said he saw rich guys who looked miserable walking dogs. He said a dog is only valuable 5% of the time. He wondered if someone could babysit the dogs, and other people could rent them.

Another start-up, called InvisibleBoyfriend, lets users create a believable social partner. This “boyfriend” is always available to talk. The result is a “boyfriend” who can text with you all day.

Founder Kyle Tabor started it to give users a fake boyfriend to stop unwanted attention. But he found that people can become attached to their computer-made partners. He said many users want companionship through conversation, not just “proof” of a relationship to make people leave them alone.

For Sullivan and Tabor, the surprise was the real relationships they formed with customers. Sullivan says he has started to feel like a relationship helper for the boyfriends.

Sullivan says he made mistakes. At first, the packages had receipts with his name, Dan Sullivan. One boyfriend wrote to him and said, “Listen, dude, she’s not mad but Cynthia found out.”

About 50% of the women who get BetterBoyfriend.me packages know Sullivan is involved. Sullivan says this is linked to age. He thinks that after people have been married for a while, they do not keep many secrets.

Over the year, Sullivan says he has gotten to know the boyfriends well. They even delivered a gift to a hospital delivery room. He said the key is to remember that his relationship is with the boyfriend.

When he first started the company, he put tags that said BetterBoyfriend.me on flower bouquets. He went to Dolores Park, a popular relaxing spot in the Mission District, where many young tech workers hang out.

He said he looked for couples and gave the flowers to the girl, but the boyfriend did not like it. He said it made sense. So he changed his plan and started giving the bouquets to the boyfriends.
