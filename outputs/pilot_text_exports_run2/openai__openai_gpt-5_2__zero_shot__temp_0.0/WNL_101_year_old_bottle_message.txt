Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8th April, 2014, she told people about a special moment. She received a message in a bottle, 101 years after her grandfather threw it into the Baltic Sea. People think it is the world’s oldest message in a bottle. A museum in Germany gave it to her and is showing it to visitors.

Erdmann, 62, said it was very surprising. She said a man came to her door and told her he had mail from her grandfather. He said a message in a bottle had been found, and the name on the card was her grandfather’s. The man was a family-history researcher. He found her in Berlin after the letter was given to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg.

The bottle was a brown beer bottle. It had been in the water for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it in his catch in the Baltic Sea near the northern city of Kiel. Holger von Neuhoff, who works at the museum, said this was the oldest bottled message he had seen. He said the museum has older documents without bottles, but with both the bottle and the document together, this is the oldest right now. He said it is in very good condition.

Researchers think Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913. He was on a hike with a nature group. He was 20 years old then.

Much of the postcard could not be read. But people could read the Berlin address on the front. They could also read the writer’s polite request that the finder send the note to his home address.

Erdmann said her grandfather also put two stamps in the bottle. This was so the finder would not have to pay. She said he did not think it would take 101 years.

She said the message made her feel emotional. She did not know her grandfather because he died at age 54, six years before she was born.

Erdmann said she knew little about him, but learned that he was a writer. She said he was open-minded, believed in freedom, and thought people should respect each other. She said he did a lot for young people and later travelled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful because she could see where her roots came from.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and travelling around the world. She said she is open-minded too. She said what he taught his two daughters, her mother taught her, and she then taught her sons.

Even though she was happy to get the message, she said she hopes others will not throw bottles with messages into the sea. She said today the sea already has many bottles and much rubbish.

The message and the bottle will be on display at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts will try to read the rest of the text. It is not clear what will happen to the bottle later, but Erdmann hopes it will stay at the museum.

She said they want to add some photos with the bottle. Then visitors can see the young man who threw the bottle into the water.
