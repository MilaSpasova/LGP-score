Some cities are trying unusual ideas to make life better. City life can be fun, but it is not always good for the environment. People in cities often face pollution, traffic, and fewer green spaces. Here are five creative ideas.

Pop-up parks
Many cities seem built more for cars than for people. A pop-up park is a simple idea: turn an empty parking space into a small park with a few plants. The PARK(ing) project started as an art experiment in San Francisco and later spread around the world. People have also made temporary city farms and ecology demonstrations. The movement has a day of action every September.

Underground bike storage
Not everyone in a city uses a car. More people are riding bikes, but they need a safe place to keep them. A Tokyo engineering company called Giken designed an “eco-cycle, anti-seismic underground bicycle park.” It is a round storage space that is only seven metres wide and goes deep underground. It can hold 204 bikes. Owners press a button, and the system brings their bike up in about 13 seconds.

Glow-in-the-dark trees
Most people think of holiday lights when they imagine glowing trees, but Dutch designer-artist Daan Roosegaarde has another idea. He created an experimental “bioluminescent” plant. The technology mixes DNA from glowing sea bacteria with part of a plant’s DNA to make a glow like a jellyfish. Trials are happening to make a larger version. Roosegaarde hopes it could one day replace normal street lights.

Footstep power
In West Ham in east London, many commuters and shoppers walk over a raised walkway near the underground station. Some may feel the surface move slightly under their feet. This small movement helps power the streetlights above. The floor has smart tiles that collect energy from footsteps and turn it into electricity. The UK company Pavegen made this system and also installed it at London’s Heathrow Airport and other places.

Supertrees
Singapore’s Gardens by the Bay has man-made “supertrees.” They are steel-framed and can be up to 50 metres high. Flowers and ferns grow on them. Their canopies help take in and spread out heat. They can also collect rainwater and help ventilate two climate-controlled biomes (large conservatories) below. Eleven of the 18 trees also have solar panels on their branches.
