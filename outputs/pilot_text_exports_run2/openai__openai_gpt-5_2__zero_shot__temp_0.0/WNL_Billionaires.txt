A thick layer of bird droppings covers a gold-colored railing at one of Britain’s most expensive homes. Pigeon bones lie among broken mirrors, and water runs through broken parts of the ceiling. This house is called The Towers. It is a £30m palace on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London. People have kept its damage secret until now.

The Towers is one of ten mansions on The Bishops Avenue. This street is a famous part of London’s expensive housing market. These mansions have been almost empty since they were bought about 25 years ago. People believe they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. Their columns are breaking. Their swimming pools, covered in mosaic tiles, are full of rubble. Nature has moved in so much that owls live there.

This empty and broken look can be seen along the street. Lloyds Bank says The Bishops Avenue is the second most expensive street in Britain. At the same time, many people in London find it hard to buy a home because prices rise by 11.2% a year. But 16 mansions on the most expensive part of the avenue sit empty. Many are behind locked gates. Their windows are covered with steel bars, and the gardens are overgrown. Guard dogs watch the grounds.

Across the street is another empty mansion worth £18m, with broken windows and paint that stops people from climbing. Another has metal bars on the windows and has been sold for £20m.

Even so, prices keep rising. Dryades, a mansion that was owned until recently by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007 and is believed to be worth about £30m today. Heath Lodge, where fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou was murdered in 1984 by a silver bullet, is worth £13m today. It was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

This can feel very upsetting for people who struggle to pay for a home in one of the world’s most expensive cities. A security guard on the street said it was frustrating to see so much space—enough to house dozens of people—falling apart.

Rich royals from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia were some of the first to come to this road near Hampstead Heath. Iranians came here after the fall of the shah. Now, Chinese buyers are following Russians and Kazakhs who have paid millions to live on this famous street. Estate agents say it is as well known as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions have been for sale for £65m and £38m. They promise Italian marble, lifts with leather inside, and special panic rooms.

But on the grounds of the empty homes, stone fountains are falling apart and lawns have turned into wet bogs. Inside one house, water drips through a big crystal chandelier onto a thick carpet. Parts of the ceiling have fallen down. Moss grows through broken bricks. Mirror tiles are scattered in a bathroom. The swimming pool has about a foot of dirty, salty water, and flowers grow through the tiles. Wooden parts of the sauna bend outward.

The most dramatic damage is at The Towers. It is a grand mansion among many trees, including hornbeams, oaks, and limes. Its large halls are full of pigeons. Water pours through three floors, and green algae covers the walls. The water drops into a huge, empty swimming pool in the basement. Unopened wooden crates marked “bullet-proof glass” show that the earlier owners worried about security.

Today, very few people live on The Bishops Avenue all the time. A security guard outside one mansion said the owners were not there. Another guard outside Royal Mansion would not say if anyone was home. A staff member at another mansion warned the Guardian about guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare businessman with personal wealth of £45m, said he is one of the few people who live there permanently.

He said, “It’s not a neighbourly place, where you can chat over the fence. To be honest, I have never seen what my neighbours look like. Next door, a Saudi princess spent £35m on a new house and I’ve never seen her. There are about three houses that are lived in 24/7 and half of the properties are occupied three to six months a year. The other half, who knows if they come or not?”

These broken, very expensive homes show a property culture where the world’s richest people buy British homes as investments. One Hyde Park, a group of flats in Knightsbridge, is another example. More than half of the flats there are registered with the council as empty or second homes.

Still, people on the avenue talk about building £5m apartments instead of £50m mansions, to bring people back.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, plans to rebuild one valuable site on the avenue. He wants to make 20 apartments with a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

He said, “If you build a big house and try and sell for £30m to £40m, it won’t sell. Locals won’t buy and so you have to bring in overseas buyers.”

But it still seems unlikely that these empty homes will help fix the housing crisis. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some of the broken homes could become affordable homes. He said the land price would be too high.

He said, “Very wealthy people own property there. Sometimes they live in them and sometimes they don’t.”
