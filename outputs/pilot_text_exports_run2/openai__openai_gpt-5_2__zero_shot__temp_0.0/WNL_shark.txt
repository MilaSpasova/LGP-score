Opposition to Western Australia’s shark cull has grown. Thousands of people went to beaches around Australia to ask the state’s premier to stop the policy. RSPCA Australia and Virgin Atlantic owner Richard Branson also spoke against it.

The policy catches and kills sharks longer than three metres. It began after what the state government called an “unprecedented” number of shark attacks on Western Australia’s coast. In November 2013, a 35-year-old surfer was killed. He was the sixth person to die from a shark attack in two years.

However, the Shark Attack File says that across all of Australia, there has been about one shark-related death a year on average for the last 50 years.

Kate Faehrmann from the Sea Shepherd Conservation Society spoke at a protest in Perth. She said the policy will not work. She said drumlines, which are used to catch sharks, can kill many animals. She said they can catch and kill sharks of any size, and also dolphins, turtles, and other animals.

People protested at Perth’s Cottesloe Beach and Sydney’s Manly Beach. Hundreds also protested at Glenelg in south-west Adelaide, and at beaches in Victoria and Queensland. Faehrmann said the protests showed many Australians want sharks protected. She said that even though people fear sharks, many people are coming out to say the ocean belongs to sharks too, and they do not want sharks killed.

Anthony Joyce, a surfer who once had his foot caught in a shark’s mouth, said the number of sharks killed will not make a real difference.

The state government has refused to give a running count of how many sharks have been killed. There have been reports that sharks smaller than three metres have been released after being caught. Drumlines are floating drums anchored to the sea bed, with bait on hooks under them.

Conservationists say there is no evidence the cull will reduce shark attacks on humans, and no earlier cull has used only drumlines. Researchers at the University of Western Australia say the recent rise in shark attacks in the state may be linked to the state having the fastest-growing population in Australia, not to more sharks.

Richard Peirce, chairman of the UK charity the Shark Trust, said the cull would not work and could even bring more predators closer to the coast. He said it is sad the government did not try other ways instead of killing. He also said that leaving drumlines in the water overnight could attract other predators, drawn by injured animals on the hooks.

Around the world in 2012, there were 80 unprovoked shark attacks, and seven were fatal. This was compared to nearly 100m sharks killed by humans each year. RSPCA Australia said it believes the cull is not justified. It said there is no evidence attacks are rising because there are more sharks. Instead, it said the rise fits with changes in population and human behaviour, because more people are in the water.

Richard Branson told Fairfax Radio the policy was backfiring. He said the premier, Colin Barnett, may have thought it would help tourism, but he believed it would do the opposite. Branson said it makes the problem look bigger than it is and may stop people from visiting Perth and the surrounding countryside. He said it may only make people worry for no reason.
