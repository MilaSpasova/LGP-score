Kenton Cool usually likes to talk, but now he can hardly speak. He climbed high mountains, and the thin air made his voice rough. He is now in Kathmandu, the capital of Nepal. He flew down from Everest base camp that morning.

Cool is thinking about three big climbs he finished over one weekend. Early on Saturday, he reached the top of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of the three main summits in the Everest “horseshoe.” This horseshoe surrounds an icy valley called the Western Cwm.

That same day, he climbed to the top of Everest. He reached the summit in complete darkness early on Sunday. Then he and his climbing partner kept going. On Monday morning, they reached the summit of Lhotse, the third peak in the three-peaks challenge.

Cool says it was a rare chance. For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. He says this does not remove what he achieved. But he thinks the next person may do even more, maybe without ropes or bottled oxygen.

Sixty years after Everest was first climbed, many stories look back to Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. People talk about an earlier time before modern business tourism and damage to the environment. I ask Cool to look ahead and imagine what top climbers might do 60 years from now.

Cool says he does not like to think about it, but he talks about the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. Steck left the mountain in April after what Cool calls “an altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck planned to climb Everest’s west ridge, first climbed in 1963. Then he planned to go down to the South Col and immediately climb Lhotse by a new route, all without fixed ropes. Cool says Steck trained extremely hard. He says Steck was a special climber, very skilled and very fit. Cool says it would have been amazing to see what Steck could have done.

Tourism is a different question. One sign of change is a helicopter rescue by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro’s angry words helped cause the conflict at Camp 2. On Tuesday, Moro flew a high-powered helicopter back to Everest to rescue a sick or injured climber at 7,800 metres.

This was the highest rescue ever done on Everest. It shows that helicopter flights have increased very fast in recent years. By 2073, the mountain might even have a helipad on the South Col. Tourists could be brought there while breathing bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing rescue possibilities for both climbers and the many trekkers who go as far as base camp.

Mountain geographer and environmentalist Alton Byers says it is not clear if the Everest region can keep handling fast-growing tourism. He says climate change and tourism together are creating new problems for the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers in the Everest region are retreating, and some are disappearing. This is already causing major effects. Byers says people everywhere talk about having less water. There is less water for farming and less water for the new lodges being built.

Byers says that in the Sherpa town of Namche Bazaar, a new five-mile pipeline is being built to bring more water. This is to meet the growing tourist demand for showers and flush toilets. The local stream is polluted with human waste, and it does not have enough water. In the busy season, the town is extremely crowded. Byers says every village digs a pit near the houses for garbage. He says Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world. At base camp, human waste is now handled well and removed in plastic barrels. But Byers says the barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley, and it could leak into the region’s water.

Byers says these problems can be solved, but people must take them seriously. He says one climber can spend $85,000 to climb Everest. He says that is fine, but people also need to deal with other needs. He says that for $500,000 a year, most of these problems could be solved.

Climate change is harder. Byers works with local conservation committees to plan for climate change. They often look for new water sources or use rainwater harvesting. Another danger is that glacial lakes are growing quickly. Some could burst and flood the Sherpa homeland. Byers says there may come a time when people will have to move out of the way.

Changing weather is also affecting tourism. More clouds during times that are usually clear are closing Lukla Airport more often. Lukla is the main gateway to the Everest region. A new road for 4x4 vehicles is being built to Lukla to make sure tourists and their money can still arrive. But Byers worries that Nepal is building roads cheaply. He says this can cause serious soil erosion and landslides.

Byers says Everest is an icon that everyone knows. He says it is like a warning sign that everyone understands. He says it is a perfect place to learn how to deal with problems like climate change and tourism.
