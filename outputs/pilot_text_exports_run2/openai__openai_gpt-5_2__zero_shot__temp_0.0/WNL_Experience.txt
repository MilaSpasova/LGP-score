One beautiful summer evening, I decided to swim near Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland. I moved there in 2012. A woman was in the water with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty often swims with a group of people.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Hundreds of people have swum with her, so she can seem tame. She was even in an Irish tourist ad where a girl says she wants to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty’s tummy. It looked safe and friendly. About 20 tourists and local people were watching from the pier. Soon after I got in the water, Dusty left the woman and suddenly became very aggressive. Later I learned Dusty is very protective of her space when she is with someone. Her tail was hitting the water hard. At first I thought she was just showing off, but then I realized she might be angry.

I tried to swim back to the pier to get out. But almost immediately, Dusty crashed into me with her nose. It was very strong and very painful. I was pushed forward.

People on the pier stared at me in shock. Dusty stayed near me, still hitting the water with her tail. That scared me the most. I thought if she hit me with her tail, I could go under the water and not come back up.

I reached the pier, but I could not climb out because I was hurt. I was terrified. I yelled for help. A man reached in and pulled me onto the steps. Then another man came and said he was an orthopaedic surgeon who specialized in injuries from the sea. He had been driving into Doolin and saw what was happening.

I was very cold and very worried. I did not know how badly I was hurt, and I was most afraid of bleeding inside my body. The surgeon checked me and said he could not feel signs of that, but he thought I probably had broken bones. Later I learned I had six fractures in my spine, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in the hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for several weeks. I could not work for five months, and I had trouble moving, with stiffness and pain. Then doctors said I had post-traumatic stress. After the near-death experience, I felt anxious and overreacted in ways I never had before. I felt like people were looking at me the wrong way. Loud noises bothered me, and I had memory loss.

Three months before the accident, I had opened a health-food shop. But I had to give it up because I could not work anymore. It was the hardest year, but now it is in the past. I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building my own osteopathy practice. I understand patients better because I have been one.

I am thankful that I am healthy. I want to stop other people from getting hurt. People often think dolphins are always kind, so they trust them. Who would expect a dolphin to attack a person? If you see a dangerous animal coming at you with its teeth showing, you feel scared. But dolphins have a wide smile that looks friendly.

I am not angry with Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her space, and she is a wild animal that can be unpredictable. People need to understand that. Many people come to swim with her and do not realize how dangerous it can be. People said my injuries were among several injuries that summer.

After the man pulled me out of the water, Dusty swam away. Then she came back and floated upright next to me, looking at me. We looked into each other’s eyes, and I felt she was sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin. The anger was gone. The people on the pier were amazed. After that moment with her, my terror ended. I felt at peace with her.
