Anitta is a new Brazilian star of funk music. She has millions of fans because she brought the sound of the favela into popular music. But people are now debating her skin colour.

Some anti-discrimination groups and writers say Anitta had to give up looking black to become popular with a mostly white, middle-class audience.

The debate started after people shared old and new photos of her. In the older photo, before she was famous, she looks darker. In the newer photo, used for marketing after she became famous, she looks lighter. Some people think this could be from skin-whitening products, cosmetic surgery, or more likely, Photoshop. The big difference made people talk again about whether a person must have lighter skin to succeed in Brazil.

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote that this shows hidden discrimination in media and advertising. She said many people do not want to accept that racism exists. She said the statistics show Brazil is not as equal as people think. She also said society often links being white with being rich and successful, and being black with the opposite.

Anitta was born Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

Now she is very famous. She released an album and had a big hit song, Show das Poderosas. The song reached the top of the charts and got 52 million views on YouTube.

Many fans like her as a pop star with strong messages and catchy songs. The people who support her also present her as a bridge between mostly black and mixed-race shanty towns on Rio’s hills and the richer and whiter areas below.

She has made baile funk less shocking for a bigger audience. She reduced suggestive dancing, gangsta references, and explicit lyrics. But now people ask if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract a richer audience. Arraes said pop stars may feel forced to change things like curly hair or a big nose. She said this can hurt self-esteem.

Race and colour are sensitive issues in Brazil. Brazil was one of the last large countries to ban slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. But in Brazil, skin colour can matter more than ancestry. There is also a clear link between skin tone and inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Until 2011, black or mixed-race students also spent, on average, two fewer years in school.

The government says this gap is getting smaller because of university quota systems and other affirmative action. But inequality is still easy to see. Most business and government leaders are white, while many low-paid jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In rich areas like Ipanema and Gávea, you are more likely to see black nannies pushing strollers with white toddlers than the other way around.

Skin colour categories in Brazil are not simple. In the 2010 census, people who called themselves white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million said black, two million said Asian, and 0.5% said indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology lecturer at the Federal University of Pernambuco, said Anitta became popular with the middle class by taking a rebellious style of music and making it calmer and easier for everyone to accept. He said this was not done by “racial whitening.” He said the main change was where she made her art: from the periphery to the centre.

Other people say the colour issue is too big. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who worked with Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot, but not her colour. He said makeup, hairstyling, and clothing can explain it. He also said he does not think she was pressured. He said Anitta always liked to sing, dance, and show her style, and that this is why she became successful.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador, said the prejudice was more in music producers and media leaders than in the public. He said the public showed they could choose music for its quality by making Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, the winner of the talent show The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for a comment. But in a recent interview, she said identity should be defined by the person. She said a powerful woman does not need to be beautiful. She said she tries to show people that we can be who we want.
