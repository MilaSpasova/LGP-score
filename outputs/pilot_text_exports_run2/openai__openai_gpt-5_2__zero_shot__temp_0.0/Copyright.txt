Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is theft. He says it is like putting your hand in someone’s pocket and taking their wallet. He wrote this in an article for Index on Censorship. Pullman is president of the Society of Authors. He strongly supports copyright laws. He says people should not download music or books without paying.

Pullman says the technology is very impressive, but people may not see that what they do is wrong. He says it is shocking that someone can take an artist’s work for free.

His article came after music industry leaders met British Prime Minister David Cameron at Downing Street. They talked about web piracy.

Pullman wrote the His Dark Materials trilogy. He says authors and musicians can spend years working in poverty and without being well known. When they finally make something people enjoy, he says they may still lose the chance to earn money from it. He says the rule is simple: if we enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

In the next issue of the magazine, Pullman is in a dialogue with Cathy Casserly. She is chief executive of Creative Commons. Creative Commons offers open content licences that let creators control how their work is shared. Casserly says copyright has problems because it was made in an “analogue age.” She says copyright often stops people from sharing, building on, and remixing other people’s work.

Casserly says artists should think of new ways to share their work and make money from it. She quotes science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees that current copyright laws do not work well in the digital age. The editor, Rachael Jolley, says the laws can treat consumers like criminals. She says we need new ways for artists, writers, and musicians to earn a living.

Illegal downloading is very large. Ofcom collected data for the UK from November 2012 to January 2013. It suggests 280 million music tracks were pirated. It also suggests 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files were pirated.

Ofcom says 18% of internet users over age 12 admit they recently pirated content. It says 9% fear getting caught. Pullman also writes that it is still surprising how fast and easy it is to get music as MP3 downloads.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron appointed Conservative MP Mike Weatherley as his adviser on the issue. A spokesman for the BPI said Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creators it protects. The spokesman said they hope Weatherley and the prime minister will support copyright in the work of the UK’s intellectual property office.
