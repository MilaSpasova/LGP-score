This invention could be like Trevor Baylis’s wind-up radio in the 1990s, before the internet. It is a cheap light that gets free power from gravity. It could stop people in Africa and India from using dangerous kerosene lamps.

But the British designer, Patrick Hunt, had trouble getting money from banks or venture capital to start selling it. He said getting funding was slow and complicated, and people did not want to take a risk.

So he tried crowdfunding on a US website called Indiegogo, which had just opened in the UK. In five days, he reached his goal and raised £36,200. By the end of 2012, in 40 days, he had raised £400,000.

The LED light uses a dynamo. The dynamo is powered when a 10kg bag of rocks slowly goes down. The bag is attached to the light and lifted about 2m high. As it slowly falls, it makes enough power for about 30 minutes of light.

Hunt is getting ready to make the light in China. He plans to test the market again by sending 1,000 lights to Africa before making many more. He hopes to make millions of units.

Hunt is part of a new group of entrepreneurs using crowdfunding to get money. Another new site is InvestingZone. It connects rich people with start-up entrepreneurs.

Indiegogo does not give shares. Instead, people who donate can get “perks” at different donation levels. People who helped fund Hunt’s light felt good about helping others, and they also got their own light.

Danae Ringelmann, a co-founder of Indiegogo, said the “gravity light” shows how crowdfunding can work and how it can test an idea. She said it is fast and fair because it is not about knowing the right people at a bank or a venture capital company. It is about proving your idea to customers and fans so they support it.

She also said that even ideas that do not get funding are worth testing. Then you can learn quickly if it was not a good idea.

Ringelmann lives in the US. She started her career as a Wall Street analyst. In 2008, she quit and tried to help her friends in the arts raise money.

At first, the site focused on films. It launched at the Sundance Film Festival that year.

Five years later, it was raising about $2m a week for new businesses that were starting or growing. In December 2012, it started services in euros and in pounds sterling. It said Britain was its third biggest market. International activity went up 41% since December.

There are many competitors, like Kickstarter, Seedrs, and Funding Circle. But Ringelmann said Indiegogo is different because anyone can start a campaign. No project is too strange.

Indiegogo takes a 4% fee from successful campaigns. If a campaign does not reach its goal, users can choose to refund all the money with no charge, or keep the money raised but pay a 9% fee.

A British woman, Lauren Pears, raised £100,000 on the site to open a “cat café” in London. It is called Lady Dinah’s Cat Emporium. It has not opened yet, but it is described as a place where people can sit in a comfortable wingback chair with a hot cup of tea, a book, and a cat.

Ringelmann said some ideas are rejected by venture capitalists because they do not know if people really want them. She said an entrepreneur can run an Indiegogo campaign, even before launching the project, and the campaign can show that there is a real market.

She said crowdfunding lets you test your market, pricing, and features. It also helps you find new ways to make money and get important feedback.

Ringelmann said ideas are common, and what matters is doing the work well. She said if you are scared someone will steal your idea and do it better and faster, then you are not the right person to do it. She said you need confidence to move fast and learn.

She said expanding into Europe and making a deal with a web payment company is part of a dream to make finance more open to everyone. This will let people pay using local card services like Maestro in the UK and Carte Bleue in France, as well as PayPal. More than 7,000 finance campaigns are live on the site.

Crowdfunding has grown as another choice besides banks. But it is less attractive to very rich investors, who want a stake in a promising business. That might start to change in the UK with the launch of InvestingZone.
