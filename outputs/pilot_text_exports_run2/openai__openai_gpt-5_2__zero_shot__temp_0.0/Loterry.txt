When the lottery balls fall and all six numbers match, some people think it is time to buy an Audi, book a holiday in the US, and call an estate agent to buy a house. A study says many lottery millionaires spend money like this.

The UK National Lottery started in 1994. Since then, it has made 3,000 millionaires. Together, they have won more than £8.5bn in total. On average, each winner got £2.8m. The study says their money has also helped create 3,780 more millionaires among their children, family, and friends. The authors of the study were Oxford Economics.

Many winners (59%) stop working right away. But 19% keep doing their job, and 31% do unpaid volunteer work. The study says 98% of winners’ spending stayed in the UK. By spending on homes, cars, and holidays, each winner is estimated to support six full-time jobs for one year.

The winners added almost £750m to GDP and created more than £500m in tax money for the Exchequer. Most money was spent on property. Winners spent £2.72bn on their main homes, and £170m on paying off debts and mortgages.

Winners also wanted to keep an income coming in, so they spent £2.125bn on investments. They gave £1.17bn as gifts to family and friends. They spent £680m on cars and holidays.

Camelot, which runs the UK National Lottery, asked for this study to mark the 3,000-winners milestone. The study used research from 100 winners who won £1m or more. It found that the 3,000 winners bought 7,958 houses or flats in the UK, which is about 2.7 each, and spent £3.3bn. Most winners (82%) moved to a new main home, spending an average of £900,000.

Many winners added extras at home. Almost a third (29%) bought a hot tub. A walk-in wardrobe was wanted by 28%. Almost a quarter (24%) chose a home with electric gates. About 22% had a games room, and 7% put in a snooker table.

Bigger homes need help to look after them. About 30% hired a cleaner and 24% hired a gardener. A small number (5%) hired a beautician.

Audis were the favourite car for 16% of winners. Range Rovers and BMWs were also popular (11% each). Mercedes were 10%, and Land Rovers were 5%. Winners spent £463m on 17,190 cars. The average price of their favourite was £46,116.

Holidays were important too. Most winners (68%) chose five-star hotels overseas. The US was the top place to go (27%), and the Caribbean was next (9%). In the UK, caravan sales also increased. In the past 18 years, 10% of millionaires bought a caravan, creating sales of about £7.4m.

Some winners (15%) started their own business. About 9% helped other people start a business, and 6% invested in or bought other people’s businesses. Businesses started or supported by winners employ 3,195 people.

Andy Logan, who wrote the report, said a win affects more people than expected. He said it changes the lives of friends and family, and it also affects the UK economy because so much money is spent in the UK. He said each win creates a “ripple effect” for this generation and often the next.
