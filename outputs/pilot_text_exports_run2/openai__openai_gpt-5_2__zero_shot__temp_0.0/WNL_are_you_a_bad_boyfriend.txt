Dan Sullivan arrived late to breakfast with the Guardian. He was late because police stopped him while he was on his motorcycle.

Sullivan says he works too much. He misses dinner dates. He forgets to give presents.

So the 27-year-old venture capitalist made a start-up to help. It is called BetterBoyfriend.me. For about $70 a month, the service sends a present each month to a girlfriend or wife.

Sullivan works in finance. He has been testing the program quietly. About 350 boyfriends have signed up. They bring in about $17,000 each month. He says most members are his Harvard and investor friends. They include venture capitalists, founders, and workers at companies like Apple, Google, and Facebook. Their girlfriends have been getting presents from Sullivan for the last year.

Each month, members pick from a list of seven gifts, like chocolate, tea sets, and manicures. The package is sent to the boyfriend with no brand name and no label.

Sullivan tests ideas by buying Facebook ads to see what happens. BetterBoyfriend.me became popular fast. Sullivan said it hit a “pain point” many people have.

BetterBoyfriend.me was not his first idea. For a few days, he thought about sharing dogs. He said he saw rich guys looking miserable walking dogs. He thought a dog is only valuable 5% of the time. He asked if someone could babysit dogs and other people could rent them.

Another start-up is called InvisibleBoyfriend. It lets users design a believable social partner who is always available to talk. The result is a “boyfriend” who can text with you all day.

Founder Kyle Tabor made it to help users stop unwanted advances by having a fake boyfriend. But he found that people became attached to their algorithmic partners. He said many users want companionship through conversation, not just “proof” of a relationship.

For Sullivan and Tabor, a surprise was the real relationships they formed with customers. Sullivan says he now feels like a relationship consultant for the boyfriends.

Sullivan says he made mistakes. At first, the packages had receipts with his name, Dan Sullivan. One boyfriend wrote to him and said, “Listen, dude, she’s not mad but Cynthia found out.”

About 50% of the women who get BetterBoyfriend.me packages know Sullivan is involved. Sullivan says this is connected to age. He thinks that after you have been married a while, you do not keep many secrets.

Over the year, Sullivan says he got to know the boyfriends well. They even delivered a package to a hospital delivery room. He said the key is to remember his relationship is with the boyfriend.

When he first started the company, he put BetterBoyfriend.me tags on flower bouquets. He went to Dolores Park in the Mission District, a popular place where many young people and tech workers hang out.

He looked for couples and gave the bouquet to the girl. This did not go well with the boyfriend. So he changed his plan and started giving the bouquets to the boyfriends.
