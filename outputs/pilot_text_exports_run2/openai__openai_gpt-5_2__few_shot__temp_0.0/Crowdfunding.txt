This invention could be very useful. It is a cheap light that gets free power from gravity. It could stop people in Africa and India from using dangerous kerosene lamps.

A British designer, Patrick Hunt, tried to get money from banks and venture capital companies to start selling his invention. But he had a problem. He said getting funding was slow and complicated, the idea was unproven, and nobody wanted to take a risk.

So he tried crowdfunding on a US website called Indiegogo, which had recently opened in the UK. In five days, he reached his goal and raised £36,200. By the end of 2012, after 40 days, he had raised £400,000.

The LED light uses a dynamo. It is powered when a 10kg bag of rocks slowly goes down. The bag is attached to the light and lifted about 2m high. When it slowly falls to the ground, it makes enough power for 30 minutes of light.

Hunt is getting ready to produce the light in China. He will test the market again by sending 1,000 lights to Africa before full mass production. He hopes to make millions of units.

Hunt is part of a new group of entrepreneurs who use crowdfunding to get money. Another new site is InvestingZone, which connects rich people with start-up entrepreneurs.

Indiegogo does not give shares. Instead, people who donate can get “perks” for different donation levels. People who helped fund Hunt’s light felt good about helping poorer people, and they also got their own light.

Danae Ringelmann, a co-founder of Indiegogo, said the “gravity light” shows how crowdfunding can quickly test an idea. She said it is fast, efficient, and based on merit. It is not about knowing decision makers in a bank or a venture capital firm. It is about showing your value to customers and fans, and getting them to support and fund your idea.

She also said even ideas that do not get funding are still worth testing. You can save time by learning early that an idea is not good, and you can learn faster.

Ringelmann lives in the US and began her career as a Wall Street analyst. In 2008, she quit to help friends in the arts raise money.

Indiegogo first focused on films and launched at the Sundance Film Festival in 2008.

Five years later, it was raising about $2m a week for new businesses in start-up and growth stages. In December 2012, it launched euro and sterling services to grow in Europe. It says Britain is its third biggest market. International activity is up 41% since December.

Indiegogo has competitors like Kickstarter, Seedrs, and Funding Circle. But Ringelmann says Indiegogo is the only crowdfunder where anyone can start a campaign, and no project is too wacky.

Indiegogo charges a 4% fee for successful campaigns. If a campaign does not reach its target, users can either return all money to contributors for no charge, or keep the money raised and pay a 9% fee.

A British woman, Lauren Pears, raised £100,000 on the site to open a “cat café” in London. It is called Lady Dinah’s Cat Emporium. It has not opened yet. It is advertised as a place where people can sit in a wingback chair, have a hot cup of tea, read a book, and be with a cat.

Ringelmann said some campaigns are rejected by venture capitalists because they are not sure the market wants the product. She said entrepreneurs can run an Indiegogo campaign without even launching the product, and the campaign can show proof that there is a market. She said this helps people test the market, pricing, and features, find new ways to make money, and get important feedback.

Ringelmann said ideas are common, and success is about doing the work well. She said if you are afraid someone will steal your idea and do it better and faster, then you are not the right person to do it. She said you need confidence to move fast and learn.

Ringelmann said Indiegogo’s expansion into Europe and a deal with a web transaction provider are part of a dream to democratize finance. This will allow payments through local card services like Maestro in the UK and Carte Bleue in France, as well as PayPal. More than 7,000 finance campaigns are live on the site.

Crowdfunding has grown as an alternative to banks, but it is less attractive to big investors who want a stake in a business. This could start to change in the UK with the launch of InvestingZone.
