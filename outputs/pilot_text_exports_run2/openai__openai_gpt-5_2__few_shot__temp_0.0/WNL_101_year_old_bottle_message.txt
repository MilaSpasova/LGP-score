Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8 April 2014, she told a special story. She got a message in a bottle 101 years after her grandfather threw it into the Baltic Sea. People think it may be the oldest message in a bottle in the world. A museum in Germany gave it to her and is showing it.

Erdmann, 62, said it was very surprising. A man came to her door and said he had post from her grandfather. He said a bottle with a message had been found. The name on the card was her grandfather’s name. The man was a family-history researcher. He found her in Berlin after the letter was taken to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg.

The bottle was a brown beer bottle. It was in the water for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it while fishing in the Baltic Sea near the city of Kiel. Holger von Neuhoff, a curator at the museum, said it was the oldest bottled message he had seen. He said the museum has older documents without bottles. But with both the bottle and the document, this is the oldest for now. He said it is in very good condition.

Researchers think Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913. He was on a hike with a nature group. He was 20 years old.

People could not read most of the postcard. But they could read the Berlin address on the front. They could also read his polite request. He asked the finder to send the note to his home address.

Erdmann said he also put two stamps in the bottle. This was so the finder would not have to pay. But he did not think it would take 101 years.

Erdmann said the message made her feel emotional. She did not know her grandfather, because he died at 54.

She said she knew very little about him. But she learned he was a writer. She said he was open-minded and believed in freedom. She said he believed everyone should respect each other. She said he did a lot for young people. Later, he travelled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful to learn about her family roots.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and travelling. She said she is open-minded too. She said what he taught his two daughters, her mother taught her, and she taught it to her sons.

She was happy to get the message, but she said people should not throw bottles with messages into the sea today. She said the sea already has many bottles and much rubbish.

The message and bottle will be on display at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts will try to read the rest of the text. It is not clear what will happen to the bottle later, but Erdmann hopes it will stay at the museum.

She said they want to show some photos with the bottle. This will help visitors see the young man who threw it into the water.
