A lot of bird droppings cover a gold rail at one of Britain’s most expensive homes. There are pigeon bones, broken mirrors, and water running through broken parts of the ceiling. The house is called The Towers. It is a £30m home on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London. People have kept its damage secret until now.

The Towers is one of ten big houses in the middle of The Bishops Avenue. These homes have been almost empty since they were bought about 25 years ago. It is believed they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. The columns are breaking. The swimming pools with mosaic tiles are full of broken pieces. Nature has taken over, and owls have moved in.

This sad scene is found all along the street. Lloyds Bank says The Bishops Avenue is the second most expensive street in Britain. At the same time, many people in London cannot afford a home because prices rise 11.2% a year. But 16 mansions on the most expensive part of the street are empty. Many are behind locked gates, with steel bars on windows, overgrown gardens, and guard dogs.

Across the street is another damaged house worth £18m, with broken windows and anti-climb paint on the walls. Another house has metal bars on the windows and was sold for £20m.

Even so, prices keep rising. Dryades, a mansion that was owned until recently by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007 and is believed to be worth about £30m today. Heath Lodge, where fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou was murdered in 1984 by a silver bullet, is worth £13m today. It was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

For people who struggle to pay for housing, these empty, falling-apart mansions can feel very painful to see. One security guard said it was upsetting to watch so much space—enough for dozens of people—being left to rot.

Rich royals from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia were some of the first to move to this curved road near Hampstead Heath. Iranians came after the fall of the shah. Now Chinese buyers are coming too, after Russians and Kazakhs spent millions to buy homes there. Estate agents say the address is as famous as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions were for sale for £65m and £38m. They offered Italian marble, lifts with leather padding, and luxury panic rooms.

But on the land around the empty mansions, stone fountains are breaking and lawns have turned into wet bogs. Inside one mansion, water drips through a huge crystal chandelier onto a thick carpet that is rotting under parts of a fallen ceiling. Moss grows through broken bricks. Broken mirror tiles are spread across a bathroom. The swimming pool has about a foot of dirty saltish water, and flowers grow through its tiles. Wooden boards stick out from the sauna.

The most dramatic ruin is The Towers. It is a large mansion among acres of hornbeams, oaks, and limes. Pigeons live in its big halls. The walls are bright green with algae. Water runs down through three floors and drops into a huge, empty basement swimming pool. Unopened wooden boxes marked “bullet-proof glass” show the security worries of the earlier owners.

Today, very few people live on The Bishops Avenue all the time. A security guard outside one mansion said the owners were not there. Another, outside Royal Mansion, would not say if anyone was home. A worker at another mansion warned the Guardian about the guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare mogul with personal wealth of £45m, said he is one of the few permanent residents.

He said, “It’s not a neighbourly place, where you can chat over the fence. To be honest, I have never seen what my neighbours look like. Next door, a Saudi princess spent £35m on a new house and I’ve never seen her. There are about three houses that are lived in 24/7 and half of the properties are occupied three to six months a year. The other half, who knows if they come or not?”

These broken, very expensive houses show a culture where the richest people in the world buy British property as an investment. One Hyde Park, a group of apartments in Knightsbridge, is another example. More than half the flats there are registered with the council as empty or second homes.

Still, people on the avenue talk about building £5m apartments instead of £50m mansions, to bring people back.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, plans to rebuild one valuable site on the avenue into 20 apartments. It would have a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

He said, “If you build a big house and try and sell for £30m to £40m, it won’t sell. Locals won’t buy and so you have to bring in overseas buyers.”

But it still seems unlikely that these empty homes will help solve the housing crisis. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some of the ruined houses could become affordable homes. He said the land price would be too high.

He said, “Very wealthy people own property there. Sometimes they live in them and sometimes they don’t.”
