From glow-in-the-dark trees to underground bike storage and solar-powered bins, this text looks at unusual ideas to make cities easier to live in.

Living in a city has many good things, but it is harder to live in a sustainable way. City people often face pollution, traffic, and fewer green spaces.

Inventors have new ideas. Here are some of the stranger solutions to make cities more livable.

Pop-up parks
Some cities look like they are built more for cars than for people. A pop-up park is simple. You take an empty car park, some spare change, and a few plants, and you make a small park. The PARK(ing) project started as an art experiment in San Francisco. It has spread around the world. People also try temporary urban farms and ecology demonstrations. The movement has a day of action every September.

Subterranean storage
Not everyone in cities uses a car. Bikes are becoming more popular. But people ask: where can you keep a bike safe? A Tokyo engineering company called Giken made an “eco-cycle, anti-seismic underground bicycle park”. It is a cylinder only seven metres wide. It goes deep into the ground and can hold 204 bikes. Owners can get their bike back by pressing a button. The system brings the bike up in about 13 seconds.

Glow-in-the-dark trees
Many people think of Christmas decorations when they think about glowing trees. Dutch designer and artist Daan Roosegaarde has a different idea. He created an experimental “bioluminescent” plant. The technology mixes DNA from glowing marine bacteria with the chloroplast genome of a plant. This makes a glow like a jellyfish. Tests are happening to make a large industrial version. Roosegaarde hopes it could one day replace normal street lights.

Footfall harvesting
In West Ham in east London, many commuters and shoppers cross a raised walkway near the underground station every day. Most do not notice the soft feeling under their feet. The rubber surface bends about five millimetres. This movement helps power the streetlights above. The floor has smart tiles that catch energy from people’s footsteps and turn it into electricity. Pavegen, a UK company, has put a similar system in London’s Heathrow Airport and other places.

Supertrees
Singapore’s Gardens by the Bay has man-made trees called “supertrees”. They are steel-framed and can be up to 50 metres tall. Flowers and ferns grow on them. Their metal tops help take in and spread out heat. They can collect rainwater. They also help provide air ventilation for two “climate-controlled biomes” below (large conservatories). Eleven of the 18 trees also have solar panels on their “branches”.
