Opposition to Western Australia’s shark cull has grown. Thousands of people went to beaches across Australia and asked the state’s premier to stop the policy. RSPCA Australia and Virgin Atlantic owner Richard Branson also spoke against it.

The cull is the catching and killing of sharks longer than three metres. It started after what the state government called an “unprecedented” number of shark attacks on the Western Australia coast. A 35-year-old surfer was killed in November 2013. He was the sixth person to die from a shark attack in two years.

However, the Shark Attack File says that across all of Australia there has been an average of one shark-related death each year for the last 50 years.

Kate Faehrmann, a board member of the Sea Shepherd Conservation Society, spoke at a protest in Perth. She said the policy will not work. She said drumlines, used to catch sharks, can kill many animals. She said they can catch sharks of many sizes, and also dolphins, turtles, and other animals. She said this is why the community does not want the policy.

Thousands of people protested at Perth’s Cottesloe Beach and Sydney’s Manly Beach. Hundreds also protested at Glenelg in south-west Adelaide, and at beaches in Victoria and Queensland. Faehrmann said the protests showed that Australians want sharks protected. She said that even with fear from movies like Jaws, people are coming out in large numbers to say the ocean is the sharks’ home and they do not want sharks killed.

Anthony Joyce, a surfer who once had his foot caught in a shark’s mouth, said the number of sharks they will kill will make no difference.

The state government has refused to give a running total of how many sharks have been killed. There have been reports that sharks smaller than three metres were released after being caught. Drumlines are floating drums fixed to the sea bed, with bait on hooks under them.

Conservationists say there is no evidence the cull will reduce shark attacks on humans. They say no earlier cull used only drumlines. Researchers at the University of Western Australia say the recent group of attacks may be linked more to Western Australia having the fastest-growing population in Australia, not to more sharks.

Richard Peirce, chairman of the UK charity the Shark Trust, said the cull will not work and could attract more predators closer to the coast. He said the government ignored good advice and chose an approach that is ineffective and counterproductive. He also said drumlines can attract other predators, especially if the lines are left in the water overnight, because animals on the hooks may be injured.

Around the world in 2012, there were 80 unprovoked shark attacks, and seven were fatal. This was compared with almost 100m sharks killed by humans each year.

RSPCA Australia said it believes the cull is not justified. It said there is no evidence the increase in attacks comes from more sharks. It said the increase fits changes in population and human behaviour, meaning more people are in the water.

Richard Branson told Fairfax Radio the policy is backfiring. He said Premier Colin Barnett may have thought it would help tourism, but he thinks it will do the opposite. He said it advertises a problem that is not large, and may stop people from wanting to visit Perth and the countryside around it. He said it will only make people worry for no good reason.
