Anitta is a new Brazilian funk star. She has millions of fans. She helped take music from the favelas into the mainstream. But there is a debate about her skin colour.

Some anti-discrimination activists and writers say she had to hide her blackness to sell music to a mostly white, middle-class market.

The debate grew after people shared old and new photos of Anitta. In the old photo, before she was famous, her skin looks darker. In a later marketing photo, after she became famous, her skin looks lighter. Some people think this happened because of skin-whitening products, cosmetic surgery, or Photoshop. The photos made people talk again about whether you need lighter skin to succeed in Brazil.

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote online about discrimination in media and marketing. She said many people do not accept that they are racist. She said statistics show Brazil is not really a fair multiracial democracy. She said society often links white skin with being rich and successful, and links black skin with the opposite. She also said this pressure can hurt self-esteem. She gave examples like feeling forced to straighten curly hair or to get a nose surgery.

Anitta’s real name is Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

She became very famous with an album and a hit song, Show das Poderosas. The song reached number one and got 52 million views on YouTube.

Many fans like her as a pop idol with a message and catchy songs. People who support her also present her as a bridge between mostly black and mixed-race poor areas on Rio’s hills and the richer and whiter areas below.

She also changed her style. She reduced suggestive dancing, gang references, and explicit lyrics from baile funk. Now people ask if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract a richer audience.

Race and colour are sensitive issues in Brazil. Brazil was one of the last big countries to end slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. In Brazil, colour can matter more than ancestry. Even though the country wants to be a multiracial democracy, there is a clear link between skin tone and inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Until 2011, black or mixed-race students also spent two fewer years in school on average.

The government says the gap is getting smaller because of university quotas and other affirmative action. But inequality is still clear. Most business and government executives are white. Many low-paid jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In rich areas like Ipanema and Gávea, you often see black nannies pushing strollers with white children.

Colour is hard to define. In the 2010 census, people who called themselves white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million said black, two million said Asian, and 0.5% said indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology lecturer at the Federal University of Pernambuco, said Anitta won middle-class fans by taking a rebellious sound and making it calmer and easier for everyone to accept. He said this was not done by making her look whiter. He said it was a change in social space: from the periphery to the centre.

Others say the focus on colour is too strong. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who worked with Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot, but not her colour. He said it was about makeup, hair styling, and clothes. He said it was not because of pressure, and that Anitta always liked to sing and dance.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador, said the prejudice is more in music producers and media executives than in the public. He said the public showed they cared about music itself by choosing Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, as a recent winner of The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for comment. But in a recent interview, Anitta said identity should be defined by the person. She said a powerful woman does not need to be beautiful. She said she tries to show people that we can be who we want.
