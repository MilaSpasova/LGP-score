Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is theft. He says it is like putting your hand in someone’s pocket and stealing a wallet. In an article for Index on Censorship, Pullman, who is president of the Society of Authors, strongly supports copyright laws. He criticizes internet users who think it is OK to download music or books without paying.

Pullman writes that the technology looks exciting, so people do not see that what they are doing is wrong. He says it is shocking that people can steal an artist’s work and not be punished.

His article was written after leaders from the music industry met British Prime Minister David Cameron at Downing Street to talk about web piracy.

Pullman, the writer of the His Dark Materials trilogy, says authors and musicians often work in poverty and without fame for years. They work hard to make art that their audiences enjoy. But when they finally might earn money, illegal downloading takes that chance away. He says the rule is simple: if we want to enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

In the next issue of the magazine, Pullman writes in a dialogue with Cathy Casserly. She is the chief executive of Creative Commons. Creative Commons offers open content licences that let creators control copyright in a different way. Casserly says copyright has many problems because it was made in an “analogue age”. She says copyright often stops people from sharing, building on, and remixing each other’s work.

Casserly says artists need to think of new ways to distribute and make money from their work. She quotes science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees that there are problems. The editor, Rachael Jolley, says current copyright laws do not work well in the digital age and may turn consumers into criminals. She says new ways are needed so artists, writers, and musicians can earn a living.

The debate is active, and illegal downloading is very large. Ofcom, the independent regulator and competition authority for the UK communications industries, collected data. It suggests that between November 2012 and January 2013 in the UK, people pirated 280 million music tracks, 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files.

Ofcom said 18% of internet users aged over 12 admit they recently pirated content. It also said 9% fear getting caught. Pullman writes that getting music quickly as MP3 downloads is still surprising to people who have been building their iTunes list for a long time.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron appointed Conservative MP Mike Weatherley as his adviser on this issue. A spokesman for the BPI, the record industry trade body, said Mike Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creative producers it protects. The spokesman said they hope Weatherley and the prime minister will influence the UK’s intellectual property office.
