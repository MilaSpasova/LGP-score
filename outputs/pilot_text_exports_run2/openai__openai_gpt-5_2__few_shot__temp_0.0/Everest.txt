Kenton Cool usually likes to talk, but now he can hardly speak. Climbing high mountains has made his voice rough. He is in Kathmandu, the capital of Nepal. He flew down from Everest base camp that morning.

Cool is thinking about three big climbs he finished the weekend before. Early on Saturday, he reached the top of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of three main summits in the Everest “horseshoe.” This horseshoe surrounds an icy valley called the Western Cwm.

Later that same day, he climbed to the top of Everest. He reached the summit in complete darkness early on Sunday. Then he and his climbing partner went on to Lhotse. They reached the top of Lhotse on Monday morning.

Cool says it was a quick chance to do it. For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. He says this does not remove the physical achievement. He says he has set a high level, but the next person may do it without ropes or bottled oxygen.

Sixty years after Everest was first climbed, many stories look back to Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. People compare that time with today, with more business and more damage to the environment. I ask Cool to imagine what top climbers might do 60 years from now.

Cool says he does not like to think about it. But he talks about the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. Steck left the mountain in April after what Cool calls an “altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck planned to climb Everest’s west ridge, first climbed in 1963. Then he planned to go down to the South Col and immediately climb Lhotse by a new route. He wanted to do this without fixed ropes. Cool says Steck trained extremely hard. He says Steck was technically brilliant and also very fit. Cool says it would have been amazing to see what Steck could have done.

Tourism is another question. One sign is a helicopter rescue by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro’s angry language helped cause the conflict at Camp 2. Moro flew back to Everest on Tuesday in a powerful helicopter. He rescued a very sick climber at 7,800 metres.

This was the highest rescue ever done on Everest. It also shows that helicopter flights have increased a lot in recent years. By 2073, there could be more buildings and support on the mountain. There could even be a helipad on the South Col, bringing tourists who breathe bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing how rescue can happen for climbers and for the many trekkers who go as far as base camp.

Mountain geographer and environmentalist Alton Byers says it is not clear if the Everest region can handle fast-growing tourism. He says climate change and tourism together are putting new pressure on the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers are shrinking, and some are disappearing. He says this is already having a big effect. He says people talk about having less water. There is less water for farming and less water for new lodges.

Byers says that in Namche Bazaar, a new five-mile pipeline is being built to bring water. It is to meet tourist demand for showers and flush toilets. The local stream has human waste in it, and it does not have enough water. In high season, the town is extremely crowded. Byers says every village digs a pit near the houses for garbage. He says Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world. At base camp, human waste is handled well and taken away in plastic barrels. But Byers says the barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley. He says it could leak into the area’s water.

Byers says these problems can be solved, but people must take them seriously. He says one climber can spend $85,000 to climb Everest. He says that is fine, but people also need to deal with other needs. He says for $500,000 a year, you could solve most of them.

Byers says climate change is different and harder. He works with local conservation committees to plan for climate change. They often look for new water sources or use rainwater harvesting. He says fast growth of glacial lakes is a constant danger. These lakes could burst and flood the Sherpa homeland. He says there will be a time when people will have to move away.

Changing weather is also affecting tourism. More cloud cover during times that are usually clear is closing Lukla Airport more often. Lukla is the main entrance to the Everest region. A new road for 4x4s is being built to Lukla to keep tourists and money coming. But Byers worries that roads in Nepal are being built cheaply and fast. He says this can cause serious soil erosion and landslides.

Byers says Everest is the famous symbol everyone knows. He says it is like a warning sign that people understand. He says it is a perfect place to learn how to deal with problems like climate change and tourism.
