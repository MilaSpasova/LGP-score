It was a warm summer evening. I decided to swim near Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland. I moved there in 2012. In the water, I saw a woman with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty often swims with a group of people, and she seems close to them.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Since then, hundreds of people have swum with her. Because of this, many people think she is tame. She was even in an Irish tourist-board advertisement, where a girl says she wants to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty’s stomach. About 20 tourists and local people were watching from the pier. I got into the water. Then Dusty suddenly left the woman and became very wild. Later I learned that she protects her space when she is with someone. Her tail was moving fast. At first I thought it was just a show. Then I realized she might be angry.

I tried to swim back to the pier to get out. But very quickly, Dusty hit me hard with her nose. It was very strong and very painful. The hit pushed me forward.

People on the pier looked down at me with shock. Dusty was still next to me, and her tail was still moving wildly. That scared me the most. I thought, if she hits me with her tail, I could go under the water and die.

I reached the pier, but I could not climb out because I was hurt. I felt great fear. I shouted for help. A man reached down and pulled me out onto the steps.

Then another man came. He said he was an orthopaedic surgeon who specialized in marine trauma. He had been driving into Doolin and saw what was happening. I was very cold and very worried. I did not know how bad my injuries were. My biggest fear was internal bleeding. He checked me and said he could not feel signs of internal bleeding, but he thought I probably had broken bones. Later I learned I had six spinal fractures, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for several weeks. I could not work for five months. I had limited movement, stiffness, and pain. Then doctors said I had post-traumatic stress. The near-death experience made me anxious about everything. I reacted strongly in ways I never did before. I felt people looked at me the wrong way. Loud noises became hard for me. I also had memory loss.

Three months before the accident, I had opened a health-food shop. But I had to give it up because I could not work. It was the hardest year. Now it is behind me. I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building my own osteopathy practice. I understand patients better because I have been a patient.

I am thankful that I am healthy. I want to stop other people from getting hurt. People have a nice idea about dolphins and trust them. Who thinks a dolphin will attack a person? If you see a dangerous animal coming with its teeth showing, you feel afraid. But dolphins have a wide smile.

I am not angry with Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her area, and she is a wild animal that can be unpredictable. People need to know this. Many people come to swim with her, and they do not understand how dangerous it can be. People said my injuries were among several injuries that summer.

After the man pulled me out, Dusty swam away. Then she came back. She was up and down in the water next to me and looked at me. We looked into each other’s eyes. I felt she was sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin. The anger was gone. The people on the pier were amazed. After that moment with her, my terror ended. I made peace with her.
