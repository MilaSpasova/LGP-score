James Hamblin is a senior editor at the American magazine The Atlantic. In his series “If Our Bodies Could Talk,” he tried a challenge: he stopped showering so much. He also stopped using shampoo and soap when he did shower.

He learned something many people have learned. When we try very hard to clean our bodies with soap and body wash, our skin may try harder to fix itself. Showering can wash away the skin’s natural oil and helpful bacteria. Hot water and cleaning products can make this stronger.

After a shower, your skin can feel tight and dry. This is because the shower washes away natural moisture. Our skin also has millions of helpful bacteria. Frequent rubbing and scrubbing can kill them. Later, bacteria grow back, but they may be the kind that makes more smell. So showering too often may make you smell worse. When you stop using soap and shower less, there can be an early period that feels unpleasant. After that, the skin often finds balance again, makes less oil, and healthy bacteria grow.

In the end, Hamblin felt the human body can work well on its own. Dermatologists also say using less soap can help acne and eczema.

Showering less can also help the environment. The average shower is seven minutes and uses 65 litres of water. That is 65 litres of clean drinking water mixed with soap and sent down the drain every day, and sometimes more than once.

Clean water is becoming more important, for example as California has another summer of drought. Clean water is one of the world’s most valuable resources, and it may soon be in short supply. Also, many body wash bottles create waste. These are strong reasons to be more natural.

If this idea makes you feel uncomfortable, relax. Many people who shower less still use deodorant if needed. Everyone’s natural smell is different. It can change because of diet, how much water you drink, and exercise. Also, washing hands with soap is still recommended to reduce the spread of infectious diseases.

You do not need to stop suddenly, like Hamblin did. But there is a middle choice. Shower a little less, use less soap, and let the skin’s bacteria grow.
