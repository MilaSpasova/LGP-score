This new light could be as important as Trevor Baylis’s wind-up radio in the 1990s, before the internet. It is a cheap light that gets free power from gravity. It could help stop people in Africa and India from using dangerous kerosene lamps.

The British designer Patrick Hunt tried to get money from banks or venture capital companies to start selling his invention. But it was hard. He said getting funding was slow and complicated, and people did not want to take the risk.

So he tried crowdfunding on a US website called Indiegogo, which had just opened in the UK. In five days, he reached his goal and raised £36,200. His campaign was very popular. In 40 days, by the end of 2012, he raised £400,000.

The LED light uses a dynamo. The dynamo is powered when a 10kg bag of rocks goes down. The bag is tied to the light and lifted about 2m high. As the bag slowly falls to the ground, it makes enough power for 30 minutes of light.

Hunt is getting ready to make the light in China. He will test the market again by sending 1,000 lights to Africa before full mass production. He hopes to make millions of units.

Hunt is part of a new group of entrepreneurs who use crowdfunding to raise money. Another new website is InvestingZone, which connects rich people with start-up entrepreneurs.

Indiegogo does not give investors shares. Instead, people who donate can get “perks” at different donation levels. People who helped fund Hunt’s light felt good about helping poorer people, and they also got their own light.

Danae Ringelmann, a co-founder of Indiegogo, says the “gravity light” shows how crowdfunding can work. She says it can quickly test if an idea is good. She said this kind of finance can be fast, efficient, and meritocratic. It is not about knowing people at a bank or a venture capital firm. It is about proving your idea to customers and fans, and getting them to support it with money.

She also says even if an idea does not get funding, it is still worth testing. Then you learn faster and do not waste a lot of time.

Ringelmann lives in the US. She started her career as a Wall Street analyst. In 2008, she quit her job and tried to help friends in the arts raise money.

At first, the site focused on films. It launched at the Sundance Film Festival that year.

Five years later, it was raising about $2m each week for new businesses that were starting or growing. In December 2012, it started services in euros and pounds. It says Britain is its third biggest market. International activity has gone up 41% since December.

Indiegogo has many competitors, like Kickstarter, Seedrs, and Funding Circle. But Ringelmann says Indiegogo is different because anyone can start a campaign. No project is too strange.

Indiegogo takes a 4% fee from successful campaigns. If a campaign does not reach its target, users can choose to give all the money back to contributors for free, or keep the money but pay a 9% fee.

A British woman, Lauren Pears, raised £100,000 on the site to open a “cat café” in London. It is called Lady Dinah’s Cat Emporium. It has not opened yet. It is described as a place where people can sit in a comfortable wingback chair, drink hot tea, read a book, and spend time with a cat.

Ringelmann says some ideas are rejected by venture capitalists because they do not know if people want the product. She says an Indiegogo campaign can show proof that there is a market. Sometimes the campaign is enough, even before the project starts.

She says crowdfunding lets entrepreneurs test the market, test prices, test features, find new ways to make money, and get important feedback.

Ringelmann says many people have ideas, but success depends on doing the work well. She says if you are afraid someone will steal your idea and do it better and faster, then you are not the right person to do it. She says it is important to move fast, learn, and be confident.

Ringelmann says expanding into Europe and making a deal with a web transaction provider are part of a dream to make finance more open to everyone. The deal will allow people to pay using local card services like Maestro in the UK and Carte Bleue in France, as well as PayPal. More than 7,000 fundraising campaigns are live on the site.

Crowdfunding has grown as a choice besides banks. But it is not as attractive to big investors, who usually want a stake in a business. That may start to change in the UK with the launch of InvestingZone.
