Dan Sullivan was late to breakfast with the Guardian. He was stopped by the police while riding his motorcycle.

Sullivan says he works too much. He misses dinner dates and forgets to give presents.

So the 27-year-old venture capitalist started a company to help: BetterBoyfriend.me. For about $70 a month, it sends a present each month to a girlfriend or wife.

Sullivan works in finance. He has been testing the service quietly. About 350 boyfriends have signed up, bringing in about $17,000 each month. Most members are his Harvard and investor friends, like venture capitalists, founders, and workers at companies like Apple, Google, and Facebook. Their girlfriends have been getting presents from Sullivan for the last year.

Each month, members pick from a list of seven gifts, like chocolate, tea sets, or manicures. The package is sent to the boyfriend with no brand name or label.

Sullivan tests ideas by buying Facebook ads to see what happens. BetterBoyfriend.me became popular. He said it solved a problem many people have.

BetterBoyfriend.me was not his first idea. For a few days, he thought about a dog-sharing business. He said he saw rich men looking unhappy walking dogs. He said a dog is only valuable 5% of the time. He thought about having someone babysit dogs so other people could rent them.

Another start-up is called InvisibleBoyfriend. It lets users create a believable social partner who can always talk. The result is a “boyfriend” who can text with you all day.

Founder Kyle Tabor started it to give users a fake boyfriend to stop unwanted attention. But he found that people can get attached to their computer-made partners. He said many users want companionship through conversation, not just “proof” of a relationship.

For Sullivan and Tabor, a surprise was the real connections they formed with customers. Sullivan says he now feels like a kind of relationship consultant for the boyfriends.

Sullivan says he made mistakes. At first, the packages had receipts with his name, Dan Sullivan. One boyfriend wrote to him and said, “Listen, dude, she’s not mad but Cynthia found out.”

About 50% of the women who get BetterBoyfriend.me packages know that Sullivan is part of it. Sullivan says this is connected to age. He thinks that after people have been married for a while, they keep fewer secrets.

Over the year, Sullivan says he has gotten to know the boyfriends well. Once, they even delivered a package to a hospital delivery room. He said the important thing is to remember that his relationship is with the boyfriend.

When he first started the company, he put BetterBoyfriend.me tags on flower bouquets and went to Dolores Park in the Mission District, a popular place for young people and tech workers.

He looked for couples and gave flowers to the girl, but the boyfriends did not like that. So he changed his plan and started giving the flowers to the boyfriends.
