Anitta is a new and very popular funk singer from Brazil. She has millions of fans. She helped bring the sound from the favela into popular music. But people are now arguing about skin colour.

Some anti-discrimination groups and writers say that, to become famous with the mostly white middle class, Anitta had to give up part of her “blackness.”

The argument started after people shared old and new photos of Anitta. In the older photo, from when she was not famous, she looks darker. In a newer photo used for marketing after she became famous, she looks lighter. People wonder if this happened because of skin-whitening products, cosmetic surgery, or more likely Photoshop. This has started the old question in Brazil: do you need pale skin to succeed?

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote a post about it. She said there is hidden discrimination in media and marketing. She said many people say they are not racist and believe Brazil is a “multiracial democracy,” but statistics show big problems. She said lighter skin is often linked to being rich and successful, and darker skin is often treated as the opposite.

Anitta’s real name is Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

She became widely famous with an album and a big hit song, “Show das Poderosas.” The song reached the top of the charts and got 52 million views on YouTube.

Many people love her as a pop star with catchy songs. Her supporters also present her as a bridge between the mostly black and mixed-race poor areas on Rio’s hills and the richer and whiter areas below.

She has made baile funk less shocking for a wider public by reducing suggestive dancing, gangsta references, and explicit lyrics. Now people ask if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract a richer audience. Arraes said this can pressure stars to change their bodies, like straightening curly hair or getting a nose operation, and that it can hurt self-esteem.

Race and colour are sensitive issues in Brazil. Brazil was one of the last big countries to end slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. But in Brazil, skin colour often matters more than race or ancestry. Even though Brazil wants to be a multiracial democracy, there is a clear link between skin colour and inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Until 2011, black or mixed-race students also spent an average of two fewer years in school.

The government says the gap is getting smaller because of quotas for university places and other affirmative action. But big differences remain. Most business and government leaders are white, while many low-paid jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In rich areas like Ipanema and Gávea, it is common to see black nannies pushing strollers with white children.

Colour is not simple to define. In the 2010 census, people who said they were white became a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million said they were black, two million said they were Asian, and 0.5% said they were indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology lecturer at the Federal University of Pernambuco, said Anitta became popular with the middle class by taking a rebellious style and making it milder and easier for everyone to accept. He said this was not racial whitening. He said it was a change in where she made her art: from the outskirts to the centre.

Other people say the colour issue is exaggerated. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who knew Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot, but not her colour. He said the difference comes from makeup, hair styling, and clothing. He also said she was not forced, and that she always liked to sing, dance, and show off, and that this natural style helped her succeed.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador, said the problem is not the public but music producers and media bosses. He said the public showed they care about the music itself when they chose Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, as the winner of The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for a comment. But in a recent interview, she said each person should define their own identity. She said a powerful woman does not need to be beautiful. She said what matters is attitude, and that people can be who they want.
