Opposition to Western Australia’s shark cull has grown. Thousands of people went to beaches across Australia to ask the state’s premier to stop the policy. RSPCA Australia and Richard Branson, the owner of Virgin Atlantic, also spoke against it.

The policy involves catching and killing sharks longer than three metres. It began after what the state government called an “unprecedented” number of shark attacks on Western Australia’s coast. A 35-year-old surfer was killed in November 2013. He was the sixth person to die from a shark attack in two years.

However, the Shark Attack File says Australia has averaged one shark-related death a year for the last 50 years.

Kate Faehrmann, from the Sea Shepherd Conservation Society, spoke at a protest in Perth. She said the policy will not work. She said drumlines, used to catch sharks, kill many animals. They can catch sharks of any size, and also dolphins, turtles, and other animals. She said this is why the community does not want the policy.

Thousands protested at Cottesloe Beach in Perth and Manly Beach in Sydney. Hundreds also protested at Glenelg in south-west Adelaide, and at beaches in Victoria and Queensland. Faehrmann said the protests showed many Australians want sharks protected. She said people are coming out to say the ocean belongs to sharks too, and they do not want them killed.

Anthony Joyce, a surfer who once had his foot caught in a shark’s mouth, said killing sharks will make no real difference.

The state government has refused to give a running total of sharks killed. There have been reports that sharks smaller than three metres were released after being caught. Drumlines are floating drums anchored to the sea bed, with bait on hooks hanging under them.

Conservationists say there is no evidence the cull will reduce shark attacks on humans. They say no previous cull has used only drumlines. Researchers at the University of Western Australia say the recent increase in attacks may be linked to the state’s fast-growing population, not to more sharks.

Richard Peirce, chairman of the UK charity the Shark Trust, said the cull will not work and could bring more predators closer to the coast. He said it is sad the government did not try other options instead of killing. He said drumlines can attract other predators, especially if they are left in the water overnight.

Around the world in 2012, there were 80 unprovoked shark attacks, and seven were fatal. Humans kill nearly 100 million sharks each year.

RSPCA Australia said the cull is not justified. It said there is no evidence attacks are rising because there are more sharks. It said the rise fits better with changes in population and human behaviour, because more people are in the water.

Richard Branson told Fairfax Radio the policy could backfire. He said the premier, Colin Barnett, may have thought it would help tourism, but it could do the opposite. He said it makes the problem seem bigger than it is, and may stop people from wanting to visit Perth and the countryside. He said it could worry people for no good reason.
