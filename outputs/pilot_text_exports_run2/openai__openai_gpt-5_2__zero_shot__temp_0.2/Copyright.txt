Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is theft. He says it is like putting your hand in someone’s pocket and stealing their wallet. He wrote this in an article for Index on Censorship. Pullman is president of the Society of Authors. He strongly supports copyright laws. He criticizes internet users who download music or books without paying.

Pullman writes that new technology looks impressive, but people may not see that what they are doing is wrong. He says it is outrageous to steal an artist’s work and not be punished.

His article was published after leaders from the music industry met British Prime Minister David Cameron at Downing Street to talk about web piracy.

Pullman is the writer of the His Dark Materials trilogy. He says authors and musicians often work for years in poverty and without attention. When their work finally becomes successful, he says illegal downloading can take away their chance to earn a living. He says the rule is simple: if we want to enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

In the next issue of the magazine, Pullman is in a discussion with Cathy Casserly. Casserly is the chief executive of Creative Commons. Creative Commons offers open content licences that let creators control copyright in different ways. Casserly says many things are wrong with copyright because it was made in an “analogue age.” She says copyright can stop people from sharing, building on, and remixing each other’s work.

Casserly says artists need to think of new ways to share and make money from their work. She quotes science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees that change is needed. The editor, Rachael Jolley, says today’s copyright laws do not work well in the digital age and could make criminals out of consumers. She says new models are needed so artists, writers, and musicians can earn a living.

Illegal downloading is happening on a very large scale. Ofcom, the UK communications regulator, collected data. It suggests that in the UK, between November 2012 and January 2013, 280 million music tracks were pirated. Also pirated were 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files.

Ofcom also said 18% of internet users over age 12 admit they recently pirated content, and 9% say they fear getting caught. Pullman writes that it is still surprising how quickly people can get music as MP3 downloads, especially to people who have been building their iTunes lists for some time.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron appointed Conservative MP Mike Weatherley as his adviser on the issue. A spokesman for the BPI, the record industry trade body, said Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creative producers it protects. The spokesman said they hope Weatherley and the prime minister will influence the UK’s intellectual property office.
