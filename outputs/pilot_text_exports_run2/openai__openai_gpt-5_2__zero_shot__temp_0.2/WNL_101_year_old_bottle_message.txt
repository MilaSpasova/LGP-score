Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8th April, 2014, she told people about a special moment. She got a message in a bottle that her grandfather had thrown into the Baltic Sea 101 years earlier. People think it may be the world’s oldest message in a bottle. A museum in Germany gave the bottle to Erdmann and is showing it in an exhibit.

Erdmann, 62, said it was a big surprise. “A man stood at my door and told me he had post from my grandfather,” she said. The man said a message in a bottle had been found, and the name on the card was her grandfather’s name. The visitor was a family-history researcher. He found Erdmann in Berlin after the letter was given to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg.

The bottle was a brown beer bottle. It had been in the water for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it while fishing in the Baltic Sea near the city of Kiel. Holger von Neuhoff, a museum curator for ocean and science, said this was the oldest bottled message he had seen. He said the museum has older documents without bottles, but with the bottle and the document together, this is the oldest right now. He also said it is in very good condition.

Researchers think Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913. He was hiking with a nature group. He was 20 years old then.

Most of the postcard cannot be read now. But the Berlin address on the front could be read. The card also asked the finder to send the note to his home address.

Erdmann said her grandfather also put two stamps from that time in the bottle, so the finder would not have to pay. But he did not think it would take 101 years.

Erdmann said the message made her feel emotional. She did not know her grandfather because he died at age 54, six years before she was born.

She said she knew little about him, but learned that he was a writer. She said he was open-minded, believed in freedom, and thought people should respect each other. She said he did a lot for young people, and later traveled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful because she could see where her family roots came from.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and traveling around the world. She said she is open-minded too. She said what her grandfather taught his two daughters, her mother taught her, and she has taught it to her sons.

Even though she was happy about the message, she said she hopes other people will not throw bottles with messages into the sea. She said the sea already has too many bottles and too much rubbish.

The message and the bottle will be shown at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts will try to read the rest of the text. It is not clear what will happen to the bottle later, but Erdmann hopes it will stay at the museum.

Erdmann said they want to show some photos with the bottle, so visitors can see the young man who threw it into the water.
