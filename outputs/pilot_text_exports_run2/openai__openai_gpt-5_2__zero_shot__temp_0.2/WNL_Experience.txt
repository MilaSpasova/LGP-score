One beautiful summer evening, I decided to go for a swim near Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland. I moved there in 2012. A woman was in the water with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty often swims with the same group of people.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Since then, hundreds of people have swum with her, so she can seem very tame. She was even in an Irish tourist-board advertisement where a girl says she wants to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty’s belly, and it looked fun. About 20 tourists and local people stood on the pier watching. Soon after I got into the water, Dusty left the woman and suddenly became wild. Later I learned that Dusty can be very protective of her space when she is with someone. Her tail was moving fast. At first I thought she was just showing off, but then I realized she might be angry.

I knew I had to get out, so I swam toward the pier. But almost right away, Dusty crashed into me with her nose. It was very strong and very painful. The speed shocked me, and I was thrown forward.

People on the pier stared down at me with their mouths open. Dusty was still beside me, and her tail kept flapping. That scared me the most. I thought if she hit me with her tail, I could go under the water and not come back up.

I reached the pier, but I could not get out because I was hurt. I felt pure fear. I yelled for help, and a man reached in and pulled me onto the steps. Then another man arrived. He said he was an orthopaedic surgeon who specialized in marine injuries. He was driving into Doolin and saw what was happening.

I was very cold and very worried. I did not know how badly I was injured, and I was most afraid of bleeding inside my body. The surgeon checked me and tried to calm me. He said he could not feel signs of internal bleeding, but he thought I probably had broken bones. Later I learned I had six fractures in my spine, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in the hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for several weeks. I could not work for five months. I had trouble moving, and I felt stiff and in pain.

Later I was diagnosed with post-traumatic stress. The near-death experience made me anxious about many things. I reacted more strongly than I ever had before. I felt like people were looking at me the wrong way. Loud noises bothered me, and I had some memory loss.

Three months before the accident, I had opened a health-food shop. But I had to give it up because I could not work. It was the hardest year, but now it is in the past. I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building my own osteopathy practice. I understand patients better because I have been one.

I am thankful that I am healthy, and I want to stop other people from getting hurt. Many people think dolphins are always gentle and safe. Who would expect a dolphin to attack a person? If a dangerous animal comes at you with its teeth showing, you know to be scared. But dolphins look like they are smiling.

I am not angry at Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her space, and she is a wild animal that can be unpredictable. People need to understand that. Many people come to swim with her and do not realize how dangerous it can be. People said my injuries were one of several injuries that summer.

After the man pulled me out, Dusty swam away. Then she came back and floated up and down next to me, looking at me. We looked into each other’s eyes, and I felt she was sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin, and the anger was gone. The people on the pier were amazed. After that moment, I did not feel terror anymore. I made peace with her.
