James Hamblin is a senior editor at the American magazine The Atlantic. He tried an experiment for his series “If Our Bodies Could Talk.” He took fewer showers. When he did shower, he stopped using shampoo and soap. He wanted to see what happens when we clean our bodies too much.

He learned something many people have learned. When we scrub and wash a lot with soap and body wash, our skin tries hard to get back to normal again. Showering can wash away the skin’s natural oil and bacteria. Hot water and cleaning products can make this even stronger.

After a shower, you might feel tight and dry. This can happen because your skin’s natural moisture is gone. Also, our skin has millions of helpful bacteria, like our gut does. Frequent rubbing and scrubbing can destroy these bacteria. When the bacteria grow back, they can sometimes include germs that make more odor. So showering too often may make you smell more. If you stop showering and using soap, your skin may go through a first adjustment period. After that, the skin often becomes balanced again, makes less oil, and healthy bacteria grow.

In the end, Hamblin felt the human body can be nice when it works on its own. It is not only about smell or looks. Dermatologists say using less soap can help acne and eczema.

Taking fewer showers can also help the environment. The average shower lasts seven minutes and uses 65 litres of water. That is 65 litres of clean, drinkable water we mix with soap and send down the drain every day, sometimes more than once.

Clean water is becoming more important, as California has another summer of drought. Clean water is one of the world’s most valuable resources, and it may soon be in short supply. Also, body wash bottles affect the environment. These are reasons to let your body be more natural.

If this idea makes you feel uncomfortable, relax. Many people who shower less still use deodorant if they need it. Everyone’s natural scent is different. It can change because of diet, drinking water, and exercise. Also, washing your hands with soap is still recommended to help stop the spread of infectious diseases.

You do not need to stop showering all at once like Hamblin did. But between showering many times a day and never showering, there is a middle choice. Reduce. Skip a few showers, use less soap, and let the helpful bacteria grow.
