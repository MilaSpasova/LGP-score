A thick layer of bird droppings covers a gold-painted railing at one of Britain’s most expensive homes. Pigeon bones are inside, mirrors are broken, and water runs through cracked walls. This place is called The Towers. It is a £30m mansion on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London. People have kept its ruin secret until now.

The Towers is one of ten big houses in the middle of The Bishops Avenue. This street is part of London’s rising property market. These houses have been almost empty for about 25 years. It is believed they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. The columns are breaking, and swimming pools with mosaic tiles are filled with broken pieces. Nature has taken over so much that owls now live there.

This empty and broken look is seen along the street. Lloyds Bank says The Bishops Avenue is the second most expensive street in Britain. At the same time, more people in London struggle to buy a home because house prices rise 11.2% a year. On the most expensive part of the street, 16 mansions are empty. Many are behind locked gates, with metal covers on the windows, overgrown gardens, and guard dogs.

Across the street is another empty mansion worth £18m, with smashed windows and walls covered in anti-climb paint. Another house has metal bars on the windows and was sold for £20m.

Even so, prices keep rising. Dryades, a mansion once owned by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007 and is believed to be worth about £30m today. Heath Lodge, where fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou was murdered in 1984 with a silver bullet, is worth £13m today. It was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

Seeing these empty homes can be painful for people who struggle to afford housing in one of the world’s most expensive cities. One security guard said it was upsetting to watch so much space—enough for dozens of people—fall apart.

Rich royals from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia were among the first to come to this curved road near Hampstead Heath. Iranians came after the fall of the shah. Now Chinese buyers are following Russians and Kazakhs, who have spent millions to live on a street that estate agents say is as famous as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions have been for sale for £65m and £38m. They promise Italian marble, lifts with leather inside, and luxury panic rooms.

But around the empty houses, stone fountains are falling apart and lawns have turned into muddy wet ground. Inside one house, water drips through a huge crystal chandelier onto a thick carpet that is rotting under parts of a fallen ceiling. Moss grows through broken bricks. Broken mirror tiles are scattered in a bathroom. The swimming pool has about a foot of dirty salty water, and flowers grow through the tiles. Wooden slats push out from the sauna.

The most dramatic damage is at The Towers. It is a grand house among acres of hornbeams, oaks, and limes. Pigeons fill its large halls. Water pours through three floors, and the walls are bright green with algae. The water drops into a huge, empty basement swimming pool. Unopened wooden crates marked “bullet-proof glass” show the old owners were worried about security.

Today, very few people live on The Bishops Avenue all the time. A security guard outside one mansion said the owners were not there. Another guard outside Royal Mansion would not say if anyone was home. A staff member at another house warned the Guardian about the guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare mogul with personal wealth of £45m, said he is one of the few people who live there permanently.

He said, “It’s not a friendly place where you can chat over the fence. To be honest, I have never seen what my neighbours look like. Next door, a Saudi princess spent £35m on a new house and I’ve never seen her. There are about three houses that are lived in 24/7 and half of the properties are occupied three to six months a year. The other half, who knows if they come or not?”

These broken, empty mansions show a property culture where the world’s richest people treat British homes as investments. One Hyde Park, a block of flats in Knightsbridge, is another example. More than half the flats there are registered with the council as empty or second homes.

Still, people on the avenue talk about building £5m apartments instead of £50m mansions to bring people back.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, plans to rebuild one of the most valuable sites on the avenue into 20 apartments. It would have a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

He said, “If you build a big house and try and sell for £30m to £40m, it won’t sell. Locals won’t buy and so you have to bring in overseas buyers.”

But using these empty homes to help solve the housing crisis still seems far away. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some of the empty houses could become affordable homes. He said the land price would be too high.

He said, “Very wealthy people own property there. Sometimes they live in them and sometimes they don’t.”
