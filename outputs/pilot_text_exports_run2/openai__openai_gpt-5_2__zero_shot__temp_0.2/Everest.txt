Kenton Cool usually likes to talk, but now he can hardly speak. Climbing high in the mountains has made his voice rough. He is in Kathmandu, the capital of Nepal. That morning, he flew down from Everest base camp.

Cool is thinking about a surprising weekend of climbs. Early on Saturday, he reached the top of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of the three main peaks in the Everest “horseshoe.” This horseshoe surrounds a icy valley called the Western Cwm.

That same day, he climbed to the top of Everest. He reached the summit in complete darkness early on Sunday. Then he and his climbing partner went on to Lhotse. They reached the top of Lhotse on Monday morning.

“It was a snatched opportunity,” he says. “For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. That doesn’t take away the physical achievement of what I did. I’ve set the bar at a certain level. But whoever comes along next will move the bar further and do it without ropes or bottled oxygen.”

Everest was first climbed 60 years ago. Many stories about Everest look back to Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. People talk about an earlier time, before modern commercial climbing and environmental damage. I ask Cool to look ahead and imagine what top climbers might do 60 years from now.

“I hate to think,” he says. But he talks about the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. In April, Steck left the mountain after what Cool calls “an altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck was planning a very hard climb: he wanted to climb Everest’s west ridge, first climbed in 1963. Then he wanted to go down to the South Col and immediately climb Lhotse by a new route, without fixed ropes. “Ueli had been training like a machine,” Cool says. “He’s a climber in a class all his own. He’s technically brilliant but he had also taken his physical condition to an astronomic level. It would have been amazing to see what he could have done.”

Tourism on Everest is another question. One clue is a helicopter rescue by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro’s angry language helped start the conflict at Camp 2. On Tuesday, Moro flew a high-powered helicopter back to Everest to rescue a sick or hurt climber at 7,800 metres.

This was the highest rescue ever done on Everest. It shows how fast helicopter flights have increased in recent years. By 2073, the mountain might even have a helipad at the South Col, bringing tourists who breathe bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing rescue chances for climbers and for the many trekkers who go as far as base camp.

Mountain geographer and environmentalist Alton Byers says it is not clear if the Everest region can keep handling fast-growing tourism. He says climate change and tourism together are creating new problems for the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers are shrinking, and some are disappearing. This is already causing big effects. “Everywhere you go, people are talking about how there’s less water. There’s less water for agriculture and less water for all the new lodges that are getting built.”

Byers says that in the Sherpa town of Namche Bazaar, workers are laying a new five-mile pipeline to bring in water. Tourists want showers and flush toilets. The local stream is polluted by human waste, and it does not have enough water during the busiest season. “Every village is digging a pit just beyond the houses for garbage. Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world,” he says. Human waste at base camp is now handled well and taken away in plastic barrels. But Byers says these barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley, and it could leak into the region’s water.

“These problems can be solved, but we need to get serious about it,” he says. “One climber can spend $85,000 climbing Everest. And that’s fine. But at some point we’re going to have to address these other priorities. For half a million dollars a year, you could solve most of them.”

Climate change is harder. Byers works with local conservation committees to plan for climate change. They often look for new water sources or collect rainwater. Another danger is that glacial lakes are growing fast. Some could burst and cause floods in the Sherpa homeland. “There’s going to come a time when people are going to have to get out of their way.”

Weather changes are also affecting tourism. More cloud cover during normally clear times is closing Lukla Airport more often. Lukla Airport is the main entrance to the Everest region. A new road for 4x4 vehicles is being built to Lukla to keep tourists and money coming. But Byers worries the road building in Nepal is being done cheaply. He says this can cause serious soil erosion and landslides.

“Everest is the icon everyone knows,” he says. “It’s the canary in the coalmine that everyone understands. It’s the perfect laboratory for figuring out how to address some of these problems, like the impacts of climate change and tourism.”
