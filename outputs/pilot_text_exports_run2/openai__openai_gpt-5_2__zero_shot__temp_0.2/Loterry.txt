When the lottery balls fall and all six numbers match, it is time to celebrate. Many lottery millionaires buy an Audi, book a holiday in the US, and call an estate agent. This is based on a study of how jackpot winners spend and invest their money.

The UK National Lottery started in 1994. Since then, it has made 3,000 millionaires. Together they have won more than £8.5bn in total, with an average of £2.8m each. The study says their money also helped make 3,780 more millionaires among their children, family, and friends. The authors of the study were Oxford Economics.

Most winners (59%) stop working right away. But 19% keep doing their normal job, and 31% do unpaid volunteer work. The study says 98% of winners’ spending stayed in the UK. By spending money on homes, cars, and holidays, each winner is estimated to keep six people in full-time jobs for one year.

The winners added almost £750m to GDP, and created more than £500m in taxes for the Exchequer. Most money was spent on property. Winners spent £2.72bn on their main homes, and £170m paying off debts and mortgages.

Winners also wanted to keep having income. They spent £2.125bn on investments. They gave £1.17bn as gifts to family and friends. They spent £680m on cars and holidays.

Camelot, the company that runs the UK National Lottery, paid for the study to mark the 3,000-winners milestone. The study used research from 100 winners who won £1m or more. It found that the 3,000 winners bought 7,958 houses or flats in the UK, about 2.7 each, spending £3.3bn. Most winners (82%) moved to a new main home, spending an average of £900,000.

Many winners added special things to their homes. Almost a third (29%) bought a hot tub. A walk-in wardrobe was wanted by 28%. Almost a quarter (24%) chose a home behind electric gates. 22% had a games room, and 7% put in a snooker table.

Bigger homes need help to look after them. 30% hired a cleaner, and 24% hired a gardener. A small number (5%) hired a beautician.

Audis were the favourite car for 16% of winners. Range Rovers and BMWs were also popular (11% each), and so were Mercedes (10%) and Land Rovers (5%). Winners spent £463m on 17,190 cars. The average price of their favourite car was £46,116.

Holidays were important too. Most winners (68%) chose five-star hotels overseas. The US was the top place to go for 27% of winners, followed by the Caribbean (9%). In the UK, caravan sales also went up. Over the past 18 years, 10% of millionaires bought a caravan, creating sales of about £7.4m.

Some winners (15%) started their own business. 9% helped others start a business, and 6% invested in or bought other people’s businesses. Businesses started or supported by lottery winners employ 3,195 people.

Andy Logan, who wrote the report, said the effect of a win spreads further than expected. He said it changes the lives of friends and family, and also affects the UK economy, especially because so much money is spent in the UK. He said each win creates a ripple effect for this generation and often the next one too.
