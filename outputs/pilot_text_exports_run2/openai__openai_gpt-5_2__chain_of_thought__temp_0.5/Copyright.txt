Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is theft and “very wrong.” He says it is as much theft as reaching into someone’s pocket and taking their wallet.

Pullman wrote this in an article for Index on Censorship. Pullman is president of the Society of Authors. In the article, he strongly defends copyright laws. He is very critical of internet users who think it is OK to download music or books without paying.

He writes that new technology can look so impressive that people do not notice how wrong their actions are. He says it is outrageous that someone can steal an artist’s work and not get punished.

His article came after leaders from the music industry met British Prime Minister David Cameron in Downing Street. They talked about web piracy.

Pullman, who wrote the His Dark Materials trilogy, says authors and musicians often work for years in poverty and in obscurity. He says they work hard to make their work good enough to delight audiences. Then, when they finally reach that point, their chance to make a living can be taken away by illegal downloading. He ends with a simple point: if we want to enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

Pullman is writing in the next issue of the group’s magazine as part of a dialogue with Cathy Casserly. Casserly is the chief executive of Creative Commons. Creative Commons offers open content licences “that lets creators take copyright into their own hands.” Casserly says there are many problems with copyright, which was created “in an analogue age.” She says copyright, by default, shuts the door on many ways people can share, build on, and remix each other’s work—ways that were hard to imagine when the laws were made.

Casserly says artists need to think in new ways about how they share and monetize their work. She quotes the science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees with this concern. The magazine’s editor, Rachael Jolley, said existing copyright laws do not work in the digital age and could make consumers into criminals. She said we need new models for how artists, writers, and musicians earn a living from their work.

The debate is active, and illegal downloading is very large. Data collected by Ofcom (the independent regulator and competition authority for the UK communications industries) suggests that, in the UK between November 2012 and January 2013, 280 million music tracks were digitally pirated. Also pirated were 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files.

Ofcom has said that 18% of internet users aged over 12 admit they have recently pirated content, and 9% say they fear getting caught. Pullman also writes that it is still amazing to many people how quickly music can be got as MP3 downloads, especially for those who have been building up their iTunes list for some time.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron appointed the Conservative MP Mike Weatherley as his adviser on the subject. A spokesman for the BPI, the record industry trade body, said that Mike Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creative producers it is meant to protect. The spokesman said they hope Weatherley’s influence, and the prime minister’s support for copyright, will affect the approach of the UK’s intellectual property office.
