Kenton Cool usually likes to talk. But now he can hardly speak. After working hard high on the mountain, his voice is rough and low.

He is now in Kathmandu, the capital of Nepal. He flew down from Everest base camp that morning. He is thinking about an amazing set of climbs he finished the weekend before.

Early on Saturday morning, he reached the top of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of three main peaks in the Everest “horseshoe,” a curve of mountains around an icy valley called the Western Cwm.

Later that same day, he climbed to the top of Everest. He reached the summit in complete darkness early on Sunday. Then he and his climbing partner kept going. On Monday morning, they reached the summit of Lhotse, the third peak in this three-mountain challenge.

“It was a snatched opportunity,” he says. “For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. That does not take away the physical achievement of what I did. I’ve set the bar at a certain level. But whoever comes along next will move the bar further and do it without ropes or bottled oxygen.”

Sixty years after people first climbed Everest, many reports look back to Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. They also talk about a more innocent time, before today’s commercial tourism and environmental damage. I ask Cool to look ahead and imagine what top climbers might do 60 years from now.

“I hate to think,” he says. But he brings up the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. In April, Steck left the mountain after what Cool calls “an altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck planned to climb Everest’s west ridge (first climbed in 1963), go down to the South Col, and then quickly climb Lhotse by a new route, all without fixed ropes. “Ueli had been training like a machine,” Cool says. “He’s a climber in a class all his own. He’s technically brilliant, and he had also taken his physical condition to an astronomic level. It would have been amazing to see what he could have done.”

What tourism will be like is another question. One sign is a helicopter rescue done by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro’s angry words helped start the fight at Camp 2. On Tuesday, Moro flew back to Everest in a powerful helicopter to rescue a sick climber at 7,800 metres.

It was the highest rescue ever done on Everest. It also shows the fast rise in helicopter flights in recent years. By 2073, the mountain could even have a helipad on the South Col, bringing tourists who breathe bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing what is possible for rescuing both climbers and the many more trekkers who go as far as base camp.

Mountain geographer and environmentalist Alton Byers says it is not clear if the Everest region can keep handling a fast-growing tourism business. He says climate change and tourism together are creating new stress for the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers are shrinking, and some are even disappearing, and this is already having a big effect. “Everywhere you go, people are talking about how there’s less water. There’s less water for agriculture and less water for all the new lodges that are getting built.”

Byers says that in the Sherpa town of Namche Bazaar, people are building a new five-mile pipeline to bring water. This is to meet tourist demand for showers and flush toilets. The local stream is polluted by human waste and does not provide enough water for a town that, in high season, is extremely crowded. “Every village is digging a pit just beyond the houses for garbage. Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world,” he says.

Human waste at base camp is now handled well and taken away in plastic barrels. But Byers says these barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley, and it could leak into the area’s rivers and streams.

“These problems can be solved, but we need to get serious about it,” he says. “One climber can spend $85,000 climbing Everest. And that’s fine. But at some point we’re going to have to address these other priorities. For half a million dollars a year, you could solve most of them.”

Climate change is harder. Byers works with local conservation groups to find and plan for climate change effects. They often look for new water sources or collect rainwater. Glacial lakes are growing quickly, and they could burst and flood the Sherpa homeland. This is a constant danger. “There’s going to come a time when people are going to have to get out of their way.”

Changing weather is also affecting tourism. More clouds during times that are usually clear is closing Lukla Airport more often. Lukla Airport is the gateway to the Everest region. A new road for 4x4 vehicles is being built to Lukla to keep tourists and their money coming. But Byers worries that roads in Nepal are being built too cheaply, which can cause serious soil erosion and landslides.

“Everest is the icon everyone knows,” he says. “It’s the canary in the coalmine that everyone understands. It’s the perfect laboratory for figuring out how to address some of these problems, like the impacts of climate change and tourism.”
