Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8th April, 2014, she told about an unusual moment. She received a message in a bottle. Her grandfather had thrown it into the Baltic Sea 101 years earlier. People think it may be the world’s oldest message in a bottle. A museum in Germany, which is showing it now, gave it to Erdmann.

Erdmann, 62, said it was very surprising. She said a man came to her door and said he had mail from her grandfather. He explained that a message in a bottle had been found, and the name on the card was her grandfather’s. The visitor was a family history researcher. He found her in Berlin after the letter was given to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg, a northern port city.

The bottle was a brown beer bottle. It had been in the water for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it in his catch while fishing in the Baltic Sea near the northern city of Kiel. Holger von Neuhoff, a curator at the museum, said this was the oldest bottled message he had seen. He said the museum has older documents, but they were found without a bottle. He said that with both the bottle and the document, this is the oldest for now. He said it is in very good condition.

Researchers believe Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913. He did it while hiking with a nature group. He was 20 years old.

Most of the postcard could not be read. But people could read the Berlin address on the front. They could also read his polite request that the finder send the note to his home address.

Erdmann said her grandfather also put two stamps in the bottle, so the finder would not have to pay. She said he did not think it would take 101 years.

She said she felt deeply touched by the message. She did not know her grandfather, because he died at age 54, six years before she was born.

She said she knew very little about him. But she learned he was a writer. She said he was open-minded, believed in freedom, and believed everyone should respect each other. She said he did a lot for young people and later traveled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful because she could see where her family came from.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and traveling around the world. She said she is open-minded too. She said what her grandfather taught his two daughters, her mother taught her, and she has taught it to her sons.

Even though she was happy to receive the message, she said she hopes others will not throw message bottles into the sea. She said the sea is now full of bottles and rubbish, so people should not throw more in.

The message and the bottle will be on display at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts will try to read the rest of the text. It is not clear what will happen to the bottle then, but Erdmann hopes it will stay at the museum.

She said they want to add a few photos with the bottle, so visitors can see the young man who threw it into the water.
