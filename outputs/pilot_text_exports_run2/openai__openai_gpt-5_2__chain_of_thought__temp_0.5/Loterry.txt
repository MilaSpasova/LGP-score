The lottery balls have fallen, and all six numbers match. Now it is time to buy an Audi, book a holiday in the US, and phone an estate agent. An analysis says this is what many lottery millionaires do.

The UK National Lottery started in 1994. Since then, it has made 3,000 millionaires. Together they have won more than £8.5bn, with an average of £2.8m each. The study’s authors, Oxford Economics, say the money also helped create 3,780 more millionaires among winners’ children, family, and friends.

Most winners (59%) stop working straight away. But 19% keep doing their regular job, and 31% do unpaid voluntary work. The study says 98% of winners’ spending stayed in the UK. It estimates that each winner’s spending on property, vehicles, and holidays keeps six people in full-time jobs for a year.

Winners added almost £750m to gross domestic product (GDP). They also created more than £500m in tax receipts for the Exchequer. Most money was spent on property. Winners spent £2.72bn on their main homes, and £170m to pay off existing debt and mortgages.

Keeping an income was important. Winners spent £2.125bn on investments. They gave £1.17bn as gifts to family and friends. They spent £680m on cars and holidays.

Camelot, which runs the UK National Lottery, paid for the study to mark the 3,000-winners milestone. The study used research from 100 winners who won £1m or more. It found that the 3,000 winners bought 7,958 houses or flats in the UK, which is 2.7 each on average. They spent £3.3bn. Most winners (82%) changed their main home, spending an average of £900,000.

Many winners added special features to their homes. Almost a third (29%) wanted a hot tub. 28% wanted a walk-in wardrobe. Almost a quarter (24%) chose a home behind electric gates. 22% had a games room, and 7% put in a snooker table.

Bigger homes need care. 30% of winners hired a cleaner, and 24% hired a gardener. A small number (5%) hired a beautician.

Audis were the favourite cars for 16% of winners. Range Rovers and BMWs were also popular (11% each), as well as Mercedes (10%) and Land Rovers (5%). Winners spent £463m on 17,190 cars. The average price of their favourite car was £46,116.

Holidays were also important. Most winners (68%) chose five-star hotels overseas. The US was the top destination for 27%, followed by the Caribbean (9%). In the UK, caravan sales also gained. Over the past 18 years, 10% of millionaires bought a caravan, creating sales of about £7.4m.

Some winners (15%) started their own businesses. 9% helped others start businesses, and 6% invested in or bought other people’s businesses. The study says businesses started or supported by lottery winners employ 3,195 people.

Andy Logan, who wrote the report, said a win has a wider effect than expected. He said it changes the lives of friends and family, and it also affects the UK economy, especially because so much money is spent in the UK. He said each win creates a ripple effect in this generation and often the next.
