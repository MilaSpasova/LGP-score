From glow-in-the-dark trees to underground bike storage and solar-powered bins, here are some unusual ideas to make cities easier and nicer to live in. City life has many good points, but it is getting harder to live in a sustainable way. People in cities deal with pollution, traffic, and fewer green spaces. Inventors have stepped in. Here are some of the wackier solutions.

Pop-up parks
Some cities seem built more for cars than for people. A pop-up park is a simple idea. You use an empty parking space, a little money, and a plant or two to make a small park. The PARK(ing) project started as an art experiment in San Francisco and then spread around the world. It has led to ideas like temporary city farms and ecology demonstrations. The movement has a day of action every September.

Subterranean storage
Not everyone in the city uses a car. Bikes are becoming more popular. But where can people keep a bike safe? An engineering company in Tokyo called Giken designed an “eco-cycle, anti-seismic underground bicycle park.” It is a cylinder that is seven metres wide and goes deep into the ground. It can hold 204 bikes. Owners can get their bike back by pressing a button. The automatic system brings the bike above ground in about 13 seconds.

Glow-in-the-dark trees
When many people think of glowing trees, they think of Christmas decorations. But Dutch designer-artist Daan Roosegaarde has created a “bioluminescent” plant. This experimental technology mixes DNA from glowing marine bacteria with part of a plant to make a glow like a jellyfish. Tests are being done to make a version that can be produced on an industrial scale. Roosegaarde hopes it could one day replace normal street lighting.

Footfall harvesting
Every day, hundreds of commuters and shoppers in West Ham in east London cross an elevated walkway near the underground station. Few notice that the ground feels springy. Even fewer know that the rubber surface flexes by five millimetres and helps power the streetlights above. The walkway has smart tiles that capture energy from people’s footsteps and turn it into electricity. Pavegen, the UK company behind it, has also installed a similar system at Heathrow Airport in London and other places around the world.

Supertrees
Eventually, people made trees that are not real. Singapore’s Gardens by the Bay has a small group of them. These steel-framed “supertrees” can be up to 50 metres tall. Flowers and ferns grow on them, and their metal tops help absorb and spread out heat. They can also collect rainwater and help provide air ventilation for two “climate-controlled biomes” (large conservatories) below. Eleven of the 18 trees also have solar panels on their “branches.”
