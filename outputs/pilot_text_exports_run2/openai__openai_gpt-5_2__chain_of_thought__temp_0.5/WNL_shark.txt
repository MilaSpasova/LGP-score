Opposition to Western Australia’s shark cull has grown. Thousands of people went to beaches around Australia to ask the state’s premier to stop the policy. RSPCA Australia and Virgin Atlantic owner Richard Branson also spoke against it.

The cull means catching and killing sharks longer than three metres. It started after what the state government called an “unprecedented” number of shark attacks on Western Australia’s coast. A 35-year-old surfer was killed in November 2013. He was the sixth person to die from a shark attack in two years.

But the Shark Attack File says Australia as a whole has averaged one shark-related death a year for the last 50 years.

Kate Faehrmann, a board member at the Sea Shepherd Conservation Society, spoke at a protest in Perth. She said they have said from the start that the policy will not work. She said drumlines, used to catch sharks, kill many kinds of animals. She said they can kill sharks of any size, and also dolphins, turtles, and other animals. She said this is why the community does not want the policy.

Thousands of people protested on Perth’s Cottesloe Beach and Sydney’s Manly Beach. Hundreds protested at Glenelg in south-west Adelaide, and at beaches in Victoria and Queensland. Faehrmann said the protests showed Australians want sharks protected. She said that even with fear from movies like Jaws, people are coming out in large numbers to say, “That’s their ocean. We respect them, we love them and we don’t want them killed.”

Anthony Joyce, a surfer who once had his foot caught in a shark’s mouth, said the number of sharks they will kill will make no difference.

The state government has refused to give a running count of sharks killed. There have been reports that sharks smaller than three metres were released after getting caught on drumlines. Drumlines are floating drums anchored to the sea bed, with bait hanging on hooks under them.

Conservationists say there is no evidence the cull will reduce shark attacks on humans, because no earlier cull used only drumlines. Researchers at the University of Western Australia say the recent group of shark attacks in the state may be linked more to the state having the fastest-growing population in Australia, not to more sharks.

Richard Peirce, chairman of the UK-based conservation charity the Shark Trust, said the cull would not work and could even bring more predators closer to the coast. He said the government ignored the best advice and chose an approach that is ineffective and counterproductive. He said the drumlines can attract other predators, especially if left in overnight, because hooked and injured animals can draw them in.

Around the world in 2012, there were 80 unprovoked shark attacks, and seven were fatal. This is compared to nearly 100m sharks killed by humans each year. RSPCA Australia said it believes the cull is not justified. It said there is no evidence that the increase in attacks is from more sharks. Instead, it said, more people are going into the water, linked to changes in population and human behaviour.

Richard Branson told Fairfax Radio the policy was backfiring. He said Western Australia Premier Colin Barnett may have thought it would help tourism, but it will likely do the opposite. He said it advertises a problem that does not exist in a major way, and it may stop people from coming to Perth and the countryside around it. He said it will only make people worry for no good reason.
