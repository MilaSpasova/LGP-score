It was a warm summer evening. I decided to go for a swim near Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland. I moved there in 2012. A woman was already in the water with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty often swims with a group of people, and she seems close to them.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Since then, hundreds of people have swum with her. This can make it seem like she is completely tame. She was even in an Irish tourist-board ad where a girl says she wants to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty’s belly. It looked friendly and safe. About 20 tourists and local people were watching from the pier. Soon after I got into the water, Dusty left the woman and suddenly became very wild. Later I learned she can be very protective of her space when she is with someone. Her tail was hitting the water hard. At first, I thought she was just showing off. Then I realized she might be angry.

I knew I had to get out, so I swam toward the pier. But almost at once, Dusty slammed into me with her nose. It was very strong and very painful. I was thrown forward.

People on the pier stared down at me in shock. Dusty stayed beside me, still hitting the water with her tail. That scared me even more. I thought if she hit me with her tail, I could sink and die.

I reached the pier but I could not climb out because I was hurt. I felt deep fear. I shouted for help. A man reached in and pulled me onto the steps. Then another man came and said he was an orthopaedic surgeon who worked with injuries from the sea. He had been driving into Doolin when he saw what was happening.

I was very cold and very worried. I did not know how bad my injuries were. My biggest fear was bleeding inside my body. He examined me and tried to calm me. He said he could not feel signs of internal bleeding, but he thought I might have broken bones. Later I learned I had six fractures in my spine, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in the hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for several weeks. I could not work for five months, and I had trouble moving, with stiffness and pain. Then doctors said I had post-traumatic stress. The near-death experience made me anxious about everything. I reacted in ways I never had before. I felt like people were looking at me badly. Loud noises became hard to deal with, and I had memory loss.

Three months before the accident, I had opened a health-food shop. But I had to give it up because I could not work anymore. That year was the hardest of my life, but now it is behind me. I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building my own osteopathy practice. I understand patients better because I have been one.

I am thankful that I am healthy. I really want to stop other people from getting hurt. People often think dolphins are always gentle and safe. Who would think a dolphin would attack a person? If a dangerous animal comes at you with its teeth showing, you feel afraid right away. But dolphins have a wide smile, so they do not look scary.

I am not angry at Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her area, and she is a wild animal that can act in unexpected ways. People need to know this. Many people come to swim with her, and they do not understand how dangerous it can be. I was told my injuries were one of several injuries that summer.

After the man pulled me out, Dusty swam away. Then she came back and floated upright next to me, looking at me. We looked into each other’s eyes. I felt she was sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin, and the anger was gone. The people on the pier were amazed. After that moment, I was no longer terrified. I felt at peace with her.
