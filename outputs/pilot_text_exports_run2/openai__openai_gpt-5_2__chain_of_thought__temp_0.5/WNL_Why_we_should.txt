James Hamblin is a senior editor at the American magazine The Atlantic. He tried living with less washing. In his series, “If Our Bodies Could Talk,” he did a no-showering challenge to see what happens when we clean our bodies too much. He took fewer showers. When he did shower, he stopped using shampoo and soap.

He found something many other people have found. The more we try very hard to clean our bodies with soap, body wash, and scrubbers, the more our skin tries to fix its balance. Then we feel like we need to wash again. Showering washes away the skin’s natural oil and bacteria. Some people say that is the whole point. But it can work too well, especially with hot water and cleaning products.

Have you ever felt tight and dry after a shower, like your skin is too small? That is because a lot of your skin’s natural moisture was washed away. Also, your skin has millions of helpful bacteria, like your gut does. Frequent rubbing and scrubbing can kill these bacteria. When bacteria grow back, they may be the kind that make more smell. So showering too often may actually make you smell more. If you stop showering and using soap, your skin may go through an early change that might seem gross. After that, the skin often returns to balance, oil production slows, and healthy bacteria grow again.

After his experiment, Hamblin learned what other people who avoid soap and showers have said for years: the human body can do well on its own. This is not only about smell or looks. Dermatologists say using less soap can help acne and eczema.

Showering less can also help the environment. The average shower lasts seven minutes and uses 65 litres of water. That is 65 litres of clean, drinkable water that we mix with soap and send down the drain every day, sometimes more than once.

Clean water is becoming harder to ignore as California enters another summer of drought. It is becoming clear that clean water is one of the world’s most valuable things, and it may soon be in short supply. Also, think about the waste from all those body wash bottles. These are strong reasons to let your body be more natural.

If this makes you feel nervous because you remember being close to people who do not shower much and still smell, relax. Many people who wash less still use deodorant when needed. Everyone’s natural scent is different and can change with diet, hydration, and exercise. Also, washing your hands with soap is still recommended to help stop infectious diseases from spreading.

You do not have to stop all at once like Hamblin did. But between cleaning many times a day and never showering, there is a simple idea: reduce. Skip a few showers, use less soap, and let the helpful bacteria grow a bit.
