Thick bird droppings cover a gold-colored railing at one of Britain’s most expensive homes. Pigeon skeletons lie near broken mirrors, and water runs through broken ceiling edges. This house is called The Towers. It is a £30m palace on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London. People did not know how badly it was falling apart until now.

The Towers is one of ten large houses in the middle of The Bishops Avenue. Lloyds Bank has said this avenue is the second most expensive street in Britain. Many of these mansions have been almost empty since they were bought about 25 years ago. It is believed they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. Their columns are cracking, and swimming pools with mosaic tiles are filled with broken pieces. Nature has moved in so much that owls now live there.

This empty and ruined scene happens again and again along the avenue. At the same time, more people in London are trying to buy a home, while house prices rise by 11.2% a year. On the most expensive part of The Bishops Avenue, 16 mansions are sitting empty. Many are behind locked gates, with steel grilles on the windows and overgrown gardens watched by guard dogs.

Across the street is another empty mansion worth £18m, with broken windows and walls covered in anti-climb paint. Another house has metal grilles on the windows and was sold for £20m.

Even so, prices keep rising. Dryades, a mansion that was owned until recently by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007 and is believed to be worth about £30m today. Heath Lodge, where fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou was murdered in 1984 with a silver bullet, is worth £13m today. It was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

For people who struggle to pay for a home in one of the world’s most expensive cities, these empty houses can feel painful to see. One security guard said it was very upsetting to watch so many tens of thousands of square feet of space—enough to house dozens of people—falling apart.

In the past, royals with oil wealth from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia were some of the first to come to this curved road near Hampstead Heath. Iranians came here after the fall of the shah. Now, Chinese buyers are following Russians and Kazakhs who have spent millions to get an address that estate agents say is as famous as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions have been for sale for £65m and £38m. They promised lots of Italian marble, lifts with leather padding, and luxury panic rooms.

But on the grounds of the empty mansions, stone fountains crumble and lawns have turned into boggy wet ground. Inside one house, water drips through a huge crystal chandelier onto a thick carpet that is rotting under parts of a collapsed ceiling. Moss grows through broken bricks, and mirrored tiles are scattered across a bathroom. The swimming pool has about a foot of salty, dirty water, and flowers grow through its tiles. Wooden slats push out from the sauna.

The most dramatic ruin is The Towers. It is a large mansion among acres of hornbeams, oaks, and limes. Pigeons fill its tall halls. Water pours through three storeys, and algae has turned the walls bright green. The water drops into a big, empty basement swimming pool. Unopened wooden crates marked “bullet-proof glass” show the old owners were worried about security.

Today, very few people live on The Bishops Avenue all the time. A security guard outside one mansion said the owners were not there. Another guard outside Royal Mansion would not say if anyone was home. A staff member at another mansion warned the Guardian about the guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare mogul with personal wealth of £45m, said he is one of the few people who live there permanently.

He said, “It’s not a neighbourly place, where you can chat over the fence. To be honest, I have never seen what my neighbours look like. Next door, a Saudi princess spent £35m on a new house and I’ve never seen her. There are about three houses that are lived in 24/7 and half of the properties are occupied three to six months a year. The other half, who knows if they come or not?”

These broken, very expensive houses show a property culture where some of the world’s richest people treat British homes as investments. One Hyde Park, a block of apartments in Knightsbridge, is another example: more than half the flats are registered with the council as empty or second homes.

People on the avenue now talk about building £5m apartments instead of £50m mansions, to try to bring residents back.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, plans to rebuild one of the most valuable sites on the avenue into 20 apartments. They would have a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

He said, “If you build a big house and try and sell for £30m to £40m, it won’t sell. Locals won’t buy and so you have to bring in overseas buyers.”

But the idea that these empty homes could help solve the housing crisis still seems far away. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some of the ruined houses could become affordable homes. He said the land price would be too high.

He said, “Very wealthy people own property there. Sometimes they live in them and sometimes they don’t.”
