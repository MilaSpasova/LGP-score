This invention could be as useful as Trevor Baylis’s wind-up radio was in the 1990s, before the internet. It is a cheap light that gets free power from gravity. It could help stop the use of dangerous kerosene lamps in Africa and India.

The British designer Patrick Hunt tried to get money from banks or venture capital firms to start selling his invention. But he had trouble. He said getting funding was slow and complicated, the idea was not proven yet, and people did not want to take the risk.

So he tried crowdfunding on a US website called Indiegogo, which had recently opened in the UK. In five days he reached his goal and raised £36,200. By the end of 2012, after 40 days, he had raised £400,000.

The LED light uses a dynamo. The dynamo is turned by a 10kg bag of rocks that slowly drops down. The bag is tied to the light and lifted to about 2m high. As it slowly falls to the ground, it makes enough power for about half an hour of light.

Hunt is getting ready to make the light in China. He will test the market again by sending 1,000 lights to Africa before full mass production. He hopes to make millions of units.

Hunt is part of a new wave of entrepreneurs using the fast-growing crowdfunding industry to raise money. Another new site is InvestingZone, which matches wealthy people with start-up entrepreneurs.

Indiegogo does not give investors shares. Instead, people who donate can get “perks” for different donation levels. People who helped fund Hunt’s light not only supported poorer communities, but also got their own light.

Danae Ringelmann, a co-founder of Indiegogo, said the “gravity light” shows how crowdfunding can be fair and fast, and how it can test an entrepreneur’s idea. She said it is not about knowing bank decision makers or venture capital contacts. It is about proving the idea to customers and fans, and getting them to support it with money. She also said that even ideas that do not get funding can still be worth testing, because you learn sooner if the idea is not good.

Ringelmann is based in the US and started her career as a Wall Street analyst. In 2008 she quit and used her skills to help friends in the arts raise money. Indiegogo first focused on films and launched at the Sundance Film Festival that year.

Five years later, Indiegogo was raising about $2m a week for new businesses that were starting or growing. In December 2012, it added euro and sterling services to grow in Europe. It says Britain is its third biggest market. It also says international activity has risen 41% since December.

There are many competitors, such as Kickstarter, Seedrs, and Funding Circle. But Ringelmann says Indiegogo is different because anyone can launch a campaign, and no project is seen as too strange.

Indiegogo charges a 4% fee for successful campaigns. If a campaign does not reach its goal, users can either return all the money to donors for free or keep the money raised and pay a 9% fee.

A British woman, Lauren Pears, raised £100,000 on Indiegogo to open a “cat café” in London. It is called Lady Dinah’s Cat Emporium. It has not opened yet, but it is described as a place where people can sit in a comfortable wingback chair with a hot cup of tea, a book, and a cat.

Ringelmann said some campaigns are rejected by venture capitalists because they are not sure the market really wants the product. She said an Indiegogo campaign can give proof that people want it, even before the product launches. She said crowdfunding lets entrepreneurs test the market, pricing, and features, find new ways to make money, and get important feedback.

Ringelmann said ideas are very common, and what matters is doing the work well. She said that if someone is afraid their idea will be stolen by a person who can do it faster and better, then they are not the right person to carry it out. She said it is important to be confident, move fast, and learn.

For Ringelmann, growing in Europe and making a deal with a web payments company are part of a dream to make finance open to more people. This deal will let people pay through local card services like Maestro in the UK and Carte Bleue in France, as well as PayPal. More than 7,000 finance campaigns are live on Indiegogo.

Crowdfunding has grown as an alternative to banks, but it does not attract many big investors, who usually want a stake in a promising business. That may start to change in the UK with the launch of InvestingZone.
