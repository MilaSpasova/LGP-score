Dan Sullivan was late to breakfast with the Guardian. He was stopped by police while riding his motorcycle.

Sullivan says he works too much. He misses dinner dates. He forgets to give presents.

So, like many people in Silicon Valley, the 27-year-old venture capitalist made a start-up to help: BetterBoyfriend.me. It is a service that sends girlfriends and wives a present every month for about $70.

Sullivan works in finance. He has been quietly testing the program. He has signed up about 350 boyfriends. That brings in about $17,000 in revenue every month. Most members, he says, are his Harvard and investor friends: venture capitalists, founders, and workers at companies like Apple, Google, and Facebook. Their girlfriends have been getting presents from Sullivan for the last year.

Each month, members pick from a list of seven gifts, like chocolate, tea sets, and manicures. The package is sent to the boyfriend with no brand name and no label.

Sullivan tests ideas by buying Facebook ads to see what kind of results they get. BetterBoyfriend.me became popular. Sullivan said, “BetterBoyfriend hit something. It’s a pain point everyone has.”

BetterBoyfriend.me was not Sullivan’s first idea. For a couple of days, he thought about dog-sharing. He said he saw rich guys looking miserable walking dogs, and he realized a dog is only valuable 5% of the time. He asked: what if a person could babysit the dogs, and other people could rent them?

Another start-up, called InvisibleBoyfriend, lets users “design and build a believable social partner who is everything you want and is always available to talk, whenever you want”. The result is a “boyfriend” who can text with you all day.

Founder Kyle Tabor launched it to give users a fake boyfriend to stop unwanted advances. But he found that people got attached to their computer-based partners. He said many users want companionship through conversation, more than “proof” of a relationship to make other people stop bothering them.

For Sullivan and Tabor, the surprise was the real relationships they have formed with customers. Sullivan says he has started to see himself as a kind of relationship consultant for the boyfriends.

Sullivan says he has made mistakes. Early on, the packages had receipts with his name, Dan Sullivan. “One of the boyfriends wrote to me and said, ‘Listen, dude, she’s not mad but Cynthia found out’.”

About 50% of the women who get BetterBoyfriend.me packages know that Sullivan is involved. He says it connects to age. He thinks that after people have been married for a while, they do not keep many secrets.

Over the year, Sullivan says he has gotten to know the boyfriends very well. They even delivered a package to a hospital delivery room. He said the key is to remember that his relationship is with the boyfriend.

When he first started the company, he put BetterBoyfriend.me tags on flower bouquets. Then he went to Dolores Park, a popular relaxing place in the Mission District, which has many tech workers and young people.

He said, “I looked for couples and gave the bouquet to the girl but that didn’t go down well with the boyfriend. Like, not at all. It made sense, though. It was cool. So, I reconsidered and started handing them out to the boyfriends.”
