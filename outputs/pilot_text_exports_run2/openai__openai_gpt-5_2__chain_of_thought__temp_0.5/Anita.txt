Brazil’s latest funk star, Anitta, has won millions of fans by bringing the favela sound into the mainstream. But she is now part of a debate about skin colour.

Some anti-discrimination groups and writers say the music industry’s fastest rising star had to give up showing her blackness to sell to a mostly white, middle-class market.

The debate grew after people shared “before and after” photos of Anitta. They seem to show her skin becoming much lighter after she signed a deal with Warner.

In the first photo, when she was not well known, she looks darker. In the second photo, a marketing picture from after she became famous, she looks paler. People asked if this change was from whitening products and cosmetic surgery or, more likely, from Photoshop. The difference has brought back a big question in Brazil: do you need to be pale to get ahead?

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote a post criticizing the hidden discrimination in media and marketing that she felt this image change showed. She wrote: “People refuse to accept that they are racist and they think they live in a multiracial democracy, but the statistics show that is far from the case. The whitening shows us a profoundly intolerant society that doesn’t support diversity. White is the image of the rich, the nice, the successful, the good, while people see black as the opposite of all that.”

Anitta was born Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

She became very famous with an album and a huge hit song, Show das Poderosas. The song reached number one and got 52 million views on YouTube.

Many people love her as a pop star with a strong message and catchy songs. Her supporters also present her as a bridge between the mostly black and mixed-race shanty towns on Rio’s hillsides and the richer, whiter areas below.

She has reduced the sexual dancing, gangsta talk, and explicit words from baile funk. Now people are asking if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract a more profitable audience. Arraes said: “If pop stars have curly hair, they are going to feel coerced into straightening it. If they have a big nose, they will be coerced into getting rhinoplasty. It creates a vicious cycle for self-esteem.”

This subject is sensitive in this largely mixed-race country. Brazil was one of the last large countries in the world to end slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. But in Brazil, race and family background often matter less than skin colour. And although Brazil aims to be a multiracial democracy, there is a clear link between skin tone and inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Until 2011, black or mixed-race students also spent about two fewer years in school on average.

The government says this gap is getting smaller because of quotas for university places and other kinds of affirmative action. But big differences remain. Most business and government leaders are white, while most low-level jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In Ipanema, Gávea, and other rich areas, you are much more likely to see black nannies pushing strollers with white toddlers than a white nanny pushing a black child.

Defining colour in Brazil is complicated. In the 2010 census, people who said they were white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million said they were black, two million said they were Asian, and 0.5% said they were indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology teacher at the Federal University of Pernambuco, believes Anitta won the middle class by taking a rebellious sound and making it calmer and easier for everyone to accept.

He said: “Was this achieved by racial whitening? No. What happened was a change of the social space where Anitta produced her art: from the periphery to the centre.”

Others say the focus on colour is too strong. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who worked with Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot, but not her colour.

He said: “I don’t believe it’s whitening; it’s more the way they are producing her with makeup, hairstylists and the way she dresses. I don’t think that was because of pressure being put on her. She always liked to show off, sing and dance. That was a natural thing for her. I believe that it is because of this naturalness that she is where she is today.”

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador in north-eastern Brazil, said the problem was not the public, but music producers and media leaders. He said the public showed they cared about music itself when they chose Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, as the recent winner of the TV talent show The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for comment. But in a recent interview, she said identity should be chosen by the person.

She said: “All-powerful is a woman who doesn’t need to be beautiful, but she has so much attitude that she is marvellous, she is powerful. What I try to pass on in my work for everyone is that we can be who we want.”
