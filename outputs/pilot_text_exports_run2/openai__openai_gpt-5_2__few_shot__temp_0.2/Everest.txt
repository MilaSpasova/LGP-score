Kenton Cool usually likes to talk, but now he can almost not speak. Climbing high mountains has made his voice rough. He is in Kathmandu, the capital of Nepal. That morning, he flew down from Everest base camp.

Cool is thinking about three big climbs he finished the weekend before. Early on Saturday, he reached the top of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of the three main summits around the Western Cwm valley.

Later that same day, he climbed Everest. He reached the top in complete darkness early on Sunday. Then he and his climbing partner continued to Lhotse. They reached the top of Lhotse on Monday morning.

Cool says it was a sudden chance. For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. He says this does not remove the hard physical work he did. But he thinks the next person will do even more, maybe without ropes or bottled oxygen.

Sixty years after Everest was first climbed, many stories look back to Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. They compare the past with today, with commercial tourism and damage to the environment. The writer asks Cool to think about what top climbers might do 60 years from now.

Cool says he does not like to think about it. But he talks about the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. Steck left the mountain in April after an “altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck planned a very hard trip: climb Everest’s west ridge (first done in 1963), go down to the South Col, and then climb Lhotse by a new route, all without fixed ropes. Cool says Steck trained extremely hard and was an amazing climber.

Tourism is another question. One sign is a helicopter rescue by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro flew back to Everest on Tuesday in a powerful helicopter. He rescued an injured climber at 7,800 metres.

This was the highest rescue on Everest so far. It also shows that helicopter flights have increased very fast in recent years. By 2073, the mountain could even have a helipad on the South Col, bringing tourists who use bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing rescue for climbers and for the many trekkers who go as far as base camp.

Alton Byers, a mountain geographer and environmentalist, says the region may not be able to handle fast-growing tourism. He says climate change and tourism together are putting new pressure on the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers are shrinking, and some are disappearing. He says people everywhere are talking about less water. There is less water for farming and for the new lodges being built.

Byers says that in Namche Bazaar, a new five-mile pipeline is being built to bring water. It is needed because more tourists want showers and flush toilets. The local stream is polluted with human waste and does not have enough water in high season. He says every village digs a pit near the houses for garbage, and Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world. At base camp, human waste is handled better now and removed in plastic barrels. But Byers says the barrels are emptied into a huge pit down the valley, and it could leak into the area’s water.

Byers says these problems can be solved, but people must take them seriously. He says one climber can spend $85,000 to climb Everest. He says that is fine, but people also need to focus on other needs. He says for half a million dollars a year, most of the problems could be fixed.

Climate change is harder. Byers works with local conservation committees to plan for its effects. They often look for new water sources or collect rainwater. He says glacial lakes are growing fast and could burst and flood the Sherpa homeland. He says a time will come when people will have to move out of the way.

Changing weather also affects tourism. More clouds during times that are usually clear are closing Lukla Airport more often. Lukla is the main gateway to the Everest region. A new road for 4x4s is being built to Lukla to keep tourists and money coming. But Byers worries that roads are being built too cheaply, which can cause soil erosion and landslides.

Byers says Everest is the famous symbol everyone knows. He says it is like a warning sign that everyone understands. He says it is a perfect place to learn how to deal with problems like climate change and tourism.
