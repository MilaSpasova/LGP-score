Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8th April, 2014, she told a special story. She received a message in a bottle, 101 years after her grandfather threw it into the Baltic Sea. People think it is the world’s oldest message in a bottle. A museum in Germany gave it to her and is now showing it.

Erdmann, 62, said it was very surprising. A man came to her door and said he had mail from her grandfather. He said a message in a bottle was found, and the name on the card was her grandfather’s. The man was a genealogical researcher. He found her in Berlin after the letter was given to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg.

The bottle was a brown beer bottle. It was in the sea for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it in his catch in the Baltic Sea near the city of Kiel. Holger von Neuhoff, a curator at the museum, said it was the oldest bottled message he had seen. He said the museum has older documents without bottles, but with the bottle and the document together, this was the oldest at the moment. He said it was in very good condition.

Researchers think Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913. He was on a hike with a nature group. He was 20 years old then.

People could not read much of the postcard. But they could read the Berlin address on the front. They could also read his polite request: the finder should send the note to his home address.

Erdmann said he also put two stamps from that time in the bottle, so the finder would not have to pay. But he did not think it would take 101 years.

Erdmann said she felt moved. She did not know her grandfather because he died at age 54, six years before she was born.

She said she knew very little about him. But she learned he was a writer. She said he was open-minded, believed in freedom, and thought everyone should respect each other. She said he did a lot for young people and later travelled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful because she could see where her roots came from.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and travelling around the world. She said she is open-minded too. She said what he taught his two daughters, her mother taught her, and she then taught it to her sons.

But she said people should not throw message bottles into the sea today. She said the sea is already full of bottles and rubbish.

The message and bottle will be shown at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts will try to read the rest of the text. It is not clear what will happen to the bottle, but Erdmann hopes it will stay at the museum.

She said they want to add some photos with the bottle, so visitors can see the young man who threw it into the water.
