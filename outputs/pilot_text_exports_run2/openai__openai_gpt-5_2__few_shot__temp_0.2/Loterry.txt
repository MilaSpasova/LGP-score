The lottery balls fall down the chute, and all six numbers match. Then it is time to buy an Audi, book a holiday in the US, and call an estate agent. An analysis says many lottery millionaires do this.

The UK National Lottery started in 1994. Since then, it has made 3,000 millionaires. They won more than £8.5bn in total, with an average of £2.8m each. A study by Oxford Economics says their money also helped create 3,780 more millionaires among their children, family, and friends.

Most winners (59%) stop working right away. But 19% keep their jobs, and 31% do unpaid volunteer work. The study says 98% of winners’ spending stayed in the UK. It estimates that each winner’s spending keeps six people in full-time work for one year.

Winners added almost £750m to GDP and created more than £500m in tax for the Exchequer. Most money went to property. Winners spent £2.72bn on their main homes, and £170m on paying off debt and mortgages.

Many winners wanted steady income, so they spent £2.125bn on investments. They gave £1.17bn as gifts to family and friends. They spent £680m on cars and holidays.

Camelot, which runs the UK National Lottery, paid for the study to mark 3,000 winners. The study used research from 100 winners who won £1m or more. It found that the 3,000 winners bought 7,958 houses or flats in the UK, about 2.7 each, and spent £3.3bn. Most winners (82%) changed their main home, and spent an average of £900,000.

Many new homes had extras. Almost a third (29%) bought a hot tub. A walk-in wardrobe was important for 28%. Almost a quarter (24%) chose a home behind electric gates. Also, 22% had a games room, and 7% put in a snooker table.

Big homes need care. About 30% hired a cleaner, and 24% hired a gardener. A small group (5%) hired a beautician.

Audis were the favourite car for 16% of winners. Range Rovers and BMWs were also popular (11% each). Mercedes were 10%, and Land Rovers were 5%. Winners spent £463m on 17,190 cars. The average price of their favourite car was £46,116.

Holidays were also important. Most winners (68%) stayed in five-star hotels overseas. The US was the top choice for 27%, then the Caribbean (9%). In the UK, caravan sales also did well. Over 18 years, 10% of millionaires bought a caravan, creating sales of about £7.4m.

Some winners (15%) started their own businesses. Another 9% helped other people start businesses. And 6% invested in or bought other people’s businesses. The study says businesses started or supported by winners employ 3,195 people.

Andy Logan, who wrote the report, said the effect of a win spreads further than they expected. He said it changes the lives of friends and family, and it also affects the UK economy because so much money is spent in the UK. He said each win creates a ripple effect for this generation, and often the next one too.
