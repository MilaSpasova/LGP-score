This invention could be as useful as Trevor Baylis’s wind-up radio in the 1990s, before the internet. It is a cheap light that uses gravity for free power. It could stop people in Africa and India from using dangerous kerosene lamps.

A British designer, Patrick Hunt, tried to get money from banks or venture capital to start selling his invention. But it was hard. He said getting funding was slow and complex. The idea was new, and people did not want to take a risk.

So he tried crowdfunding on a US website called Indiegogo, which had just opened in the UK. In five days, he reached his target and raised £36,200. By the end of 2012, after 40 days, he had raised £400,000.

The LED light uses a dynamo. The dynamo is powered by a 10kg bag of rocks that goes down slowly. The bag is attached to the light and lifted to about 2m high. As it falls, it makes enough power for 30 minutes of light.

Hunt is getting ready to produce the light in China. He will test the market again by sending 1,000 lights to Africa. After that, he hopes to make millions of units.

He is part of a new group of entrepreneurs using crowdfunding to get money. Another new site is InvestingZone. It connects rich people with start-up entrepreneurs.

Indiegogo does not give shares. Instead, people who donate can get “perks.” People who gave money for Hunt’s light helped poorer people, and they also got their own light.

Danae Ringelmann, a co-founder of Indiegogo, says the “gravity light” shows how crowdfunding can work. She says it is fast, efficient, and merit-based. It is not about knowing people at a bank or a venture capital company. It is about showing customers and fans that your idea is good, and getting them to support it with money.

She also says it is good to test ideas that may not get funding. If the idea is not good, you find out sooner and save time.

Ringelmann lives in the US. She first worked as a Wall Street analyst. In 2008, she quit to help friends in the arts raise money.

At first, the site focused on films. It launched at the Sundance Film Festival that year.

Five years later, Indiegogo raises about $2m a week for new businesses that are starting or growing. In December 2012, it started services in euros and pounds. It says Britain is its third biggest market. International activity has gone up 41% since December.

Indiegogo has many competitors, like Kickstarter, Seedrs, and Funding Circle. But Ringelmann says Indiegogo is different because anyone can start a campaign. No project is too strange.

Indiegogo charges a 4% fee for successful campaigns. If a campaign does not reach its target, users can refund all the money with no charge. Or they can keep the money, but then the fee is 9%.

A British woman, Lauren Pears, raised £100,000 on Indiegogo to open a “cat café” in London. It is called Lady Dinah’s Cat Emporium. It has not opened yet. It is described as a place where people can sit in a comfortable chair, have hot tea, read a book, and be with a cat.

Ringelmann says venture capitalists sometimes reject ideas because they do not know if people really want them. But an Indiegogo campaign can show proof that there is a market, even before the project starts.

She says crowdfunding helps you test your market, price, and features. It can help you find new ways to make money and get important feedback.

Ringelmann says ideas are very common. What matters is doing the work well. She says if you are afraid someone will steal your idea and do it better and faster, then you are not the right person to do it. She says you need confidence, you need to move fast, and you need to learn.

For Ringelmann, expanding into Europe is part of a dream to make finance open to more people. Indiegogo is working with a web transaction provider. This will let people pay with local card services like Maestro in the UK and Carte Bleue in France, and also with PayPal. More than 7,000 campaigns are live on the site.

Crowdfunding has grown as a choice instead of banks, but it is not very attractive to big investors. Big investors usually want a stake in a promising business. This could start to change in the UK with the launch of InvestingZone.
