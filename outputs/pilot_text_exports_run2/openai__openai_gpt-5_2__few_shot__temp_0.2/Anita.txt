Anitta is a new star of Brazilian funk music. She has millions of fans. She helped bring music from the favela into the mainstream. But now people are debating her skin colour.

Some anti-discrimination groups and writers say she had to change how “black” she looks to sell music to a mostly white, middle-class market.

The debate grew after people shared old and new photos of Anitta. In the older photo, before she was famous, her skin looked darker. In the newer photo, used for marketing after she became famous, her skin looks lighter. Some people think this could be from skin-whitening products, cosmetic surgery, or more likely, Photoshop. This made people talk again about whether you need lighter skin to succeed in Brazil.

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote that this shows hidden discrimination in media and marketing. She said many people do not accept that they are racist and think Brazil is a multiracial democracy, but statistics show this is not true. She said whitening suggests society does not support diversity. She said people often connect “white” with rich, nice, successful, and good, and see “black” as the opposite.

Anitta’s real name is Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

Now she is very famous. She released an album and had a big hit song, Show das Poderosas. It reached number one on the charts and got 52 million views on YouTube.

Many people love her as a pop idol with a strong message and catchy songs. The people supporting her present her as a bridge between the mostly black and mixed-race shanty towns on Rio’s hills and the richer and whiter areas below.

She changed her style to reach more people. She reduced the suggestive dancing, “gangsta” references, and explicit lyrics of baile funk. But now some people ask if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract a richer audience. Arraes said that pop stars can feel forced to change their hair or their nose. She said this can hurt self-esteem.

Race and colour are a sensitive issue in Brazil. Brazil was one of the last big countries to ban slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. But in Brazil, colour often matters more than race or ancestry. And even though the country wants to be a multiracial democracy, skin tone is clearly linked to inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Until 2011, black or mixed-race students spent about two fewer years in school on average.

The government says this gap is getting smaller because of university quota systems and other affirmative action. But big differences still remain. Most business and government leaders are white. Many low-paid jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In rich areas like Ipanema and Gávea, you are more likely to see black nannies pushing strollers with white toddlers than the opposite.

Colour categories in Brazil are complicated. In the 2010 census, people who called themselves white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million said black, two million said Asian, and 0.5% said indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology lecturer at the Federal University of Pernambuco, said Anitta won middle-class fans by taking a rebellious sound and making it tamer and easier for everyone to accept. He said this was not done by racial whitening. He said it was a change in where she made her art: from the periphery to the centre.

Other people say the focus on colour is too strong. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who knew Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot but not her colour. He said the change came from makeup, hairstylists, and clothing, not from pressure. He said she always liked to show off, sing, and dance, and that this natural style helped her succeed.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador, said the prejudice is not in the public but in music producers and media executives. He said the public showed they cared about music itself when they chose Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, as the recent winner of The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for comment. But in a recent interview, she said identity should be defined by the person. She said an “all-powerful” woman does not need to be beautiful, but has strong attitude and is marvellous and powerful. She said her work tries to show that we can be who we want.
