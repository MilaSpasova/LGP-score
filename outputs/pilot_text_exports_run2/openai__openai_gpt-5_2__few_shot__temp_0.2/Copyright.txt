Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is theft. He says it is like putting your hand in someone’s pocket and stealing their wallet. In an article for Index on Censorship, Pullman, who is president of the Society of Authors, strongly supports copyright laws. He criticizes internet users who think it is OK to download music or books without paying.

Pullman writes that the technology is very impressive, so people do not see that what they are doing is wrong. He says it is terrible that people can steal an artist’s work and not be punished.

His article came after leaders in the music industry met British Prime Minister David Cameron in Downing Street. They talked about web piracy.

Pullman, the writer of the His Dark Materials trilogy, says authors and musicians often work for years in poverty and without being known. When they finally make good work and might earn a living, illegal downloading takes that chance away. He says the rule is simple: if we want to enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

Pullman’s writing appears in the next issue of the magazine as a dialogue with Cathy Casserly. She is the chief executive of Creative Commons. Creative Commons offers open content licences that let creators control how they use copyright. Casserly says there are many problems with copyright, which was made in an “analogue age”. She says copyright laws can stop many ways people could share, build on, and remix each other’s work.

She says artists need to think of new ways to distribute and monetize their work. She quotes science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees. The editor, Rachael Jolley, says current copyright laws do not work in the digital age and may criminalize consumers. She says new models are needed so artists, writers, and musicians can earn a living from their work.

The debate is active, and illegal downloading is very large. Ofcom data suggests that in the UK, between November 2012 and January 2013, people pirated 280 million music tracks, 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files.

Ofcom says 18% of internet users over age 12 admit they recently pirated content, and 9% say they fear getting caught. Pullman writes that it is still amazing how easily and quickly music can be gotten as MP3 downloads, even for people who have been building up their iTunes list for some time.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron appointed the Conservative MP Mike Weatherley as his adviser on this issue. A spokesman for the BPI, the record industry trade body, said Mike Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creative producers it protects. They hope his influence, and the prime minister’s support for copyright, will affect the approach of the UK’s intellectual property office.
