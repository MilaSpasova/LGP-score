Dan Sullivan was late to breakfast with the Guardian. He was stopped by the police while riding his motorcycle.

Sullivan says he works too much. He misses dinner dates. He forgets to give gifts.

So the 27-year-old venture capitalist made a start-up to help: BetterBoyfriend.me. This service sends girlfriends and wives a gift every month for about $70.

Sullivan works in finance. He has been testing the program quietly. About 350 boyfriends have joined. The service makes about $17,000 each month. Most members are his Harvard friends and investor friends. Many are venture capitalists, founders, or employees at companies like Apple, Google, and Facebook. Their girlfriends have been getting gifts for the last year.

Each month, members choose one gift from a list of seven. The gifts include chocolate, tea sets, and manicures. The package is sent to the boyfriend with no brand name and no label.

Sullivan tests ideas by buying Facebook ads to see if people are interested. BetterBoyfriend.me became popular fast. Sullivan said it solved a problem many people have.

BetterBoyfriend.me was not his first idea. For a few days, he thought about a dog-sharing idea. He said he saw rich guys looking miserable walking dogs. He said a dog is only valuable 5% of the time. He thought about paying someone to babysit dogs, and letting other people rent them.

Another start-up is called InvisibleBoyfriend. It lets users create a believable social partner who is always available to talk. The result is a “boyfriend” who can text with you all day.

Founder Kyle Tabor started it to help users stop unwanted advances. But he found that people became attached to their computer-made partners. He said many users want companionship through conversation, not just proof of a relationship.

For Sullivan and Tabor, a surprise was the real relationships they formed with customers. Sullivan says he now feels like a kind of relationship consultant for the boyfriends.

Sullivan says he made mistakes. At first, the packages included receipts with his name, Dan Sullivan. One boyfriend wrote to him and said, “Listen, dude, she’s not mad but Cynthia found out.”

Sullivan says about 50% of the women who get the packages know he is involved. He says this connects with age. He thinks that after people have been married for a while, they do not keep many secrets.

Over the year, Sullivan says he has gotten to know the boyfriends very well. They even sent a gift to a hospital delivery room. He says the most important thing is that his relationship is with the boyfriend.

When he first started the company, he put tags that said BetterBoyfriend.me on flower bouquets. He went to Dolores Park in the Mission District, a popular place for tech and young people.

He looked for couples and gave the flowers to the girl. The boyfriend did not like that. Sullivan said it made sense. So he changed his plan and started giving the flowers to the boyfriends.
