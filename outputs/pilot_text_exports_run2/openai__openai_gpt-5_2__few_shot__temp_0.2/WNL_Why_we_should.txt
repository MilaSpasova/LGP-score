James Hamblin is a senior editor at the American magazine The Atlantic. In his series “If Our Bodies Could Talk,” he tried a challenge: he stopped showering for a while. He took fewer showers, and when he did shower, he did not use shampoo or soap. He wanted to see what happens when we clean our bodies too much.

He found something many people have noticed. When we use a lot of soap and body wash, our skin tries harder to bring itself back to balance. Showers can remove the skin’s natural oil and bacteria. Hot water and cleaning products can make this effect stronger.

After a shower, you may feel dry and tight. This happens because natural moisture is washed away. Our skin also has millions of helpful bacteria. Frequent washing and scrubbing can kill these bacteria. When the bacteria grow back, the new microbes can cause more body smell. So showering too often may make you smell more. If you stop using soap and stop showering, there is usually a first period that can feel unpleasant. After that, the skin often becomes balanced again, oil production slows, and healthy bacteria grow.

In the end, Hamblin felt the human body can work well on its own. Dermatologists also say using less soap can help acne and eczema.

Taking fewer showers can also help the environment. The average shower lasts seven minutes and uses 65 litres of water. This is 65 litres of clean drinking water that goes down the drain with soap every day, sometimes more than once.

Clean water is becoming more important, for example as California has another summer of drought. Clean water is one of the world’s most valuable resources, and it may soon be in short supply. There is also waste from many body wash bottles. These are strong reasons to live more “au naturel.”

If this idea makes you feel nervous, relax. Many people who wash less still use deodorant if they need it. Natural smell is different for each person and can change with diet, hydration, and exercise. Also, washing your hands with soap is still important to reduce the spread of infectious diseases.

You do not need to stop showering completely like Hamblin did. But you can reduce a little: skip some showers, use less soap, and let your skin’s bacteria grow.
