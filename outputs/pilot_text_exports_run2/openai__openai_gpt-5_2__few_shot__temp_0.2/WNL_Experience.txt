One beautiful summer evening, I decided to swim near Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland. I moved there in 2012. A woman was in the water with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty often swims with a group of people, and she seems close to them.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Since then, hundreds of people have swum with her. Many people think she is completely tame. She was even in an Irish tourist-board advertisement, where a girl says she wants to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty’s tummy. It looked friendly. About 20 tourists and local people were on the pier watching.

When I got into the water, Dusty left the woman and suddenly became very wild. Later I learned that she is very territorial when she is with someone. Her tail was moving fast. At first, I thought she was showing off, but then I thought she might be angry.

I tried to swim to the pier to get out. But very quickly, Dusty hit me hard with her nose. It was very strong and very painful. I was pushed forward.

People on the pier stared at me. Dusty stayed next to me and her tail was still moving wildly. That scared me most. I thought if she hit me with her tail, I could go under the water and die.

I reached the pier, but I could not get out because I was hurt. I felt very afraid. I shouted for help. A man put his arm in the water and pulled me onto the steps.

Then another man came. He said he was an orthopaedic surgeon and he specialized in marine trauma. He had been driving into Doolin and saw what was happening. I was very cold and very worried. My biggest fear was bleeding inside my body. He checked me and said he could not feel signs of internal bleeding, but he thought I might have broken bones.

Later, I learned my injuries were serious. I had six fractures in my spine, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for several weeks. I could not work for five months. I had limited movement, stiffness, and pain.

Then doctors said I had post-traumatic stress. After the near-death experience, I felt anxious about everything and overreacted. I felt people were looking at me the wrong way. Loud noises were hard for me. I also had memory loss.

Three months before the accident, I opened a health-food shop. But I had to give it up because I could not work. It was the hardest year, but now it is behind me.

I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building my own osteopathy practice. I understand patients better because I have been a patient.

I am thankful that I am healthy. I want to stop other people from getting hurt. People have a nice idea about dolphins and trust them. Who would think a dolphin could attack a person? If you see a very dangerous animal coming with its teeth showing, you feel scared. But dolphins have a wide smile.

I am not angry with Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her territory, and she is a wild and unpredictable animal. People need to know this. Many people come to swim with her, and they do not understand how dangerous it can be. People said my injuries were among several injuries that summer.

After I was pulled out, Dusty swam away. Then she came back. She was upright in the water next to me and looked at me. We looked into each other’s eyes. I felt she was truly sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin, and the anger was gone. People on the pier were amazed. After that moment, my terror ended. I made peace with her.
