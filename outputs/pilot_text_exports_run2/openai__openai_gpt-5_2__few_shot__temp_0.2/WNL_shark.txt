More people are now against Western Australia’s shark cull. Thousands of people went to beaches around Australia to ask the state’s premier to stop the policy. RSPCA Australia and Richard Branson, the owner of Virgin Atlantic, also spoke against it.

The government started catching and killing sharks longer than three metres. It began after what the government called an “unprecedented” number of shark attacks on the coast. A 35-year-old surfer was killed in November 2013. He was the sixth person to die from a shark attack in two years.

But the Shark Attack File says Australia has had about one shark-related death each year on average for the last 50 years.

Kate Faehrmann from the Sea Shepherd Conservation Society said at a protest in Perth that the policy will not work. She said drumlines, which are used to catch sharks, can kill many animals. She said they can kill sharks of any size and also dolphins, turtles, and other animals. She said this is why the community does not want it.

Thousands of people protested at Perth’s Cottesloe Beach and Sydney’s Manly Beach. Hundreds protested at Glenelg in south-west Adelaide, and at beaches in Victoria and Queensland. Faehrmann said the protests showed many Australians want sharks protected. She said people are coming to say the ocean is the sharks’ home and they do not want them killed.

Surfer Anthony Joyce, who once had his foot caught in a shark’s mouth, said killing sharks will not make a real difference.

The state government has not given a running total of how many sharks have been killed. There have been reports that sharks smaller than three metres were released after being caught on drumlines. Drumlines are floating drums fixed to the sea bed, with bait on hooks under them.

Conservationists say there is no evidence the cull will reduce shark attacks on humans. They say no earlier cull used only drumlines. Researchers at the University of Western Australia say the recent shark attacks may be linked to Western Australia having the fastest-growing population in Australia, not to more sharks.

Richard Peirce from the UK charity the Shark Trust said the cull will not work and might bring more predators near the coast. He said it is sad the government did not try other ways instead of killing. He also said drumlines can attract other predators, especially if the lines are left in overnight.

Around the world in 2012, there were 80 unprovoked shark attacks, and seven were fatal. Humans kill almost 100m sharks each year. RSPCA Australia said the cull is not justified. It said there is no evidence that more attacks are caused by more sharks. It said the rise fits with more people going into the water.

Richard Branson told Fairfax Radio the policy is backfiring. He said the premier, Colin Barnett, may have thought it would help tourism, but it may do the opposite. He said it makes people think there is a big problem when there is not, and it may stop people from coming to Perth and the countryside around it. He said it will only worry people for no reason.
