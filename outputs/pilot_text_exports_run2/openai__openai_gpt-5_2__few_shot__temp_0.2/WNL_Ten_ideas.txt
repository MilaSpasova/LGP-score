From glow-in-the-dark trees to underground bike sheds and solar-powered bins, people are trying unusual ideas to make cities better places to live.

Living in a city has many good things, but living in a sustainable way is becoming harder. Pollution, traffic, and less green space are some problems that many city people face every day.

Here are five of the wackier ideas.

Pop-up parks
Some cities look like they are built more for cars than for people. A pop-up park is a simple idea. You take an empty car park, a little money, and one or two plants, and you make a small temporary park. The PARK(ing) project started as an art experiment in San Francisco. Since then, it has spread around the world. People have also made temporary city farms and ecology demonstrations. The movement has a day of action every September.

Subterranean storage
Not everyone in a city uses a car. Bikes are becoming more popular. But where can you keep your bike safe? A Tokyo engineering firm called Giken made an idea called an “eco-cycle, anti-seismic underground bicycle park”. It is a round storage system that is only seven metres wide. It goes deep into the ground and can hold 204 bikes. Owners press a button, and the system brings the bike back above ground in about 13 seconds.

Glow-in-the-dark trees
Many people think of Christmas decorations when they imagine glowing trees. But Daan Roosegaarde thinks differently. The Dutch designer-artist created an experimental “bioluminescent” plant. The technology mixes DNA from glowing marine bacteria with the chloroplast genome of a plant, to make a glow like a jellyfish. Trials are happening to make an industrial-scale version. Roosegaarde hopes it could one day replace normal street lights.

Footfall harvesting
In West Ham in east London, many commuters and shoppers cross a raised walkway near the underground station every day. Most people do not notice that the ground feels slightly springy. It moves about five millimetres. This movement helps power the streetlights above. The walkway uses smart tiles that capture energy from footsteps and turn it into electricity. Pavegen, the UK firm behind this, has installed a similar system at London’s Heathrow Airport and other places in the world.

Supertrees
Singapore’s Gardens by the Bay has man-made trees called “supertrees”. They are steel-framed and can be up to 50 metres high. Flowers and ferns grow on them. Their metal tops help absorb and spread heat. They can collect rainwater and help ventilate two “climate-controlled biomes” (large conservatories) below. Eleven of the 18 trees also have solar panels on their “branches”.
