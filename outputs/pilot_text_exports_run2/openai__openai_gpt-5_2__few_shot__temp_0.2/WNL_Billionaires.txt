There is a thick layer of bird droppings on a gold-colored railing at one of Britain’s most expensive homes. There are pigeon skeletons, broken mirrors, and water running through broken parts of the ceiling. This house is called The Towers. It is a palace on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London, worth £30m. Its damage has been kept secret until now.

The Towers is one of ten big houses in the middle of The Bishops Avenue. This street is in the centre of London’s rising property market. People believe these houses were bought about 25 years ago for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. Most of the houses have been almost empty since then. Their columns are breaking, and their swimming pools are full of rubble. Nature has taken over so much that owls now live there.

This empty and broken scene is seen along the street. Lloyds Bank says The Bishops Avenue is the second most expensive street in Britain. At the same time, more people struggle to buy a home in London as prices rise by 11.2% each year. On the most expensive part of the street, 16 mansions are empty. Many are behind locked gates, with windows covered by steel bars. The gardens are overgrown, and guard dogs patrol.

Across the street is another empty mansion worth £18m, with broken windows and walls covered in anti-climb paint. Another house has metal bars on the windows, and it was sold for £20m.

Even so, prices keep rising. Dryades, a mansion that was owned until recently by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007 and is believed to be worth about £30m today. Heath Lodge is worth £13m today. It was the place of the 1984 murder, by silver bullet, of fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou. Heath Lodge was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

This is painful for people who cannot afford housing in one of the world’s most expensive cities. One security guard said it was upsetting to see so much space falling apart. He said it was enough space to house dozens of people.

In the past, rich royals from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia came to this road near Hampstead Heath. Later, Iranians came after the fall of the shah. Now, Chinese buyers are following Russians and Kazakhs who have spent millions to buy homes there. Estate agents say the address is as famous as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions were for sale for £65m and £38m. They promised Italian marble, lifts with leather padding, and luxury panic rooms.

But in the grounds of the empty houses, fountains are breaking and lawns have turned into wet bogs. Inside one house, water drips through a huge crystal chandelier onto a thick carpet. Parts of the ceiling have fallen and the carpet is rotting. Moss grows through broken bricks. A bathroom has shattered tiles and broken mirrors. The swimming pool has about a foot of dirty water, and flowers grow through the tiles. Wooden slats in the sauna bend outward.

The most dramatic ruin is The Towers. It is a grand mansion among many trees. Pigeons live in its large halls. Water pours through three floors, and the walls have turned bright green with algae. The water falls into a big, empty basement swimming pool. There are unopened wooden crates marked “bullet-proof glass”, showing the old owners were worried about security.

Today, very few people live on The Bishops Avenue all the time. A security guard outside one mansion said the owners were not there. Another guard outside Royal Mansion would not say if anyone was home. A staff member at another mansion warned the Guardian about the guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare mogul with personal wealth of £45m, said he is one of the few permanent residents.

He said it is not a friendly place where you can talk to neighbours. He said he has never seen what his neighbours look like. He said a Saudi princess next door spent £35m on a new house, and he has never seen her. He said about three houses are lived in 24/7. He said half the properties are lived in for three to six months a year. He said for the other half, it is not clear if the owners come or not.

These ruined mansions show a property culture where the world’s richest people see British homes as investments. One Hyde Park, a block of flats in Knightsbridge, is another example. More than half the flats there are registered with the council as empty or second homes.

Even so, people on the street talk about building £5m apartments instead of £50m mansions, to bring people back.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, plans to rebuild one valuable site as 20 apartments. They will have a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

He said if you build a big house and try to sell it for £30m to £40m, it will not sell. He said local people will not buy, so you must bring in overseas buyers.

But using these empty homes to help solve the housing crisis still seems far away. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some of the damaged homes could become affordable homes. He said the land price would be too high.

He said very wealthy people own property there. He said sometimes they live in the homes, and sometimes they do not.
