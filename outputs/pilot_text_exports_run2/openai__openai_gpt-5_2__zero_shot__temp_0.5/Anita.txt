Brazil’s new funk star, Anitta, has become very famous. She brought music from the favela into popular music. But now people are arguing about her skin colour.

Some people who fight discrimination say Anitta had to give up showing her blackness to succeed with mostly white, middle-class audiences.

The argument grew after “before and after” photos were shared. In the first photo, before she was famous, she looked darker. In the second photo, used for marketing after she became famous, she looks lighter. People are not sure why. It could be skin-lightening products, cosmetic surgery, or more likely Photoshop. The photos made people talk again about whether lighter skin helps people succeed in Brazil.

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote online that the change shows hidden discrimination in media and marketing. She said many people do not want to admit they are racist and think Brazil is a fair multiracial democracy, but statistics show it is not. She said society often connects white skin with being rich, successful, and good, and connects black skin with the opposite. She also said this creates a harmful cycle for self-esteem. She gave examples, like pop stars feeling forced to straighten curly hair or get nose surgery.

Anitta’s real name is Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

She later became widely famous with an album and a hit song, “Show das Poderosas.” The song reached the top of the charts and got 52 million views on YouTube.

Many people love her as a pop idol with a strong message and catchy songs. Her supporters present her as a bridge between the mostly black and mixed-race shanty towns on Rio’s hillsides and the richer and whiter areas below.

She has also changed her style. She reduced the suggestive dancing, gangsta references, and explicit lyrics from baile funk. Now some people ask if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract richer customers.

Race and colour are sensitive issues in Brazil, where many people are mixed-race. Brazil was one of the last big countries to ban slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. In Brazil, colour often matters more than ancestry. Even though the country wants to be a multiracial democracy, skin tone is strongly linked to inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Until 2011, black or mixed-race students spent about two fewer years in school on average.

The government says the gap is getting smaller because of university quotas and other affirmative action. But big differences remain. Most business and government leaders are white, while many lower-level jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In rich areas like Ipanema and Gávea, you are more likely to see black nannies pushing strollers with white toddlers than the other way around.

Colour can also be hard to define. In the 2010 census, people who called themselves white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million black, two million Asian, and 0.5% indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology lecturer at the Federal University of Pernambuco, said Anitta won over the middle class by making a rebellious sound calmer and easier for everyone to accept. He said this was not “racial whitening.” He said it was a change in where she made her art: from the edges of society to the centre.

Others also say the colour issue is being made too big. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who worked with Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot, but not her colour. He said the difference is more about makeup, hairstyling, and clothing. He also said it was not because of pressure. He said Anitta always liked to show off, sing, and dance, and that this natural style helped her succeed.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador, said the prejudice is not mainly from the public, but from music producers and media executives. He said the public showed they can choose music for its own value by making Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, the winner of the talent show The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for comment. But in a recent interview, she said identity should be decided by the person. She said a powerful woman does not need to be beautiful. She said what matters is attitude, and that she wants people to know they can be who they want.
