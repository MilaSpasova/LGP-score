Kenton Cool usually likes to talk, but right now he can hardly speak. Climbing high mountains has made his voice rough. He is in Kathmandu, the capital of Nepal. That morning, he flew down from Everest base camp.

Cool is thinking about a surprising set of climbs he finished the weekend before. Early on Saturday morning, he reached the top of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of the three main summits in the Everest “horseshoe.” This horseshoe surrounds the icy valley called the Western Cwm.

On the same day, he climbed to the top of Everest. He reached the summit in complete darkness early on Sunday. Then he and his climbing partner kept going. On Monday morning, they reached the top of Lhotse, the third peak in this three-mountain challenge.

“It was a snatched opportunity,” he says. “For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. That doesn’t take away the physical achievement of what I did. I’ve set the bar at a certain level. But whoever comes along next will move the bar further and do it without ropes or bottled oxygen.”

Sixty years after Everest was first climbed, many stories look back to Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. They talk about an earlier time, before modern commercialism and damage to the environment. I ask Cool to look ahead and imagine what top climbers might do 60 years from now.

“I hate to think,” he says. He talks about the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. Steck left the mountain in April after what Cool calls “an altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck planned to climb Everest’s west ridge, first climbed in 1963. Then he would go down to the South Col and immediately climb Lhotse by a new route, all without fixed ropes. “Ueli had been training like a machine,” Cool says. “He’s a climber in a class all his own. He’s technically brilliant but he had also taken his physical condition to an astronomic level. It would have been amazing to see what he could have done.”

Tourism is a different question. One clue is a helicopter rescue by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro’s angry language helped start the confrontation at Camp 2. On Tuesday, Moro flew a high-powered helicopter back to Everest to rescue a sick or hurt climber at 7,800 metres.

This was the highest rescue ever done on Everest. It shows that helicopter flights have grown very fast in recent years. By 2073, the mountain could even have a helipad on the South Col, bringing tourists who breathe bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing how rescuers can help both climbers and the many trekkers who go as far as base camp.

Mountain geographer and environmentalist Alton Byers says it is not clear if the Everest region can keep handling a fast-growing tourism business. He says climate change and tourism together are putting new pressure on the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers in the Everest region are shrinking, and some are disappearing. This is already causing big problems. “Everywhere you go, people are talking about how there’s less water. There’s less water for agriculture and less water for all the new lodges that are getting built.”

Byers says that in the Sherpa town of Namche Bazaar, workers are building a new five-mile pipeline to bring water. This is to meet the growing tourist demand for showers and flush toilets. The local stream has human waste in it and does not have enough water during high season, when the town is very crowded. “Every village is digging a pit just beyond the houses for garbage. Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world,” he says. At base camp, human waste is now handled well and removed in plastic barrels. But Byers says these barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley, and it could leak into the region’s water.

“These problems can be solved, but we need to get serious about it,” he says. “One climber can spend $85,000 climbing Everest. And that’s fine. But at some point we’re going to have to address these other priorities. For half a million dollars a year, you could solve most of them.”

Climate change is harder. Byers works with local conservation committees to find and plan for climate change effects. They often look for new water sources or collect rainwater. He says glacial lakes are growing quickly, and they could burst and flood the Sherpa homeland. “There’s going to come a time when people are going to have to get out of their way.”

Changing weather is also affecting tourism. More clouds during times that are usually clear are closing Lukla Airport more often. Lukla Airport is the main entrance to the Everest region. Nepal is building a new road for 4x4 vehicles to Lukla to make sure tourists and money can still come. But Byers worries the road building is being done cheaply, which could cause serious soil erosion and landslides.

“Everest is the icon everyone knows,” he says. “It’s the canary in the coalmine that everyone understands. It’s the perfect laboratory for figuring out how to address some of these problems, like the impacts of climate change and tourism.”
