Opposition to Western Australia’s shark cull has grown. Thousands of people went to beaches around Australia and asked the state’s premier to stop the policy. RSPCA Australia and Virgin Atlantic owner Richard Branson also spoke against it.

The policy catches and kills sharks longer than three metres. It began after what the state government called an “unprecedented” number of shark attacks on Western Australia’s coast. A 35-year-old surfer was killed in November 2013. He was the sixth person to die from a shark attack in two years.

But the Shark Attack File says that Australia as a whole has averaged one shark-related death each year for the last 50 years.

Kate Faehrmann from the Sea Shepherd Conservation Society spoke at a protest in Perth. She said the policy will not work. She said drumlines, which are used to catch sharks, kill many kinds of animals. They can catch and kill sharks of different sizes, and also dolphins, turtles, and other animals.

People protested at Perth’s Cottesloe Beach and Sydney’s Manly Beach. Hundreds also protested at Glenelg in south-west Adelaide, and at beaches in Victoria and Queensland. Faehrmann said the protests showed many Australians want sharks protected. She said that even though people may fear sharks, many are saying the ocean is the sharks’ home and they do not want them killed.

Anthony Joyce, a surfer who once had his foot caught in a shark’s mouth, said killing sharks would not make a real difference.

The state government has refused to give an ongoing count of how many sharks have been killed. There have been reports that sharks smaller than three metres were released after being caught. Drumlines are floating drums anchored to the sea bed, with bait hanging on hooks under them.

Conservationists say there is no evidence the cull will reduce shark attacks on humans. They also say no previous cull has used only drumlines. Researchers at the University of Western Australia say the recent attacks may be linked to Western Australia having the fastest-growing population in Australia, not to more sharks.

Richard Peirce, chairman of the UK conservation charity the Shark Trust, said the cull would not work and could bring more predators closer to the coast. He said it is sad the government did not try other ways to deal with shark and human interactions. He also said that even if drumlines are checked during the day, leaving them in overnight could attract other predators because animals on the hooks may be injured.

Around the world in 2012, there were 80 unprovoked shark attacks, and seven were fatal. Humans kill nearly 100 million sharks each year. RSPCA Australia said it believes the cull is not justified. It said there is no evidence that more attacks are caused by more sharks. It said the increase matches changes in population and human behaviour, because more people are in the water.

Richard Branson told Fairfax Radio the policy could have the opposite effect of what was intended. He said it might harm tourism. He said it makes the problem seem bigger than it is and could stop people from wanting to come to Perth and the countryside nearby. He said it could make people worry for no good reason.
