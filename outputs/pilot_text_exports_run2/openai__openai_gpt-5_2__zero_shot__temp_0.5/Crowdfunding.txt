This new light could be like Trevor Baylis’s wind-up radio in the 1990s, before the internet. It is a cheap light that uses power from gravity. It could stop people in Africa and India from using dangerous kerosene lamps.

A British designer named Patrick Hunt made this light. First, he tried to get money from banks or venture capital. But he had trouble. He said it was slow and complicated, and his idea was new, so people did not want to take a risk.

So he tried crowdfunding on a US website called Indiegogo, which had just opened in the UK. In five days, he reached his goal and raised £36,200. By the end of 2012, in 40 days, he had raised £400,000.

The LED light works with a dynamo. The dynamo is turned by a 10kg bag of rocks that slowly falls. The bag is tied to the light and lifted about 2m high. As it slowly falls to the ground, it makes enough power for about 30 minutes of light.

Hunt is getting ready to make the light in China. He will test the market again by sending 1,000 lights to Africa before making many more. He hopes to make millions of them.

Hunt is part of a new group of entrepreneurs who use crowdfunding to get money. Another new site is InvestingZone. It matches rich people with start-up entrepreneurs.

Indiegogo does not give shares. Instead, people who donate can get “perks” at different donation levels. People who helped fund Hunt’s light felt good about helping poorer people, and they also got their own light.

Danae Ringelmann is a co-founder of Indiegogo. She says the “gravity light” shows how crowdfunding can be fair and can test an idea. She says money can be fast and efficient because it is not about knowing the right people at a bank or a venture capital firm. It is about showing customers and fans that your idea is good, and getting them to support it.

She also says that even ideas that do not get funding are worth testing. Then you find out sooner if the idea is not good and you can learn faster.

Ringelmann lives in the US and started as a Wall Street analyst. In 2008 she quit her job and tried to help friends in the arts raise money. The site first focused on films and launched at the Sundance Film Festival that year.

Five years later, the site was raising about $2m a week for new businesses. In December 2012, it added euro and sterling services to grow in Europe. Indiegogo says Britain is its third biggest market. International activity is up 41% since December.

There are many competitors, like Kickstarter, Seedrs, and Funding Circle. But Ringelmann says Indiegogo is different because anyone can start a campaign. No project is too strange.

Indiegogo charges a 4% fee for successful campaigns. If a campaign does not reach its goal, users can refund all the money for free, or keep all the money but pay a 9% fee.

A British woman named Lauren Pears raised £100,000 on the site to open a “cat café” in London. It is called Lady Dinah’s Cat Emporium. It has not opened yet, but it is described as a place where people can sit in a comfortable wingback chair, have hot tea, read a book, and be with a cat.

Ringelmann says some ideas are rejected by venture capitalists because they do not know if people really want the product. She says entrepreneurs can run an Indiegogo campaign, and even if they do not launch the product, the campaign can prove there is a market. Then venture capitalists may believe the idea can work.

She says crowdfunding helps you test your market, your price, and your features. It can also help you find new ways to make money and get important feedback.

Ringelmann says many people have ideas, but what matters is doing the work well. She says if you are afraid someone will steal your idea and do it better and faster, then you may not be the right person to do it. She says you need confidence to move fast and learn.

She says Indiegogo’s growth in Europe, and a deal that lets people pay with local card services like Maestro in the UK and Carte Bleue in France, as well as PayPal, are part of a dream to make finance more open to everyone. More than 7,000 campaigns are live on the site.

Crowdfunding as an option besides banks has grown, but it does not interest many very rich investors. They often want a stake in a business. This could start to change in the UK with the launch of InvestingZone.
