When the lottery balls fall and all six numbers match, many winners start spending. They may buy an Audi, book a holiday in the US, and call an estate agent to buy a home. A study looked at how lottery jackpot winners spend and invest their money.

The UK National Lottery started in 1994. Since then, it has made 3,000 millionaires. Together they have won more than £8.5bn, which is about £2.8m each on average. The study says this has also helped create 3,780 more millionaires among winners’ children, family, and friends. The study was written by Oxford Economics.

After winning, 59% of people stop working right away. But 19% keep their job, and 31% do unpaid volunteer work. The study says 98% of winners’ spending stayed in the UK. By spending money on homes, cars, and holidays, each winner is estimated to support six full-time jobs for one year.

The study says winners added almost £750m to GDP and created more than £500m in tax money for the Exchequer. Most of the money went on property. Winners spent £2.72bn on their main homes, and £170m on paying off debts and mortgages.

Many winners wanted to keep an income, so they spent £2.125bn on investments. They gave £1.17bn as gifts to family and friends. They also spent £680m on cars and holidays.

Camelot, which runs the UK National Lottery, paid for the study to mark the 3,000-winners milestone. The research used information from 100 winners who won £1m or more. It found that the 3,000 winners bought 7,958 houses or flats in the UK, which is 2.7 each on average, and spent £3.3bn. Most winners (82%) moved to a new main home, spending about £900,000 on average.

Some winners added extra features to their homes. Almost a third (29%) bought a hot tub. A walk-in wardrobe was chosen by 28%. Nearly a quarter (24%) bought a home behind electric gates. About 22% had a games room, and 7% installed a snooker table.

Big homes need care. 30% of winners hired a cleaner, and 24% hired a gardener. A small number (5%) hired a beautician.

For cars, 16% of winners chose Audis most often. Range Rovers and BMWs were also popular (11% each). Mercedes were 10%, and Land Rovers were 5%. Winners spent £463m on 17,190 cars. The average price of their favourite car was £46,116.

Holidays were important too. Most winners (68%) stayed in five-star hotels abroad. The US was the top choice for 27%, followed by the Caribbean (9%). Some also helped UK caravan sales. Over the past 18 years, 10% of millionaires bought a caravan, creating about £7.4m in sales.

Some winners used money for business. 15% started their own business. 9% helped other people start a business. 6% invested in or bought other people’s businesses. Businesses started or supported by winners employ 3,195 people, the study says.

Andy Logan, the report’s author, said the effects of winning spread further than expected. He said it changes the lives of friends and family, and it also affects the UK economy because so much money is spent in the UK. He said each win creates a “ripple effect” in this generation and often the next.
