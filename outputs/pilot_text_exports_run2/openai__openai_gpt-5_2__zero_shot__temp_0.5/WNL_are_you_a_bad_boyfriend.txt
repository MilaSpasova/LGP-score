Dan Sullivan was late to breakfast with the Guardian. He was late because police stopped him while he was riding his motorcycle.

Sullivan says he works too much. He misses dinner dates. He forgets to give presents.

So the 27-year-old venture capitalist made a start-up called BetterBoyfriend.me. It sends a present to girlfriends and wives every month for about $70.

Sullivan works in finance. He has been testing the program quietly. About 350 boyfriends have signed up. The service makes about $17,000 each month. He says most members are his friends from Harvard and from investing. They include venture capitalists, founders, and workers at companies like Apple, Google, and Facebook. Their girlfriends have been getting presents from Sullivan for the last year.

Each month, members choose one gift from a list of seven. The gifts include chocolate, tea sets, and manicures. The package is sent to the boyfriend with no brand name and no label.

Sullivan tests ideas by buying Facebook ads to see what happens. BetterBoyfriend.me became popular fast. Sullivan said it solved a problem many people have.

BetterBoyfriend.me was not Sullivan’s first idea. For a few days, he thought about sharing dogs. He said he saw rich guys looking unhappy while walking dogs. He said a dog is only valuable 5% of the time. His idea was to have someone babysit the dogs, and other people could rent them.

Another start-up is called InvisibleBoyfriend. It lets users create a believable social partner who is always available to talk. The result is a “boyfriend” who can text with you all day.

The founder, Kyle Tabor, made it to help users stop unwanted attention from other people. But he found that people became attached to their computer-made partners. He said many users want companionship through conversation, not just “proof” of a relationship.

Sullivan and Tabor were surprised by real relationships they formed with customers. Sullivan says he now feels like a relationship consultant for the boyfriends.

Sullivan admits he made mistakes. At first, the packages had receipts with his name, Dan Sullivan. One boyfriend wrote to him and said, “Listen, dude, she’s not mad but Cynthia found out.”

Sullivan says about 50% of the women who get packages know he is involved. He says this is connected to age. He thinks that after people have been married a while, they do not keep many secrets.

Over the year, Sullivan says he got to know the boyfriends well. They even delivered a package to a hospital delivery room. He said the key is to remember that his relationship is with the boyfriend.

When he first started the company, he put BetterBoyfriend.me tags on flower bouquets. He went to Dolores Park, a popular relaxing place in the Mission District, where there are many tech workers and young people.

He looked for couples and gave the bouquet to the girl. But the boyfriend did not like it at all. Sullivan said it made sense. So he changed his plan and started giving the bouquets to the boyfriends.
