James Hamblin is a senior editor at The Atlantic. In his series, “If Our Bodies Could Talk,” he tried a challenge: he stopped showering so much. He took fewer showers, and when he did shower, he did not use shampoo or soap. He wanted to see what happens when we clean our bodies too much.

He found something many people have found. When we scrub and wash a lot with soap and body wash, our skin tries harder to get back to normal. Showering removes the skin’s natural oil and bacteria. Hot water and cleaning products can remove too much.

After a shower, your skin may feel tight and dry. This happens because the shower washes away natural moisture. Also, our skin has millions of helpful bacteria. Frequent washing and scrubbing can kill them. When the bacteria grow back, the new microbes may cause more smell. So, showering too often might make you smell more. If you stop using soap and shower less, there can be an early period that feels unpleasant. After that, the skin often returns to balance, makes less oil, and healthy bacteria grow again.

Hamblin decided the human body can work well on its own. This is not only about smell or looks. Dermatologists say using less soap can help acne and eczema. Taking fewer showers and using fewer products can also help the environment. The average shower is seven minutes and uses 65 litres of water. That is 65 litres of clean drinking water mixed with soap and sent down the drain every day, sometimes more than once.

Clean water is becoming more important, especially as California has another summer of drought. Clean water is one of the world’s most valuable resources, and it may soon be in short supply. Also, body wash bottles create environmental waste. These are strong reasons to use fewer products and let your body be more natural.

If this idea makes you feel uncomfortable because you have been near people who do not shower much and still smell, relax. Many people who shower less still use deodorant if they need it. Everyone’s natural smell is different, and it can change with diet, hydration, and exercise. Also, washing your hands with soap is still important to help stop infectious diseases from spreading.

You do not need to stop all showers right away like Hamblin did. But there is a middle choice. Try taking fewer showers, use less soap, and let your skin’s bacteria grow a little.
