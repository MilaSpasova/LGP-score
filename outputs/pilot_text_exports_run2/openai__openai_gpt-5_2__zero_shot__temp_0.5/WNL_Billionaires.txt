A thick layer of bird droppings covers a shiny gold railing at one of Britain’s most expensive homes. There are pigeon bones, broken mirrors, and water running through broken parts of the building. This home is called The Towers. It is a palace worth £30m on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London. People have kept its damage secret until now.

The Towers is one of ten big houses in the middle of The Bishops Avenue. This street is a famous part of London’s expensive housing market. These houses have been almost empty since they were bought about 25 years ago. People believe they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. The tall columns are breaking, and the swimming pools with mosaic tiles are full of rubble. Nature has taken over so much that owls now live there.

This sad sight is seen along the whole street. Lloyds Bank says The Bishops Avenue is the second most expensive street in Britain. At the same time, many people in London struggle to buy a home because house prices rise by 11.2% each year. But 16 mansions on the most expensive part of the street are empty. Many are behind locked gates, with windows covered by steel bars. The gardens are overgrown, and guard dogs patrol the grounds.

Across the street is another empty mansion worth £18m, with broken windows and anti-climb paint on the walls. Another house has metal bars on its windows and was sold for £20m.

Even so, prices still rise. Dryades, a mansion that was owned until recently by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007 and is believed to be worth about £30m today. Heath Lodge, where fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou was murdered in 1984 by a silver bullet, is worth £13m today. It was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

For people who struggle to pay for housing in one of the world’s most expensive cities, these empty, falling-apart homes can feel very painful. A security guard said it is frustrating to see so much space—enough to house dozens of people—being left to crumble.

In the past, royals with oil money from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia were among the first to move to this curved road near Hampstead Heath. Iranians came here after the fall of the shah. Now, Chinese buyers are following Russians and Kazakhs who have paid millions for an address that estate agents say is as famous as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions were for sale for £65m and £38m. They promised lots of Italian marble, lifts with leather padding, and luxury panic rooms.

But on the empty properties, stone fountains are breaking and lawns have turned into muddy bogs. Inside one house, water drips through a huge crystal chandelier onto a thick carpet that is rotting under fallen parts of the ceiling. Moss grows through broken bricks. Mirror tiles are scattered in a bathroom. The swimming pool has about a foot of dirty water and flowers growing through the tiles. Wooden slats in the sauna are pushing out.

The most dramatic ruin is The Towers. It is a grand mansion set among acres of hornbeams, oaks, and limes. Its large halls are full of pigeons. Water pours through three storeys, and algae has turned the walls bright green. The water drops into a huge, empty basement swimming pool. There are unopened wooden crates marked “bullet-proof glass,” showing the earlier owners worried about security.

Today, very few people live on The Bishops Avenue all the time. A security guard outside one mansion said the owners were not there. Another guard outside Royal Mansion would not say if anyone was home. At another mansion, a staff member warned the Guardian about the guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare mogul with personal wealth of £45m, said he is one of the few people who lives there permanently.

He said, “It’s not a neighbourly place, where you can chat over the fence. To be honest, I have never seen what my neighbours look like. Next door, a Saudi princess spent £35m on a new house and I’ve never seen her. There are about three houses that are lived in 24/7 and half of the properties are occupied three to six months a year. The other half, who knows if they come or not?”

These broken, expensive houses show a culture where the richest people in the world treat British homes as investments. One Hyde Park, an apartment building in Knightsbridge, is another example. More than half the flats there are listed with the council as empty or as second homes.

Still, people on the avenue talk about building £5m apartments instead of £50m mansions to bring people back.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, plans to rebuild one of the most valuable sites on the street as 20 apartments. The building would have a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

He said, “If you build a big house and try and sell for £30m to £40m, it won’t sell. Locals won’t buy and so you have to bring in overseas buyers.”

But using the empty homes to help solve the housing crisis still seems far away. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some of the ruined homes could become affordable housing. He said the land price would be too high.

He said, “Very wealthy people own property there. Sometimes they live in them and sometimes they don’t.”
