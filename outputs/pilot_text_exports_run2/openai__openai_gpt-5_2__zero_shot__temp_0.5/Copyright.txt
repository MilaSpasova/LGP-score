Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is theft. He says it is like reaching into someone’s pocket and stealing their wallet. In an article for Index on Censorship, he strongly supports copyright laws. He criticizes internet users who download music or books without paying.

Pullman writes that new technology can look amazing, but people may not see that what they are doing is wrong. He says it is outrageous that people can steal an artist’s work and not be punished.

His article came after music industry leaders met British Prime Minister David Cameron at Downing Street to talk about web piracy.

Pullman, who wrote the His Dark Materials trilogy, says authors and musicians often work for years in poverty and without attention. When their work finally becomes good enough to make people happy, he says their chance to earn money can be taken away by illegal downloading. He says the rule is simple: if we want to enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

Pullman is writing in the next issue of the magazine in a dialogue with Cathy Casserly. She is the chief executive of Creative Commons. Creative Commons offers open content licences that let creators choose how to use copyright. Casserly says copyright has many problems because it was made in an “analogue age.” She says copyright can stop people from sharing, building on, and remixing each other’s work.

Casserly says artists should think of new ways to distribute and monetize their work. She quotes science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees. The editor, Rachael Jolley, says current copyright laws do not work well in the digital age and could make consumers into criminals. She says new models are needed so artists, writers, and musicians can earn a living.

Illegal downloading is very large. Ofcom says that in the UK, between November 2012 and January 2013, people pirated 280 million music tracks. They also pirated 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files.

Ofcom says 18% of internet users over age 12 admit they recently pirated content. It also says 9% say they are afraid of getting caught. Pullman writes that it is still surprising how quickly people can get music as MP3 downloads, especially for people who have been building up their iTunes list for some time.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron appointed the Conservative MP Mike Weatherley to advise him on this topic. A spokesman for the BPI, a record industry trade body, said Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creative producers that copyright protects. The spokesman said they hope Weatherley and the prime minister will influence the UK’s intellectual property office.
