One beautiful summer evening, I decided to swim near Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland. I moved there in 2012. A woman was in the water with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty often swims with a group of people.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Since then, hundreds of people have swum with her, so she can seem very friendly. She was even in an Irish tourist advertisement.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty’s belly. About 20 tourists and local people watched from the pier. Soon after I got into the water, Dusty left the woman and suddenly became very wild. Later I learned that she protects her space when she is with someone. Her tail was moving hard. At first I thought she was just showing off, but then I realized she might be angry.

I tried to swim back to the pier, but very quickly Dusty hit me with her nose. The hit was very strong and painful. I was pushed forward fast.

People on the pier stared at me in shock. Dusty stayed near me, still hitting the water with her tail. That scared me the most. I thought if she hit me with her tail, I could go under the water.

I reached the pier, but I could not climb out because I was hurt. I felt terrified. I shouted for help. A man reached down and pulled me onto the steps. Then another man came and said he was an orthopaedic surgeon who worked with marine injuries. He had been driving into Doolin and saw what was happening. I was very cold and very worried. I was afraid I might be bleeding inside. He checked me and told me he could not feel signs of internal bleeding, but he thought I had broken bones.

Later I found out I had six fractures in my spine, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in the hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for weeks. I could not work for five months. I had trouble moving, and I felt stiff and in pain. Then doctors said I had post-traumatic stress. After the near-death experience, I felt anxious about everything and reacted more strongly than before. I felt people were looking at me the wrong way. Loud noises became hard for me. I also had memory loss.

Three months before the accident, I had opened a health-food shop. But I had to give it up because I could not work anymore. That year was very hard, but now it is in the past. I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building my own osteopathy practice. I understand patients better because I have been one.

I am thankful I am healthy, and I want to stop other people from getting hurt. Many people think dolphins are always safe. Who expects a dolphin to attack a person? A dangerous animal with sharp teeth looks scary, but dolphins look like they are smiling.

I am not angry at Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her area, and she is a wild animal that can act in an unpredictable way. People need to understand that. Many people come to swim with her and do not know how dangerous it can be. People said my injuries were one of several injuries that summer.

After the man pulled me out, Dusty swam away. Then she came back and floated upright next to me, looking at me. We looked into each other’s eyes, and I felt she was sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin, and the anger was gone. The people on the pier were amazed. After that moment with her, my terror ended. I felt at peace with her.
