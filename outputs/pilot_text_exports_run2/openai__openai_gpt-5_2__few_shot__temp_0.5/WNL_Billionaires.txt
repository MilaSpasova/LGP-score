Thick bird droppings cover a gold-painted railing at one of Britain’s most expensive homes. Pigeon bones lie near broken mirrors, and water runs through broken ceiling edges. This home is The Towers. It is a £30m mansion on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London. Its serious damage has been kept secret until now.

The Towers is one of ten mansions on The Bishops Avenue. This road is in the middle of London’s rising property market. These mansions have been almost empty since they were bought about 25 years ago. It is believed they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. The columns are cracking. Swimming pools with mosaic tiles are full of broken stones. Nature has taken over, and owls have moved in.

This empty and broken scene appears again and again on this famous road. Lloyds Bank says The Bishops Avenue is the second most expensive street in Britain. At the same time, more people struggle to buy homes in London as prices rise by 11.2% a year. On the most expensive part of the road, 16 mansions are empty. Many are behind locked gates. Windows are covered with metal bars, and the grounds are overgrown and watched by guard dogs.

Across the street is another ruined mansion worth £18m, with broken windows and walls covered in anti-climb paint. Another mansion has metal bars on the windows and has been sold for £20m.

Even so, prices keep rising. Dryades, a mansion that until recently was owned by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007 and is believed to be worth about £30m today. Heath Lodge, where fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou was murdered in 1984 by a silver bullet, is worth £13m today. It was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

Seeing these empty homes can be very painful for people who struggle to pay for housing in one of the world’s most expensive cities. One security guard said it was upsetting to watch so much space fall apart. He said it was enough space to house dozens of people.

In the past, royals with oil money from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia were among the first to come to this road near Hampstead Heath. Iranians came here after the fall of the shah. Now, Chinese buyers are following Russians and Kazakhs. They have spent millions to get an address that estate agents say is as famous as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions were for sale for £65m and £38m. They promised Italian marble, lifts with leather padding, and luxury panic rooms.

But around the empty mansions, stone fountains are breaking and lawns have turned into wet bogs. Inside one mansion, water drips through a huge crystal chandelier onto a thick carpet. Parts of the ceiling have fallen down. Moss grows through broken bricks. Mirrored tiles are scattered in a bathroom. The swimming pool has about a foot of dirty, salty water, and flowers grow through the tiles. Wooden boards in the sauna bend outward.

The most dramatic ruin is The Towers. It is a large house among acres of hornbeams, oaks, and limes. Pigeons live in its big halls. Water pours through three storeys, and the walls have turned bright green with algae. The water drops into a large, empty basement swimming pool. Unopened wooden boxes marked “bullet-proof glass” show the security fears of the past owners.

Today, very few people live on The Bishops Avenue all the time. A security guard outside one mansion said the owners were not there. Another guard outside Royal Mansion would not say if anyone was home. A worker at another mansion warned the Guardian about the guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare mogul with personal wealth of £45m, said he is one of the few permanent residents.

He said, “It’s not a neighbourly place, where you can chat over the fence. To be honest, I have never seen what my neighbours look like. Next door, a Saudi princess spent £35m on a new house and I’ve never seen her. There are about three houses that are lived in 24/7 and half of the properties are occupied three to six months a year. The other half, who knows if they come or not?”

These broken, multi-million-pound homes show a property culture where the world’s richest people treat British homes as investments. One Hyde Park in Knightsbridge is another example. More than half the flats there are registered with the council as empty or second homes.

Even so, people on the avenue talk about building £5m apartments instead of £50m mansions to bring residents back.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, plans to rebuild one valuable site as 20 apartments. They will have a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

He said, “If you build a big house and try and sell for £30m to £40m, it won’t sell. Locals won’t buy and so you have to bring in overseas buyers.”

But using the empty properties to help solve the housing crisis still seems far away. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some ruined homes could become affordable homes. He said the land price would be too high.

He said, “Very wealthy people own property there. Sometimes they live in them and sometimes they don’t.”
