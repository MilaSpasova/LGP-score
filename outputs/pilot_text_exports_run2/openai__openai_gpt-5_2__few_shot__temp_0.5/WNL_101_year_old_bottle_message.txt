Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8th April, 2014, she talked about a special moment. She got a message in a bottle. Her grandfather threw it into the Baltic Sea 101 years earlier. People think it is the world’s oldest message in a bottle. A museum in Germany gave it to her, and the museum is showing it now.

Erdmann is 62. She said it was very surprising. A man came to her door and said he had mail from her grandfather. He said a message in a bottle was found, and the name on the card was her grandfather’s. The man was a family history researcher. He found her in Berlin after the letter was given to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg.

The bottle was a brown beer bottle. It stayed in the water for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it while fishing in the Baltic Sea near Kiel. Holger von Neuhoff, a curator at the museum, said this was the oldest bottled message he had seen. He said the museum has older documents without bottles, but with the bottle and the document together, this is the oldest right now. He also said it is in very good condition.

Researchers think Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913. He was hiking with a nature group. He was 20 years old then.

People could not read much of the postcard. But they could read the Berlin address on the front. They could also read his polite request. He asked the finder to send the note to his home address.

Erdmann said he also put two stamps in the bottle, so the finder would not have to pay. But he did not think it would take 101 years.

She said she felt emotional when the message arrived. She did not know her grandfather, because he died at age 54, six years before she was born.

She said she knew very little about him. But she learned he was a writer. She said he was open-minded, believed in freedom, and thought everyone should respect each other. She said he did a lot for young people. Later he travelled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful, because she could see where her roots came from.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and travelling around the world. She also said she is open-minded. She said what he taught his two daughters, her mother taught her, and she then taught her sons.

Even though she was happy to get the message, she said she hopes other people will not throw bottles with messages into the sea. She said the sea today is full of bottles and rubbish, so people should not throw more in.

The bottle and the message will be shown at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts will try to read the rest of the text. It is not clear what will happen to the bottle later, but Erdmann hopes it will stay in the museum.

She said they want to share some photos to put with the bottle, so visitors can see the young man who threw it into the water.
