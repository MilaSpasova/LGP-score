From glow-in-the-dark trees to underground bike sheds and solar-powered bins, there are many unusual ideas to make cities better places to live.

City life has many good points, but it is harder and harder to live in a sustainable way. People in cities often face pollution, traffic, and fewer green spaces.

Inventors are trying to help. Here are some of the wackier ideas, from a list of ten, for making cities more livable.

Pop-up parks
Some cities look like they are built more for cars than for people. A pop-up park is a simple idea. You take an empty car park, a little money, and one or two pot plants, and you make a small park.

The PARK(ing) project started as an art experiment in San Francisco. It has now spread around the world. The movement has inspired ideas like temporary urban farms and ecology demonstrations. It also has a day of action every September.

Subterranean storage
Not everyone in a city uses a car. Bikes are becoming more popular. But people need a safe place to store them.

A Tokyo-based engineering firm, Giken, has an idea called an “eco-cycle, anti-seismic underground bicycle park”. It is a cylinder only seven metres wide. It goes deep into the ground and can hold 204 bikes. Owners can get their bike back by pressing a button. The system brings the bike up in about 13 seconds.

Glow-in-the-dark trees
When people think of trees that glow in the dark, they often think of Christmas decorations. But Daan Roosegaarde thinks differently.

This Dutch designer-artist has created a “bioluminescent” plant. The experimental technology mixes DNA from glowing marine bacteria with part of a plant (the chloroplast genome). This makes a glow like a jellyfish. Trials are happening to make a large-scale version. Roosegaarde hopes it could one day replace normal street lighting.

Footfall harvesting
Every day, hundreds of commuters and shoppers in West Ham, in east London, cross an elevated walkway near the underground station. Most do not notice that the ground feels a little springy.

The rubber surface bends by five millimetres. This movement helps power the streetlights above. The walkway has smart tiles that capture energy from people’s footsteps and turn it into electricity. Pavegen, the UK firm behind this idea, has also installed a similar system at London’s Heathrow Airport and in other places around the world.

Supertrees
People have made man-made trees. There is a group of them at Singapore’s Gardens by the Bay.

These steel-framed “supertrees” can be up to 50 metres high. Flowers and ferns grow on them. Their metal tops also absorb and spread out heat. They can collect rainwater and help with air ventilation for two “climate-controlled biomes” (large conservatories) below. Eleven of the 18 trees also have solar panels on their “branches”.
