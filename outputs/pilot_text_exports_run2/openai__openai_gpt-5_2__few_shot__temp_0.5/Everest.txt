Kenton Cool usually likes to talk, but now he can hardly speak. Climbing high in the mountains has made his voice rough. He is now in Kathmandu, the capital of Nepal. That morning, he flew down from Everest base camp.

Cool is thinking about an unusual set of climbs he finished the weekend before. Early on Saturday morning, he reached the top of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of the three main summits in the Everest “horseshoe.” This horseshoe surrounds a snowy valley called the Western Cwm.

The same day, he climbed to the top of Everest. He reached the summit in total darkness early on Sunday. Then he and his climbing partner continued to the top of Lhotse. They reached Lhotse on Monday morning. This was the third mountain in the three-peaks challenge.

“It was a snatched opportunity,” he says. “For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. That does not remove the physical achievement. I set the bar at a certain level. But the next person will move the bar higher and do it without ropes or bottled oxygen.”

It has been 60 years since Everest was first climbed. Many reports look back to Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. They describe an earlier time, before today’s commercial tourism and environmental damage. I ask Cool to look forward and imagine what top climbers may do 60 years from now.

“I hate to think,” he says. He talks about the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. Steck left the mountain in April after what Cool calls “an altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck planned a big climb: he wanted to climb Everest’s west ridge (first done in 1963), go down to the South Col, and then climb Lhotse right away by a new route. He planned to do all of this without fixed ropes. “Ueli had been training like a machine,” Cool says. “He is in a class by himself. He is technically brilliant, and his fitness was extremely high. It would have been amazing to see what he could have done.”

Tourism is a different question. One sign of change is a helicopter rescue by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro’s rude language helped cause the conflict at Camp 2. On Tuesday, Moro flew a powerful helicopter back to Everest to rescue a sick climber at 7,800 metres.

This was the highest rescue ever done on Everest. It also shows how fast helicopter flights have increased in recent years. By 2073, the mountain might even have a helipad on the South Col. Tourists could arrive there using bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing what is possible for rescuing climbers and also the many trekkers who go as far as base camp.

Mountain geographer and environmentalist Alton Byers says it is not clear if the Everest region can keep handling fast-growing tourism. He says climate change and tourism together are putting new pressure on the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers in the region are shrinking, and some are disappearing. This is already causing big problems. “Everywhere you go, people say there is less water,” he says. “There is less water for farming and less water for the new lodges being built.”

Byers says that in the Sherpa town of Namche Bazaar, workers are building a new five-mile pipeline to bring water. It is needed because more tourists want showers and flush toilets. The local stream is polluted with human waste. It also does not have enough water for a town that is extremely crowded in the busy season. “Every village is digging a pit just beyond the houses for garbage. Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world,” he says. Human waste at base camp is handled well now and taken away in plastic barrels. But Byers says these barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley. This pit could leak into the region’s rivers and streams.

“These problems can be solved, but we need to take them seriously,” he says. “One climber can spend $85,000 to climb Everest. That is fine. But at some point we must deal with these other needs. For $500,000 a year, you could solve most of them.”

Climate change is harder. Byers works with local conservation groups to find and plan for climate change effects. They often look for new water sources or set up rainwater collection. Another danger is the fast growth of glacial lakes. These lakes could burst and flood the Sherpa homeland. “A time will come when people will have to get out of their way,” he says.

Weather changes also affect tourism. More clouds during times that are usually clear are closing Lukla Airport more often. Lukla is the main entrance to the Everest region. A new road for 4x4 vehicles is being built to Lukla to keep tourists and their money coming. But Byers worries that roads in Nepal are being built too cheaply and too fast. He says this can cause serious soil erosion and landslides.

“Everest is the famous symbol everyone knows,” he says. “It is a warning sign that everyone understands. It is the perfect place to learn how to deal with problems like climate change and tourism.”
