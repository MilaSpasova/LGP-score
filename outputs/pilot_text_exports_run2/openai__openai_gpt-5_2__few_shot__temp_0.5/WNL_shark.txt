More people are now against Western Australia’s shark cull. Thousands of people went to beaches across Australia to ask the state’s premier to stop the policy. RSPCA Australia and Richard Branson, who owns Virgin Atlantic, also spoke against it.

The policy catches and kills sharks longer than three metres. It started after what the state government called an “unprecedented” number of shark attacks on the coast of Western Australia. A 35-year-old surfer was killed in November 2013. He was the sixth person to die from a shark attack in two years.

But the Shark Attack File says that across all Australia, there has been about one shark-related death each year on average for the last 50 years.

Kate Faehrmann from the Sea Shepherd Conservation Society spoke at a protest in Perth. She said the policy will not work. She said drumlines, used to catch sharks, kill many animals, not only big sharks. She said they can also kill dolphins, turtles, and other animals. She said this is why the community does not want the policy.

Thousands protested at Cottesloe Beach in Perth and at Manly Beach in Sydney. Hundreds more protested at Glenelg in south-west Adelaide, and at beaches in Victoria and Queensland. Faehrmann said the protests showed many Australians want sharks protected. She said that even though people fear sharks because of movies like Jaws, many people are saying the ocean is the sharks’ home and they do not want sharks killed.

Anthony Joyce, a surfer who once had his foot caught in a shark’s mouth, said killing these sharks will not make a real difference.

The state government has refused to give a running total of how many sharks have been killed. Reports say some sharks smaller than three metres were caught on drumlines and then released. Drumlines are floating drums tied to the sea bed, with bait on hooks under them.

Conservationists say there is no evidence the cull will reduce shark attacks on humans. They say no earlier cull used only drumlines. Researchers at the University of Western Australia say the recent increase in attacks may be linked to Western Australia having the fastest-growing population in Australia, not to more sharks.

Richard Peirce from the UK charity Shark Trust said the cull will not work and could bring more predators closer to the coast. He said it is sad the government chose killing instead of trying other methods. He also said that leaving drumlines in the water overnight can attract other predators, because sharks and other animals may be hooked and injured.

Around the world in 2012, sharks made 80 unprovoked attacks, and seven were fatal. But humans kill nearly 100m sharks every year. RSPCA Australia said it believes the cull is not justified. It said there is no evidence that more attacks are caused by more sharks. It said the increase fits with changes in population and human behaviour, because more people are in the water.

Richard Branson told Fairfax Radio the policy is backfiring. He said the premier, Colin Barnett, may have thought it would help tourism, but Branson thinks it will do the opposite. He said it makes people think there is a big problem when there is not, and it may stop people from coming to Perth and the countryside nearby. He said it will only make people worry for no reason.
