Dan Sullivan was late to breakfast with the Guardian. Police stopped him while he was riding his motorcycle.

Sullivan says he works too much. He misses dinner dates. He forgets to buy gifts.

So the 27-year-old venture capitalist in Silicon Valley made a start-up to help: BetterBoyfriend.me. For about $70 a month, it sends a gift every month to a girlfriend or wife.

Sullivan works in finance. He has been testing the service quietly. About 350 boyfriends have signed up. They bring in about $17,000 each month. Many members are his friends from Harvard and investing. They are venture capitalists, founders, and workers at companies like Apple, Google, and Facebook. Their girlfriends have been getting gifts for the last year.

Each month, members pick one gift from a list of seven choices, like chocolate, tea sets, or manicures. The package is sent to the boyfriend with no brand name and no label.

Sullivan tests ideas by buying Facebook ads to see how much interest he gets. BetterBoyfriend.me became popular. Sullivan said it solved a problem many people have.

BetterBoyfriend.me was not his first idea. For a few days, he thought about sharing dogs. He said he saw rich guys looking unhappy while walking dogs. He thought a dog is only valuable 5% of the time. He suggested paying someone to watch the dogs, so other people could rent them.

Another start-up is called InvisibleBoyfriend. It lets users create a believable social partner who is always ready to talk. The result is a “boyfriend” who can text with you all day.

Founder Kyle Tabor started it to help users stop unwanted attention. But he found that people can become attached to their computer-made partners. He said many users want companionship through conversation, not just “proof” of a relationship.

For Sullivan and Tabor, a surprise was that real relationships formed with customers. Sullivan says he now feels like a relationship consultant for the boyfriends.

Sullivan says he made mistakes. At first, the packages had receipts with his name, Dan Sullivan. One boyfriend wrote to him and said, “Listen, dude, she’s not mad but Cynthia found out.”

About 50% of the women who get BetterBoyfriend.me packages know that Sullivan is involved. Sullivan says this is connected to age. He thinks that after people have been married a while, they keep fewer secrets.

Over the year, Sullivan says he got to know the boyfriends well. One time, they even delivered a package to a hospital delivery room. He said the important thing is that his relationship is with the boyfriend.

When he first started the company, he put BetterBoyfriend.me tags on flower bouquets. He went to Dolores Park, a popular place in the Mission District. He looked for couples and gave flowers to the girl. The boyfriend did not like it. So he changed his plan and started giving the flowers to the boyfriends.
