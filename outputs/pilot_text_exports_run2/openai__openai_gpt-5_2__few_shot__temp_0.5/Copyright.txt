Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is theft. He says it is like putting your hand in someone’s pocket and stealing a wallet. He wrote this in an article for Index on Censorship. Pullman is president of the Society of Authors. He strongly supports copyright laws. He criticizes internet users who download music or books without paying.

Pullman writes that new technology looks amazing, but people do not see that what they do is wrong. He says it is shocking that people can steal an artist’s work and not get punished.

He wrote the article after music industry leaders met British Prime Minister David Cameron at Downing Street. They talked about web piracy.

Pullman, who wrote the His Dark Materials trilogy, says many authors and musicians work in poverty and are not known for years. They work hard to create things that audiences enjoy. But when they finally can earn money, illegal downloading takes that chance away. He says the rule is simple: if we want to enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

In the next issue of the magazine, Pullman is in a dialogue with Cathy Casserly. She is chief executive of Creative Commons. Creative Commons offers open content licences that let creators control copyright in their own way. Casserly says copyright has many problems because it was made in an “analogue age”. She says copyright often stops people from sharing, building on, and remixing each other’s work.

Casserly says artists need new and creative ways to share and make money from their work. She quotes science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees with Casserly. The editor, Rachael Jolley, says current copyright laws do not work in the digital age and may turn consumers into criminals. She says we need new ways for artists, writers, and musicians to earn money.

The debate is strong, and illegal downloading is very large. Ofcom says that in the UK, between November 2012 and January 2013, people pirated 280 million music tracks. They also pirated 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files.

Ofcom says 18% of internet users over 12 say they recently pirated content. Also, 9% say they are afraid of getting caught. Pullman says MP3 downloads make it very easy and fast to get music, and this is still surprising to some people.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron appointed Conservative MP Mike Weatherley as his adviser on this topic. A spokesman for the BPI, the record industry trade body, said Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creative producers it protects. The spokesman said they hope Weatherley and the prime minister will influence the UK’s intellectual property office.
