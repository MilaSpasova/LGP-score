This invention could be a great new idea, like Trevor Baylis’s wind-up radio in the 1990s before the internet. It is a cheap light that gets free power from gravity. It could stop people in Africa and India from using dangerous kerosene lamps.

British designer Patrick Hunt tried to get money from banks or venture capital to start selling his invention. But he had a problem. He said getting funding was slow and complex. The idea was unproven, and nobody wanted to take the risk.

So he tried crowdfunding on a US website called Indiegogo, which had just opened in the UK. In five days, he reached his goal and raised £36,200. By the end of 2012, in 40 days, he had raised £400,000.

The LED light uses a dynamo. The dynamo is powered when a 10kg bag of rocks slowly falls. The weight is attached to the light and lifted to about 2m high. As it falls to the ground, it makes enough power for 30 minutes of light.

Hunt is getting ready to make the product in China. He will test the market again by sending 1,000 lights to Africa before making many more. He hopes to make millions of units.

Hunt is part of a new wave of entrepreneurs who use crowdfunding to get money. Another new site is InvestingZone. It matches wealthy people with start-up entrepreneurs.

Indiegogo does not offer shares. Instead, people who donate can get “perks” at different donation levels. People who funded Hunt’s light could feel good about helping poorer people, and they also got their own light.

Danae Ringelmann, a co-founder of Indiegogo, says the “gravity light” shows how crowdfunding can work. She says finance can be fast and fair, because it is not about knowing people in a bank or a venture capital firm. It is about showing your idea to customers and fans, and getting them to support it with money.

She also says it is useful to test ideas that do not get funding. Then you find out faster if the idea is not good, and you learn.

Ringelmann lives in the US. She first worked as a Wall Street analyst. In 2008, she quit and tried to help friends in the arts raise money.

Indiegogo first focused on films. It launched at the Sundance Film Festival in 2008.

Five years later, the site was raising about $2m a week for new businesses. In December 2012, it started euro and sterling services to grow in Europe. It says Britain is its third biggest market. International activity has gone up 41% since December.

There are many competitors, such as Kickstarter, Seedrs, and Funding Circle. But Ringelmann says Indiegogo is different because anyone can start a campaign. No project is too strange.

Indiegogo takes a 4% fee from successful campaigns. If a campaign does not reach its goal, users can choose to refund all the money with no charge. Or they can keep the money they raised, but then Indiegogo takes a 9% fee.

A British woman, Lauren Pears, raised £100,000 on the site to open a “cat café” in London. It is called Lady Dinah’s Cat Emporium. It has not opened yet. It is described as a place where people can sit in a comfortable wingback chair, have hot tea, read a book, and spend time with a cat.

Ringelmann says venture capitalists sometimes reject ideas because they do not know if people really want them. But an Indiegogo campaign can show proof that there is a market, even before the project launches.

She says crowdfunding helps entrepreneurs test their market, price, and features. It can also help them find new revenue streams and get important feedback.

Ringelmann says many people have ideas, but success depends on doing the work. She says if you are afraid someone will steal your idea and do it better and faster, then you may not be the right person to do it. She says you need confidence to move fast and to learn.

Ringelmann says expanding in Europe is part of a dream to make finance more open to everyone. Indiegogo has a deal with a web transaction provider. This will let people pay with local card services like Maestro in the UK and Carte Bleue in France, as well as PayPal. More than 7,000 campaigns are live on the site.

Crowdfunding has grown as an alternative to banks. But it is less interesting to big investors, who usually want a stake in a promising business. This might start to change in the UK with the launch of InvestingZone.
