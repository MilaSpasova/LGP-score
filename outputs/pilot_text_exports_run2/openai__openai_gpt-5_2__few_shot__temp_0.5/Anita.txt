Anitta is a new Brazilian funk star. She has millions of fans. She brought the sound of the favela into popular music. But now people are debating her skin colour.

Some anti-discrimination groups and writers say she had to give up part of her black identity to sell music to a mostly white, middle-class market.

The debate grew after old and new photos were published. In the older photo, before she was famous, she looks darker. In the newer photo, used for marketing after she became famous, she looks paler. People ask if this happened because of skin-whitening products, cosmetic surgery, or more likely, Photoshop. The photos made people talk again about whether you need to be pale to succeed in Brazil.

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote that this shows hidden discrimination in media and marketing. She said many people do not want to admit racism. She said statistics show Brazil is not as equal as people think. She also said society often connects white skin with being rich and successful, and black skin with the opposite.

Anitta was born Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

Now she is very famous. She released an album and a big hit song, “Show das Poderosas.” The song reached number one and got 52 million views on YouTube.

Many people love her as a pop idol with a message and catchy songs. Her supporters also present her as a link between mostly black and mixed-race poor areas on Rio’s hills and the richer, whiter areas below.

She changed some parts of baile funk to reach more people. She reduced sexy dancing, gang references, and explicit lyrics. Now some people ask if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract richer fans. Arraes said pop stars can feel forced to change things like hair or nose shape. She said this can harm self-esteem.

This topic is sensitive in Brazil, where many people are mixed race. Brazil was one of the last big countries to ban slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. But in Brazil, colour can matter more than race or ancestry. Even though Brazil wants to be a multiracial democracy, skin tone is linked to inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Until 2011, black or mixed-race students spent about two fewer years in school on average.

The government says the gap is getting smaller because of university quota systems and other affirmative action policies. But inequality is still clear. Most business and government leaders are white. Many low-level jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In rich areas like Ipanema and Gávea, you are more likely to see black nannies with white children than the reverse.

Colour is hard to define. In the 2010 census, people who said they were white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million said black, two million said Asian, and 0.5% said indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology lecturer at the Federal University of Pernambuco, said Anitta won middle-class fans by making a rebellious style calmer and easier for everyone to accept. He said this was not done by making her look whiter. He said it was a change in where she made her art: from the outskirts to the centre.

Other people also say the colour issue is too big. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst, worked with Anitta when she was an intern. He said her image changed a lot, but not her colour. He said it was about makeup, hairstyling, and clothing, and not pressure. He said she always liked to sing, dance, and show her talent, and that this natural style helped her succeed.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador, said the problem was not the public, but music producers and media executives. He said the public showed they care about music by choosing Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, as a recent winner of the show The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for comment. But in a recent interview, she said identity should be defined by the person. She said a powerful woman does not need to be beautiful, but can be strong because of her attitude. She said her work tries to show that we can be who we want.
