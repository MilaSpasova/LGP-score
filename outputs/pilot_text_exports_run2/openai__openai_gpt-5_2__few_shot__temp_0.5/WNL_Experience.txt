One beautiful summer evening, I decided to swim near Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland. I moved there in 2012. A woman was in the water with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty often swims with a group of people.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Since then, hundreds of people have swum with her. This made her seem very tame. She was even in an Irish tourist ad. In the ad, a girl says she wants to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty on her stomach. It looked safe and friendly. About 20 tourists and local people were on the pier watching.

Just after I got into the water, Dusty left the woman and suddenly became very wild. Later I learned that she is very protective of her space when she is with someone. Her tail was moving fast. At first, I thought it was just a show. Then I understood she might be angry.

I knew I had to get out, so I swam to the pier. But very quickly Dusty hit me hard with her nose. It was very strong and very painful. I was pushed forward.

People on the pier looked down at me in shock. Dusty stayed next to me, and her tail was still moving fast. That scared me the most. I thought, if she hits me with her tail, I could go under the water and die.

I reached the pier, but I could not climb out because I was hurt. I was very scared. I shouted for help. A man reached in and pulled me onto the steps.

Then another man came. He said he was an orthopaedic surgeon who specialized in marine trauma. He had been driving into Doolin and saw what was happening. I was very cold and very worried. I did not know how bad my injuries were. My biggest fear was bleeding inside my body. He checked me and tried to calm me. He said he could not feel signs of internal bleeding, but he thought I probably had broken bones.

Later I learned my injuries were serious. I had six fractures in my spine, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for several weeks. I could not work for five months. I had trouble moving, and I had stiffness and pain.

Later I was diagnosed with post-traumatic stress. After the near-death experience, I felt anxious about everything. I overreacted in ways I never did before. I felt people were looking at me the wrong way. Loud noises were hard for me. I also had memory loss.

Three months before the accident, I opened a health-food shop. But I had to give it up because I could not work anymore.

It was the hardest year of my life. But now it is in the past. I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building up my own osteopathy practice. I understand patients better now because I have been a patient.

I am thankful that I am healthy. I really want to stop other people from getting hurt. People often think dolphins are always kind and safe. Most people would never think a dolphin could attack a person. If a dangerous animal comes at you with teeth showing, it is clearly scary. But dolphins have a wide smile, so they seem friendly.

I am not angry with Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her territory, and she is a wild animal. She can be unpredictable. People need to know this. Many people come to swim with her, and they do not understand how dangerous it can be. My injuries were reported as one of several injuries that summer.

After the man pulled me out, Dusty swam away. Then she came back. She floated upright next to me and looked at me. We looked into each other’s eyes. I felt she was sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin. The anger was gone. The people on the pier were amazed. After that moment, I did not feel terror anymore. I made peace with her.
