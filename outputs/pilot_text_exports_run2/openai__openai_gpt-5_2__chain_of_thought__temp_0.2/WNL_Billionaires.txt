A thick layer of bird droppings covers a shiny railing at one of Britain’s most expensive homes. Pigeon skeletons are on the floor among broken mirrors. Water runs through broken roof edges. This place is called The Towers. It is a £30m mansion on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London. Its bad condition has been kept secret until now.

The Towers is one of ten mansions in the middle of The Bishops Avenue. This road is linked to London’s rising house prices. These homes have been almost empty since they were bought about 25 years ago. It is believed they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. Their Greek-style columns are breaking. Their swimming pools, covered with mosaic tiles, are full of broken pieces. Nature has taken over so much that owls now live there.

This empty and broken look is seen along this road, even though it is meant to be very special. Lloyds Bank says it is the second most expensive street in Britain. At the same time, more people cannot afford a home in London. House prices rise by 11.2% each year. Yet 16 mansions on the most expensive part of The Bishops Avenue are empty. Many are behind locked gates. Their windows are covered with steel bars. Their gardens are overgrown, and guard dogs patrol.

Across the street is another empty mansion worth £18m. Its windows are smashed, and its walls have anti-climb paint. Another mansion has metal bars on the windows and was sold for £20m.

Even so, prices keep going up. Dryades, a mansion that was owned until recently by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007. It is believed to be worth about £30m today. Heath Lodge, where fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou was murdered in 1984 by a silver bullet, is worth £13m today. It was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

These empty, falling-apart homes can feel painful to people who struggle to pay for housing in one of the world’s most expensive cities. A security guard on the road said it was upsetting to see so much space going to waste. He said the homes have tens of thousands of square feet, enough to house dozens of people, but they are falling apart.

Royals with oil money from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia were among the first to move to this curved road near Hampstead Heath. Iranians came here after the fall of the shah. Now, Chinese buyers are following Russians and Kazakhs. They have spent millions to get an address that estate agents say is as famous as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions were for sale for £65m and £38m. They promised lots of Italian marble, lifts with padded leather walls, and luxury panic rooms.

But on the grounds of the empty homes, stone fountains are breaking and lawns have turned into muddy bogs. Inside one mansion, water drips through a huge crystal chandelier. It falls onto a thick carpet that is rotting under parts of a collapsed ceiling. Moss grows through broken bricks. Shards of mirror tiles are spread across a bathroom. The swimming pool has about a foot of dirty, salty water, and flowers grow through its tiles. Wooden boards bend away from the sauna.

The most dramatic ruin is The Towers. It is a large mansion set among acres of hornbeams, oaks, and limes. Pigeons fill its big halls with high ceilings. Water runs through three storeys, and the walls have turned bright green with algae. The water drops into a huge, empty basement swimming pool. Unopened wooden crates marked “bullet-proof glass” show that the past owners worried about security.

Today, very few people live on The Bishops Avenue all the time. A security guard outside one mansion said the owners were not there. Another guard, outside Royal Mansion, would not say if anyone was home. A staff member at another mansion only warned the Guardian about the guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare mogul with personal wealth of £45m, said he is one of the few full-time residents.

He said, “It’s not a neighbourly place, where you can chat over the fence. To be honest, I have never seen what my neighbours look like. Next door, a Saudi princess spent £35m on a new house and I’ve never seen her. There are about three houses that are lived in 24/7 and half of the properties are occupied three to six months a year. The other half, who knows if they come or not?”

These broken, very expensive homes show a property culture where the richest people in the world treat British homes as investments. One Hyde Park, a group of apartments in Knightsbridge, is another example. More than half the flats there are registered with the council as empty or as second homes.

Even so, people on the road talk about building £5m apartments instead of £50m mansions, to try to bring people back.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, plans to rebuild one of the most valuable sites on the road. He wants to make 20 apartments with a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

He said, “If you build a big house and try and sell for £30m to £40m, it won’t sell. Locals won’t buy and so you have to bring in overseas buyers.”

But using these empty homes to help fix the housing crisis still seems far away. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some of the empty homes could become affordable homes. He said the price of the land would be too high.

He said, “Very wealthy people own property there. Sometimes they live in them and sometimes they don’t.”
