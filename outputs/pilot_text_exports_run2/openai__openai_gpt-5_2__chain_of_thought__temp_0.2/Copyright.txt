Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is theft. He says it is like reaching into someone’s pocket and stealing their wallet. In an article for Index on Censorship, Pullman (who is president of the Society of Authors) strongly supports copyright laws. He criticizes internet users who think it is OK to download music or books without paying.

Pullman writes that new technology is so impressive that some people do not notice the wrong thing they are doing. He says it is wrong that someone can steal an artist’s work and not get punished.

His article came after music industry leaders met British Prime Minister David Cameron at Downing Street to talk about web piracy.

Pullman, the writer of the His Dark Materials trilogy, says authors and musicians often work in poverty and without attention for years. He says that when they finally make work that audiences enjoy, their chance to earn a living from it can be taken away by illegal downloading. He ends with a simple idea: if we want to enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

Pullman is writing in the next issue of the group’s magazine in a dialogue with Cathy Casserly. Casserly is the chief executive of Creative Commons, which offers open content licences that let creators manage copyright in different ways. Casserly says there are many problems with copyright because it was made “in an analogue age.” She says copyright can stop many ways people could share, build on, and remix each other’s work.

Casserly says artists need to think of new ways to share and make money from their work. She quotes science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees. The magazine’s editor, Rachael Jolley, said existing copyright laws do not work in the digital age and could treat consumers like criminals. She said we need new ways for artists, writers, and musicians to earn a living from their work.

The debate is active, and illegal downloading is very large. Data collected by Ofcom (the independent regulator and competition authority for the UK communications industries) suggests that in the UK, between November 2012 and January 2013, 280 million music tracks were pirated. Also pirated were 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files.

Ofcom said 18% of internet users aged over 12 admit they recently pirated content, and 9% say they fear getting caught. Pullman writes that it is still surprising how easy and fast it is to get music as MP3 downloads, especially for people who have been building up their iTunes list for some time.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron appointed Conservative MP Mike Weatherley to be his adviser on the issue. A spokesman for the BPI, the record industry trade body, said Mike Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creative producers it is meant to protect. The spokesman said they hope Weatherley and the prime minister will support copyright in the work of the UK’s intellectual property office.
