Opposition to Western Australia’s shark cull has grown stronger. Thousands of people went to beaches across Australia to ask the state’s premier to stop the policy. RSPCA Australia and Virgin Atlantic owner Richard Branson also spoke against it.

The policy catches and kills sharks longer than three metres. It began after what the state government called an “unprecedented” number of shark attacks on Western Australia’s coast. In November 2013, a 35-year-old surfer was killed. He was the sixth person to die from a shark attack in two years.

But the Shark Attack File says that, across all of Australia, there has been an average of one shark-related death each year for the last 50 years.

Kate Faehrmann, a board member of the Sea Shepherd Conservation Society, spoke at a protest in Perth. She said the policy will not work. She said drumlines are used to catch sharks, but they catch many kinds of animals. She said they can kill sharks of any size, and also dolphins, turtles, and other animals. She said this is why the community does not want the policy.

Thousands protested at Perth’s Cottesloe Beach and Sydney’s Manly Beach. Hundreds also protested at Glenelg in south-west Adelaide, and at beaches in Victoria and Queensland. Faehrmann said the protests showed many Australians want sharks protected. She said that even though people are scared because of movies like Jaws, many people are saying the ocean is the sharks’ home and they do not want them killed.

Anthony Joyce, a surfer who once had his foot caught in a shark’s mouth, said killing sharks will make no real difference.

The state government has not given a running count of how many sharks have been killed. There have been reports that sharks smaller than three metres were released after being caught. Drumlines are floating drums tied to the sea bed, with bait on hooks below them.

Conservationists say there is no proof the cull will lower shark attacks on humans. They say no earlier cull used only drumlines. Researchers at the University of Western Australia say the recent rise in shark attacks may be linked to the state having the fastest-growing population in Australia, not to more sharks.

Richard Peirce, chairman of the UK charity the Shark Trust, said the cull would not work and could bring more predators closer to the coast. He said the government ignored advice and chose a plan that is ineffective and makes things worse. He also said that even if drumlines are checked during the day, leaving them in the water overnight could attract other predators to the area because animals may be hooked and injured.

Around the world in 2012, there were 80 unprovoked shark attacks, and seven were fatal. At the same time, humans kill nearly 100m sharks every year. RSPCA Australia said it believes the cull is not justified. It said there is no evidence that more attacks are caused by more sharks. Instead, it said the change fits with more people being in the water.

Richard Branson told Fairfax Radio the policy would backfire. He said Premier Colin Barnett may have thought it would help tourism, but Branson thinks it will do the opposite. He said it makes the problem look bigger than it is, and may stop people from coming to Perth and the surrounding countryside. He said it will only make people worry when they do not need to.
