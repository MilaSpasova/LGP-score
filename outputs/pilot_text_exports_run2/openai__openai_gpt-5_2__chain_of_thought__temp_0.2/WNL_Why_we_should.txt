James Hamblin is a senior editor at the American magazine The Atlantic. In his series, “If Our Bodies Could Talk,” he tried a challenge: he stopped showering for a while. He took fewer showers, and when he did shower, he did not use shampoo or soap. He wanted to see what happens when we clean our bodies too much.

He found something many other people have found. The more we try to clean our skin with soaps, body washes, and scrubbers, the more our skin tries to fix itself and get back to normal. Showering washes away the skin’s natural oil and bacteria. Many people think that is the point, but it can work too well, especially with hot water and cleaning products.

After a shower, you may feel dry and tight. This is because a lot of your skin’s natural moisture has been washed away. Also, your skin has millions of helpful bacteria, like your gut does. Frequent rubbing and scrubbing can destroy these helpful bacteria. When the bacteria grow back, the ones that return may make more body smell. So showering too often may actually make you smell more. When you stop showering and stop using soap, your skin may go through an early adjustment time that can seem gross. After that, the skin often gets back into balance, makes less oil, and the healthy bacteria grow again.

After his experiment, Hamblin learned what other people who avoid soap and showers have said for years: the human body can do well on its own. It is not only about smell or looks. Dermatologists say that using soap less can help acne and eczema.

Taking fewer showers and using fewer cleaning products can also help the environment. The average shower lasts seven minutes and uses 65 litres of water. That is 65 litres of clean, drinkable water that we mix with soap and send down the drain each day—sometimes more than once.

Clean water is becoming harder to ignore, as California faces another summer of drought. It is clear that clean water is one of the world’s most valuable things, and it may soon be in short supply. Also, all those body wash bottles affect the environment. These are strong reasons to let your body be more natural.

If this idea makes you feel uneasy because you remember being close to people who seem to skip showers and still smell, relax. Many people who shower less still use deodorant if they need it. People’s natural smell is different, and it can change with diet, hydration, and exercise. Also, washing your hands with soap is still recommended to help stop the spread of infectious diseases.

You do not have to stop suddenly like Hamblin did. But between cleaning many times a day and never showering at all, there is a simple idea: reduce. Skip a few showers, use less soap, and let the helpful bacteria on your skin grow a bit.
