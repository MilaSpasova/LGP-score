From glow-in-the-dark trees to underground bike storage and solar-powered bins, here are some unusual ideas to make cities nicer places to live.

Living in a city has many good sides, but it is getting harder to live in a sustainable way. People in cities often face pollution, traffic, and fewer green spaces.

Now inventors are trying new ideas. Here are some of the wackier solutions to make cities more livable.

Pop-up parks
Some cities seem built more for cars than for people. A pop-up park is a simple idea. You take an empty car park, spend a little money, add one or two pot plants, and make a small park. The PARK(ing) project started as an art experiment in San Francisco and then spread around the world. People have also made temporary city farms and ecology demonstrations. The movement has a day of action every September.

Subterranean storage
Not everyone in a city uses a car. Bikes are becoming more popular. But where can you keep a bike safe? A Tokyo engineering firm called Giken made a solution called an “eco-cycle, anti-seismic underground bicycle park.” It is a cylinder that is only seven metres wide. It goes deep underground and can hold 204 bikes. Owners can get their bike back by pressing a button. The system brings the bike up in about 13 seconds.

Glow-in-the-dark trees
When most people think of glowing trees, they think of Christmas lights. But Daan Roosegaarde does not. This Dutch designer-artist created a “bioluminescent” plant. The experimental technology mixes DNA from glowing sea bacteria with part of a plant’s genes, so the plant glows like a jellyfish. Tests are happening to make a large-scale version. Roosegaarde hopes it could one day replace normal street lights.

Footfall harvesting
Every day, hundreds of commuters and shoppers in West Ham in east London walk across a raised footbridge near the underground station. Most people do not notice that the ground feels a bit springy. Even fewer know that the surface bending by five millimetres helps power the streetlights above. The floor has smart tiles that catch the energy from people’s footsteps and turn it into electricity. Pavegen, the UK company behind it, has also put a similar system in London’s Heathrow Airport and other places.

Supertrees
It was bound to happen: trees made by people. Singapore’s Gardens by the Bay has a small group of them. These steel “supertrees” can be up to 50 metres tall. Flowers and ferns grow on them, and their metal tops help take in and spread out heat. They can collect rainwater and help move air for two “climate-controlled biomes” (large conservatories) below. Eleven of the 18 trees also have solar panels on their “branches.”
