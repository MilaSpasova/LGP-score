It was a warm summer evening. I decided to swim near Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland, where I moved in 2012. In the water, a woman was swimming with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty often swims with a group of people.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Since then, hundreds of people have swum with her, so she seems very friendly. She was even in an Irish tourist ad where a girl says she wants to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty’s belly. It looked safe and fun. About 20 tourists and local people stood on the pier and watched. Soon after I got into the water, Dusty left the woman and suddenly became very wild. Later I learned that Dusty can be very protective of her space when she is with someone. Her tail was hitting the water hard. At first I thought she was just showing off, but then I realized she might be angry.

I tried to swim back to the pier, but Dusty rushed at me right away and hit me with her nose. It was very strong and hurt a lot. I was thrown forward. People on the pier stared at me. Dusty stayed close, and her tail kept flapping. That scared me the most. I thought if her tail hit me, I could go under the water.

I reached the pier, but I could not climb out because I was hurt. I was very afraid. I yelled for help. A man reached in and pulled me onto the steps. Then another man came. He said he was an orthopaedic surgeon who specialized in injuries from the sea. He had been driving into Doolin and saw what was happening. I was very cold and worried. My biggest fear was bleeding inside my body. He checked me and told me he could not feel signs of that, but he thought I probably had broken bones. Later I learned I had six fractures in my spine, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in the hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for several weeks. I could not work for five months, and I had trouble moving, plus stiffness and pain. Later I was diagnosed with post-traumatic stress. The near-death experience made me anxious, and I overreacted in ways I never did before. I felt like people were looking at me the wrong way. Loud noises bothered me, and I had memory loss. Three months before the accident, I had opened a health-food shop, but I had to give it up because I could not work.

That year was the hardest ever, but now it is behind me. I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building my own osteopathy practice. I understand my patients better because I have been a patient.

I am thankful that I am healthy, and I want to stop other people from getting hurt. People often think dolphins are always safe and kind. Who expects a dolphin to attack a person? If a dangerous animal comes at you with its teeth showing, it looks scary. But dolphins have a wide smile.

I am not angry with Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her space, and she is a wild animal that cannot always be trusted. People need to know that. Many people come to swim with her, and they do not understand how dangerous it can be. My injuries were said to be one of several that summer.

After the man pulled me out, Dusty swam away. Then she came back and floated upright next to me, looking at me. We looked at each other, and I felt she was sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin, and the anger was gone. The people on the pier were amazed. After that moment, I stopped feeling so terrified. I felt at peace with her.
