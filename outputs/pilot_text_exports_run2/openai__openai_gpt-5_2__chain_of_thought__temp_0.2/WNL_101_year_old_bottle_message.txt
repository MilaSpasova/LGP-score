Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8th April, 2014, she told people about an unusual moment. She received a message in a bottle, 101 years after her grandfather threw it into the Baltic Sea. People think it may be the world’s oldest message in a bottle. A museum in Germany, which is showing it now, gave it to her.

Erdmann is 62. She said it was very surprising. A man came to her door and said he had post from her grandfather. He said a message in a bottle had been found, and the name on the card was her grandfather’s. The visitor was a family-history researcher. He found Erdmann in Berlin after the letter was given to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg.

The brown beer bottle had been in the water for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it in his catch while fishing in the Baltic Sea near the northern city of Kiel. Holger von Neuhoff, a museum curator for ocean and science, said this bottle message was the oldest he had seen. He said the museum has older documents that were found without a bottle. But with both the bottle and the document, this is the oldest right now. He said it is in very good condition.

Researchers believe Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913. He was hiking with a nature group. He was 20 years old then.

Most of the postcard cannot be read. But the Berlin address on the front can be read. The card also asked the person who found it to send the note to his home address.

Erdmann said he also put two stamps from that time in the bottle, so the finder would not have to pay. But he did not think it would take 101 years.

Erdmann said she was touched by the message. She did not know her grandfather because he died at age 54, six years before she was born.

She said she knew very little about him. But she learned he was a writer. She said he was open-minded, believed in freedom, and believed people should respect each other. She said he helped young people. Later he traveled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful because she could see where her roots came from.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and traveling around the world. She said she is open-minded too. She said what her grandfather taught his two daughters, her mother taught her, and she then taught her sons.

Even though she was happy to get the message, she said she hopes people will not copy what her grandfather did and throw bottles with messages into the sea. She said the sea is now full of many bottles and rubbish, so people should not throw more in.

The message and bottle will be on display at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts will try to read the rest of the text. It is not clear what will happen to the bottle then, but Erdmann hopes it will stay at the museum.

She said they want to share some photos to put with the bottle, so visitors can see the young man who threw it into the water.
