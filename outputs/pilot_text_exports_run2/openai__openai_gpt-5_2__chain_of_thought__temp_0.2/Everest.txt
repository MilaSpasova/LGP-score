Kenton Cool usually likes to talk, but now he can hardly speak. He worked very hard high in the mountains, and his voice is rough. He is now in Kathmandu, the capital of Nepal. That morning, he flew down from Everest base camp.

Cool is thinking about an amazing set of climbs he finished over the last weekend. Early on Saturday morning, he reached the top (the summit) of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of the three main summits in the Everest “horseshoe.” These mountains surround an icy valley called the Western Cwm.

On the same day, he climbed to the summit of Everest. He reached the top early on Sunday, in complete darkness. Then he and his climbing partner went on to the summit of Lhotse on Monday morning. Lhotse was the third peak in this three-mountain challenge.

“It was a snatched opportunity,” he says. “For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. That doesn’t take away the physical achievement of what I did. I’ve set the bar at a certain level. But whoever comes along next will move the bar further and do it without ropes or bottled oxygen.”

Sixty years after Everest was first climbed, many news stories look back at Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. They compare their time to today, with modern commercialism and environmental damage. I ask Cool to look ahead and imagine what top climbers might do 60 years from now.

“I hate to think,” he says. But he talks about the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. In April, Steck left the mountain after what Cool calls “an altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck planned to climb Everest’s west ridge (first climbed in 1963). Then Steck planned to go down to the South Col and immediately climb Lhotse by a new route, all without fixed ropes. “Ueli had been training like a machine,” Cool says. “He’s a climber in a class all his own. He’s technically brilliant but he had also taken his physical condition to an astronomic level. It would have been amazing to see what he could have done.”

Tourism is another question. One sign of change is a helicopter rescue by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro’s angry language helped start the fight at Camp 2. On Tuesday, Moro flew back to Everest in a powerful helicopter. He rescued a climber who was very sick at 7,800 metres.

This was the highest rescue ever done on Everest. It also shows that helicopter flights have increased very fast in recent years. By 2073, there could even be a helipad on the South Col. Tourists could be brought there while breathing bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing what is possible for rescuing both climbers and the many more trekkers who go as far as base camp.

Mountain geographer and environmentalist Alton Byers says it is not clear if the Everest region can keep handling a growing tourism business. He says climate change and tourism together are putting new pressure on the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers in the Everest region are shrinking, and some are disappearing. This is already having a big effect. “Everywhere you go, people are talking about how there’s less water. There’s less water for agriculture and less water for all the new lodges that are getting built.”

Byers says that in the Sherpa town of Namche Bazaar, workers are building a new five-mile pipeline to bring water. This is to meet the rising tourist demand for showers and flush toilets. The local stream has been polluted by human waste. It also does not provide enough water for a place that, in high season, is extremely crowded. “Every village is digging a pit just beyond the houses for garbage. Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world,” he says.

Human waste at base camp is now handled well and removed in plastic barrels. But Byers says these barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley. That pit could leak into the region’s waterways.

“These problems can be solved, but we need to get serious about it,” he says. “One climber can spend $85,000 climbing Everest. And that’s fine. But at some point we’re going to have to address these other priorities. For half a million dollars a year, you could solve most of them.”

Climate change is harder. Byers works with local conservation committees to find and plan for climate change effects. Often this means finding new water sources or collecting rainwater. Another danger is that glacial lakes are growing quickly. These lakes could burst and flood the Sherpa homeland. “There’s going to come a time when people are going to have to get out of their way.”

Weather changes are also affecting tourism. More clouds during times that are usually clear are closing Lukla Airport more often. Lukla Airport is the main entrance to the Everest region. A new road for 4x4 vehicles is being built to Lukla to make sure tourists and their money can keep coming. But Byers worries that Nepal’s fast-growing road system is being built cheaply. He says this could cause serious soil erosion and landslides.

“Everest is the icon everyone knows,” he says. “It’s the canary in the coalmine that everyone understands. It’s the perfect laboratory for figuring out how to address some of these problems, like the impacts of climate change and tourism.”
