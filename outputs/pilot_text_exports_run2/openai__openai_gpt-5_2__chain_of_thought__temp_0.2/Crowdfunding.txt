This new light could be as helpful as Trevor Baylis’s wind-up radio was in the 1990s, before the internet. It is a cheap light that uses gravity to make free power. It could stop people using dangerous kerosene lamps in Africa and India.

A British designer named Patrick Hunt tried to get money from banks or venture capital to start selling his invention. But it did not work. He said getting funding was slow and complicated, and the idea was not proven, so people did not want to take the risk.

So Hunt tried crowdfunding on a US website called Indiegogo, which had just opened in the UK. In five days, he reached his goal and raised £36,200. By the end of 2012, his campaign was so popular that in 40 days he raised £400,000.

The LED light uses a dynamo. The dynamo is turned by a 10kg bag of rocks falling down. The bag is tied to the light and lifted about 2m high. As it slowly drops to the ground, it makes enough power for 30 minutes of light.

Hunt is getting ready to make the light in China. He will test the market again by sending 1,000 lights to Africa before full mass production. He hopes to make millions of units.

Hunt is part of a new group of business owners using the fast-growing crowdfunding industry to raise money. Another new site is InvestingZone. It connects rich people with start-up entrepreneurs.

Indiegogo does not give people shares. Instead, it lets campaigns offer “perks” for different donation amounts. People who donated to Hunt’s light could feel good about helping poorer people, and they also got their own light.

Danae Ringelmann, a co-founder of Indiegogo, says the “gravity light” shows how crowdfunding can work. She says it can quickly test if an idea is good. She says funding can be fast and fair because it is not about knowing the right people at a bank or a venture capital firm. It is about proving your idea to customers and fans, and getting them to support it with money.

Ringelmann also says that even ideas that do not get funded are still worth testing. This is because you can learn sooner that the idea is not good and save time.

Ringelmann lives in the US. She started her career as a Wall Street analyst. In 2008, she quit and used her skills to help friends in the arts raise money.

At first, the site focused on films and launched at the Sundance Film Festival that year.

Five years later, the site was raising about $2m each week for new businesses that were starting or growing. In December 2012, it added euro and sterling services to grow in Europe. It says Britain is its third biggest market. It also says international activity has gone up 41% since December.

Indiegogo has many competitors, including Kickstarter, Seedrs, and Funding Circle. But Ringelmann says Indiegogo is different because anyone can start a campaign. No project is considered too strange.

Indiegogo charges a 4% fee for campaigns that reach their goal. If a campaign does not reach its goal, users can choose to refund all the money to donors for free, or keep the money raised and pay a 9% fee.

A British woman named Lauren Pears raised £100,000 on the site to open a “cat café” in London. It is called Lady Dinah’s Cat Emporium. It has not opened yet, but it is described as a place where people can sit in a wingback chair, have a hot cup of tea, read a book, and be with a cat.

Ringelmann says some ideas are rejected by venture capitalists because they are not sure the market wants them. She says entrepreneurs can run an Indiegogo campaign, even before launching the product, and use the campaign as proof that people want it.

She says crowdfunding lets you test your market, pricing, and features, find new ways to earn money, and get important feedback.

Ringelmann says that many people have ideas, but success depends on doing the work well. She says that if you are scared someone will steal your idea and do it better and faster, then you may not be the right person to do it. She says you need confidence to move fast and learn.

Ringelmann says expanding in Europe and making a deal with a web payment company are part of a dream to make finance open to more people. The deal will let people pay using local card services like Maestro in the UK and Carte Bleue in France, as well as PayPal. More than 7,000 funding campaigns are live on the site.

Crowdfunding has grown as another choice instead of banks. But it does not interest some very rich investors, who want at least a stake in a business. This could start to change in the UK with the launch of InvestingZone.
