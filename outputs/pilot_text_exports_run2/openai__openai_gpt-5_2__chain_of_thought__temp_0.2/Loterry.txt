The lottery balls have dropped down the chute and all six numbers match. Now it may be time to buy an Audi, book a holiday in the US, and call an estate agent. An analysis says many lottery millionaires do these kinds of things.

Since it started in 1994, the lottery has created 3,000 millionaires. Together they have won more than £8.5bn in total, with an average of £2.8m each. The study authors, Oxford Economics, say this money also helped create 3,780 more millionaires among winners’ children, family, and friends.

Most winners (59%) stop working right away. But 19% keep doing their job, and 31% do unpaid volunteer work. The study says 98% of winners’ spending stayed in the UK. By spending on homes, cars, and holidays, each winner is estimated to keep six people in full-time jobs for one year.

Winners have added almost £750m to gross domestic product (GDP). They have also created more than £500m in tax money for the Exchequer. Most of the money went to property. Winners spent £2.72bn on their main homes, and £170m on paying off old debt and mortgages.

Keeping an income was important, so winners spent £2.125bn on investments. They gave £1.17bn in gifts to family and friends. They spent £680m on cars and holidays.

Camelot, the company that runs the UK National Lottery, asked for the study to mark the milestone of 3,000 winners. The study used research from 100 winners who won £1m or more. It found that the 3,000 winners bought 7,958 houses or flats in the UK, which is 2.7 each, and spent £3.3bn. Most winners (82%) changed their main home, spending an average of £900,000.

Some people added extras to their new homes. Almost a third (29%) bought a hot tub. A walk-in wardrobe was important for 28%. Nearly a quarter (24%) chose a home behind electric gates. Also, 22% had a games room, and 7% put in a snooker table.

Bigger homes need care. So 30% of winners hired a cleaner, and 24% hired a gardener. A small number (5%) hired a beautician.

Audi was the favourite car for 16% of winners. Range Rovers and BMWs were also popular (11% each), as well as Mercedes (10%) and Land Rovers (5%). Winners spent £463m on 17,190 cars. The average price of their favourite car was £46,116.

Holidays were important too. Most winners (68%) chose five-star hotels in other countries. The US was the top place to go for 27%, followed by the Caribbean (9%). In the UK, caravan sales also gained. Over the past 18 years, 10% of millionaires bought a caravan, creating sales of about £7.4m.

Some winners (15%) started their own businesses. Another 9% helped other people start businesses, and 6% invested in or bought other people’s businesses. The study says businesses started or supported by lottery winners employ 3,195 people.

Andy Logan, who wrote the report, said a win affects more people than they expected. He said it changes the lives of friends and family, and each win also has a clear effect on the UK economy, because so much money is spent in the UK. He said each win creates a ripple effect for this generation and often the next.
