Brazil has a new funk star named Anitta. She became famous by bringing the sound of the favela into popular music. But she is now in the middle of a debate about skin colour.

Anti-discrimination groups and writers say Anitta, the music industry’s fastest rising star, had to give up part of her black identity to succeed in a mostly white, middle-class market.

The debate grew after people shared “before and after” photos of Anitta. They show her skin looking much lighter since she signed a deal with Warner.

In the first photo, from when she was not well known, she looked darker. In the second photo, a marketing picture after she became famous, she looks paler. People argue about why this happened. Some mention whitening products and cosmetic surgery. Others say it was more likely Photoshop. The difference has started a new discussion about whether you need to be pale to get ahead in Brazil.

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote a post criticizing hidden discrimination in media and marketing. She said the change in Anitta’s image showed this problem. Arraes wrote that many people do not accept that they are racist, and think they live in a multiracial democracy, but statistics show that is not true. She said the “whitening” points to a society that does not support diversity. She also said people often connect “white” with being rich and successful, and see “black” as the opposite.

Anitta was born Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

She later became very famous with an album and a big hit song, Show das Poderosas. The song reached number one and got 52 million views on YouTube.

Many fans love her as a pop idol with a strong message and catchy songs. The people supporting her also describe her as a bridge between the mostly black and mixed-race shanty towns on Rio’s hillsides and the richer, whiter areas below.

She has reduced the sexual dancing, “gangsta” talk, and explicit lyrics found in baile funk. But now some people ask if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract a richer audience. Arraes said this can pressure pop stars. She said stars with curly hair may feel forced to straighten it, and stars with a big nose may feel forced to get rhinoplasty. She said this can create a harmful cycle for self-esteem.

Race and colour are sensitive issues in Brazil, a country with many mixed-race people. Brazil was one of the last large countries to ban slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. But in Brazil, colour can matter more than race or family background. And even though the country wants to be a multiracial democracy, skin tone is clearly linked to inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Up to 2011, black or mixed-race students also spent an average of two fewer years in school.

The government says the gap is getting smaller because of quotas for university places and other affirmative action. But the difference is still easy to see. Most business and government executives are white, while many low-paid jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In rich areas like Ipanema and Gávea, you are much more likely to see black nannies pushing strollers with white toddlers than a white nanny pushing a black child.

Colour is also hard to define. In the 2010 census, people who called themselves white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million said they were black, two million said they were Asian, and 0.5% said they were indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology lecturer at the Federal University of Pernambuco, believes Anitta won middle-class fans by taking a rebellious sound and making it calmer and easier for more people to accept.

He said this was not done by racial whitening. He said the main change was the social place where Anitta made her art: from the periphery to the centre.

Other people say the colour debate is too big. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who worked with Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot, but not her colour.

He said he did not believe it was whitening. He said it was more about how they present her, with makeup, hairstylists, and clothing. He also said he did not think she was being pressured. He said Anitta always liked to show off, sing, and dance, and that this was natural for her. He believed this naturalness helped her succeed.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador, said the prejudice was not mainly from the public, but from music producers and media executives. He said the public showed they cared about music itself by choosing Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, as the recent winner of the talent show The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for comment. But in a recent interview, she said identity should be defined by the person.

She said an “all-powerful” woman does not need to be beautiful. She said a woman can be marvellous and powerful because of her attitude. She said her work tries to tell everyone that we can be who we want.
