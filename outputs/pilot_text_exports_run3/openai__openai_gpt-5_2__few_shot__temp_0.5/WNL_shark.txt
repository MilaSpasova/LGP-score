More people are now against Western Australia’s shark cull. Thousands of people went to beaches across Australia to ask the state’s premier to stop it. RSPCA Australia and Richard Branson, the owner of Virgin Atlantic, also spoke against it.

The state began catching and killing sharks longer than three metres after what it called an “unprecedented” number of shark attacks on its coast. A 35-year-old surfer was killed in November 2013. He was the sixth person to die from a shark attack in two years.

But the Shark Attack File says that across all of Australia, there has been an average of one shark-related death each year for the last 50 years.

Kate Faehrmann from the Sea Shepherd Conservation Society spoke at a protest in Perth. She said the policy will not work. She said drumlines, which are used to catch sharks, can kill many animals. They can catch sharks of any size, and also dolphins, turtles, and other animals. She said this is why many people do not want the policy.

Thousands protested at Cottesloe Beach in Perth and Manly Beach in Sydney. Hundreds also protested at Glenelg in south-west Adelaide, and at beaches in Victoria and Queensland. Faehrmann said the protests showed that many Australians want sharks protected. She said that even with fear from movies like Jaws, many people are saying, “That’s their ocean. We respect them, we love them and we don’t want them killed.”

Anthony Joyce, a surfer who once had his foot caught in a shark’s mouth, said the number of sharks killed will not change much.

The state government has refused to give a running total of sharks killed. There have been reports that sharks smaller than three metres were released after being caught. Drumlines are floating drums fixed to the seabed, with bait on hooks under them.

Conservationists say there is no evidence the cull will reduce shark attacks on humans. They say no earlier cull used only drumlines. Researchers at the University of Western Australia say the recent increase in attacks may be linked to Western Australia having the fastest-growing population in Australia, not to more sharks.

Richard Peirce from the UK charity the Shark Trust said the cull will not work and could bring more predators close to the coast. He said leaving drumlines in overnight can attract other predators to the area because sharks and other animals may be hooked and injured.

Worldwide in 2012, there were 80 unprovoked shark attacks, and seven were fatal. Humans kill nearly 100m sharks each year. RSPCA Australia said it believes the cull is not justified. It said there is no evidence that more attacks are caused by more sharks. It said the increase fits with changes in population and human behaviour, with more people in the water.

Richard Branson told Fairfax Radio the policy is backfiring. He said the premier, Colin Barnett, may have thought it would help tourism, but it may do the opposite. He said it makes people think there is a big problem, and it may stop people from coming to Perth and the countryside around it. He said it will only worry people for no good reason.
