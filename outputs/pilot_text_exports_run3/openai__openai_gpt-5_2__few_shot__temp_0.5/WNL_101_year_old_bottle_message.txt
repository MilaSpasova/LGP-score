Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8th April 2014, she talked about a special moment. She received a message in a bottle. Her grandfather had thrown it into the Baltic Sea 101 years earlier. People think it is the world’s oldest message in a bottle. A museum in Germany gave it to her and is showing it now.

Erdmann, 62, said it was very surprising. A man came to her door and said he had mail from her grandfather. He said a bottle had been found, and the name on the card was her grandfather’s name. The man was a genealogical researcher. He found her in Berlin after the letter was given to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg.

The bottle was a brown beer bottle. It had been in the water for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it while fishing in the Baltic Sea near the city of Kiel. Holger von Neuhoff, a museum curator for ocean and science, said it was the oldest bottled message he had seen. He said the museum has older documents without a bottle, but this one has both the bottle and the document. He said it is in very good condition.

Researchers believe her grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913. He was hiking with a nature appreciation group. He was 20 years old.

Most of the postcard could not be read. But people could read the Berlin address on the front. They could also read a polite request from the writer. He asked the finder to send the note to his home address.

Erdmann said her grandfather also put two stamps in the bottle. This was so the finder would not have to pay. But he did not think it would take 101 years.

Erdmann said the message made her emotional. She did not know her grandfather because he died at age 54, before she was born.

She said she knew little about him. Later, she learned he was a writer who was open-minded. He believed in freedom and that everyone should respect each other. She said he did a lot for young people. Later he traveled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful to learn about her family roots.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and traveling. She said she is open-minded too. She said what her grandfather taught his two daughters was taught to her by her mother, and she then taught it to her sons.

She was happy to get the message, but she hopes people do not throw message bottles into the sea today. She said the sea already has too many bottles and too much rubbish.

The bottle and message will be on display at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts will try to read the rest of the text. It is not clear what will happen to the bottle then, but Erdmann hopes it will stay in the museum.

She said they want to add some photos with the bottle. This way visitors can see the young man who threw the bottle into the water.
