One warm summer evening, I decided to go for a swim near Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland. I moved there in 2012. A woman was in the water with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty often swims with a group of people.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Since then, hundreds of people have swum with her. This made her seem very tame. She was even in an Irish tourist-board advertisement.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty on the belly. About 20 tourists and local people were watching from the pier. I went into the water too.

Soon, Dusty left the woman and suddenly became very wild. Later I learned she can be very protective of her space when she is with someone. Her tail was hitting the water hard. At first I thought she was just showing off, but then I thought she might be angry.

I tried to swim back to the pier to get out. But very quickly, Dusty hit me with her nose. It was very strong and very painful, and she was moving very fast. I was thrown forward.

People on the pier stared down at me. Dusty was still beside me, hitting the water with her tail. That scared me most. I thought if she hit me with her tail, I could go under the water.

I reached the pier, but I could not climb out because I was hurt. I felt great fear. I shouted for help. A man reached in and pulled me onto the steps.

Then another man came. He said he was an orthopaedic surgeon who specialized in marine trauma. He had been driving into Doolin and saw what was happening. I was very cold and very worried. I was afraid of bleeding inside my body.

He checked me and said he could not feel signs of internal bleeding, but he thought I probably had broken bones. Later I learned my injuries were serious: six spinal fractures, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for several weeks. I could not work for five months, and I had limited movement, stiffness, and pain.

Later, doctors said I had post-traumatic stress. After the near-death experience, I felt anxious about everything and reacted too strongly. I felt people were looking at me in a bad way. Loud noises were hard for me. I also had memory loss.

Three months before the accident, I had opened a health-food shop. But I had to give it up because I could not work. It was the hardest year, but now it is behind me.

I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building my own osteopathy practice. I understand patients better because I have been one.

I am thankful that I am healthy, and I want to stop other people from getting hurt. People often think dolphins are always friendly. Who would expect a dolphin to attack a person? A dangerous animal with its teeth showing looks scary, but dolphins look like they are smiling.

I am not angry with Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her area, and she is a wild animal and can be unpredictable. People need to know this. Many people come to swim with her, and they do not understand how dangerous it can be. My injuries were said to be among several injuries that summer.

After the man pulled me out, Dusty swam away. Then she came back and floated upright next to me, looking at me. We looked into each other’s eyes. I felt she was sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin, and the anger was gone. The people on the pier were amazed. After that moment, my terror ended. I made peace with her.
