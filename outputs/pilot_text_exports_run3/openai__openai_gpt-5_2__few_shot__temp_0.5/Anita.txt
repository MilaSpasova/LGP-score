Anitta is a new and very popular Brazilian funk singer. She has millions of fans. She helped bring music from the favelas into the mainstream. But now people are debating her skin colour.

Some people who fight discrimination say Anitta had to hide or reduce her black identity to become popular with Brazil’s mostly white, middle-class market.

The debate grew after people shared old and new photos of her. In the older photo, before she was famous, her skin looks darker. In a newer marketing photo, after she became famous, her skin looks lighter. Some people think this could be from skin-whitening products, cosmetic surgery, or Photoshop. This has started the discussion again about whether you need lighter skin to succeed in Brazil.

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote a post about this. She said the media and marketing show hidden discrimination. She wrote that many people say they are not racist and that Brazil is a multiracial democracy, but statistics show something different. She said society often connects being white with being rich, successful, and good, and connects being black with the opposite. She said this can hurt people’s self-esteem. She also said that pop stars may feel forced to change things like curly hair or a big nose.

Anitta’s real name is Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

She became very famous with an album and a hit song, “Show das Poderosas.” The song reached number one on the charts and got 52 million views on YouTube.

Many fans love her as a pop idol with catchy songs and a strong message. People who support her present her as a bridge between the mostly black and mixed-race shanty towns on Rio’s hills and the richer and whiter areas below.

She also changed her style. She reduced the suggestive dancing, gang references, and explicit lyrics of baile funk. Now some people ask if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract a richer audience.

This topic is sensitive in Brazil, a country with many mixed-race people. Brazil was one of the last large countries to ban slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. In Brazil, colour often matters more than race or ancestry. Even though the country wants to be a multiracial democracy, there is a clear link between skin tone and inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Until 2011, black or mixed-race students spent about two fewer years in school on average.

The government says the gap is getting smaller because of quotas for university places and other affirmative action. But big differences remain. Most business and government leaders are white. Many low-level jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In rich areas like Ipanema and Gávea, it is more common to see black nannies pushing strollers with white toddlers than the opposite.

Colour is also hard to define. In the 2010 census, people who said they were white became a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million said they were black, two million said they were Asian, and 0.5% said they were indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology lecturer at the Federal University of Pernambuco, said Anitta became popular with the middle class by taking a rebellious sound and making it calmer and easier for more people to accept. He said this was not because of racial whitening. He said the big change was the social place where she made her art: from the periphery to the centre.

Other people also say the colour issue is too big. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who worked with Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot, but not her colour. He said it is more about makeup, hairstylists, and clothes. He said he does not think she was pressured. He said she always liked to show off, sing, and dance, and that this natural style helped her succeed.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador, said the prejudice is not mainly in the public, but in music producers and media executives. He said the public showed they care about music itself by choosing Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, as a recent winner of the talent show The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for a comment. But in a recent interview, she said identity should be decided by the person. She said an “all-powerful” woman does not need to be beautiful, but has strong attitude. She said her work tries to show that we can be who we want.
