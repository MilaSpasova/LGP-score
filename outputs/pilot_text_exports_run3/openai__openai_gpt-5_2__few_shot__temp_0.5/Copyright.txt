Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is theft. He says it is like putting your hand in someone’s pocket and stealing their wallet. He wrote this in an article for Index on Censorship. Pullman is president of the Society of Authors. He strongly supports copyright laws. He criticizes internet users who think it is OK to download music or books without paying.

Pullman writes that new technology looks very impressive, but people do not see that what they do is wrong. He says it is terrible that people can steal an artist’s work and not be punished.

His article came after leaders from the music industry met British Prime Minister David Cameron at Downing Street. They talked about web piracy.

Pullman, the writer of the His Dark Materials trilogy, says authors and musicians often work for years with little money and little attention. He says when their work finally becomes good and people enjoy it, their chance to earn money can be taken away by illegal downloading. He ends with a simple idea: if we want to enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

In the next issue of the magazine, Pullman is in a dialogue with Cathy Casserly. She is the chief executive of Creative Commons. Creative Commons offers open content licences that let creators control copyright in a different way. Casserly says copyright has many problems because it was made in an “analogue age”. She says copyright rules can stop people from sharing, building on, and remixing each other’s work.

She says artists need to think of new ways to share and make money from their work. She quotes science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees. The editor, Rachael Jolley, says current copyright laws do not work in the digital age and may make consumers into criminals. She says we need new models so artists, writers, and musicians can earn a living from their work.

The debate is active, and illegal downloading is very large. Ofcom (the independent regulator and competition authority for the UK communications industries) collected data. It suggests that in the UK, between November 2012 and January 2013, people pirated 280 million music tracks, 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files.

Ofcom also said 18% of internet users over age 12 say they recently pirated content, and 9% say they are afraid of getting caught. Pullman writes that it is still amazing how easily and quickly people can get music as MP3 downloads.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron appointed Conservative MP Mike Weatherley as his adviser on this issue. A spokesman for the BPI, the record industry trade body, said Mike Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creative producers it protects. The spokesman said they hope Weatherley’s influence and the prime minister’s support for copyright will affect the UK’s intellectual property office.
