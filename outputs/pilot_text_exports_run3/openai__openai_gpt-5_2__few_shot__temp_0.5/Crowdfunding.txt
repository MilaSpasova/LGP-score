This invention may be as useful as Trevor Baylis’s wind-up radio was in the 1990s, before the internet. It is a cheap light that gets free power from gravity. It could stop people in Africa and India from using dangerous kerosene lamps.

The British designer Patrick Hunt first tried normal funding from banks or venture capital. But he could not get money because the process was slow and complicated, the idea was not proven, and people did not want to take the risk.

So he used crowdfunding on a US website called Indiegogo, which had just opened in the UK. In five days he reached his target and raised £36,200. By the end of 2012, after 40 days, he had raised £400,000.

The LED light uses a dynamo. The dynamo is turned by a 10kg bag of rocks that slowly drops down. The weight is tied to the light and is lifted to about 2m. As it slowly falls to the ground, it makes enough power for about 30 minutes of light.

Hunt is getting ready to make the product in China. He will test the market again by sending 1,000 lights to Africa before full mass production. He hopes mass production will be millions of units.

Hunt is part of a new group of entrepreneurs who use the fast-growing crowdfunding industry to get money. Another new site is InvestingZone, which matches wealthy people with start-up entrepreneurs.

Indiegogo does not offer shares. Instead, people can give different “perks” for different donation levels. People who donated to Hunt’s light helped poorer people, and they also got their own light.

Danae Ringelmann, a co-founder of Indiegogo, says the “gravity light” shows how crowdfunding can be fair and can test an idea. She says it is no longer only about getting access to bank decision makers or knowing people in venture capital. It is about proving the idea to customers and fans, getting them to support it, and paying for it. She also says even ideas that do not get funding are worth testing, because you learn faster and you do not waste time.

Ringelmann is based in the US. She first worked as a Wall Street analyst. In 2008, she quit and used her skills to help friends in the arts raise money. The site first focused on films and launched at the Sundance Film Festival that year.

Five years later, Indiegogo was raising about $2m each week for new businesses in start-up and growth stages. In December 2012, it started euro and sterling services to grow in Europe. It says Britain is its third biggest market. International activity has risen 41% since December.

There are many competitors, such as Kickstarter, Seedrs, and Funding Circle. But Ringelmann says Indiegogo is different because anyone can start a campaign, and no project is seen as too strange.

Indiegogo takes a 4% fee for successful campaigns. If a campaign does not reach its target, users can either refund all money to contributors for free, or keep the money raised and pay a 9% fee.

A British woman, Lauren Pears, raised £100,000 on the site to open a “cat café” in London. It is called Lady Dinah’s Cat Emporium. It has not opened yet, but it is described as a place where people can sit in a comfortable wingback chair, have hot tea, read a book, and be with a cat.

Ringelmann says some campaigns are rejected by venture capitalists because they do not know if the market wants the idea. She says an Indiegogo campaign can be enough proof that there is a market, even if the project is not fully launched. She says it lets entrepreneurs test the market, pricing, and features, find new revenue streams, and get important feedback.

Ringelmann says ideas are common, and success depends on how well you do the work. She says if you are afraid someone else will steal your idea and do it faster and better, then you are not the right person to do it. She says it is about having confidence to move fast and learn.

For Ringelmann, expanding in Europe and making a deal with a web transaction provider are part of a dream to make finance more open to everyone. The deal will allow payments through local card services like Maestro in the UK and Carte Bleue in France, as well as PayPal. More than 7,000 finance campaigns are live on the site.

Crowdfunding as an option instead of banks has grown, but it does not attract many big-money investors. These investors usually want a stake in a good business. This may start to change in the UK with the launch of InvestingZone.
