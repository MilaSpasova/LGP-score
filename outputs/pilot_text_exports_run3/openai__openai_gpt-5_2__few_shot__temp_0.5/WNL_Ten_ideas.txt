From glow-in-the-dark trees to underground bike sheds and solar-powered bins, there are some unusual ideas to make cities nicer to live in.

Living in a city has many good points, but it is getting harder to live in a sustainable way. Pollution, traffic, and the loss of green spaces are some of the problems people face every day.

Inventors are trying to help. Here are some of the wackier solutions for making cities more livable.

Pop-up parks
Some cities look like they are built more for cars than for people. A pop-up park is a simple idea. You take an empty car park, a little money, and one or two plants, and you make a small park for a short time. The PARK(ing) project started as an art experiment in San Francisco. It has since spread around the world. People have also created temporary urban farms and ecology demonstrations. The movement has a day of action every September.

Subterranean storage
Not all city people use cars. Bikes are becoming more popular. The question is where to keep a bike safe. A Tokyo-based engineering firm called Giken made a plan for an “eco-cycle, anti-seismic underground bicycle park.” The storage place is a cylinder that is seven metres wide. It goes deep underground and can hold 204 bikes. Owners can get their bike back by pressing a button. The automatic system brings the bike above ground in about 13 seconds.

Glow-in-the-dark trees
When many people think of glowing trees, they think of Christmas decorations. But Daan Roosegaarde has a different idea. This Dutch designer-artist has created a “bioluminescent” plant. The experimental technology mixes DNA from glowing marine bacteria with the chloroplast genome of a plant. This makes a glow like a jellyfish. Trials are happening to make an industrial-scale version of this biomimicry-inspired technology. Roosegaarde hopes it could one day replace normal street lighting.

Footfall harvesting
Every day, hundreds of commuters and shoppers in West Ham in east London walk over an elevated pedestrian walkway near the underground station. Few people notice that the floor feels a bit springy. Even fewer people connect the five-millimetre flex in the rubber surface with the streetlights above. The walkway has smart tiles. They capture the kinetic energy from people’s footsteps and turn it into electricity. Pavegen, the UK firm behind this idea, has installed a similar system at London’s Heathrow Airport and in other places around the world.

Supertrees
There are also man-made trees. Singapore’s Gardens by the Bay has a small group of them. These steel-framed “supertrees” can be up to 50 metres high. Flowers and ferns grow on them. Their metal tops help absorb and spread heat. They can collect rainwater too. They also help ventilate two “climate-controlled biomes” (large conservatories) below. Eleven of the 18 trees also have solar panels on their “branches.”
