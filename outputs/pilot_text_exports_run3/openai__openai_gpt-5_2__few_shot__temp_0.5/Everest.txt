Kenton Cool usually likes to talk. But now he can hardly speak. Climbing hard at high altitude has made his voice rough.

He is now in Kathmandu, the capital of Nepal. He flew down from Everest base camp that morning. He is thinking about three big climbs he finished over the last weekend.

Early on Saturday morning, he reached the top of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of the three main summits in the Everest “horseshoe”. The three peaks surround an icy valley called the Western Cwm.

Later that same day, he climbed to the summit of Everest. He arrived at the top in total darkness early on Sunday. Then he and his climbing partner went on to the summit of Lhotse on Monday morning. Lhotse was the third peak in this three-peaks challenge.

“It was a snatched opportunity,” he says. “For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. That does not remove the physical achievement of what I did. I have set the bar at a certain level. But whoever comes next will raise the bar more and do it without ropes or bottled oxygen.”

Sixty years after Everest was first climbed, many stories look back to Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. They describe an earlier time, before today’s commercialism and environmental damage. I ask Cool to look forward and imagine what top climbers might do 60 years from now.

“I hate to think,” he says. He talks about the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. Steck left the mountain in April after what Cool calls “an altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck planned to climb Everest’s west ridge, first climbed in 1963. Then he planned to go down to the South Col and immediately climb Lhotse by a new route, all without fixed ropes. “Ueli had been training like a machine,” Cool says. “He is a climber in a class all his own. He is technically brilliant, and his fitness was at an astronomic level. It would have been amazing to see what he could have done.”

Tourism is another question. One clue is a helicopter rescue by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro’s angry words helped start the confrontation at Camp 2. Moro flew back to Everest on Tuesday in a powerful helicopter. He rescued a sick climber at 7,800 metres.

It was the highest rescue ever done on Everest. It also shows the fast growth in helicopter flights in recent years. By 2073, the mountain might even have a helipad on the South Col. Tourists could be brought there while breathing bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing what is possible for rescuing climbers and the many more trekkers who go as far as base camp.

Mountain geographer and environmentalist Alton Byers says it is not clear if the Everest region can keep coping with fast-growing tourism. He says climate change and tourism together are creating new pressure on the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers in the Everest region are shrinking, and some are disappearing. This is already having a big effect. “Everywhere you go, people are talking about how there’s less water,” he says. “There’s less water for agriculture and less water for all the new lodges that are getting built.”

Byers says that in the Sherpa town of Namche Bazaar, workers are building a new five-mile pipeline. It will bring water for the growing tourist demand for showers and flush toilets. The local stream is polluted with human waste. It also does not give enough water for a town that, in high season, is extremely crowded. “Every village is digging a pit just beyond the houses for garbage. Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world,” he says.

Human waste at base camp is now handled well and taken away in plastic barrels. But Byers says the barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley. That pit could leak into the region’s waterways.

“These problems can be solved, but we need to get serious about it,” he says. “One climber can spend $85,000 climbing Everest. And that’s fine. But at some point we’re going to have to address these other priorities. For half a million dollars a year, you could solve most of them.”

Climate change is harder. Byers works with local conservation committees to find and plan for climate change effects. This often means finding new water sources or collecting rainwater. Fast-growing glacial lakes that could burst and flood the Sherpa homeland are a constant danger. “There’s going to come a time when people are going to have to get out of their way,” he says.

Changing weather is also affecting tourism. More cloud cover during normally clear times is closing Lukla Airport more often. Lukla Airport is the main entry point to the Everest region. A new road for 4x4s is being built to Lukla to keep tourists and their money coming. But Byers worries the road network in Nepal is being built cheaply. He says this can cause terrible soil erosion and landslides.

“Everest is the icon everyone knows,” he says. “It’s the canary in the coalmine that everyone understands. It’s the perfect laboratory for figuring out how to address some of these problems, like the impacts of climate change and tourism.”
