Anitta is a new star of Brazilian funk music. She became very popular by bringing music from the favelas into the mainstream. But now people are debating her skin colour.

Some anti-discrimination activists and writers say she had to look “less black” to sell music to a mostly white, middle-class market.

The debate started after people shared old and new photos of Anitta. In the older photo, from when she was not famous, she looks darker. In the newer photo, used for marketing after she became famous, she looks lighter. People argue about why this happened. Some mention skin-whitening products or cosmetic surgery, but others say it is more likely because of Photoshop. The photos made people talk again about whether you need to have lighter skin to succeed in Brazil.

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote that this shows hidden discrimination in media and marketing. She said many people say they are not racist and think Brazil is a multiracial democracy, but statistics show this is not true. She wrote that society often connects being white with being rich and successful, and being black with the opposite.

Anitta was born Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

She became very famous with an album and a hit song, “Show das Poderosas.” The song reached number one and got 52 million YouTube views.

Many people love her as a pop idol with catchy songs. The people who support her also present her as a bridge between the mostly black and mixed-race shanty towns on Rio’s hills and the richer and whiter areas below.

She made her music style softer. She reduced the suggestive dancing, gang references, and explicit lyrics from baile funk. Now some people ask if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract a richer audience. Arraes said this can pressure artists to change their hair or face, like straightening curly hair or getting a nose surgery, and it can hurt self-esteem.

Race and colour are sensitive issues in Brazil. Brazil was one of the last large countries to end slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. In Brazil, colour can matter more than race or ancestry, and there is a clear link between skin tone and inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Until 2011, black or mixed-race students also spent about two fewer years in school on average.

The government says the gap is getting smaller because of quotas for university places and other affirmative action. But big differences remain. Most business and government leaders are white, and many low-paid jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In rich areas like Ipanema and Gávea, it is more common to see black nannies pushing strollers with white children than the opposite.

Colour is also hard to define. In the 2010 census, people who called themselves white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million said black, two million said Asian, and 0.5% said indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology lecturer at the Federal University of Pernambuco, said Anitta won middle-class fans by taking a rebellious sound and making it calmer and easier for everyone to accept. He said this was not because of racial whitening. He said it was because Anitta changed the social space where she made her art, from the periphery to the centre.

Other people say the colour issue is too big. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who knew Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot, but not her colour. He said changes came from makeup, hairstylists, and clothing. He also said he does not think she was pressured, and that she always liked to sing, dance, and show her style.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador, said the problem is not the public but music producers and media leaders. He said the public showed they care about the music itself by choosing Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, as a recent winner of the talent show The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for comment. But in a recent interview, she said identity should be defined by the person. She said an “all-powerful” woman does not need to be beautiful. She said she wants her work to show that people can be who they want.
