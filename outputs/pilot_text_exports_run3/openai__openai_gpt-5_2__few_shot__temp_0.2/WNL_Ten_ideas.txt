From glow-in-the-dark trees to underground bike sheds and solar-powered bins, here are some unusual ideas to make cities more livable.

Living in a city has many good points, but it is becoming harder to live in a sustainable way. Pollution, traffic, and the loss of green spaces are daily problems for city people.

Here are ten of the stranger ideas to improve city life.

Pop-up parks
Some cities look like they are built more for cars than for people. A pop-up park is a simple idea. You take an empty car park, a little money, and one or two pot plants, and you make a small private park. The PARK(ing) project started as an art experiment in San Francisco and later spread around the world. People have also made temporary urban farms and ecology demonstrations. The movement has a day of action every September.

Subterranean storage
Not everyone in a city uses a car. Bikes are becoming more popular. But where can you keep a bike safe? A Tokyo engineering firm called Giken has an idea: an "eco-cycle, anti-seismic underground bicycle park". It is a cylinder that is only seven metres wide. It goes deep into the ground and can store 204 bikes. Owners can get their bike back by pressing a button. The automated system brings it above ground in about 13 seconds.

Glow-in-the-dark trees
Many people think of Christmas lights when they think of things that glow. But Daan Roosegaarde does not. This Dutch designer-artist created a "bioluminescent" plant. The technology combines DNA from glowing marine bacteria with the chloroplast genome of a plant, to make a glow like a jellyfish. Trials are happening to make an industrial-scale version. Roosegaarde hopes it could one day replace normal street lighting.

Footfall harvesting
In West Ham in east London, many commuters and shoppers cross an elevated walkway near the underground station every day. Most people do not notice that the ground feels a little springy. Even fewer people know that the five-millimetre flex in the rubber surface helps power the streetlights above. The floor has smart tiles that capture energy from footsteps and change it into electricity. Pavegen, the UK firm behind this idea, has also installed a similar system at Heathrow Airport in London and in other places around the world.

Supertrees
Man-made trees now exist. Singapore’s Gardens by the Bay has a small group of them. These steel-framed "supertrees" are up to 50 metres high. Flowers and ferns grow on them. Their metal tops also help absorb and spread out heat. They can collect rainwater too. They also provide air ventilation for two "climate-controlled biomes" below (large conservatories). Eleven of the 18 trees also have solar panels on their "branches".
