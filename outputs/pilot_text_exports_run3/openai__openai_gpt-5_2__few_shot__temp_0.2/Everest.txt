Kenton Cool usually likes to talk, but now he can hardly speak. Climbing hard at high altitude has made his voice rough. He is in Kathmandu, the capital of Nepal. He flew down from Everest base camp that morning.

Cool is thinking about three big climbs he finished over the last weekend. Early on Saturday, he reached the top of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of the three main summits in the Everest “horseshoe” around the icy valley called the Western Cwm.

That same day, he climbed to the top of Everest. He reached the summit in complete darkness early on Sunday. Then he and his climbing partner went on to the summit of Lhotse on Monday morning.

“It was a snatched opportunity,” he says. “For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. That does not remove the physical achievement. I have set the bar at a certain level. But the next person will move the bar higher and do it without ropes or bottled oxygen.”

Sixty years after Everest was first climbed, many stories look back to Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. They talk about a more innocent time, before today’s commercialism and environmental damage. I ask Cool to look forward and imagine what top climbers might do 60 years from now.

“I hate to think,” he says. He mentions the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. Steck left the mountain in April after what Cool calls “an altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck planned to climb Everest’s west ridge, first climbed in 1963. Then he would go down to the South Col and immediately climb Lhotse by a new route, all without fixed ropes. “Ueli had been training like a machine,” Cool says. “He is in a class of his own. He is technically brilliant, and his fitness was at an astronomic level. It would have been amazing to see what he could have done.”

Tourism is another question. One sign is a helicopter rescue by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro’s angry language helped start the confrontation at Camp 2. On Tuesday, Moro flew back to Everest in a powerful helicopter to rescue a sick or injured climber at 7,800 metres.

It was the highest rescue ever on Everest. It also shows how helicopter flights have increased very fast in recent years. By 2073, the mountain could even have a helipad on the South Col, bringing tourists who breathe bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing how people can rescue climbers and the many trekkers who go as far as base camp.

Mountain geographer and environmentalist Alton Byers says it is not clear if the Everest region can keep handling fast-growing tourism. He says climate change and tourism together are creating new pressure on the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers in the region are shrinking, and some are disappearing. This is already having a big effect. “Everywhere you go, people talk about less water,” he says. “There is less water for farming and less water for the new lodges being built.”

Byers says that in the Sherpa town of Namche Bazaar, workers are building a new five-mile pipeline. It will bring water for the growing tourist demand for showers and flush toilets. The local stream is polluted with human waste and does not give enough water. In high season, the town is very crowded. “Every village is digging a pit just beyond the houses for garbage. Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world,” he says. Human waste at base camp is now handled better and taken away in plastic barrels. But Byers says the barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley, and it could leak into the region’s waterways.

“These problems can be solved, but we need to take it seriously,” he says. “One climber can spend $85,000 to climb Everest, and that is fine. But we will have to deal with these other needs too. For $500,000 a year, you could solve most of them.”

Climate change is harder. Byers works with local conservation committees to find and plan for climate change effects. They often look for new water sources or set up rainwater harvesting. Glacial lakes are growing fast, and they could burst and flood the Sherpa homeland. “There will be a time when people will have to get out of their way,” he says.

Weather changes also affect tourism. More clouds during normally clear times are closing Lukla Airport more often. Lukla is the main entrance to the Everest region. A new road for 4x4s is being built to Lukla to make sure tourists and their money can still arrive. But Byers worries that road building in Nepal is being done cheaply. He says this can cause serious soil erosion and landslides.

“Everest is the icon everyone knows,” he says. “It is the canary in the coalmine that everyone understands. It is the perfect place to learn how to deal with problems like climate change and tourism.”
