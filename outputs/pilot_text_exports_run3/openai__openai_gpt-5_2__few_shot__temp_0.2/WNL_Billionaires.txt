A thick layer of bird droppings covers a gold-painted railing at one of Britain’s most expensive homes. Dead pigeons lie near broken mirrors, and water runs through broken parts of the ceiling. This place is called The Towers. It is a £30m house on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London. People have kept its serious damage secret until now.

The Towers is one of ten large houses on The Bishops Avenue. This road is in the centre of London’s rising property market. These houses have been almost empty since they were bought about 25 years ago. It is believed they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. The Greek-style columns are breaking. The swimming pools with mosaic tiles are full of broken stone. Nature has taken over so much that owls now live there.

This empty and ruined scene is common on the avenue. Lloyds Bank says The Bishops Avenue is the second most expensive street in Britain. At the same time, more people in London cannot afford a home because prices rise by 11.2% a year. On the most expensive part of the road, 16 mansions are empty. Many are behind locked gates. Their windows are covered with steel bars, the gardens are overgrown, and guard dogs patrol the grounds.

Across the street there is another empty house worth £18m, with broken windows and anti-climb paint on the walls. Another house, sold for £20m, has metal bars on the windows.

Even so, prices keep rising. Dryades, a mansion that was owned until recently by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007 and is believed to be worth about £30m today. Heath Lodge, where fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou was murdered in 1984 with a silver bullet, is worth £13m today. It was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

This is painful to see for people who struggle to pay for a home in one of the world’s most expensive cities. A security guard on the avenue said it was frustrating to watch so much living space—enough for dozens of people—falling apart.

Rich royals from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia were among the first to come to this curved road near Hampstead Heath. Iranians came here after the fall of the shah. Now, Chinese buyers are following Russians and Kazakhs, who have spent millions to get an address that estate agents say is as famous as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions have been for sale for £65m and £38m. They promised Italian marble, lifts with leather padding, and luxury panic rooms.

But on the empty properties, stone fountains are breaking and lawns have turned into muddy wet ground. Inside one house, water drips through a large crystal chandelier onto a thick carpet. Parts of the ceiling have fallen in, and the carpet is rotting. Moss grows through broken bricks, and pieces of mirror lie in a bathroom. The swimming pool has about a foot of dirty, salty water, and flowers grow through the tiles. Wooden slats push out from the sauna.

The worst ruin is The Towers. It is a large house surrounded by acres of hornbeam, oak, and lime trees. Pigeons fill its big halls with high ceilings. Water runs through three floors, and the walls have turned bright green with algae. The water drops into a huge, empty swimming pool in the basement. There are unopened wooden boxes marked “bullet-proof glass,” showing the old owners were worried about security.

Today, very few people live on The Bishops Avenue all the time. One guard said the owners of a mansion were not there. Another guard, outside Royal Mansion, would not say if anyone was home. A worker at another mansion only warned the Guardian about the guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare businessman with personal wealth of £45m, said he is one of the few people who live there permanently.

He said it is not a friendly place where neighbours talk over the fence. He said he has never seen what his neighbours look like. He said a Saudi princess next door spent £35m on a new house, and he has never seen her. He said about three houses are lived in all day and night. He said half the properties are used only three to six months a year, and the rest may not be used at all.

These broken, multi-million-pound houses show a property culture where the world’s richest people treat British homes as investments. One Hyde Park, a block of flats in Knightsbridge, is another example. More than half the flats there are registered with the council as empty or second homes.

Even so, people on the avenue talk about building £5m apartments instead of £50m mansions, to bring residents back.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, wants to rebuild one valuable site on the avenue. He plans 20 apartments with a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

He said that if you build a big house and try to sell it for £30m to £40m, it will not sell. He said local people will not buy, so you must find buyers from overseas.

But using these empty homes to help with the housing crisis still seems far away. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some of the ruined homes could become affordable housing. He said the land would cost too much.

He said very wealthy people own property there, and sometimes they live in it and sometimes they do not.
