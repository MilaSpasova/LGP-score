Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8th April, 2014, she talked about a special moment. She received news about a message in a bottle. Her grandfather had thrown the bottle into the Baltic Sea 101 years earlier. People think it is the world’s oldest message in a bottle. A museum in Germany gave it to Erdmann and is showing it there.

Erdmann, 62, said it was very surprising. A man came to her door and said he had mail from her grandfather. He said a message in a bottle had been found, and the name on the card was her grandfather’s. The visitor was a family-history researcher. He found her in Berlin after the letter was given to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg.

The bottle was a brown beer bottle. It had been in the water for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it in his catch in the Baltic Sea near Kiel. Holger von Neuhoff, a curator at the museum, said it was the oldest bottled message he had seen. He said the museum has older documents without bottles, but with the bottle and the document together, this was the oldest. He said it was in very good condition.

Researchers think Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913. He was hiking with a nature group. He was 20 years old then.

Much of the postcard could not be read. But the Berlin address on the front was clear. The card also asked the finder to send the note to his home address.

Erdmann said her grandfather also put two stamps in the bottle. This was so the finder would not have to pay. She said he did not think it would take 101 years.

Erdmann said the message made her feel very emotional. She did not know her grandfather because he died at age 54.

She said she learned he was a writer. She said he was open-minded and believed in freedom and in people respecting each other. She said he helped young people, and later travelled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful because she could see where her roots came from.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and travelling around the world. She said she is open-minded too. She said what her grandfather taught his two daughters, her mother taught her, and she then taught her sons.

Even though she was happy, she said she hopes people will not throw message bottles into the sea today. She said the sea is already full of bottles and rubbish.

The message and bottle will be on display at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts will try to read the rest of the text. It is not clear what will happen to the bottle later, but Erdmann hopes it will stay at the museum.

She said they want to show some photos with the bottle. This is so visitors can see the young man who threw the bottle into the water.
