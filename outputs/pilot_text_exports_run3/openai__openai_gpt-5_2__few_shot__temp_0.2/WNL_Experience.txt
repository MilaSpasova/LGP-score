It was a nice summer evening. I decided to swim near Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland. I moved there in 2012. In the water, there was a woman with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty often swims with a group of people, and she knows them.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Since then, hundreds of people have swum with her. Many people think she is completely tame. She was even in an Irish tourist ad. In the ad, a girl says she wants to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty on the belly. It looked friendly. About 20 tourists and local people were watching from the pier. After I got into the water, Dusty left the woman and suddenly went wild. Later I learned she is very territorial when she is with someone. Her tail was moving fast. At first I thought it was just a show, but then I realized she might be angry.

I tried to swim back to the pier to get out. But very quickly, Dusty hit me with her nose. It was very strong and painful. I was pushed forward.

People on the pier stared at me. Dusty stayed next to me, still hitting the water with her tail. That scared me even more. I thought, if she hits me with her tail, I could go under the water.

I reached the pier, but I could not get out because I was hurt. I felt great fear. I shouted for help. A man reached in and pulled me out onto the steps. Then another man came. He said he was an orthopaedic surgeon and he specialized in marine trauma. He was driving into Doolin and saw what was happening.

I was very cold and very worried. I did not know how bad my injuries were. My biggest fear was bleeding inside my body. He checked me and said he could not feel any sign of that, but he thought I probably had broken bones. Later I learned I had six spinal fractures, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for several weeks. I could not work for five months. I had trouble moving, and I had stiffness and pain. Then doctors said I had post-traumatic stress. The near-death experience made me anxious about everything. I started to overreact in ways I never did before. I felt that people were looking at me the wrong way. Loud noises became hard for me. I also had memory loss.

Three months before the accident, I opened a health-food shop. But I had to give it up because I could not work anymore. It was the hardest year of my life, but now it is behind me. I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building my own osteopathy practice. I understand patients better now because I was a patient.

I am thankful that I am healthy. I want to stop other people from getting hurt. People often have a sweet idea about dolphins and trust them. Who would think a dolphin could attack a person? If a dangerous animal comes at you with its teeth showing, it is scary. But dolphins have a wide smile, so they do not look dangerous.

I am not angry with Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her area, and she is a wild animal that can be unpredictable. People need to know this. Many people come to swim with her, and they do not understand how dangerous it can be. People said my injuries were one of several that summer.

After the man pulled me out, Dusty swam away. Then she came back. She was upright in the water near me and looking at me. We looked into each other’s eyes. I felt she was sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin, and the anger was gone. People on the pier were amazed. After that moment, I was not terrified anymore. I made peace with her.
