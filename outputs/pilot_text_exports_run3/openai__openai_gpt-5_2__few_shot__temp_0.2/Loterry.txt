The lottery balls fall down the chute and all six numbers match. Now it is time to buy an Audi, book a holiday in the US, and call an estate agent. An analysis says this is what many lottery millionaires do.

The UK National Lottery started in 1994. Since then, it has made 3,000 millionaires. Together they have won more than £8.5bn in total. On average, each won £2.8m. Oxford Economics says their money also helped create 3,780 more millionaires among their children, family, and friends.

Most winners (59%) stop working right away. But 19% keep doing their job, and 31% do unpaid voluntary work. The study says 98% of winners’ spending stayed in the UK. It estimates that each winner’s spending keeps six people in full-time jobs for one year.

Winners added almost £750m to GDP and created more than £500m in taxes for the Exchequer. Most money was spent on property. Winners spent £2.72bn on their main homes. They also spent £170m paying off debt and mortgages.

Keeping income was important, so winners spent £2.125bn on investments. They gave £1.17bn as gifts to family and friends. They spent £680m on cars and holidays.

Camelot, which runs the UK National Lottery, paid for the study when the number of winners reached 3,000. The study used research from 100 winners who won £1m or more. It found that the 3,000 winners bought 7,958 houses or flats in the UK, about 2.7 each, spending £3.3bn. Most winners (82%) changed their main home, spending an average of £900,000.

Many new homes had extra features. Almost a third (29%) wanted a hot tub. A walk-in wardrobe was important for 28%. Almost a quarter (24%) chose a home behind electric gates. Also, 22% had a games room, and 7% put in a snooker table.

Big homes need help. About 30% paid for a cleaner and 24% paid for a gardener. A small number (5%) paid for a beautician.

Audis were the top choice for 16% of winners. Range Rovers and BMWs were also popular (11% each). Mercedes were 10% and Land Rovers were 5%. Winners spent £463m on 17,190 cars. The average price of their favourite car was £46,116.

Holidays were also important. Most winners (68%) chose five-star hotels in other countries. The US was the top place (27%), followed by the Caribbean (9%). In the UK, caravan sales also grew. In the past 18 years, 10% of winners bought a caravan, creating sales of about £7.4m.

Some winners (15%) started their own businesses. Another 9% helped other people start businesses, and 6% invested in or bought other people’s businesses. The study says these businesses employ 3,195 people.

Andy Logan, who wrote the report, said the effect of a win is bigger than expected. It changes the lives of friends and family. Each win also affects the UK economy, because so much of the money is spent in the UK. He said each win creates a ripple effect for this generation and often the next.
