Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is theft. He says it is like putting your hand in someone’s pocket and taking their wallet. In an article for Index on Censorship, Pullman, who is president of the Society of Authors, strongly supports copyright laws. He criticizes internet users who think it is OK to download music or books without paying.

Pullman writes that new technology is so impressive that people do not see how wrong their actions are. He says it is shocking that someone can steal an artist’s work and not be punished. He repeats that it is theft, like stealing a wallet.

His article was published after music industry leaders met British Prime Minister David Cameron at Downing Street. They discussed web piracy.

Pullman, the writer of the His Dark Materials trilogy, says authors and musicians often work for years in poverty and without attention. They work until their art gives joy to audiences, but then they cannot make a living because people take it for free. He ends with a simple idea: if we want to enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

Pullman’s article is part of a dialogue with Cathy Casserly, the chief executive of Creative Commons. Creative Commons offers open content licences that let creators control copyright in a different way. Casserly says many things are wrong with copyright, which was made in an “analogue age”. She says copyright often stops people from sharing, building on, and remixing each other’s work.

Casserly says artists should think in new ways about how they share and make money from their work. She quotes science fiction writer Cory Doctorow: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees. The editor, Rachael Jolley, says current copyright laws do not work in the digital age and may turn consumers into criminals. She says new ways are needed so artists, writers, and musicians can earn a living.

The argument is strong, and illegal downloading is very common. Ofcom says that in the UK, between November 2012 and January 2013, 280 million music tracks were pirated. Also pirated were 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files.

Ofcom says 18% of internet users over age 12 say they recently pirated content, and 9% say they are afraid of getting caught. Pullman writes that it is still surprising how easily and quickly people can get music as MP3 downloads, even for people who have been building their iTunes list for a long time.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron chose Conservative MP Mike Weatherley to advise him on this issue. A BPI spokesman said Mike Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creative producers it protects. The spokesman said they hope Weatherley’s influence and the prime minister’s support will affect the work of the UK’s intellectual property office.
