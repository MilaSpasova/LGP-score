Brazil’s newest funk star, Anitta, has won millions of fans by bringing favela music into the mainstream. But she is now in the middle of a debate about skin colour.

Anti-discrimination groups and social writers say the music industry’s fastest-rising star had to give up part of her black identity to succeed in a mostly white, middle-class market.

The argument grew after “then-and-now” photos were published. They show Anitta’s skin looking much lighter after she signed a deal with Warner.

In the first photo, from when she was not well known, she looked darker. In the second photo, a marketing picture after she became famous, she looks paler. Some people asked if this happened because of skin-whitening products, cosmetic surgery, or, more likely, Photoshop. The big difference brought back the question of whether you need to be pale to get ahead in Brazil.

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote a post criticizing what she saw as hidden discrimination in media and marketing. She said many people do not accept that they are racist and think Brazil is a multiracial democracy, but statistics show this is not true. She said whitening shows a society that does not support diversity. She said white is often seen as rich, nice, successful, and good, and black is seen as the opposite.

Anitta was born Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

She has now become very famous with an album and a major hit single, Show das Poderosas. The song reached number one and got 52 million views on YouTube.

Many people love her as a pop idol with a strong message and catchy songs. The people supporting her also present her as a cultural bridge between the mostly black and mixed-race shanty towns on Rio’s hillsides and the wealthier and whiter areas below.

She has made baile funk less extreme for a wider audience. She reduced the suggestive dancing, gangsta references, and explicit lyrics. Now some people ask if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract a richer audience. Arraes said that if pop stars have curly hair, they may feel pushed to straighten it, and if they have a big nose, they may feel pushed to get rhinoplasty. She said this can create a harmful cycle for self-esteem.

This topic is sensitive in Brazil, a country with many mixed-race people. Brazil was one of the last large countries to ban slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. But in Brazil, race and ancestry matter less than skin colour. And even though the country aims to be a multiracial democracy, there is a clear link between skin tone and inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Until 2011, black or mixed-race students also spent two fewer years in school on average.

The government says this gap is getting smaller because of university quotas and other affirmative action policies. But the difference is still easy to see. Most business and government leaders are white, while most low-paid jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In Ipanema, Gávea, and other rich areas, you are much more likely to see black nannies pushing strollers with white toddlers than the other way around.

How people define colour in Brazil is complicated. In the 2010 census, people who called themselves white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million said black, two million said Asian, and 0.5% said indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology lecturer at the Federal University of Pernambuco, said Anitta won middle-class fans by taking a rebellious sound and making it calmer and easier for everyone to accept. He said this was not done through racial whitening. He said it was a change in where she made her art: from the periphery to the centre.

Others say the colour issue is being made too big. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who worked with Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot, but not her colour. He said it was more about makeup, hairstylists, and clothes. He said he did not think it was because of pressure, and that she always liked to show off, sing, and dance. He said this natural style is why she is successful.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador in north-eastern Brazil, said the prejudice was not mainly in the public, but in music producers and media leaders. He said the public showed they care about music itself by choosing Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, as the recent winner of the talent show The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for comment. But in a recent interview, she said identity should be defined by the person. She said an “all-powerful” woman does not need to be beautiful, because her attitude makes her marvellous and powerful. She said her work tries to show that we can be who we want.
