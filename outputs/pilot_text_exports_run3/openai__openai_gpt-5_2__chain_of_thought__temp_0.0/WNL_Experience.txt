It was a beautiful summer evening, and I decided to go for a swim near Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland. I moved there in 2012. In the water, I saw a woman with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty often swims with a group of people, and she seems to know them.

Dusty arrived in Doolin around 2008. Since then, hundreds of people have swum with her, so she can seem very tame. She was even in an Irish tourist-board advertisement where a girl says she would like to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty’s tummy, and it looked safe and fun. About 20 tourists and local people were on the pier watching. Soon after I got into the water, Dusty left the woman and suddenly became very wild. I later learned she is very protective of her space when she is with someone. Her tail was flapping hard. At first I thought she was just showing off, but then I realized she might be angry.

I tried to swim to the pier to get out. But almost right away, Dusty hit me with her nose. It was very strong and very painful. The speed shocked me, and I was thrown forward.

People on the pier stared down at me with their mouths open. Dusty stayed close, still flapping her tail wildly. That scared me most. I thought if she hit me with her tail, I could sink.

I reached the pier, but I could not climb out because I was hurt. I felt terrified. I shouted for help. A man reached in and pulled me onto the steps. Then another man came and said he was an orthopaedic surgeon who specialized in marine trauma. He had been driving into Doolin and saw what was happening.

I was very cold and very worried. I did not know how badly I was injured, and I feared internal bleeding. The surgeon checked me and told me he could not feel signs of internal bleeding, but he thought I probably had broken bones. Later I learned I had six spinal fractures, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for several weeks. I could not work for five months, and I had limited movement, stiffness, and pain. Then doctors said I had post-traumatic stress. After my near-death experience, I felt anxious all the time and reacted in ways I never had before. I felt like people were looking at me the wrong way. Loud noises became hard for me. I also had memory loss.

Three months before the accident, I had opened a health-food shop. But I had to give it up because I could not work anymore. That year was the hardest, but now it is in the past. I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building up my own osteopathy practice. I understand patients better because I have been one.

I am thankful that I am healthy, and I want to stop other people from getting hurt. People often think dolphins are always friendly. Who would expect a dolphin to attack a person? If you see a dangerous animal with its teeth showing, you feel afraid. But dolphins have a wide smile, so they do not look scary.

I am not angry with Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her territory, and she is a wild animal that can act in unexpected ways. People need to know this. Many people come to swim with her and do not understand how dangerous it can be. Reports said my injuries were among several injuries that summer.

After the man pulled me out, Dusty swam away. Then she came back and floated upright next to me, looking at me. We looked into each other’s eyes, and I felt she was sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin, and the anger was gone. People on the pier were amazed. After that moment, my terror ended. I made peace with her.
