From glow-in-the-dark trees to underground bike sheds and solar-powered bins, people are trying unusual ideas to make cities nicer places to live. City life has many good points, but living in a sustainable way is getting harder. Pollution, traffic, and the loss of green spaces are everyday problems for people in cities.

Now inventors are stepping in. Here are some of the stranger ideas for making cities more livable.

Pop-up parks
Many cities look like they are built more for cars than for people. A pop-up park is a simple idea: use an empty car park, a little money, and one or two plants to make a small park for a short time. The PARK(ing) project started as an art experiment in San Francisco and has spread around the world. People have also tried temporary urban farms and ecology demonstrations. The movement has a day of action every September.

Subterranean storage
Not everyone in cities uses a car. Bicycles are becoming more popular. But where can you store a bike safely? A Tokyo engineering company called Giken designed an “eco-cycle, anti-seismic underground bicycle park.” It is a round storage system that is only seven metres wide and goes deep underground. It can hold 204 bikes. Owners can get their bike back by pressing a button. The machine brings the bike up to ground level in about 13 seconds.

Glow-in-the-dark trees
When many people think of glowing trees, they think of Christmas decorations. Dutch designer and artist Daan Roosegaarde thinks differently. He has created an experimental “bioluminescent” plant. The technology mixes DNA from glowing marine bacteria with part of a plant’s genetic material to make a glow like a jellyfish. Tests are happening to make an industrial-scale version. Roosegaarde hopes it could one day replace normal street lighting.

Footfall harvesting
Every day, many commuters and shoppers in West Ham in east London walk across a raised footbridge near the underground station. Few people notice the slight bounce under their feet. The rubber surface flexes by about five millimetres. The floor has smart tiles that capture energy from footsteps and turn it into electricity. This electricity powers the streetlights above. The UK company Pavegen made this system. It has also installed a similar system at London’s Heathrow Airport and in other places around the world.

Supertrees
Singapore’s Gardens by the Bay has man-made trees called “supertrees.” They are steel-framed and can be up to 50 metres tall. Flowers and ferns grow on them. Their metal tops help absorb and spread out heat. They can collect rainwater and help move air for two “climate-controlled biomes” (large conservatories) below. Eleven of the 18 trees also have solar panels on their “branches.”
