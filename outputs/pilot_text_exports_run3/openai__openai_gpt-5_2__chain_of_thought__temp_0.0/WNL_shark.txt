Opposition to Western Australia’s shark cull has grown. Thousands of people went to beaches across Australia to ask the state’s premier to end the policy. RSPCA Australia and Virgin Atlantic owner Richard Branson also spoke against it.

The policy catches and kills sharks longer than three metres. It began after what the state government called an “unprecedented” number of shark attacks on Western Australia’s coast. A 35-year-old surfer was killed in November 2013. He was the sixth person to die from a shark attack in two years.

However, the Shark Attack File says Australia has averaged one shark-related death a year for the last 50 years.

Kate Faehrmann, a board member at the Sea Shepherd Conservation Society, spoke at a protest in Perth. She said the policy will not work. She said drumlines, used to catch sharks, kill many animals and are not choosy. She said they can kill sharks of different sizes, and also dolphins, turtles, and other animals. She said that is why the community does not want the policy.

Thousands of people protested at Perth’s Cottesloe Beach and Sydney’s Manly Beach. Hundreds protested at Glenelg in south-west Adelaide, and at beaches in Victoria and Queensland. Faehrmann said the protests showed Australians want sharks protected. She said many people in Australia love sharks, and people are coming out across the country to say they respect sharks and do not want them killed.

Anthony Joyce, a surfer who once had his foot caught in a shark’s mouth, said killing sharks will make no real difference.

The state government has refused to give an updated count of how many sharks have been killed. There have been reports of sharks smaller than three metres being released after getting caught on drumlines. Drumlines are floating drums anchored to the sea bed, with bait hanging on hooks under them.

Conservationists say there is no evidence the cull will reduce shark attacks on humans. They also say no previous cull has used only drumlines. Researchers at the University of Western Australia say the recent increase in shark attacks may be more connected to the state having the fastest-growing population in Australia, not to more sharks.

Richard Peirce, chairman of the UK-based conservation charity the Shark Trust, said the cull would not work and could even bring more predators closer to the coast. He said it is sad the government did not try other choices instead of killing animals in a way that is not choosy. He also said that even if drumlines are checked during the day, leaving them overnight could attract other predators to the area, because sharks and other animals may be hooked and hurt.

Around the world in 2012, there were 80 unprovoked shark attacks, and seven were fatal. This was compared to almost 100m sharks killed by humans each year.

RSPCA Australia said it believes the cull is not justified. It said there is no evidence that more attacks are happening because there are more sharks. Instead, it said the increase fits with changes in population and human behaviour, meaning more people are in the water.

Richard Branson told Fairfax Radio the policy was backfiring. He said Western Australia Premier Colin Barnett may have thought it would encourage tourism, but Branson said it would do the opposite. He said it makes the problem look bigger than it is, may stop people from coming to Perth and nearby areas, and may worry people for no good reason.
