Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is theft. He says it is as wrong as putting your hand in someone’s pocket and taking their wallet. In an article for Index on Censorship, Pullman, who is president of the Society of Authors, strongly supports copyright laws. He criticizes internet users who think it is OK to download music or books without paying.

Pullman writes that the technology is so impressive that some people do not see how wrong their actions are. He says it is outrageous that someone can steal an artist’s work and not be punished. He repeats that it is theft, like stealing a wallet.

His article came after music industry leaders met British Prime Minister David Cameron in Downing Street to discuss web piracy.

Pullman, the writer of the His Dark Materials trilogy, says authors and musicians often work for years in poverty and without attention to make work that audiences enjoy. He says that when they finally succeed, their chance to make a living can be taken away by illegal downloading. He ends with a simple idea: if we want to enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

Pullman’s writing appears in the next issue of the group’s magazine as a dialogue with Cathy Casserly. She is the chief executive of Creative Commons, which offers open content licences that let creators control copyright in a different way. Casserly says many things are wrong with copyright, which was created in an “analogue age”. She says copyright often blocks many ways people could share, build on, and remix each other’s work—ways that were hard to imagine when the laws were made.

Casserly says artists need to think of new ways to share and make money from their work. She quotes science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees. The editor, Rachael Jolley, says current copyright laws do not work well in the digital age and could turn consumers into criminals. She says new models are needed so artists, writers, and musicians can earn a living from their work.

The debate is active, and illegal downloading is very large. Data collected by Ofcom (the independent regulator and competition authority for the UK communications industries) suggests that in the UK, between November 2012 and January 2013, 280 million music tracks were pirated. It also says 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files were pirated.

Ofcom has said 18% of internet users aged over 12 admit they recently pirated content, and 9% say they fear getting caught. Pullman also writes that it is still amazing how easily and quickly music can be obtained as MP3 downloads, especially for people who have been building their iTunes list for some time.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron appointed Conservative MP Mike Weatherley as his adviser on the issue. A spokesman for the BPI, the record industry trade body, said Mike Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creative producers it protects. The spokesman said they hope Weatherley’s influence, and the prime minister’s support for copyright, will affect the approach of the UK’s intellectual property office.
