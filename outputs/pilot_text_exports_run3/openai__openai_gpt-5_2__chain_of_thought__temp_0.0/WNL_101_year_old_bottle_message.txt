Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8th April, 2014, she talked about a special moment. She received a message in a bottle. Her grandfather had thrown it into the Baltic Sea 101 years earlier. People think it is the world’s oldest message in a bottle. A museum in Germany gave it to her and is showing it there.

Erdmann, 62, said it was very surprising. She said a man came to her door and said he had mail from her grandfather. He said a message in a bottle had been found, and the name on the card was her grandfather’s name. The visitor was a family-history researcher. He found her in Berlin after the letter was given to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg.

The brown beer bottle had been in the water for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it in his catch while fishing in the Baltic Sea near the northern city of Kiel. Holger von Neuhoff, a museum worker for ocean and science, said it was the oldest bottled message he had seen. He said the museum has older papers that were found without a bottle. But with both the bottle and the paper together, this one is the oldest right now. He said it is in very good condition.

Researchers believe Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913. He did this during a hike with a nature group. He was 20 years old then.

Much of the postcard could not be read. But the Berlin address on the front could be read. The card also politely asked the finder to send the note to his home address.

Erdmann said her grandfather also put two stamps from that time in the bottle. This was so the finder would not have to pay. She said he did not think it would take 101 years.

She said the message made her feel very touched. She did not know her grandfather because he died at age 54, six years before she was born.

She said she knew very little about him. But she learned he was a writer who was open-minded. She said he believed in freedom and that everyone should respect each other. She said he did a lot for young people. Later he travelled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful because she could see where her family roots came from.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and travelling around the world. She said she is open-minded too. She said what her grandfather taught his two daughters, her mother taught her, and she then taught it to her sons.

Even though she was happy to get the message, she said she hopes people will not throw more message bottles into the sea today. She said the sea is already full of many bottles and rubbish.

The message and the bottle will be on show at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts will try to read the rest of the text. It is not clear what will happen to the bottle later, but Erdmann hopes it will stay at the museum.

She said they want to share some photos to put with the bottle. This way visitors can see the young man who threw the bottle into the water.
