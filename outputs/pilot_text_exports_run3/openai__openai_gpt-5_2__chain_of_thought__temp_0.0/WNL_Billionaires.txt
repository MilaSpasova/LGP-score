A thick layer of bird droppings covers a gold-colored railing at one of Britain’s most expensive homes. Pigeon bones are on the floor. Mirrors are broken, and water runs through damaged parts of the building. This home is called The Towers. It is a £30m palace on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London. Its terrible condition has been kept secret until now.

The Towers is one of ten large houses in the middle of The Bishops Avenue. This road is at the center of London’s fast-rising property market. These houses have been almost empty since they were bought about 25 years ago. It is believed they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. The houses have Greek-style columns that are breaking. Swimming pools with mosaic tiles are full of broken pieces of building. Nature has taken over so much that owls live there.

This empty and broken-down scene appears along the avenue, even though Lloyds Bank says it is the second most expensive street in Britain. At the same time, more and more people in London struggle to buy a home because house prices rise by 11.2% a year. On the most expensive stretch of The Bishops Avenue, 16 mansions sit empty. Many are behind locked gates. Their windows are covered with steel bars, the gardens are overgrown, and guard dogs patrol the land.

Across the street is another empty mansion worth £18m, with broken windows and walls covered with anti-climb paint. Another house has metal bars on its windows and has been sold for £20m.

Even so, prices keep rising. Dryades, a mansion that was until recently owned by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007 and is believed to be worth about £30m today. Heath Lodge, where fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou was murdered in 1984 with a silver bullet, is worth £13m today. It was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

For people who struggle to pay for housing in one of the world’s most expensive cities, seeing these empty homes can be painful. A security guard on the avenue said it was frustrating to watch so many tens of thousands of square feet of space—enough to house dozens of people—fall apart.

Wealthy royals from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia were among the first to come to this curved road near Hampstead Heath. Iranians came here after the fall of the shah. Now, Chinese buyers are following Russians and Kazakhs who have spent millions to get an address that estate agents say is as famous as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions were for sale for £65m and £38m. They promised Italian marble, lifts with leather padding, and luxury panic rooms.

But on the land around these empty mansions, stone fountains are falling apart and lawns have turned into muddy bogs. Inside one house, water drips through a huge crystal chandelier onto a thick carpet that is rotting under parts of a collapsed ceiling. Moss grows through broken bricks, and pieces of mirror tile lie across a bathroom. The swimming pool has about a foot of dirty salty water, and flowers grow through its tiles. Wooden boards are bending away from the sauna.

The most dramatic wreck is The Towers. It is a grand mansion among acres of hornbeam, oak, and lime trees. Pigeons fill its large high-ceiling halls. Water pours through three floors, and algae turns the walls bright green. The water drips into a huge, empty basement swimming pool. Wooden crates marked “bullet-proof glass” were never opened, showing the previous owners worried about security.

Today, very few people live on The Bishops Avenue all the time. A security guard outside one mansion said the owners were not there. Another guard, outside Royal Mansion, would not say if anyone was home. A staff member at another mansion warned the Guardian about the guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare mogul with personal wealth of £45m, said he is one of the few people who lives there permanently.

He said, “It’s not a neighbourly place, where you can chat over the fence. To be honest, I have never seen what my neighbours look like. Next door, a Saudi princess spent £35m on a new house and I’ve never seen her. There are about three houses that are lived in 24/7 and half of the properties are occupied three to six months a year. The other half, who knows if they come or not?”

These broken, multi-million-pound homes show a property culture where the world’s richest people treat British property as investments. One Hyde Park, a block of apartments in Knightsbridge, is another example. More than half the flats there are registered with the council as empty or second homes.

Still, people on the avenue talk about building £5m apartments instead of £50m mansions, to try to get people to live there again.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, plans to rebuild one of the most valuable sites on the avenue as 20 apartments. They would have a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

He said, “If you build a big house and try and sell for £30m to £40m, it won’t sell. Locals won’t buy and so you have to bring in overseas buyers.”

But using these empty homes to help solve the housing crisis still seems far away. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some of the empty houses could become affordable homes. He said the land price would be too high.

He said, “Very wealthy people own property there. Sometimes they live in them and sometimes they don’t.”
