This new invention could be as useful as Trevor Baylis’s wind-up radio was in the 1990s before the internet. It is a cheap light that makes free power from gravity. It could help stop the use of dangerous kerosene lamps in Africa and India.

A British designer named Patrick Hunt tried the usual way to get money for his invention, like banks or venture capital. But he had trouble. He said getting funding was slow and complicated, the idea was not yet proven, and no one wanted to take the risk.

So he tried crowdfunding on a US website called Indiegogo, which had recently started working in the UK. In five days, he reached his goal and raised £36,200. By the end of 2012, his campaign was so popular that in 40 days he raised £400,000.

The LED light uses a dynamo that is turned by a 10kg bag of rocks moving down. The weight is attached to the light and lifted to about 2m high. When it slowly falls to the ground, it makes enough power for about 30 minutes of light.

Hunt is getting ready to make the lights in China. He will test the market again by sending 1,000 lights to Africa before full mass production, which he hopes will be millions of units.

Hunt is part of a new group of entrepreneurs using the fast-growing crowdfunding industry to raise money. Another new site is InvestingZone, which connects wealthy people with start-up entrepreneurs.

Indiegogo does not give shares. Instead, it lets users offer “perks” for different donation amounts. People who helped fund Hunt’s light could feel good about helping poorer people, and they also got their own light.

Danae Ringelmann, a co-founder of Indiegogo, says the “gravity light” is a great example of how crowdfunding can work and how it can test an idea. She says it can be fast and efficient because it is not about knowing decision makers at a bank or knowing people in a venture capital firm. She says it is about proving your value to customers and fans, getting them to support your idea, and funding it.

She also says even ideas that do not get funding are worth testing, because you can save time by learning early that it was not a good idea.

Ringelmann lives in the US. She started her career as a Wall Street analyst. In 2008, she quit and used her skills to help friends in the arts raise money.

At first, the site focused on films. It launched at the Sundance Film Festival that year.

Five years later, it was raising about $2m a week for new businesses in start-up and growth stages. In December 2012, it started euro and sterling services to grow on this side of the Atlantic. It says Britain is its third biggest market. International activity has risen 41% since December.

There are many competitors, like Kickstarter, Seedrs, and Funding Circle. But Ringelmann says Indiegogo is different because anyone can start a campaign, and no project is too wacky.

Indiegogo charges a 4% fee for successful campaigns. If a campaign does not reach its target, users can choose to refund all the money to donors for free, or keep all the money raised but pay a 9% fee.

A British woman named Lauren Pears raised £100,000 on the site to open a “cat café” in London. It is called Lady Dinah’s Cat Emporium. It has not opened yet, but it is described as a place where people can “come in from the cold to a comfortable wingback chair, a hot cup of tea, a book and a cat”.

Ringelmann says she has seen campaigns rejected by venture capitalists who say, “Great idea but no idea if the market actually wants it; it could be a gadget that no one cares about.”

She says entrepreneurs can run an Indiegogo campaign even before they launch the project. The campaign itself can show proof of market interest, so venture capitalists can see there is a market.

She says it helps you test your market, pricing, and features, find new ways to earn money, and get important feedback.

With her Wall Street experience and her work helping 100,000 businesses and services raise money, Ringelmann gives advice to new entrepreneurs.

She says, “Ideas are a dime a dozen.” She says success depends on doing the work well. She also says if you are afraid someone will steal your idea and do it better and faster, then you are not the right person to carry it out. She says it is about having confidence to move fast and learn.

Ringelmann says expanding into Europe and making a deal with a web transaction provider are part of a dream to make finance more open to everyone. The deal will let people pay using local card services like Maestro in the UK and Carte Bleue in France, as well as PayPal. More than 7,000 finance campaigns are live on the site.

Crowdfunding has grown as another choice instead of banks, but it does not attract many big investors. These investors usually want a stake in a promising business. That may start to change in the UK with the launch of InvestingZone.
