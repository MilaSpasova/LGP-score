The lottery balls have fallen down the chute and all six numbers match. Now it may be time to buy an Audi, book a holiday in the US, and call an estate agent. An analysis says this is what many lottery millionaires do.

Since it started in 1994, the lottery has made 3,000 millionaires. Together they have won more than £8.5bn, which is an average of £2.8m each. The study says this money has also helped create 3,780 more millionaires among their children, family, and friends. The study was written by Oxford Economics.

Most winners (59%) stop working right away. But 19% keep doing their job, and 31% do unpaid voluntary work. For the economy, 98% of winners’ spending stayed in the UK. The study estimates that each winner’s spending on property, vehicles, and holidays supports six full-time jobs for one year.

Winners have added almost £750m to gross domestic product (GDP) and created more than £500m in tax for the Exchequer. Most money went on property. Winners spent £2.72bn on their main homes, and £170m paying off debt and mortgages.

Keeping an income was important, so £2.125bn was spent on investments. Gifts to family and friends were £1.17bn. Cars and holidays cost £680m.

Camelot, which runs the UK National Lottery, paid for the study to mark 3,000 winners. The study used research from 100 winners who won £1m or more. It found that the 3,000 winners bought 7,958 houses or flats in the UK, which is 2.7 each on average, spending £3.3bn. Most winners (82%) moved to a new main home and spent an average of £900,000.

Many new homes had extra features. Almost a third (29%) bought a hot tub. A walk-in wardrobe was wanted by 28%. Almost a quarter (24%) chose a home behind electric gates. Also, 22% had a games room, and 7% put in a snooker table.

Bigger homes need help to look after them. Some winners hired staff: 30% employed a cleaner and 24% a gardener. A small group (5%) employed a beautician.

Audi was the favourite car for 16% of winners. Range Rovers and BMWs were also popular (11% each), as well as Mercedes (10%) and Land Rovers (5%). Winners spent £463m on 17,190 cars. The average price of their favourite car was £46,116.

Holidays were important too. Most winners (68%) chose five-star hotels overseas. The US was the top destination for 27%, followed by the Caribbean (9%). In the UK, caravan sales also helped. Over the past 18 years, 10% of millionaires bought a caravan, creating sales worth about £7.4m.

Some winners (15%) started their own businesses. Another 9% helped other people start businesses, and 6% invested in or bought other people’s businesses. Businesses started or supported by lottery winners employ 3,195 people, the study says.

Andy Logan, who wrote the report, said the effect of winning spreads further than expected. He said it changes the lives of friends and family, and each win also affects the UK economy, especially because so much money is spent in the UK. He said each win creates a ripple effect for this generation and often the next.
