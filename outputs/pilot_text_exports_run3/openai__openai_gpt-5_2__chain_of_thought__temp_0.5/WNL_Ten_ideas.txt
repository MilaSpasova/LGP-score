From glow-in-the-dark trees to underground bike storage and solar-powered bins, we look at some unusual ideas to make cities nicer to live in.

City life has many good points, but living in a way that is good for the planet is getting harder. Pollution, traffic, and fewer green spaces are some of the problems people in cities face every day.

Inventors are trying to help. Here are some of the wackier ideas for making cities more livable.

Pop-up parks
Some cities look like they are built more for cars than for people. A pop-up park is a simple idea. You take an empty parking space, a little money, and one or two potted plants, and you make a small park. The PARK(ing) project started as an art experiment in San Francisco and later spread around the world. People have also tried ideas like temporary city farms and ecology demonstrations. The movement has a day of action every September.

Subterranean storage
Not everyone in a city uses a car. Bikes are becoming more popular. But where can you keep a bike safe? An engineering company in Tokyo called Giken designed an “eco-cycle, anti-seismic underground bicycle park.” It is a cylinder that is only seven metres wide, but it goes deep into the ground and can hold 204 bikes. Owners can get their bike back by pressing a button. The automatic system brings the bike above ground in about 13 seconds.

Glow-in-the-dark trees
When people think of trees that glow, they often think of Christmas decorations. Dutch designer and artist Daan Roosegaarde thought of something else. He created an experimental “bioluminescent” plant. The technology mixes DNA from glowing marine bacteria with part of a plant to make it glow like a jellyfish. Tests are happening to make a large, industrial version. Roosegaarde hopes this could one day replace normal street lights.

Footfall harvesting
Every day, hundreds of commuters and shoppers in West Ham, east London, cross an elevated walkway near the underground station. Many may not notice the slight bounce under their feet. The rubber surface bends about five millimetres, and this helps power the streetlights above. The walkway has smart tiles that capture energy from people’s footsteps and turn it into electricity. A UK company called Pavegen made this system. It has also installed a similar system at London’s Heathrow Airport and in other places around the world.

Supertrees
Man-made trees now exist. Singapore’s Gardens by the Bay has a small group of them. These steel-framed “supertrees” can be up to 50 metres tall. Flowers and ferns grow on them, and their metal tops help take in and spread out heat. They can collect rainwater and help provide air ventilation for two “climate-controlled biomes” (large conservatories) below. Eleven of the 18 trees also have solar panels on their “branches.”
