Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8 April 2014, she told people about a special moment. She got a message in a bottle. Her grandfather had thrown it into the Baltic Sea 101 years earlier. People think it may be the oldest message in a bottle in the world. A museum in Germany gave it to Erdmann. The museum is now showing it.

Erdmann, 62, said it was a big surprise. She said a man came to her door and told her he had mail from her grandfather. He said a bottle had been found, and the name on the card was her grandfather’s name. The man was a family-history researcher. He found her in Berlin after the letter was given to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg, a port city in northern Germany.

The bottle was a brown beer bottle. It had been in the water for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it in his catch. He was fishing in the Baltic Sea near the northern city of Kiel. Holger von Neuhoff, a museum worker for ocean and science, said this bottle message was the oldest he had seen. He said the museum has older documents, but those were found without a bottle. He said this one, with both the bottle and the document, is the oldest right now. He also said it is in very good condition.

Researchers believe Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913. They think he did it while hiking with a nature group. He was 20 years old then.

Most of the postcard cannot be read. But the Berlin address on the front can be read. The card also asks the person who finds it to send it to his home address.

Erdmann said her grandfather put two stamps in the bottle too, so the finder would not have to pay. She said he did not think it would take 101 years.

Erdmann said the message made her feel emotional. She did not know her grandfather because he died at age 54, six years before she was born.

She said she knew very little about him. But she learned he was a writer. She said he was open-minded, believed in freedom, and thought people should respect each other. She said he did a lot for young people, and later traveled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful because she could see where her family came from.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and traveling around the world. She said she is open-minded too. She said what her grandfather taught his two daughters was taught to her by her mother, and she then taught it to her sons.

Even though she was happy to get the message, she said she hopes others do not throw message bottles into the sea today. She said the sea already has too many bottles and too much rubbish.

The message and bottle will be shown at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts will try to read the rest of the text. It is not clear what will happen to the bottle later, but Erdmann hopes it will stay at the museum.

She said they want to add some photos with the bottle. Then visitors can see the young man who threw the bottle into the water.
