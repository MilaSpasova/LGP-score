This new light could be as important as Trevor Baylis’s wind-up radio was in the 1990s before the internet. It is a cheap light that gets free power from gravity. It could help stop the use of dangerous kerosene lamps in Africa and India.

The British designer Patrick Hunt tried to get money in the usual way, from banks or venture capital firms, to start selling his invention. But it was hard. He said they tried to get funding, but it was slow and complicated. The idea was unproven, and nobody wanted to take the risk.

So he tried crowdfunding on a US website called Indiegogo, which had just opened in the UK. In five days, he reached his goal and raised £36,200. His campaign was very popular at the end of 2012. In 40 days, he had raised £400,000.

The LED light is powered by a dynamo. The dynamo is turned by a 10kg bag of rocks going down. The weight is attached to the light. You lift it to about 2m high. As it slowly falls to the ground, it makes enough power for 30 minutes of light.

Hunt is getting ready to make the light in China. He will test the market again by sending 1,000 lights to Africa before full mass production. He hopes to make millions of units.

Hunt is part of a new group of business creators using the fast-growing crowdfunding industry to get money. Another new site is InvestingZone. It connects wealthy people with start-up entrepreneurs.

Indiegogo does not offer shares. Instead, people can offer “perks” for different sizes of donations. People who helped fund Hunt’s light not only felt good about helping poorer people, but they also got their own light.

Danae Ringelmann, a co-founder of Indiegogo, said the “gravity light” shows how crowdfunding can work in a fair way and test an idea. She said finance can be fast and efficient because it is not about knowing people at a bank or a venture capital firm. It is about showing value to customers and fans, getting them to support the idea, and funding it.

Ringelmann also said it is worth testing even ideas that do not get funding. Then you learn sooner that it was not a good idea, and you can learn faster.

Ringelmann is based in the US. She began her career as a Wall Street analyst. In 2008, she quit and decided to use her skills to help friends in the arts raise money.

At first, the site focused on films. It launched at the Sundance Film Festival that year.

Five years later, it was raising about $2m a week for new businesses that were starting or growing. In December 2012, it added euro and sterling services to grow on this side of the Atlantic. It says Britain is its third biggest market. International activity has risen 41% since December.

There are many competitors, such as Kickstarter, Seedrs, and Funding Circle. But Ringelmann says Indiegogo is different because anyone can start a campaign. No project is seen as too strange.

Indiegogo charges a 4% fee for successful campaigns. If a campaign does not reach its goal, the user can choose to refund all the money to supporters for free, or keep all the money raised but pay a 9% fee.

A British woman, Lauren Pears, raised £100,000 on the site to open a “cat café” in London. It is called Lady Dinah’s Cat Emporium. It has not opened yet, but it is described as a place where people can “come in from the cold” to sit in a comfortable wingback chair with a hot cup of tea, a book, and a cat.

Ringelmann said some campaigns are rejected by venture capitalists. They may say it is a great idea, but they do not know if people really want it.

She said entrepreneurs can run an Indiegogo campaign, even without launching the project. The campaign itself can be proof that there is a market.

She said it lets you test your market, your prices, and your features. It can also help you find new ways to earn money and get important feedback.

With her Wall Street experience, and after helping 100,000 businesses and services raise money, Ringelmann has shaped her advice for new entrepreneurs.

She said ideas are very common, and what matters is doing the work. She said if you are scared someone will steal your idea and do it better and faster, then you are not the right person to carry it out. She said it is about being confident, moving fast, and learning.

Ringelmann said expanding into Europe and making a deal with a web transaction provider are part of a dream to make finance more open to everyone. The deal will let people pay through local card services like Maestro in the UK and Carte Bleue in France, as well as PayPal. More than 7,000 finance campaigns are live on the site.

Crowdfunding has grown as another choice besides banks. But it does not attract many big investors, because they usually want a stake in a promising business. This could start to change in the UK with the launch of InvestingZone.
