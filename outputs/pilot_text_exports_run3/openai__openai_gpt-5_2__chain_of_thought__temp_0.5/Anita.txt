Anitta is a new Brazilian funk star. She has won millions of fans by bringing favela music into the mainstream. But she is now in the middle of an argument about skin colour.

Anti-discrimination activists and writers say that to sell music to the mostly white middle-class market, she had to give up part of her “blackness.”

The argument grew after people shared “before and after” photos of Anitta. The photos show her skin looking much lighter after she signed a deal with Warner.

In the first photo, from when she was not well known, she looks darker. In the second photo, a marketing picture after she became famous, she looks paler. People argued about why this happened. Some said it could be skin-lightening products or plastic surgery, but others said it was more likely Photoshop. The strong difference brought back the question of whether you need to be pale to succeed in Brazil.

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote a post about what she saw as hidden discrimination in media and marketing. She said many people do not accept that they are racist and think Brazil is a multiracial democracy, but statistics show it is not like that. She said the “whitening” shows a society that does not support diversity, and that people often link being white with being rich and successful, and being black with the opposite.

Anitta was born Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

She later became very famous with an album and a hit song, “Show das Poderosas.” The song reached number 1 on the charts and got 52 million views on YouTube.

Many people love her as a pop idol with a strong message and catchy songs. Her supporters also present her as a bridge between the mostly black and mixed-race shanty towns on Rio’s hills and the richer and whiter neighbourhoods below.

She has made baile funk less extreme for a wider audience. She reduced sexual dancing, gang references, and very explicit lyrics. But now some people ask if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract a richer group of fans. Arraes said this can pressure pop stars to change their looks, like straightening curly hair or getting a nose operation, and that this harms self-esteem.

Race and colour are a sensitive subject in Brazil. Brazil was one of the last big countries to end slavery. It has the largest population of people of African descent outside Africa. But people often focus less on ancestry and more on skin colour. Even though Brazil says it wants to be a multiracial democracy, there is a clear link between skin tone and inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Up to 2011, black or mixed-race students also spent about two fewer years in school on average.

The government says the gap is getting smaller because of university quotas and other affirmative action. But big differences remain. Most business and government leaders are white, and many low-paid jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In rich areas like Ipanema and Gávea, you are more likely to see black nannies pushing strollers with white toddlers than a white nanny pushing a black child.

Deciding colour is not simple. In the 2010 census, people who said they were white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million said black, two million said Asian, and 0.5% said indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology lecturer at the Federal University of Pernambuco, said Anitta won middle-class fans by taking a rebellious sound and making it calmer and easier for more people to enjoy. He said this was not because of racial whitening. He said it was because Anitta’s art moved from “the periphery to the centre.”

Other people also say the colour issue is exaggerated. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who worked with Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot, but not her skin colour. He said it was more about makeup, hair styling, and clothing. He also said he did not think she was forced, and that she always liked to sing, dance, and perform.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador, said the problem was not mainly society, but music producers and media leaders. He said the public showed they cared about the music itself when they chose Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, as a recent winner of the TV show The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for a comment. But in a recent interview, she said identity should be defined by the person. She said a powerful woman does not need to be beautiful, and that her work tries to show that people can be who they want.
