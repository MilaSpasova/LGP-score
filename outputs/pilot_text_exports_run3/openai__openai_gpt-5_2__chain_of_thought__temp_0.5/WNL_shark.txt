Opposition to Western Australia’s shark cull has grown. Thousands of people went to beaches across Australia to ask the state’s premier to stop the policy. RSPCA Australia and Virgin Atlantic owner Richard Branson also spoke against it.

The policy catches and kills sharks longer than three metres. It began after what the state government called an “unprecedented” number of shark attacks on Western Australia’s coast. A 35-year-old surfer was killed in November 2013. He was the sixth person to die from a shark attack in two years.

However, the Shark Attack File says Australia as a whole has averaged one shark-related death a year for the last 50 years.

Kate Faehrmann, a board member at the Sea Shepherd Conservation Society, spoke at a protest in Perth. She said the policy will not work. She said drumlines are used to catch sharks, but they catch many animals. She said they can kill sharks of any size, and also dolphins, turtles, and other animals. She said that is why the community does not want the policy.

Thousands of people protested on Perth’s Cottesloe Beach and Sydney’s Manly Beach. Hundreds protested at Glenelg in south-west Adelaide, and at beaches in Victoria and Queensland. Faehrmann said the protests showed Australians want sharks protected. She said that even though people have fear because of Jaws, many people are coming out to say, “That’s their ocean. We respect them, we love them and we don’t want them killed.”

Anthony Joyce, a surfer who once had his foot caught in a shark’s mouth, said the number of sharks killed will not make a difference.

The state government has refused to give a running total of sharks killed. There have been reports that sharks smaller than three metres were released after being caught on drumlines. Drumlines are floating drums anchored to the sea bed, with bait on hooks below.

Conservationists say there is no evidence the cull will reduce shark attacks on humans. They say no previous cull has only used drumlines. Researchers at the University of Western Australia say the recent increase in shark attacks may be more linked to the state having the fastest-growing population in Australia, not to having more sharks.

Richard Peirce, chairman of the UK-based Shark Trust, said the cull would not work and could bring more predators closer to the coast. He said leaving drumlines in overnight can attract other predators. They may be drawn by sharks and other animals that are hooked and injured.

Around the world in 2012, there were 80 unprovoked shark attacks, and seven were fatal. This was compared to nearly 100m sharks killed by humans each year.

RSPCA Australia said it believes the cull is not justified. It said there is no evidence the rise in attacks is because there are more sharks. It said it fits with changes in population and human behaviour, meaning more people are in the water.

Richard Branson told Fairfax Radio the policy was backfiring. He said Western Australia Premier Colin Barnett may have thought it would help tourism, but Branson said it would do the opposite. He said it advertises a problem that does not exist in a major way, and it may stop people from wanting to visit Perth and the countryside. He said it will only make people worry for no good reason.
