A thick layer of bird droppings covers a gold-colored railing at one of Britain’s most expensive homes. Pigeon bones lie among broken mirrors, and water runs through broken ceiling edges. This home is called The Towers. It is a £30m palace on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London. Its serious damage has been kept secret until now.

The Towers is one of ten mansions in the middle of The Bishops Avenue. Lloyds Bank says this is the second most expensive street in Britain. These mansions have been almost completely empty since they were bought about 25 years ago. It is believed they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. Their Greek-style columns are breaking. Their mosaic swimming pools are full of rubble. Nature has moved in so much that owls now live there.

This empty and broken scene happens all along the avenue. At the same time, many people find it hard to buy a home in London because house prices rise by 11.2% each year. On the most expensive part of The Bishops Avenue, 16 mansions are empty. Many are behind locked gates. Their windows are covered with steel bars, the gardens are overgrown, and guard dogs patrol.

Across the street is another empty mansion worth £18m, with broken windows and walls covered in anti-climb paint. Another mansion has metal bars on the windows and has been sold for £20m.

Even so, prices keep rising. Dryades, a mansion that was until recently owned by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007 and is believed to be worth about £30m today. Heath Lodge, where fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou was murdered in 1984 by a silver bullet, is worth £13m today. It was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

For people who struggle to afford housing in one of the world’s most expensive cities, seeing these empty mansions can be painful. One security guard said it was upsetting to watch so many tens of thousands of square feet of space—enough to house dozens of people—fall apart.

In the past, royals with oil wealth from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia were among the first to move to this curved road near Hampstead Heath. Iranians came after the fall of the shah. Now, Chinese buyers are following Russians and Kazakhs who have spent millions to get an address that estate agents say is as famous as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions were for sale for £65m and £38m. They promised Italian marble, leather-padded lifts, and luxury panic rooms.

But on the empty properties, stone fountains crumble and lawns have turned into muddy bogs. Inside one mansion, water drips through a huge crystal chandelier onto a thick carpet that is rotting under parts of a collapsed ceiling. Moss grows through broken bricks, and mirrored tiles are scattered across a bathroom. The swimming pool has about a foot of dirty, salty water, and flowers grow through its tiles. Wooden boards in the sauna are bending outward.

The most dramatic ruin is The Towers. It is a grand mansion set among acres of hornbeams, oaks, and limes. Pigeons fill its huge high-ceiling rooms. Water pours through three floors, and algae has turned the walls bright green. The water drips into a large, empty basement swimming pool. Unopened wooden crates marked “bullet-proof glass” show the security worries of the previous owners.

Today, very few people live on The Bishops Avenue all the time. A security guard outside one mansion said the owners were not there. A guard outside Royal Mansion would not say if anyone was home. A staff member at another mansion warned the Guardian about the guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare mogul with personal wealth of £45m, said he is one of the few permanent residents.

He said, “It’s not a neighbourly place, where you can chat over the fence. To be honest, I have never seen what my neighbours look like. Next door, a Saudi princess spent £35m on a new house and I’ve never seen her. There are about three houses that are lived in 24/7 and half of the properties are occupied three to six months a year. The other half, who knows if they come or not?”

These broken, very expensive homes show a culture where the richest people in the world treat British homes as investments. One Hyde Park, a group of flats in Knightsbridge, is another example. More than half the flats there are registered with the council as empty or second homes.

People on the avenue are now talking about building £5m apartments instead of £50m mansions, to try to bring people back.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, plans to rebuild one of the most valuable sites on the avenue into 20 apartments. They would have a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

He said, “If you build a big house and try and sell for £30m to £40m, it won’t sell. Locals won’t buy and so you have to bring in overseas buyers.”

But the idea that these empty properties could help solve the housing crisis still seems far away. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some of the ruined homes could become affordable housing. He said the land price would be too high.

He said, “Very wealthy people own property there. Sometimes they live in them and sometimes they don’t.”
