The lottery balls have fallen down the chute, and all six numbers match. Now it is time to buy an Audi, book a holiday in the US, and phone the estate agent. An analysis says this is what many lottery millionaires do.

Since it started in 1994, the lottery has made 3,000 millionaires. Together they have won more than £8.5bn in total, with an average of £2.8m each. The study authors, Oxford Economics, say this money also helped create 3,780 more millionaires among the winners’ children, family, and friends.

Most winners (59%) stop working right away. But 19% keep doing their normal job, and 31% do unpaid volunteer work. The study says 98% of winners’ spending stayed in the UK. By spending on property, vehicles, and holidays, it is estimated that each winner supports six full-time jobs for one year.

Winners have added almost £750m to gross domestic product (GDP) and brought in more than £500m in tax for the Exchequer. Most of the money was spent on property. Winners spent £2.72bn on their main homes, and £170m on paying off debt and mortgages.

Keeping a steady income was important, so £2.125bn was spent on investments. Gifts to family and friends were £1.17bn. Cars and holidays cost £680m.

Camelot, which runs the UK National Lottery, asked for the study to mark the milestone of 3,000 winners. The study used research from 100 winners who won £1m or more. It found that the 3,000 winners bought 7,958 houses or flats in the UK, which is 2.7 each on average, spending £3.3bn. Most winners (82%) moved to a new main home, spending an average of £900,000.

Many new homes had extra features. Almost a third (29%) bought a hot tub. A walk-in wardrobe was chosen by 28%. Almost a quarter (24%) chose a home behind electric gates. A games room was chosen by 22%, and 7% installed a snooker table.

Bigger homes need more care. Some winners hired help: 30% employed a cleaner, and 24% employed a gardener. A small number (5%) employed a beautician.

Audis were the favourite cars for 16% of winners. Range Rovers and BMWs were also popular (11% each), as well as Mercedes (10%) and Land Rovers (5%). Winners spent £463m on 17,190 cars. The average price of their favourite car was £46,116.

Holidays were also important. Most winners (68%) chose five-star hotels overseas. The US was the top place to go for 27%, followed by the Caribbean (9%). In the UK, caravan sales also gained. Over the past 18 years, 10% of millionaires bought a caravan, creating sales worth about £7.4m.

Some winners (15%) started their own business. Another 9% helped other people start businesses, and 6% invested in or bought other people’s businesses. The study says businesses started or supported by lottery winners employ 3,195 people.

Andy Logan, who wrote the report, said the effects of a win spread further than expected. He said it changes life for friends and family, and each win also has a clear effect on the UK economy because so much money is spent in the UK. He said each win creates a ripple effect in this generation and often the next.
