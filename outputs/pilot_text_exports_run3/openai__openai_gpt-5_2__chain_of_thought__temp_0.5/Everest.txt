Kenton Cool usually likes to talk, but now he can hardly speak. After working hard high up in the mountains, his voice sounds rough. He is in Kathmandu, the capital city of Nepal. He flew down from Everest base camp that morning.

Cool is thinking about an amazing set of climbs he finished the weekend before. Early on Saturday morning, he reached the top (the summit) of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of three main summits in the Everest “horseshoe.” This horseshoe surrounds an icy valley called the Western Cwm.

Later that same day, he climbed to the summit of Everest. He reached the top in complete darkness early on Sunday. Then he and his climbing partner continued to Lhotse. They reached the summit of Lhotse on Monday morning. Lhotse was the third peak in this three-peaks challenge.

“It was a snatched opportunity,” he says. “For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. That does not take away the physical achievement of what I did. I’ve set the bar at a certain level. But whoever comes along next will move the bar further and do it without ropes or bottled oxygen.”

Sixty years after Everest was first climbed, many news stories look back to Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. They talk about an earlier time, before modern commercialism and damage to the environment. I ask Cool to look forward and imagine what top climbers might do 60 years from now.

“I hate to think,” he says. He talks about the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. Steck left the mountain in April after what Cool calls “an altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck planned to climb Everest’s west ridge, which was first climbed in 1963. Then Steck planned to go down to the South Col and immediately climb Lhotse by a new route. He planned to do this without fixed ropes. “Ueli had been training like a machine,” Cool says. “He’s a climber in a class all his own. He’s technically brilliant and he had also taken his physical condition to an astronomic level. It would have been amazing to see what he could have done.”

What tourism will be like is a different question. One clue is a helicopter rescue done by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. His angry language helped cause the fight at Camp 2. Moro flew back to Everest on Tuesday in a high-powered helicopter. He rescued a very sick or injured climber at 7,800 metres.

It was the highest rescue ever done on Everest. It also shows that helicopter flights have risen very fast in recent years. By 2073, the mountain could even have a helipad on the South Col. Tourists might be brought there while breathing bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing what is possible for rescuing climbers and also the many trekkers who go as far as base camp.

Mountain geographer and environmentalist Alton Byers says it is not clear if the Everest region can keep handling fast-growing tourism. He says climate change and tourism together are adding new pressure to the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers in the Everest region are shrinking, and some are disappearing. This is already causing big problems. “Everywhere you go, people are talking about how there’s less water,” he says. “There’s less water for agriculture and less water for all the new lodges that are getting built.”

Byers says that in the Sherpa town of Namche Bazaar, a new five-mile pipeline is being built to bring in water. It is needed because more tourists want showers and flush toilets. The local stream has been polluted with human waste. It also does not give enough water for a place that, in high season, is extremely crowded. “Every village is digging a pit just beyond the houses for garbage. Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world,” he says.

Human waste at base camp is now handled well and taken away in plastic barrels. But Byers says these barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley. That pit could leak into the area’s rivers and streams.

“These problems can be solved, but we need to get serious about it,” he says. “One climber can spend $85,000 climbing Everest. And that’s fine. But at some point we’re going to have to address these other priorities. For half a million dollars a year, you could solve most of them.”

Climate change is harder. Byers works with local conservation groups to find and plan for climate change effects. They usually look for new water sources or collect rainwater. Another danger is that glacial lakes are growing quickly. These lakes could burst and flood the Sherpa homeland. “There’s going to come a time when people are going to have to get out of their way,” he says.

Changing weather is also affecting tourism. More clouds during times that are usually clear are causing Lukla Airport to close more often. Lukla Airport is the main entrance to the Everest region. A new road for 4x4 vehicles is being built to Lukla to keep tourists, and their money, coming. But Byers worries the road network in Nepal is being built cheaply and too fast. He says this can cause serious soil erosion and landslides.

“Everest is the icon everyone knows,” he says. “It’s the canary in the coalmine that everyone understands. It’s the perfect laboratory for figuring out how to address some of these problems, like the impacts of climate change and tourism.”
