It was a nice summer evening. I decided to go swimming near Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland. I moved there in 2012. In the water, I saw a woman swimming with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty often swims with a group of people.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Since then, hundreds of people have swum with her, so she seems very tame. She was even in an Irish tourist-board advertisement where a girl says she wants to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty’s tummy. It looked safe and fun. About 20 tourists and local people were watching from the pier. Soon after I got in the water, Dusty suddenly left the woman and became very wild. Later I learned she is very protective of her space when she is with someone. Her tail was hitting the water hard. At first I thought she was just showing off. Then I realized she might be angry.

I knew I had to get out, so I swam toward the pier. But in a split second, Dusty crashed into me with her nose. It was very strong and painful, and she was moving very fast. I was thrown forward.

People on the pier stared at me in shock. Dusty stayed beside me, still hitting the water with her tail. That scared me the most. I thought, if she hits me with her tail, I could go under the water.

I reached the pier, but I could not get out because I was hurt. I felt extreme fear. I shouted for help. A man reached in, grabbed me, and pulled me onto the steps. Then another man came and said he was an orthopaedic surgeon who specialized in injuries from the sea. He had been driving into Doolin when he saw what was happening.

I was very cold and very worried. I did not know how bad my injuries were. I was most afraid of bleeding inside my body. The surgeon checked me and told me he could not feel signs of internal bleeding, but he thought I had broken bones. Later I learned I had six fractures in my spine, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in the hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for several weeks. I could not work for five months, and I had trouble moving. I was stiff and in pain. Then doctors said I had post-traumatic stress. After my near-death experience, I felt anxious about everything and reacted strongly in ways I never had before. I felt like people were looking at me the wrong way. Loud noises became hard for me. I also had memory loss.

Three months before the accident, I had opened a health-food shop. But I had to give it up because I could not work anymore. It was the hardest year, but now it is behind me. I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building up my own osteopathy practice. I understand patients better because I have been one.

I am thankful I am healthy. I really want to stop other people from getting hurt. People often think dolphins are always kind and safe. Who would think a dolphin would attack a person? If you see a dangerous animal coming at you with its teeth showing, you feel afraid. But dolphins have a wide smile, so they can seem harmless.

I am not angry at Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her space, and she is a wild animal that can act in unexpected ways. People need to know this. Many people come to swim with her, and they do not understand how dangerous it can be. People said my injuries were among several that summer.

After I was pulled out of the water, Dusty swam away. Then she came back. She floated upright next to me and looked at me. We looked into each other’s eyes, and I felt she was sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin, and the anger was gone. The people on the pier were amazed. After that moment with her, my terror ended. I made peace with her.
