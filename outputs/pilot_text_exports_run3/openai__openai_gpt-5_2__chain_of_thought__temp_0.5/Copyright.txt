Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is theft. He says it is as wrong as putting your hand in someone’s pocket and stealing their wallet.

Pullman wrote an article for Index on Censorship. Pullman is president of the Society of Authors. In the article, he strongly supports copyright laws. He criticizes internet users who think it is OK to download music or books without paying.

He writes that new technology is very impressive, but people do not see the wrong thing they are doing. He says it is shocking that someone can steal an artist’s work and not get punished.

His article was written after leaders from the music industry met British Prime Minister David Cameron at Downing Street. They talked about web piracy.

Pullman is the writer of the His Dark Materials trilogy. He says authors and musicians often work for years in poverty and without attention to make work that gives joy to audiences. He says that when they finally succeed, their chance to earn a living can be taken away by illegal downloading. He ends with a simple idea: if we want to enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

In the next issue of the group’s magazine, Pullman writes in a dialogue with Cathy Casserly. Casserly is chief executive of Creative Commons. Creative Commons offers open content licences that “lets creators take copyright into their own hands”. Casserly says copyright has many problems because it was made “in an analogue age”. She says copyright often stops people from sharing, building on, and remixing each other’s work in many ways.

Casserly says artists need to think of new ways to share and make money from their work. She quotes science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees that there are problems. The magazine’s editor, Rachael Jolley, said current copyright laws do not work well in the digital age and could make consumers into criminals. She says new models are needed so artists, writers, and musicians can earn a living from their work.

The argument is strong, and illegal downloading is very large. Data from Ofcom (the independent regulator and competition authority for the UK communications industries) suggests that in the UK, between November 2012 and January 2013, 280 million music tracks were pirated. Also pirated were 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files.

Ofcom said 18% of internet users over age 12 admit they recently pirated content. Of these users, 9% say they fear getting caught. Pullman writes that it is still surprising to some people how quickly music can be gotten as MP3 downloads, even for those who have been building up their iTunes list for some time.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron appointed Conservative MP Mike Weatherley as his adviser on this issue. A spokesman for the BPI, the record industry trade body, said Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creative producers it protects. The spokesman said they hope Weatherley and the prime minister’s support for copyright will influence the UK’s intellectual property office.
