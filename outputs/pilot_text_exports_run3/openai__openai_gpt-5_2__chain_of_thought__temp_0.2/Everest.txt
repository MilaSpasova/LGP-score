Kenton Cool usually likes to talk. But now he can hardly speak. Climbing high up in the mountains has made his voice rough and scratchy.

He is now in Kathmandu, the capital of Nepal. That morning, he flew down from Everest base camp. He is thinking about an amazing set of climbs he finished the weekend before.

Early on Saturday morning, he reached the top (the summit) of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of three main summits. These three mountains form the Everest “horseshoe” around an icy valley called the Western Cwm.

Later that same day, he climbed to the summit of Everest. He reached the top in total darkness early on Sunday. Then he and his climbing partner kept going. They reached the summit of Lhotse on Monday morning. Lhotse was the third mountain in this three-peaks challenge.

“It was a snatched opportunity,” he says. “For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. That does not take away the physical achievement of what I did. I’ve set the bar at a certain level. But whoever comes along next will move the bar further and do it without ropes or bottled oxygen.”

Sixty years after Everest was first climbed, many news stories look back to Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. People compare their time, before modern commercialism and environmental damage, with today. I asked Cool to look ahead and imagine what top climbers might do 60 years from now.

“I hate to think,” he says. But he talks about the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. Steck left the mountain in April after what Cool calls “an altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck was planning to climb Everest’s west ridge, first climbed in 1963. Then Steck planned to go down to the South Col and immediately climb Lhotse by a new route, all without fixed ropes. “Ueli had been training like a machine,” Cool says. “He’s a climber in a class all his own. He’s technically brilliant but he had also taken his physical condition to an astronomic level. It would have been amazing to see what he could have done.”

Tourism is another question. One clue is a helicopter rescue by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro’s angry words helped start the fight at Camp 2. Moro flew back to Everest on Tuesday in a powerful helicopter. He rescued a very sick climber at 7,800 metres.

It was the highest rescue ever done on Everest. It also shows how fast helicopter flights have increased in recent years. By 2073, the mountain could even have a helicopter landing pad (a helipad) on the South Col, bringing tourists who breathe bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing how rescues can happen for both climbers and the many trekkers who go as far as base camp.

Mountain geographer and environmentalist Alton Byers says it is not clear if the Everest region can keep up with fast-growing tourism. He says climate change and tourism together are putting new pressure on the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers in the Everest region are shrinking, and some are disappearing. This is already having a big effect. “Everywhere you go, people are talking about how there’s less water. There’s less water for agriculture and less water for all the new lodges that are getting built.”

Byers says that in the Sherpa town of Namche Bazaar, workers are building a new five-mile pipeline to bring water. This is to meet the growing tourist demand for showers and flush toilets. The local stream is polluted by human waste. It also does not provide enough water for a place that, in high season, is very crowded. “Every village is digging a pit just beyond the houses for garbage. Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world,” he says. Human waste at base camp is now handled well and taken away in plastic barrels. But Byers says the barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley. That pit could leak into the region’s rivers and streams.

“These problems can be solved, but we need to get serious about it,” he says. “One climber can spend $85,000 climbing Everest. And that’s fine. But at some point we’re going to have to address these other priorities. For half a million dollars a year, you could solve most of them.”

Climate change is harder. Byers works with local conservation committees to find and plan for climate change effects. They often look for new water sources or collect rainwater. Glacial lakes are growing quickly, and they could burst and flood the Sherpa homeland. This is a constant danger. “There’s going to come a time when people are going to have to get out of their way.”

Changing weather is also affecting tourism. More clouds during times that are usually clear are closing Lukla Airport more often. Lukla Airport is the main entry point to the Everest region. A new road for 4x4 vehicles is being built to Lukla to make sure tourists, and their money, can still get in. But Byers worries the road building in Nepal is being done cheaply. He says this could cause serious soil loss and landslides.

“Everest is the icon everyone knows,” he says. “It’s the canary in the coalmine that everyone understands. It’s the perfect laboratory for figuring out how to address some of these problems, like the impacts of climate change and tourism.”
