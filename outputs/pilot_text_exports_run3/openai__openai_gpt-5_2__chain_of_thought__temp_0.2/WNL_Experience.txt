It was a beautiful summer evening. I decided to go swimming near Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland. I moved there in 2012. A woman was in the water with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty often swims with a group of people, and she seems close to them.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Since then, hundreds of people have swum with her, so she can seem totally tame. She was even in an Irish tourist-board advertisement, where a girl says she would like to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty’s belly. It looked safe and fun. About 20 tourists and local people stood on the pier watching. Soon after I got into the water, Dusty left the woman and suddenly became very wild. Later I learned she can be very protective of her space when she is with someone. Her tail was flapping hard. At first I thought she was just showing off, but then I realized she might be angry.

I knew I needed to get out, so I swam toward the pier. But almost at once, Dusty crashed into me with her nose. It was very strong and very painful. She was moving very fast, and I was thrown forward.

People on the pier stared down at me with their mouths open. Dusty stayed beside me, still hitting the water with her tail. That scared me the most. I thought if she hit me with her tail, I could sink.

I reached the pier, but I could not climb out because I was hurt. I felt terrified. I shouted for help, and a man reached down and pulled me onto the steps.

Then another man came. He said he was an orthopaedic surgeon who specialized in injuries from the sea. He was driving into Doolin when he saw what was happening. I was very cold and very worried. I did not know how badly I was hurt, and I feared bleeding inside my body. He examined me and tried to calm me. He said he could not feel signs of internal bleeding, but he thought I probably had broken bones.

Later I learned I had six fractures in my spine, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in the hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for several weeks. I could not work for five months. I had limited movement, stiffness, and pain. Later, doctors said I had post-traumatic stress. After my near-death experience, I felt anxious about everything. I overreacted in ways I never had before. I felt people were looking at me the wrong way. Loud noises bothered me. I also had memory loss.

Three months before the accident, I had opened a health-food shop. I had to give it up because I could not work. That year was the hardest one, but now it is behind me. I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building up my own osteopathy practice. I understand patients better because I have been one.

I am thankful that I am healthy, and I want to stop other people from getting hurt. People have a nice idea about dolphins and trust them. Who would think a dolphin would attack a person? If you see a fierce animal coming at you with its teeth showing, you feel scared. But dolphins have a wide smile that looks friendly.

I am not angry at Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her area, and she is a wild animal that can act in unexpected ways. People need to know this. Many people come to swim with her, and they do not understand how dangerous it can be. My injuries were said to be among several injuries that summer.

After the man pulled me out of the water, Dusty swam away. Then she came back. She floated upright next to me and looked at me. We looked into each other’s eyes. I felt she was completely sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin. The anger was gone. The people on the pier were amazed. That moment ended my fear. I accepted what happened, and I made peace with her.
