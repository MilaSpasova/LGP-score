Brazil’s new funk star, Anitta, has won millions of fans by bringing favela music into the mainstream. But she is now in the middle of an argument about skin colour.

Some anti-discrimination groups and writers say the music industry’s fastest rising star had to give up part of her “blackness” to sell music to a mostly white, middle-class audience.

The argument grew after people published “before and after” photos. They show Anitta’s skin looking much lighter after she signed a deal with Warner.

In the first photo, from when she was not well known, she looks darker. In the second photo, a marketing picture after she became famous, she looks paler. People disagree about why. Some mention skin-whitening products and cosmetic surgery, but others say it is more likely Photoshop changes. The difference has started the question again: do you need to be pale to succeed in Brazil?

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote a post about what she called hidden discrimination in media and marketing. She said many people do not accept that they are racist and think Brazil is a “multiracial democracy,” but that statistics show this is not true. She said the “whitening” shows a society that does not support diversity. She wrote that people often connect being white with being rich, nice, successful, and good, and see black as the opposite.

Anitta was born Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

She became very famous with an album and her hit single, Show das Poderosas. It reached number one on the charts and got 52 million YouTube views.

Many fans like her as a pop star with catchy songs and a strong message. The people backing her also present her as a bridge between the mostly black and mixed-race shanty towns on Rio’s hillsides and the richer and whiter neighbourhoods below.

She has also made baile funk less shocking for a wider audience. She reduced the suggestive dancing, gangsta talk, and explicit lyrics. Now some people ask if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract a richer audience. Arraes said that if pop stars have curly hair, they may feel pushed to straighten it, and if they have a big nose, they may feel pushed to get a nose operation (rhinoplasty). She said this can harm self-esteem.

This topic is sensitive in Brazil, a country with many mixed-race people. Brazil was one of the last big countries to end slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. But in Brazil, colour often matters more than race or family background. Even though the country aims to be a multiracial democracy, skin tone is clearly linked to inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Up to 2011, black or mixed-race students also spent two fewer years in school on average.

The government says the gap is shrinking because of quota systems for university places and other forms of affirmative action. But the difference is still easy to see. Most business and government leaders are white. Many lower-paid jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In Ipanema, Gávea, and other rich areas, you are more likely to see black nannies pushing strollers with white toddlers than the other way around.

Describing colour in Brazil is complicated. In the 2010 census, people who called themselves white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million said black, two million said Asian, and 0.5% said indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology lecturer at the Federal University of Pernambuco, said Anitta won over the middle class by taking a rebellious sound and making it calmer and easier for many people to accept. He said this was not done by “racial whitening.” He said what changed was the social place where Anitta made her art: from the periphery to the centre.

Other people also say the colour argument is too big. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who worked with Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot, but not her colour. He said he thinks it is about makeup, hair styling, and clothing. He also said he does not think she changed because of pressure. He said she always liked to sing, dance, and show her style, and that this natural way is why she is successful.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador, said the problem is not the public but music producers and media leaders. He said the public showed they care about music itself by choosing Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, as the recent winner of the talent show The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for comment. But in a recent interview, she said identity should be defined by the person. She said an “all-powerful” woman does not need to be beautiful; she can be powerful because of her attitude. She said her work tries to show that people can be who they want.
