A thick layer of bird droppings covers a gold-painted railing at one of Britain’s most expensive homes. Pigeon bones lie among broken mirrors, and water runs through cracked ceilings. This home is called The Towers. It is a £30m palace on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London. Its collapse has been kept secret until now.

The Towers is one of ten large houses in the middle of The Bishops Avenue. This road is a key part of London’s fast-rising property market. The houses have been almost empty for about 25 years. It is believed they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. Their Greek-style columns are breaking. Their swimming pools, covered with mosaic tiles, are full of broken stone. Nature has taken over so much that owls have moved in.

This sad sight appears again and again along the avenue. Lloyds Bank says The Bishops Avenue is the second most expensive street in Britain. At the same time, more people struggle to buy a home in London as house prices rise by 11.2% a year. On the most expensive part of the avenue, 16 mansions sit empty. Many are behind locked gates. Their windows are covered with steel bars, their gardens are overgrown, and guard dogs patrol the grounds.

Across the street is another empty mansion worth £18m, with smashed windows and walls covered in anti-climb paint. Another house has metal bars on the windows and has been sold for £20m.

Even so, prices keep rising. Dryades, a mansion once owned by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007 and is believed to be worth about £30m today. Heath Lodge is worth £13m today. It was the place of the 1984 murder, by silver bullet, of fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou. The house was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

For people who struggle to afford housing, seeing these empty homes can be painful. One security guard on the avenue said it was upsetting to watch so much space fall apart. He said the houses have tens of thousands of square feet—enough room for dozens of people.

Royals with oil money from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia were among the first to move to this curved road near Hampstead Heath. Iranians came here after the fall of the shah. Now, Chinese buyers are following Russians and Kazakhs who have spent millions to get an address that estate agents compare to the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions have been for sale for £65m and £38m. They promise Italian marble, lifts with leather padding, and luxury panic rooms.

But inside the empty homes, things are falling apart. Stone fountains break down and lawns turn into wet bogs. In one house, water drips through a huge crystal chandelier onto a thick carpet that is rotting under parts of a collapsed ceiling. Moss grows through broken bricks. Mirror tiles are scattered across a bathroom. The swimming pool has about a foot of dirty water, and flowers grow through its tiles. Wooden boards in the sauna bend outward.

The worst damage is at The Towers. It is a grand house set among acres of hornbeams, oaks, and limes. Pigeons fill its large halls with high ceilings. Water pours through three storeys, turning walls bright green with algae, and drops into a huge, empty basement swimming pool. Unopened wooden crates marked “bullet-proof glass” show the security worries of the earlier owners.

Today, very few people live on The Bishops Avenue all the time. A security guard outside one mansion said the owners were not there. Another guard, outside Royal Mansion, would not say if anyone was home. A staff member at another house warned the Guardian about the guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare mogul with personal wealth of £45m, said he is one of the few permanent residents.

He said, “It’s not a neighbourly place, where you can chat over the fence. To be honest, I have never seen what my neighbours look like. Next door, a Saudi princess spent £35m on a new house and I’ve never seen her. There are about three houses that are lived in 24/7 and half of the properties are occupied three to six months a year. The other half, who knows if they come or not?”

These broken, very expensive homes show a culture where the richest people treat British property as an investment. One Hyde Park, a group of flats in Knightsbridge, is another example. More than half the flats there are registered with the council as empty or as second homes.

People on the avenue now talk about building £5m apartments instead of £50m mansions, to bring people back.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, plans to rebuild one of the most valuable sites on the avenue. He wants to make 20 apartments with a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

He said, “If you build a big house and try and sell for £30m to £40m, it won’t sell. Locals won’t buy and so you have to bring in overseas buyers.”

But using the empty homes to help the housing crisis still seems far away. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some of the empty houses could become affordable homes. He said the land price would be too high.

He said, “Very wealthy people own property there. Sometimes they live in them and sometimes they don’t.”
