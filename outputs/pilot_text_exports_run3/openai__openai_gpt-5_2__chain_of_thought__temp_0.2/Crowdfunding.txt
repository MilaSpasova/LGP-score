This invention could be as helpful as Trevor Baylis’s wind-up radio was in the 1990s, before most people used the internet. It is a cheap light that gets free power from gravity. It could stop people in Africa and India from using dangerous kerosene lamps.

A British designer named Patrick Hunt tried to get money from banks or venture capital companies to start selling his invention. But he had trouble. He said getting funding was slow and complicated. The idea was new, and people did not want to take the risk.

So he tried crowdfunding on a US website called Indiegogo, which had just started working in the UK. In five days, he reached his goal and raised £36,200. His campaign was very popular at the end of 2012. In 40 days, he raised £400,000.

The LED light uses a dynamo. The dynamo is turned by a 10kg bag of rocks that slowly drops down. The bag is attached to the light. You lift it to about 2m high. As it slowly falls to the ground, it makes enough power for 30 minutes of light.

Hunt is getting ready to make the light in China. Before making many more, he will test it again by sending 1,000 lights to Africa. He hopes later to make millions.

He is part of a new group of business starters who use crowdfunding to get money. Another new website is InvestingZone. It connects rich people with start-up entrepreneurs.

Indiegogo does not give people shares. Instead, people who donate can get “perks” at different donation levels. People who helped fund Hunt’s light could feel good about helping poorer people, and they also got their own light.

Danae Ringelmann, a co-founder of Indiegogo, said this “gravity light” shows how crowdfunding can work. She said it can quickly test an entrepreneur’s idea. She said finance can be fast and fair because it is not about knowing the right people at a bank or a venture capital firm. It is about showing your value to customers and fans, and getting them to support your idea with money.

She also said that even ideas that do not get funding are still worth testing. That way, you can learn sooner if an idea is not good, and you can learn faster.

Ringelmann lives in the US. She first worked as a Wall Street analyst. In 2008, she quit and decided to use her skills to help friends in the arts raise money.

At first, the site focused on films. It launched at the Sundance Film Festival that year.

Five years later, Indiegogo was raising about $2m each week for new businesses that are starting or growing. In December 2012, it started services in euros and pounds so it could grow in Europe. It says Britain is its third biggest market. Since December, activity from other countries is up 41%.

Indiegogo has many competitors, like Kickstarter, Seedrs, and Funding Circle. But Ringelmann says Indiegogo is different because anyone can start a campaign. No project is seen as too strange.

Indiegogo charges a 4% fee for successful campaigns. If a campaign does not reach its goal, users can choose to refund all the money to donors for free. Or they can keep the money they raised, but then they pay a 9% fee.

A British woman named Lauren Pears raised £100,000 on the site to open a “cat café” in London. It is called Lady Dinah’s Cat Emporium. It has not opened yet, but it is described as a place where people can sit in a comfortable wingback chair with a hot cup of tea, a book, and a cat.

Ringelmann said venture capitalists sometimes reject ideas because they do not know if people really want them. She said an Indiegogo campaign can show proof that there is a market, even if the project is not launched yet.

She said crowdfunding lets you test your market, your price, and your product features. It can help you find new ways to make money and get important feedback.

Ringelmann said she has helped 100,000 businesses and services raise money. She shared advice for new entrepreneurs. She said there are many ideas, and what matters is doing the work to make them happen. She said if you are scared someone will steal your idea and do it better and faster, then you may not be the right person to carry it out. She said it is important to be confident, move fast, and learn.

Ringelmann said expanding in Europe and making a deal with a web payment company are part of her dream to make finance more open to everyone. The deal will allow payments through local cards like Maestro in the UK and Carte Bleue in France, and also through PayPal. More than 7,000 campaigns are live on the site.

Crowdfunding has grown as another choice besides banks. But it is not as attractive to very rich investors, who usually want a stake in a business. This could start to change in the UK with the launch of InvestingZone.
