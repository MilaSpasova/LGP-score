The balls drop down the machine, and all six numbers match. Then it is time to buy an Audi, book a holiday in the US, and call an estate agent. An analysis says this is what many lottery millionaires do.

Since it started in 1994, the lottery has made 3,000 millionaires. Together they have won more than £8.5bn, about £2.8m each on average. The study says their money also helped create 3,780 more millionaires among their children, family, and friends. The study was by Oxford Economics.

Most winners (59%) stop working straight away. But 19% keep doing their normal job, and 31% do unpaid volunteer work. The study says 98% of winners’ spending stayed in the UK. It estimates that each winner’s spending on homes, cars, and holidays keeps six people in full-time work for one year.

The winners added almost £750m to GDP and created more than £500m in taxes for the Exchequer. Most of the money went on property. Winners spent £2.72bn on their main homes, and £170m on paying off debt and mortgages.

Keeping an income was important, so winners spent £2.125bn on investments. Gifts to family and friends were £1.17bn. Cars and holidays were £680m.

Camelot, the company that runs the UK National Lottery, asked for the study to mark the 3,000-winners milestone. The research used information from 100 winners who won £1m or more. It found that the 3,000 winners bought 7,958 houses or flats in the UK, which is 2.7 each, and spent £3.3bn. Most winners (82%) changed their main home, spending £900,000 on average.

Many also added special features to their homes. Almost a third (29%) bought a hot tub. A walk-in wardrobe was chosen by 28%. Almost a quarter (24%) chose a home behind electric gates. A games room was chosen by 22%, and 7% put in a snooker table.

Bigger homes need care. Some winners hired workers: 30% employed a cleaner and 24% a gardener. A small number (5%) employed a beautician.

Audis were the favourite cars for 16% of winners. Range Rovers and BMWs were also popular (11% each), plus Mercedes (10%) and Land Rovers (5%). Winners spent £463m on 17,190 cars. The average price of their favourite car was £46,116.

Holidays were important too. Most winners (68%) chose five-star hotels abroad. The US was the top place to visit for 27%, followed by the Caribbean (9%). In the UK, caravan sales also gained. Over the past 18 years, 10% of millionaires bought a caravan, creating about £7.4m in sales.

Some winners (15%) started their own business. Another 9% helped other people start businesses, and 6% invested in or bought other people’s businesses. Businesses started or helped by winners employ 3,195 people.

Andy Logan, who wrote the report, said the effects of a win spread farther than expected. He said it changes the lives of friends and family, and it also affects the UK economy because so much money is spent in the UK. He said each win creates a “ripple effect” for this generation and often the next.
