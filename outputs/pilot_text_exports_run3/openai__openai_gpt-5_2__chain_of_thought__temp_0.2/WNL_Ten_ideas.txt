From glow-in-the-dark trees to underground bike storage and solar-powered bins, people are trying unusual ideas to make cities better places to live.

Living in a city has many good points, but living in a way that is good for the environment is getting harder. Pollution, traffic, and fewer green spaces are some of the problems city people face every day.

Now inventors are stepping in. Here are five of the wackier ideas to make cities more livable.

Pop-up parks
Some cities look like they are built more for cars than for people. A pop-up park is a simple idea. You take an empty car park, a little money, and a pot plant or two, and you make a small park. The PARK(ing) project started as an art experiment in San Francisco and then spread around the world. People have also tried temporary city farms and ecology demonstrations. The movement has a day of action every September.

Subterranean storage
Not everyone in cities depends on cars. Bikes are becoming more popular. But where can you keep a bike safe? An engineering firm in Tokyo, called Giken, made an idea called an “eco-cycle, anti-seismic underground bicycle park.” It is a round storage space that is only seven metres wide, but it goes deep into the ground and can hold 204 bikes. Owners can get their bike back by pressing a button. The system brings the bike up in about 13 seconds.

Glow-in-the-dark trees
When many people think of trees that glow in the dark, they think of Christmas decorations. But Daan Roosegaarde does not. This Dutch designer-artist created an experimental “bioluminescent” plant. The technology mixes DNA from glowing marine bacteria with part of a plant, to make a glow like a jellyfish. Trials are happening to make a version that can be made on an industrial scale. Roosegaarde hopes it could one day replace normal street lights.

Footfall harvesting
Every day, hundreds of commuters and shoppers in West Ham in east London cross a raised walkway near the underground station. Most people probably do not notice that the ground feels a little springy. The rubber surface bends about five millimetres. Under the walkway are smart tiles that take the energy from people’s footsteps and turn it into electricity. This electricity powers the streetlights above. The UK firm Pavegen made this system. It has also installed a similar system at London’s Heathrow Airport and in other international places.

Supertrees
Singapore’s Gardens by the Bay has man-made trees called “supertrees.” They are steel-framed and can be up to 50 metres high. Flowers and ferns grow on them. Their metal tops help take in and spread out heat. They can collect rainwater and help provide air ventilation for two “climate-controlled biomes” below (large conservatories). Eleven of the 18 trees also have solar panels along their “branches.”
