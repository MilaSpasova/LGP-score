Opposition to Western Australia’s shark cull has grown. Thousands of people went to beaches across Australia to ask the state’s premier to end the policy. RSPCA Australia and Virgin Atlantic owner Richard Branson also spoke against it.

The cull means catching and killing sharks longer than three metres. It started after what the state government called an “unprecedented” number of shark attacks on Western Australia’s coast. In November 2013, a 35-year-old surfer was killed. He was the sixth person to die from a shark attack in two years.

But the Shark Attack File says that across all of Australia, there has been an average of one shark-related death each year for the last 50 years.

Kate Faehrmann, a board member of the Sea Shepherd Conservation Society, spoke at a protest in Perth. She said the policy will not work. She said drumlines, which are used to catch sharks, can kill many animals. She said they can kill sharks of any size, and also dolphins, turtles, and other animals. She said this is why the community does not want the policy.

Thousands of people protested at Perth’s Cottesloe Beach and Sydney’s Manly Beach. Hundreds also protested at Glenelg in south-west Adelaide, and at beaches in Victoria and Queensland. Faehrmann said the protests showed many Australians want sharks protected. She said that even with fear from movies like Jaws, people are coming out in large numbers to say they respect sharks and do not want them killed.

Anthony Joyce, a surfer who once had his foot caught in a shark’s mouth, said the number of sharks they plan to kill will not make a difference.

The state government has refused to give an ongoing count of how many sharks have been killed. There have been reports that sharks smaller than three metres were released after being caught on drumlines. Drumlines are floating drums anchored to the seabed, with bait hanging on hooks under them.

Conservationists say there is no evidence the cull will reduce shark attacks on humans. They say no earlier cull used only drumlines. Researchers at the University of Western Australia say the recent increase in shark attacks in the state may be linked to Western Australia having the fastest-growing population in Australia, not to more sharks.

Richard Peirce, chairman of the UK conservation charity the Shark Trust, said the cull would not work and could even bring more predators closer to the coast. He said the government could have tried other ways to deal with shark and human interactions, but chose a plan that is ineffective and makes things worse. He also said drumlines can attract other predators, especially if the lines stay in the water overnight, because animals on the hooks may be injured.

Around the world in 2012, there were 80 unprovoked shark attacks, and seven were fatal. At the same time, humans kill nearly 100m sharks each year. RSPCA Australia said it believes the cull is not justified. It said there is no evidence that more attacks are caused by more sharks. Instead, it said the rise fits with changes in population and human behaviour, meaning more people are in the water.

Richard Branson told Fairfax Radio the policy was backfiring. He said Western Australia Premier Colin Barnett may have thought it would help tourism. Branson said it may do the opposite. He said it makes the problem look bigger than it is, and may stop people from coming to Perth and the countryside nearby. He said it could worry people for no good reason.
