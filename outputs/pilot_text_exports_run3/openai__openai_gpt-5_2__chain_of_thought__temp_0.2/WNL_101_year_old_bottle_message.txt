Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8 April 2014, she told about a special moment. She received a message in a bottle. Her grandfather had thrown it into the Baltic Sea 101 years earlier. People think it may be the world’s oldest message in a bottle. A museum in Germany gave it to her and is showing it.

Erdmann is 62. She said it was very surprising. A man came to her door and said he had mail from her grandfather. He said a message in a bottle had been found, and the name on the card was her grandfather’s name. The man was a family history researcher. He found her in Berlin after the letter was given to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg.

The bottle was a brown beer bottle. It had been in the water for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it in his catch. He was fishing in the Baltic Sea near the northern city of Kiel. Holger von Neuhoff, a museum worker for ocean and science, said this was the oldest bottled message he had seen. He said the museum has older documents without bottles. But with both the bottle and the document, this is the oldest right now. He said it was in very good condition.

Researchers think Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913. He was hiking with a nature group. He was 20 years old then.

Most of the postcard could not be read. But people could read the Berlin address on the front. They could also read his polite request. He asked the finder to send the note to his home address.

Erdmann said he also put two stamps from that time in the bottle. This way, the finder would not have to pay. She said he did not think it would take 101 years.

Erdmann said the message made her feel very emotional. She did not know her grandfather because he died at age 54, six years before she was born.

She said she knew very little about him. But she learned he was a writer. She said he was open-minded and believed in freedom, and that everyone should respect each other. She said he helped young people and later traveled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful because she could see where her family came from.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and traveling around the world. She said she is open-minded too. She said what her grandfather taught his two daughters, her mother taught her, and she has taught her sons.

Even though she was happy to get the message, she said she hopes people will not do the same thing today. She said the sea is already full of bottles and rubbish, so people should not throw more in.

The message and the bottle will be shown at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts will try to read the rest of the text. It is not clear what will happen to the bottle later, but Erdmann hopes it will stay at the museum.

She said they want to add some photos with the bottle. This will help visitors see the young man who threw the bottle into the water.
