Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is theft. He says it is like reaching into someone’s pocket and stealing their wallet. He calls it a kind of “moral squalor”.

Pullman wrote this in an article for Index on Censorship. He is president of the Society of Authors. He strongly supports copyright laws. He is very critical of internet users who think it is OK to download music or books without paying.

He writes: “The technical brilliance is so dazzling that people can’t see the moral squalor of what they’re doing,” and “It is theft, as surely as reaching into someone’s pocket and taking their wallet is theft.”

His article came after music industry leaders met British Prime Minister David Cameron at Downing Street. They talked about web piracy.

Pullman, who wrote the His Dark Materials trilogy, says authors and musicians often work in poverty and are not well known for years. He says that when their work finally brings joy to audiences, their chance to make a living from it can be taken away by illegal downloading. He ends with this idea: “if we want to enjoy the work that someone does, we should pay for it.”

In the next issue of the campaign group’s magazine, Pullman is in a dialogue with Cathy Casserly. She is chief executive of Creative Commons. Creative Commons offers open content licences “that lets creators take copyright into their own hands”. Casserly says there are problems with copyright, which was created “in an analogue age”. She writes that copyright can stop many ways people can share, build on, and remix each other’s work.

Casserly says artists need to think in new ways about how they share and make money from their work. She quotes the science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees that today’s laws are not working well. The magazine’s editor, Rachael Jolley, said: “Existing copyright laws don’t work in the digital age and risk criminalizing consumers. We need new models for how artists, writers and musicians earn a living from their work.”

Illegal downloading is very widespread. Ofcom (the independent regulator and competition authority for the UK communications industries) collected data. It suggests that between November 2012 and January 2013 in the UK, 280 million music tracks were digitally pirated. Also pirated were 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files.

Ofcom has said 18% of internet users aged over 12 admit they have recently pirated content. Also, 9% say they fear getting caught. Pullman also writes that getting music as MP3 downloads is very easy and fast.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron appointed the Conservative MP Mike Weatherley to be his adviser on this issue. A spokesman for the BPI, the record industry trade body, said Mike Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creative producers it protects. The spokesman said they hope Weatherley’s influence, and the prime minister’s support for copyright, will affect the work of the UK’s intellectual property office.
