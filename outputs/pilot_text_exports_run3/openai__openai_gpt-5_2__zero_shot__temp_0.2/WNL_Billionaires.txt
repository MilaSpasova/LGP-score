A thick layer of bird droppings covers the gold-colored railing of one of Britain’s most expensive homes. Pigeon skeletons are on the floor among broken mirrors, and water runs through broken parts of the ceiling. This home is called The Towers. It is a £30m palace on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London. People have kept how ruined it is a secret until now.

The Towers is one of ten large houses in the middle of The Bishops Avenue. This street is part of London’s fast-growing property market. These houses have been almost empty since they were bought about 25 years ago. People believe they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. The big columns are cracking. The swimming pools with mosaic tiles are full of broken stones and rubbish. Nature has taken over so much that owls now live there.

This sad scene is found all along the avenue. Lloyds Bank says it is the second most expensive street in Britain. House prices in London are rising by 11.2% a year, and many people struggle to buy a home. But 16 mansions on the most expensive part of The Bishops Avenue are empty. Many are behind locked gates. Their windows have steel bars, and the gardens are overgrown. Guard dogs patrol the grounds.

Across the street is another empty mansion worth £18m, with smashed windows and special paint to stop people climbing. Another mansion has metal bars on the windows and was sold for £20m.

Even so, prices keep rising. Dryades, a mansion that was recently owned by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007 and is believed to be worth about £30m today. Heath Lodge, where fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou was murdered in 1984 with a silver bullet, is worth £13m today. It was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

Seeing these empty, falling-down mansions can be very painful for people who are trying to afford housing in one of the world’s most expensive cities. One security guard said it was upsetting to see so much space—enough for dozens of people—being left to fall apart.

Rich royals from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia were some of the first to move to this curved road near Hampstead Heath. Iranians came here after the fall of the shah. Now, Chinese buyers are following Russians and Kazakhs who have spent millions to get an address that estate agents say is as famous as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions were for sale for £65m and £38m. They offered Italian marble, lifts with leather padding, and luxury panic rooms.

But in the gardens of the empty mansions, stone fountains are breaking and lawns have turned into muddy swamps. Inside one house, water drips through a huge crystal chandelier onto a thick carpet that is rotting under parts of a collapsed ceiling. Moss grows through broken bricks. Broken mirrored tiles lie across a bathroom. The swimming pool has a foot of dirty water and flowers growing through its tiles. Wooden pieces stick out from the sauna.

The most dramatic ruin is The Towers. It is a grand mansion among many trees, including hornbeams, oaks, and limes. Pigeons fill its large halls with high ceilings. Water pours through three floors, and the walls have turned bright green with algae. The water drops into a huge, empty basement swimming pool. Unopened wooden boxes marked “bullet-proof glass” show the security worries of the previous owners.

Today, very few people live on The Bishops Avenue all the time. A security guard outside one mansion said the owners were not there. Another guard outside Royal Mansion would not say if anyone was home. A staff member at another mansion warned the Guardian about guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare businessman worth £45m, said he is one of the few people who lives there permanently.

He said, “It’s not a friendly place where you can talk over the fence. To be honest, I have never seen what my neighbours look like. Next door, a Saudi princess spent £35m on a new house and I’ve never seen her. There are about three houses that are lived in 24/7 and half of the properties are occupied three to six months a year. The other half, who knows if they come or not?”

These broken, very expensive homes show a property culture where the world’s richest people buy British property as investments. One Hyde Park, a block of flats in Knightsbridge, is another example. More than half the flats are registered with the council as empty or second homes.

Even so, people on the avenue are talking about building £5m apartments instead of £50m mansions to bring people back.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, decided to rebuild one valuable site on the avenue into 20 apartments. The plan includes a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

He said, “If you build a big house and try and sell for £30m to £40m, it won’t sell. Locals won’t buy and so you have to bring in overseas buyers.”

But using these empty homes to help solve the housing crisis still seems far away. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some of the ruined housing could become affordable homes. He said the land price would be too high.

He said, “Very wealthy people own property there. Sometimes they live in them and sometimes they don’t.”
