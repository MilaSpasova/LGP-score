One beautiful summer evening, I decided to swim near Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland. I moved there in 2012. A woman was in the water with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty often swims with a group of people and seems close to them.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Hundreds of people have swum with her, so she can seem tame. She was even in an Irish tourist ad where a girl says she wants to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty’s tummy. It looked tempting. About 20 tourists and local people were watching from the pier. Soon after I got in the water, Dusty left the woman and became wild. Later I learned she can be very protective of her space when she is with someone. Her tail was flapping hard. At first I thought it was a show, but then I realized she might be angry.

I knew I had to get out, so I swam to the pier. But almost instantly, Dusty crashed into me with her nose. It was very strong and very painful. I was thrown forward.

People on the pier stared at me in shock. Dusty stayed beside me, still flapping her tail. That scared me most. I thought if she hit me with her tail, I could go under the water and die.

I reached the pier, but I could not climb out because I was hurt. I felt terrible fear. I yelled for help. A man reached in and pulled me onto the steps. Then another man came and said he was an orthopaedic surgeon who worked with marine injuries. He had been driving into Doolin and saw what was happening. I was very cold and very worried. My biggest fear was bleeding inside my body. He checked me and told me he could not feel signs of that, but he thought I had broken bones. Later I found out I had six fractures in my spine, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in the hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for weeks. I could not work for five months, and I had trouble moving, plus stiffness and pain. Then doctors said I had post-traumatic stress. After almost dying, I felt anxious about everything and reacted more strongly than before. I felt like people were looking at me the wrong way. Loud noises were hard for me, and I had memory loss.

Three months before the accident, I opened a health-food shop. But I had to give it up because I could not work anymore. It was the hardest year, but now it is in the past. I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building my own osteopathy practice. I understand patients better because I have been one.

I am thankful I am healthy, and I want to stop other people from getting hurt. Many people think dolphins are always safe. Who would expect a dolphin to attack a person? If a dangerous animal runs at you with its teeth showing, it looks scary. But dolphins have a wide smile, so they do not look dangerous.

I am not angry at Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her space, and she is a wild animal that can be unpredictable. People need to understand that. Many come to swim with her and do not know how dangerous it can be. My injuries were said to be among several injuries that summer.

After the man pulled me out, Dusty swam away. Then she came back and floated upright next to me, looking at me. We looked into each other’s eyes, and I felt she was sorry. She seemed different, and the anger was gone. The people on the pier were amazed. After that moment, I stopped feeling such terror. I made peace with her.
