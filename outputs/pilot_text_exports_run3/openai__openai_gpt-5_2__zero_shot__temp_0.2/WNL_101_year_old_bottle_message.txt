Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8th April, 2014, she told about an amazing moment. She received a message in a bottle, 101 years after her grandfather threw it into the Baltic Sea. People think it may be the world’s oldest message in a bottle. A museum in Germany gave it to her and is now showing it.

Erdmann, 62, said it was a big surprise. She said a man came to her door and told her he had mail from her grandfather. He said a message in a bottle had been found, and the name on the card was her grandfather’s. The visitor was a family-history researcher. He found Erdmann in Berlin after the letter was given to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg.

The bottle was a brown beer bottle. It had been in the water for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it in his catch while fishing in the Baltic Sea near the northern city of Kiel. Holger von Neuhoff, a curator at the museum, said it was the oldest bottled message he had seen. He said the museum has older documents, but not with the bottle. With both the bottle and the document, this was the oldest at the moment. He said it was in very good condition.

Researchers think Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913. He was hiking with a nature group. He was 20 years old then.

Most of the postcard could not be read. But people could read the address in Berlin on the front. They could also read a polite request asking the finder to send the note to his home address.

Erdmann said her grandfather also put two stamps in the bottle. This was so the finder would not have to pay. But he did not think it would take 101 years.

Erdmann said she was very touched by the message. She did not know her grandfather because he died at age 54, six years before she was born.

She said she knew very little about him. But she learned he was a writer who was open-minded. He believed in freedom and that everyone should respect each other. She said he did a lot for young people, and later traveled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful because she could see where her family came from.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and traveling, like her grandfather. She said she is open-minded too. She said what he taught his two daughters, her mother taught her, and she has taught it to her sons.

Even though she was happy, she said she hoped other people would not throw bottles with messages into the sea. She said the sea today has too many bottles and too much rubbish.

The message and bottle were to be shown at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts would try to read the rest of the text. It was not clear what would happen to the bottle later, but Erdmann hoped it would stay at the museum.

She said they wanted to add some photos with the bottle, so visitors could see the young man who threw it into the water.
