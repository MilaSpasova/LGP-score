Brazil’s new funk pop star, Anitta, has become very famous. She brought music from the favelas into the mainstream. But people are arguing about her skin colour.

Some anti-discrimination groups and writers say she had to give up looking black to sell music to a mostly white, middle-class audience.

The argument started after “before and after” photos were shared. They show her skin looking much lighter after she signed a deal with Warner. In the older photo, when she was not well known, she looked darker. In the newer marketing photo, after she became famous, she looks paler. Some people think it could be whitening products or cosmetic surgery, but others think it is more likely Photoshop. The photos made people talk again about whether you need to be pale to succeed in Brazil.

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote online that the change shows hidden discrimination in media and marketing. She said many people do not want to admit racism and say Brazil is a “multiracial democracy,” but statistics show this is not true. She said society often connects white skin with being rich, nice, successful, and good, and sees black skin as the opposite. She also said famous people can feel forced to change things like curly hair or a big nose, which can hurt self-esteem.

Anitta was born Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

She became very popular with an album and her hit song “Show das Poderosas.” The song reached the top of the charts and got 52 million views on YouTube.

Many fans love her as a pop idol with catchy songs. People who support her also show her as a link between the mostly black and mixed-race shanty towns on Rio’s hills and the richer, whiter areas below.

She has changed baile funk to fit a wider audience. She reduced sexual dancing, gang references, and explicit lyrics. Now some people ask if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract a richer group of customers.

This topic is sensitive in Brazil, where many people are mixed-race. Brazil was one of the last big countries to ban slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. In Brazil, colour often matters more than race or ancestry. Even though the country aims to be a multiracial democracy, skin tone is clearly linked to inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Up to 2011, black and mixed-race students also spent two fewer years in school on average.

The government says the gap is getting smaller because of university quota systems and other affirmative action. But big differences remain. Most business and government leaders are white, while many low-paid jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In rich areas like Ipanema and Gávea, it is common to see black nannies pushing strollers with white toddlers.

Colour is also hard to define. In the 2010 census, people who called themselves white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million said black, two million Asian, and 0.5% indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology lecturer at the Federal University of Pernambuco, said Anitta won middle-class fans by taking a rebellious style and making it softer and easier for everyone to accept. He said this was not because of racial whitening. He said it was because her music moved from the “periphery” to the “centre” of society.

Other people say the colour issue is exaggerated. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who worked with Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot, but not her colour. He said it is more about makeup, hairstylists, and clothing. He also said he does not think she was pressured. He said she always liked singing, dancing, and showing off, and that this natural style helped her succeed.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador, said the problem is not the public, but music producers and media leaders. He said people showed they care about music itself by choosing Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, as the recent winner of the talent show The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for comment. But in a recent interview, she said identity should be defined by the person. She said an “all-powerful” woman does not need to be beautiful, but can be powerful through attitude. She said her message is that we can be who we want.
