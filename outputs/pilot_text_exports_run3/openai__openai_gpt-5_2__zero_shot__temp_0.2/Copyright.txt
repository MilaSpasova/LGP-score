Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is theft. He says it is like putting your hand in someone’s pocket and taking their wallet. In an article for Index on Censorship, Pullman supports copyright laws. He criticizes internet users who think it is OK to download music or books without paying.

Pullman writes that the technology is so impressive that people do not notice the wrong thing they are doing. He says it is wrong that someone can steal an artist’s work and not get punished.

His article came after leaders from the music industry met British Prime Minister David Cameron at Downing Street. They talked about web piracy.

Pullman is the writer of the His Dark Materials trilogy. He says authors and musicians can be poor and unknown for years while they work. He says that when they finally create work that audiences enjoy, their chance to earn a living can be taken away by illegal downloading. He says the rule is simple: if we enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

Pullman is writing in the next issue of the magazine as part of a dialogue with Cathy Casserly. She is the chief executive of Creative Commons. Creative Commons offers open content licences that let creators choose how to use copyright. Casserly says copyright has many problems because it was made in an “analogue age.” She says copyright can stop people from sharing, building on, and remixing other people’s work.

Casserly says artists need to think of new ways to share and make money from their work. She quotes science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees that there are problems. The editor, Rachael Jolley, says today’s copyright laws do not work well in the digital age and could turn consumers into criminals. She says we need new ways for artists, writers, and musicians to earn money.

Illegal downloading is very common. Ofcom collected data for the UK from November 2012 to January 2013. It suggests that 280 million music tracks were pirated. Also, 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files were pirated.

Ofcom says 18% of internet users aged over 12 admit they have recently pirated content. It says 9% fear getting caught. Pullman also writes that it is still surprising how easy and fast it is to get music as MP3 downloads.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron appointed Conservative MP Mike Weatherley as an adviser on this topic. A spokesman for the BPI, the record industry trade body, said Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creators it protects. The spokesman said they hope Weatherley and the prime minister’s support will influence the UK’s intellectual property office.
