From glow-in-the-dark trees to underground bike storage and solar-powered bins, people are trying unusual ideas to make cities nicer places to live.

Living in a city has good points, but it can be hard to live in a clean, healthy way. People face pollution, heavy traffic, and fewer green spaces. So inventors are trying new ideas to improve city life. Here are some of them.

Pop-up parks
Some cities seem built more for cars than for people. A pop-up park is a simple idea. You take an empty parking space, a little money, and a few plants, and you make a small park. The PARK(ing) project started as an art experiment in San Francisco. It later spread around the world. People have also made temporary city farms and nature demonstrations. The movement has a day of action every September.

Subterranean storage
Not everyone in a city uses a car. Bicycles are becoming more popular. But people need a safe place to keep them. A Tokyo engineering company called Giken designed an underground bicycle park called an “eco-cycle, anti-seismic underground bicycle park.” It is a round storage space that is seven metres wide. It goes deep into the ground and can hold 204 bikes. Owners press a button, and the system brings the bike back above ground in about 13 seconds.

Glow-in-the-dark trees
Many people think of Christmas lights when they think of things that glow. But Dutch designer-artist Daan Roosegaarde made a “bioluminescent” plant. The experimental technology mixes DNA from glowing sea bacteria with part of a plant to make a glow like a jellyfish. Tests are happening to make a large-scale version. Roosegaarde hopes it could one day replace normal street lights.

Footstep power
In West Ham, east London, many commuters and shoppers cross a raised walkway near the underground station. They may feel the ground is a bit springy. Under their feet, smart tiles move about five millimetres. These tiles capture energy from footsteps and turn it into electricity to power streetlights. The UK company Pavegen made this system. Pavegen has also installed a similar system at Heathrow Airport in London and in other places around the world.

Supertrees
In Singapore, Gardens by the Bay has man-made “supertrees.” They are steel-framed and can be up to 50 metres tall. Flowers and ferns grow on them. Their metal tops help absorb and spread out heat. They also collect rainwater and help with air ventilation for two climate-controlled biomes below (large conservatories). Eleven of the 18 trees also have solar panels on their “branches.”
