This invention could be as important as Trevor Baylis’s wind-up radio in the 1990s, before the internet. It is a cheap light that gets free power from gravity. It could help stop the use of dangerous kerosene lamps in Africa and India.

A British designer named Patrick Hunt tried to get money from banks or venture capital to start selling his invention. But he had trouble. He said funding was slow and complicated, and people did not want to take a risk on something new.

So he tried crowdfunding on a US website called Indiegogo, which had just opened in the UK. In five days, he reached his goal and raised £36,200. By the end of 2012, after 40 days, he had raised £400,000.

The LED light uses a dynamo. It is powered by a 10kg bag of rocks that slowly drops down. The weight is attached to the light and lifted to about 2m high. As it slowly falls to the ground, it makes enough power for 30 minutes of light.

Hunt is getting ready to make the lights in China. He will test the market again by sending 1,000 lights to Africa before making them in much larger numbers. He hopes to make millions of units.

Hunt is part of a new group of entrepreneurs who use crowdfunding to get money. Another new site is InvestingZone, which connects rich people with start-up entrepreneurs.

Indiegogo does not give people shares in a business. Instead, it lets campaign owners offer “perks” for different donation amounts. People who helped fund Hunt’s light felt good about helping poorer people, and they also got their own light.

Danae Ringelmann, a co-founder of Indiegogo, says the “gravity light” shows how crowdfunding can work. She says it can test an idea quickly. She says it is not about knowing people at a bank or a venture capital firm. It is about showing customers and fans that the idea is good, so they support it and pay for it.

She also says even ideas that do not get funded are still worth testing, because it saves time and helps people learn sooner.

Ringelmann lives in the US and started as a Wall Street analyst. In 2008, she quit to help friends in the arts raise money. The site first focused on films and launched at the Sundance Film Festival that year.

Five years later, Indiegogo was raising about $2m a week for new businesses that are starting or growing. In December 2012, it added euro and sterling services to grow in Europe. It says Britain is its third biggest market. It also says international activity is up 41% since December.

Indiegogo has many competitors, like Kickstarter, Seedrs, and Funding Circle. Ringelmann says Indiegogo is different because anyone can start a campaign. No project is seen as too strange.

Indiegogo charges a 4% fee for successful campaigns. If a campaign does not reach its target, users can choose to return all the money to donors for no charge, or keep the money raised and pay a 9% fee.

A British woman named Lauren Pears raised £100,000 on the site to open a “cat café” in London. It is called Lady Dinah’s Cat Emporium. It has not opened yet, but it is described as a place where people can sit in a comfortable wingback chair, drink hot tea, read a book, and be with a cat.

Ringelmann says some campaigns are rejected by venture capitalists because they are not sure people really want the product. She says entrepreneurs can run an Indiegogo campaign, even before launching the project, and the campaign can show proof that there is a market.

She says crowdfunding lets people test the market, price, and features, find new ways to earn money, and get important feedback.

Ringelmann says there are many ideas, but success depends on doing the work well. She says if you are afraid someone will steal your idea and do it better and faster, then you may not be the right person to do it. She says it is important to be confident, move fast, and learn.

Ringelmann says expanding into Europe and making it possible to pay through local card services like Maestro in the UK and Carte Bleue in France, as well as PayPal, are part of a dream to make finance open to more people. More than 7,000 campaigns for finance are live on the site.

Crowdfunding has grown as a choice besides banks, but it is not always attractive to very rich investors. Many of them want a stake in a business. That could start to change in the UK with the launch of InvestingZone.
