When the lottery balls drop and all six numbers match, some people think it is time to buy an Audi, book a holiday in the US, and call an estate agent. An analysis says many lottery millionaires do things like this.

Since it started in 1994, the UK National Lottery has made 3,000 millionaires. Together they have won more than £8.5bn in total. On average, each winner got £2.8m. A study by Oxford Economics says their money also helped make 3,780 more millionaires among their children, family, and friends.

Most winners (59%) stop working straight away. But 19% keep doing their normal job, and 31% do unpaid voluntary work. The study says 98% of winners’ spending stayed in the UK. It is estimated that each winner’s spending keeps six people in full-time jobs for a year.

The winners added almost £750m to GDP and created more than £500m in tax money for the Exchequer. Most of the money went on property. Winners spent £2.72bn on their main homes, and £170m on paying off debts and mortgages.

Many winners wanted to keep a steady income, so they spent £2.125bn on investments. They gave £1.17bn as gifts to family and friends. They spent £680m on cars and holidays.

Camelot, the company that runs the UK National Lottery, asked for the study to mark the 3,000-winners milestone. The study used research from 100 winners who won £1m or more. It found that the 3,000 winners bought 7,958 houses or flats in the UK, about 2.7 each, spending £3.3bn. Most winners (82%) moved to a new main home, spending an average of £900,000.

Many winners added extra features at home. Almost a third (29%) bought a hot tub. A walk-in wardrobe was important for 28%. Almost a quarter (24%) chose a home behind electric gates. Some (22%) had a games room, and 7% put in a snooker table.

Bigger homes need care. About 30% hired a cleaner and 24% hired a gardener. A small number (5%) hired a beautician.

Audi was the favourite car for 16% of winners. Range Rovers and BMWs were also popular (11% each), plus Mercedes (10%) and Land Rovers (5%). Winners spent £463m on 17,190 cars. The average price of their favourite car was £46,116.

Holidays were also important. Most winners (68%) chose five-star hotels abroad. The US was the top place to go for 27%, followed by the Caribbean (9%). Some winners also bought caravans in the UK. Over the past 18 years, 10% of millionaires bought a caravan, creating sales of about £7.4m.

Some winners (15%) started their own business. Some (9%) helped other people start a business, and 6% invested in or bought other people’s businesses. The study says businesses started or supported by winners employ 3,195 people.

Andy Logan, who wrote the report, said a win affects more people than expected. He said it changes the lives of friends and family, and it also affects the UK economy, especially because so much money is spent in the UK. He said each win creates a “ripple effect” for this generation and often the next.
