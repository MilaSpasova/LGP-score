Kenton Cool usually likes to talk, but now he can hardly speak. Climbing high mountains has made his voice rough. He is in Kathmandu, the capital of Nepal. That morning he flew down from Everest base camp.

Cool is thinking about an amazing set of climbs he finished over the weekend. Early on Saturday morning, he reached the top of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of three main summits in the Everest “horseshoe.” This horseshoe surrounds an icy valley called the Western Cwm.

That same day, Cool climbed to the top of Everest. He reached the summit in complete darkness early on Sunday. Then he and his climbing partner went on to the top of Lhotse on Monday morning. Lhotse is the third peak in this three-peak challenge.

“It was a snatched opportunity,” he says. “For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. That does not take away the physical achievement of what I did. I’ve set the bar at a certain level. But whoever comes along next will move the bar further and do it without ropes or bottled oxygen.”

Sixty years after Everest was first climbed, many stories look back to Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. People compare their time with today’s world of commercialism and damage to the environment. I ask Cool to look ahead and imagine what top climbers might do 60 years from now.

“I hate to think,” he says. He talks about the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. Steck left the mountain in April after what Cool calls “an altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck planned a big climb: he wanted to climb Everest’s west ridge (first climbed in 1963), go down to the South Col, and then immediately climb Lhotse by a new route. He wanted to do all of this without fixed ropes. “Ueli had been training like a machine,” Cool says. “He’s a climber in a class all his own. He’s technically brilliant, and he had also taken his physical condition to an astronomic level. It would have been amazing to see what he could have done.”

Tourism is a different question. One clue is a helicopter rescue done by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro’s angry words helped cause the conflict at Camp 2. Moro flew back to Everest on Tuesday in a powerful helicopter. He rescued a sick or hurt climber at 7,800 metres.

This was the highest rescue ever done on Everest. It shows how fast helicopter flights have increased in recent years. By 2073, there might even be a helipad on the South Col. Tourists could be brought there while breathing bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing rescue options for both climbers and the many trekkers who go as far as base camp.

Mountain geographer and environmentalist Alton Byers says it is not clear if the Everest region can keep handling fast-growing tourism. He says climate change and tourism together are creating new pressures on the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers in the Everest region are shrinking, and some are disappearing. This is already causing big problems. “Everywhere you go, people are talking about how there’s less water. There’s less water for agriculture and less water for all the new lodges that are getting built.”

Byers says that in the Sherpa town of Namche Bazaar, workers are building a new five-mile pipeline to bring in water. It is needed because more tourists want showers and flush toilets. The local stream has been polluted by human waste. It also does not provide enough water for a town that is extremely crowded in the high season. “Every village is digging a pit just beyond the houses for garbage. Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world,” he says. Human waste at base camp is now handled well and taken away in plastic barrels. But Byers says the barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley, and it could leak into the region’s water.

“These problems can be solved, but we need to get serious about it,” he says. “One climber can spend $85,000 climbing Everest. And that’s fine. But at some point we’re going to have to address these other priorities. For half a million dollars a year, you could solve most of them.”

Climate change is harder. Byers works with local conservation groups to plan for climate change. They often look for new water sources or collect rainwater. Another danger is that glacial lakes are growing quickly. Some could burst and flood the Sherpa homeland. “There’s going to come a time when people are going to have to get out of their way.”

Changing weather also affects tourism. There are more clouds during times that are usually clear. This closes Lukla Airport more often. Lukla Airport is the main entrance to the Everest region. A new road for 4x4 vehicles is being built to Lukla so tourists and their money can still arrive. But Byers worries that roads in Nepal are being built cheaply and quickly. He says this can cause serious soil erosion and landslides.

“Everest is the icon everyone knows,” he says. “It’s the canary in the coalmine that everyone understands. It’s the perfect laboratory for figuring out how to address some of these problems, like the impacts of climate change and tourism.”
