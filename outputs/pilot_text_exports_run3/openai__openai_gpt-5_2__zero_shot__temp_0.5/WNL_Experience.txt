It was a nice summer evening. I decided to swim near Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland. I moved there in 2012. A woman was in the water with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty often swims with a group of people.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Since then, hundreds of people have swum with her. This makes her seem very friendly and tame. She was even in an Irish tourist ad where a girl says she wants to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty’s tummy. It looked safe and fun. About 20 tourists and local people were watching from the pier. Soon after I got into the water, Dusty left the woman and suddenly became very wild. Later I learned she can be very protective of her space when she is with someone. Her tail was moving fast. At first I thought she was just showing off, but then I realized she might be angry.

I knew I had to get out. I swam toward the pier, but almost instantly Dusty hit me with her nose. It was very strong and very painful. I was pushed forward.

People on the pier stared at me in shock. Dusty was still next to me, with her tail moving crazily. That was the scariest part. I thought, if she hits me with her tail, I could go under the water.

I reached the pier but I could not climb out because I was hurt. I felt terrified. I shouted for help. A man reached down and pulled me out onto the steps. Then another man came and said he was an orthopaedic surgeon who worked with marine injuries. He had been driving into Doolin when he saw what was happening.

I was very cold and very worried. I did not know how bad my injuries were, and I was most afraid of bleeding inside my body. The surgeon checked me and said he could not feel signs of internal bleeding, but he thought I probably had broken bones. Later I learned I had six fractures in my spine, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for several weeks. I could not work for five months, and I had trouble moving, with stiffness and pain. Then doctors said I had post-traumatic stress. The near-death experience made me anxious and made me react strongly. I felt like people were looking at me the wrong way. Loud noises were hard for me. I also had memory loss.

Three months before the accident, I had opened a health-food shop. But I had to give it up because I could not work anymore. That year was the hardest year, but now it is behind me. I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building my own osteopathy practice. I understand patients better because I have been a patient.

I am thankful that I am healthy, and I want to stop other people from getting hurt. People often think dolphins are always safe and friendly. Who would think a dolphin would attack a person? If you see a dangerous animal coming at you with its teeth showing, you feel scared. But dolphins have a wide smile, so they can seem harmless.

I am not angry at Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her area, and she is a wild animal that can be unpredictable. People need to know this. Many people come to swim with her and do not understand how dangerous it can be. My injuries were reported to be among several injuries that summer.

After the man pulled me out, Dusty swam away. Then she came back and floated upright next to me, looking at me. We looked into each other’s eyes. I felt she was sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin, and her anger was gone. The people on the pier were amazed. After that moment, I stopped feeling terror. I made peace with her.
