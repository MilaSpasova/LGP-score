From glow-in-the-dark trees to bike storage under the ground and solar-powered bins, people are trying unusual ideas to make cities better places to live.

Living in a city has good points, but living in a way that is good for the planet is getting harder. Pollution, traffic, and less green space are problems many people face every day.

Now inventors are trying new ideas. Here are some of the wackier solutions to help make cities more livable.

Pop-up parks
Some cities seem built more for cars than for people. A pop-up park is a simple idea. You take an empty parking space, a little money, and a plant or two, and you make a small park for a short time. The PARK(ing) project started as an art experiment in San Francisco and then spread around the world. People have also made temporary city farms and ecology demonstrations. The movement has a day of action every September.

Subterranean storage
Not everyone in cities uses a car. Bikes are becoming more popular. But where can you keep a bike safe? A Tokyo engineering company called Giken made a plan for an “eco-cycle, anti-seismic underground bicycle park.” It is a cylinder that is only seven metres wide, and it goes deep into the ground. It can hold 204 bikes. Owners can get their bike back by pressing a button. The system brings the bike up to the surface in about 13 seconds.

Glow-in-the-dark trees
When people think of things that glow on trees, they may think of Christmas decorations. But Daan Roosegaarde thinks of something different. This Dutch designer-artist made a “bioluminescent” plant. The experimental technology mixes DNA from glowing marine bacteria with part of a plant to make it glow like a jellyfish. Tests are happening to make a large-scale version. Roosegaarde hopes it could one day replace normal street lights.

Footfall harvesting
In West Ham in east London, many commuters and shoppers cross a raised walkway near the underground station each day. Most people do not notice that the ground feels a little springy. Even fewer people know that a five-millimetre bend in the rubber surface helps power the streetlights above. The walkway has smart tiles that capture energy from footsteps and turn it into electricity. Pavegen, the UK company that made it, has also put a similar system in London’s Heathrow Airport and other places.

Supertrees
People have now made man-made trees. Singapore’s Gardens by the Bay has a small group of them. These steel “supertrees” can be up to 50 metres high. Flowers and ferns grow on them. Their metal tops can also take in and spread out heat. They can collect rainwater and help move air for two “climate-controlled biomes” below, which are large conservatories. Eleven of the 18 trees also have solar panels on their “branches.”
