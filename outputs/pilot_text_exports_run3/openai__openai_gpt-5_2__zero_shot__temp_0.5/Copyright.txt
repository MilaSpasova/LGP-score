Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is theft. He says it is like putting your hand in someone’s pocket and taking their wallet. Pullman wrote this in an article for Index on Censorship. He is president of the Society of Authors. He strongly supports copyright laws. He criticizes internet users who download music or books without paying.

Pullman writes that new technology looks amazing, but people do not see that what they are doing is wrong. He says it is unfair that someone can steal an artist’s work and not get punished.

His article came after leaders from the music industry met British Prime Minister David Cameron at Downing Street. They talked about web piracy.

Pullman, who wrote the His Dark Materials trilogy, says writers and musicians often work for years with little money and little attention. He says that when their work finally becomes popular, they can lose the chance to earn a living because of illegal downloading. He says the rule is simple: if we enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

In the next issue of the magazine, Pullman is in a dialogue with Cathy Casserly. She is chief executive of Creative Commons. Creative Commons offers open content licences that let creators choose how copyright is used. Casserly says copyright has many problems because it was made in an “analogue age.” She says copyright can stop people from sharing, building on, and remixing other people’s work.

Casserly says artists should think of new ways to share and make money from their work. She quotes science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees with her. The editor, Rachael Jolley, says current copyright laws do not work well in the digital age. She says they could turn consumers into criminals. She says new ways are needed to help artists, writers, and musicians earn money.

Many people download illegally. Ofcom, the UK communications regulator, collected data for the UK from November 2012 to January 2013. It suggests 280 million music tracks were pirated. Also pirated were 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files.

Ofcom says 18% of internet users aged over 12 admit they recently pirated content. It also says 9% fear getting caught. Pullman writes that it is still surprising how fast people can get music as MP3 downloads, especially for people who have been building up their iTunes list for some time.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron appointed Conservative MP Mike Weatherley as his adviser on the issue. A spokesman for the BPI, the record industry trade body, said Weatherley supports copyright and the artists and creative producers it protects. The spokesman said they hope Weatherley and the prime minister will support copyright in the work of the UK’s intellectual property office.
