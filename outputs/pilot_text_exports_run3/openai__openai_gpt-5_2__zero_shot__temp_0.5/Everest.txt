Kenton Cool usually likes to talk, but now he can hardly speak. Working hard high on the mountains has made his voice rough. He is in Kathmandu, the capital of Nepal. That morning he flew down from Everest base camp.

Cool is thinking about three big climbs he finished last weekend. Early on Saturday morning, he reached the top of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of the three main summits in the Everest “horseshoe.” This horseshoe surrounds an icy valley called the Western Cwm.

That same day, he climbed to the top of Everest. He reached the summit in complete darkness early on Sunday. Then he and his climbing partner kept going. On Monday morning, they reached the summit of Lhotse, the third peak in this three-peaks challenge.

“It was a snatched opportunity,” he says. “For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. That does not take away the physical achievement of what I did. I have set the bar at a certain level. But whoever comes along next will move the bar further and do it without ropes or bottled oxygen.”

Sixty years after Everest was first climbed, many stories look back to Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. People talk about their more innocent time, before modern commercialism and damage to the environment. I ask Cool to look ahead and imagine what top climbers might do 60 years from now.

“I hate to think,” he says. He talks about the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. In April, Steck left the mountain after what Cool calls “an altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck planned to climb Everest’s west ridge, first climbed in 1963. Then he planned to go down to the South Col and immediately climb Lhotse by a new route. He planned to do all of this without fixed ropes. “Ueli had been training like a machine,” Cool says. “He is a climber in a class all his own. He is technically brilliant, and he had also taken his physical condition to an astronomic level. It would have been amazing to see what he could have done.”

What tourism will look like is another question. One clue is a helicopter rescue done by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro’s angry words helped start the confrontation at Camp 2. Moro flew back to Everest on Tuesday in a powerful helicopter. He rescued a sick climber at 7,800 metres.

It was the highest rescue yet on Everest. It also shows the fast rise in helicopter flights in recent years. By 2073, the mountain could even have a helipad on the South Col. Tourists could arrive there while breathing bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing rescue for both climbers and the many trekkers who go only as far as base camp.

Alton Byers is a mountain geographer and environmentalist. He says it is not clear if the Everest region can keep handling fast-growing tourism. He says climate change and tourism together are putting new stress on the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers in the region are pulling back, and some are disappearing. This is already causing big effects. “Everywhere you go, people are talking about how there’s less water,” he says. “There’s less water for agriculture and less water for all the new lodges that are getting built.”

Byers says that in the Sherpa town of Namche Bazaar, workers are building a new five-mile pipeline to bring water. This is to meet the growing tourist demand for showers and flush toilets. The local stream is now polluted with human waste. It also does not have enough water for a town that, in high season, is extremely crowded. “Every village is digging a pit just beyond the houses for garbage. Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world,” he says. Human waste at base camp is now handled well and taken away in plastic barrels. But Byers says these barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley. That pit could leak into the region’s waterways.

“These problems can be solved, but we need to get serious about it,” he says. “One climber can spend $85,000 climbing Everest. And that’s fine. But at some point we are going to have to address these other priorities. For half a million dollars a year, you could solve most of them.”

Climate change is a different problem. Byers works with local conservation groups to find and plan for climate change effects. Most often, they look for new water sources or start collecting rainwater. Another danger is the fast growth of glacial lakes. These lakes could burst and flood the Sherpa homeland. “There’s going to come a time when people are going to have to get out of their way,” he says.

Changing weather is also affecting tourism. More clouds during times that are usually clear are closing Lukla Airport more often. Lukla Airport is the main entry point to the Everest region. A new road for 4x4 vehicles is being built to Lukla to make sure tourists and their money keep coming. But Byers worries that Nepal is building roads too cheaply. He says this could lead to serious soil erosion and landslides.

“Everest is the icon everyone knows,” he says. “It’s the canary in the coalmine that everyone understands. It’s the perfect laboratory for figuring out how to address some of these problems, like the impacts of climate change and tourism.”
