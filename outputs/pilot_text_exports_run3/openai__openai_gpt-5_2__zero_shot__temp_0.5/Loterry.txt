When the lottery balls fall and all six numbers match, some people start spending. An analysis says many lottery millionaires buy a new car, book a holiday in the US, and call an estate agent.

The UK National Lottery started in 1994. Since then, it has made 3,000 millionaires. Together, they have won more than £8.5bn in total. On average, each winner got £2.8m. The study says their money also helped make 3,780 more millionaires among their children, family, and friends. The study was by Oxford Economics.

Most winners (59%) stop working right away. But 19% keep doing their normal job, and 31% do unpaid voluntary work. The study says 98% of winners’ spending stayed in the UK. It estimates that each winner’s spending on homes, vehicles, and holidays keeps six people in full-time jobs for one year.

The study says winners added almost £750m to GDP and created more than £500m in tax money for the Exchequer. Most of the money went on property. Winners spent £2.72bn on their main homes, and £170m paying off debt and mortgages.

Winners also wanted to keep an income coming in, so they spent £2.125bn on investments. They gave £1.17bn as gifts to family and friends. They spent £680m on cars and holidays.

Camelot, which runs the UK National Lottery, paid for the study to mark the 3,000-winners milestone. It used research from 100 winners who won £1m or more. It found that all 3,000 winners together bought 7,958 houses or flats in the UK, about 2.7 each, spending £3.3bn. Most winners (82%) moved to a new main home and spent about £900,000 on average.

Many winners upgraded their homes. Almost a third (29%) bought a hot tub. 28% wanted a walk-in wardrobe. 24% chose a home behind electric gates. 22% had a games room, and 7% put in a snooker table.

Bigger homes need help to look after them. 30% hired a cleaner and 24% hired a gardener. A small number (5%) hired a beautician.

Audi was the favourite car for 16% of winners. Range Rovers and BMWs were also popular (11% each), and so were Mercedes (10%) and Land Rovers (5%). Winners spent £463m on 17,190 cars. The average price of their favourite car was £46,116.

Holidays were important too. Most winners (68%) chose five-star hotels overseas. The US was the top place to visit for 27%, followed by the Caribbean (9%). In the UK, caravan sales also grew. Over the past 18 years, 10% of millionaires bought a caravan, creating sales of about £7.4m.

Some winners (15%) started their own business. 9% helped other people start one, and 6% invested in or bought other people’s businesses. The study says businesses started or supported by lottery winners employ 3,195 people.

Andy Logan, who wrote the report, said a win affects more people than expected. He said it can change the lives of friends and family, and it also affects the UK economy because so much money is spent in the UK. He said each win can create a “ripple effect” for this generation and often the next one.
