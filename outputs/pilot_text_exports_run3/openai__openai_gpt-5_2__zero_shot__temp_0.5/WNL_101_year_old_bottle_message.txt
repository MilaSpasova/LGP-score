Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8th April, 2014, she talked about a special moment. She received a message in a bottle. Her grandfather had thrown the bottle into the Baltic Sea in 1913, and it was found 101 years later. People think it may be the world’s oldest message in a bottle. A museum in Germany showed it to Erdmann, and the museum is now displaying it.

Erdmann, who was 62, said it was very surprising. A man came to her door and said he had mail from her grandfather. He explained that a message in a bottle had been found, and the name on the card was her grandfather’s. The visitor was a family-history researcher. He found Erdmann in Berlin after the letter was given to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg.

The bottle was a brown beer bottle. It had been in the water for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it while fishing in the Baltic Sea near Kiel. Holger von Neuhoff, a museum curator, said it was the oldest bottled message he had seen. He said the museum has older documents without bottles, but this bottle and its document are the oldest pair for now. He said it was in very good condition.

Researchers believe Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913 while hiking with a nature group. He was 20 years old.

Most of the postcard could not be read. But people could read the Berlin address on the front. They could also read a polite request asking the finder to send the note to his home address.

Erdmann said her grandfather also put two stamps in the bottle, so the finder would not have to pay. She said he did not think it would take 101 years.

She said the message made her feel emotional. She did not know her grandfather because he died at age 54, six years before she was born.

She said she knew little about him, but she learned he was a writer. She said he was open-minded, believed in freedom, and thought people should respect each other. She said he did a lot for young people and later travelled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful to learn more about her family.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and travelling, and she is open-minded too. She said what her grandfather taught his daughters was taught to her by her mother, and she has taught it to her sons. But she said she hopes people today will not throw message bottles into the sea, because the sea already has too many bottles and too much rubbish.

The message and the bottle were going to be on display at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts would try to read the rest of the text. It was not clear what would happen to the bottle later, but Erdmann hoped it would stay in the museum.

She said they wanted to add some photos with the bottle, so visitors could see the young man who threw it into the water.
