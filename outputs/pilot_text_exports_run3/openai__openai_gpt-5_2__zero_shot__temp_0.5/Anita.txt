Brazil’s new funk star, Anitta, has become very popular. She brought the sound of the favela into the mainstream. But people are now debating her skin colour.

Some anti-discrimination groups and writers say Anitta had to give up showing her “blackness” to succeed in the mostly white, middle-class market.

The debate grew after “before and after” photos were shared. In an older photo, when she was not famous, she looked darker. In a newer marketing photo, taken after she became famous and signed with Warner, she looks lighter. People asked if this change was from whitening products, cosmetic surgery, or more likely Photoshop. The photos made people talk again about whether you need to look pale to succeed in Brazil.

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote a post criticizing hidden discrimination in media and advertising. She said many people do not want to admit racism. She said the “whitening” shows a society that does not support diversity. She also said people often connect being white with being rich, successful, and good, and connect being black with the opposite.

Anitta’s real name is Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

She became very famous with an album and a big hit song, Show das Poderosas. The song reached number one and got 52 million views on YouTube.

Many people love her as a pop idol with catchy songs and a strong message. Her supporters also present her as a bridge between the mostly black and mixed-race shanty towns on Rio’s hills and the wealthier, whiter areas below.

She changed her style to fit a wider audience. She reduced the suggestive dancing, gangsta references, and explicit lyrics of baile funk. Now some people ask if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract a richer audience. Arraes said pop stars may feel forced to straighten curly hair or get nose surgery. She said this can hurt self-esteem.

This topic is sensitive in Brazil, a country with many mixed-race people. Brazil was one of the last large countries to end slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. But in Brazil, skin colour can matter more than race or ancestry. There is also a clear link between skin tone and inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Until 2011, black or mixed-race students spent about two fewer years in school on average.

The government says the gap is getting smaller because of university quotas and other affirmative action. But the difference is still easy to see. Most business and government leaders are white, while most low-paid jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In rich areas like Ipanema and Gávea, it is common to see black nannies pushing strollers with white toddlers.

Colour and identity can be hard to define. In the 2010 census, people who called themselves white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million said black, two million said Asian, and 0.5% said indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology lecturer at the Federal University of Pernambuco, said Anitta won over the middle class by making a rebellious sound calmer and easier for everyone to accept. He said this was not “racial whitening.” He said it was a change in where she made her art: from the periphery to the centre.

Others also say the colour issue is being made too big. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who worked with Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot, but not her colour. He said the change came from makeup, hairstylists, and clothing. He said it was not because of pressure. He said she always liked to sing, dance, and show herself, and that this natural style helped her succeed.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador, said the prejudice was not mainly from the public, but from music producers and media leaders. He said the public showed they could choose music for its own sake when they picked Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, as the recent winner of The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for a comment. But in a recent interview, she said identity should be defined by the person. She said a powerful woman does not need to be beautiful, but has strong attitude. She said her message is that people can be who they want.
