A thick layer of bird droppings covers a shiny railing at one of Britain’s most expensive homes. There are pigeon bones, broken mirrors, and water running through broken parts of the building. This home is called The Towers. It is a £30m palace on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London. Until now, people did not know how badly it was falling apart.

The Towers is one of ten big houses in the middle of The Bishops Avenue. This road is a famous and very expensive place in London. These houses have been almost empty since they were bought about 25 years ago. People believe they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. The houses have columns that are cracking. Swimming pools with mosaic tiles are full of rubble. Nature has taken over so much that owls have moved in.

This empty and ruined look is seen along the whole road. Lloyds Bank says The Bishops Avenue is the second most expensive street in Britain. At the same time, many people in London struggle to buy a home because prices rise by 11.2% each year. But 16 mansions on the most expensive part of the road are sitting empty. Many are behind locked gates. Their windows are covered with steel bars, and the grounds are overgrown. Guard dogs patrol the land.

Across the street is another empty mansion worth £18m, with smashed windows and paint that stops people from climbing. Another house has metal bars on the windows and was sold for £20m.

Even so, prices keep rising. Dryades, a mansion that was owned until recently by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007 and is believed to be worth about £30m now. Heath Lodge, where fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou was murdered in 1984 with a silver bullet, is worth £13m today. It was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

Seeing these empty homes can be painful for people who struggle to afford a place to live in one of the world’s most expensive cities. A security guard on the road said it was frustrating to see so much space—enough to house dozens of people—falling apart.

In the past, royals with oil wealth from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia were among the first to come to this road near Hampstead Heath. Iranians came here after the fall of the shah. Now, Chinese buyers are following Russians and Kazakhs who have spent millions to live on a road that estate agents say is as famous as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions were for sale for £65m and £38m. They promised lots of Italian marble, lifts with leather padding, and luxury panic rooms.

But on the land around the empty mansions, stone fountains are breaking and lawns have turned into muddy, wet ground. Inside one house, water drips through a huge crystal chandelier onto a thick carpet. Parts of the ceiling have fallen in, and the carpet is rotting. Moss grows through broken bricks. Mirror tiles are scattered in a bathroom. The swimming pool has about a foot of dirty, salty water, and flowers are growing through the tiles. Wooden boards in the sauna bend outward.

The most dramatic ruin is The Towers. It is a grand house among acres of hornbeams, oaks, and limes. Pigeons fill its large halls with high ceilings. The walls have turned bright green from algae. Water pours through three floors and drops into a big, empty basement swimming pool. Unopened wooden crates marked “bullet-proof glass” show that the earlier owners worried about security.

Today, very few people live on The Bishops Avenue all the time. A security guard outside one mansion said the owners were not there. Another guard outside Royal Mansion would not say if anyone was home. A staff member at another mansion warned the Guardian about the guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare mogul worth £45m, said he is one of the few people who live there permanently.

He said, “It’s not a friendly place where you chat over the fence. To be honest, I have never seen what my neighbours look like. Next door, a Saudi princess spent £35m on a new house and I’ve never seen her. There are about three houses that are lived in 24/7 and half of the properties are occupied three to six months a year. The other half, who knows if they come or not?”

These ruined mansions show a property culture where the richest people in the world treat British homes as investments. One Hyde Park, a block of flats in Knightsbridge, is another example. More than half of its flats are listed with the council as empty homes or second homes.

People on the road talk about building £5m apartments instead of £50m mansions, to try to bring people back.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, plans to rebuild one valuable site as 20 apartments. The building would have a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

He said, “If you build a big house and try and sell for £30m to £40m, it won’t sell. Locals won’t buy and so you have to bring in overseas buyers.”

But it still seems unlikely that these empty homes will be used to help solve the housing crisis. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some of the ruined homes could become affordable housing. He said the land price would be too high.

He said, “Very wealthy people own property there. Sometimes they live in them and sometimes they don’t.”
