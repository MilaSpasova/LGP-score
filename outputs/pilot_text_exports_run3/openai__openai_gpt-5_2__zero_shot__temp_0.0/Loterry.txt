When the lottery balls fall and all six numbers match, many winners start spending. They may buy an Audi, book a holiday in the US, and call an estate agent. A study says this is common for lottery millionaires.

The UK National Lottery started in 1994. Since then, it has made 3,000 millionaires. Together they have won more than £8.5bn, which is about £2.8m each on average. The study by Oxford Economics says their money also helped create 3,780 more millionaires among their children, family, and friends.

Many winners stop working right away (59%). Some keep their jobs (19%). Others do unpaid volunteer work (31%). The study says 98% of winners’ spending stayed in the UK. It estimates that each winner’s spending supports six full-time jobs for one year.

The winners added almost £750m to GDP and created more than £500m in tax money for the Exchequer. Most money was spent on homes. Winners spent £2.72bn on their main homes, and £170m to pay off debts and mortgages.

Winners also wanted to keep an income. They spent £2.125bn on investments. They gave £1.17bn as gifts to family and friends. They spent £680m on cars and holidays.

Camelot, which runs the UK National Lottery, paid for the study to mark 3,000 winners. The study used research from 100 winners who won £1m or more. It found that all 3,000 winners bought 7,958 houses or flats in the UK, or 2.7 each on average, spending £3.3bn. Most winners (82%) moved to a new main home, spending about £900,000 on average.

Some winners added special things to their homes. Almost a third (29%) bought a hot tub. A walk-in wardrobe was chosen by 28%. Nearly a quarter (24%) chose a home behind electric gates. About 22% had a games room, and 7% put in a snooker table.

Big homes need care. About 30% hired a cleaner, and 24% hired a gardener. A small number (5%) hired a beautician.

Audi was the favourite car for 16% of winners. Range Rovers and BMWs were also popular (11% each). Mercedes were chosen by 10%, and Land Rovers by 5%. Winners spent £463m on 17,190 cars. The average price of their favourite car was £46,116.

Holidays were also important. Most winners (68%) chose five-star hotels overseas. The US was the top destination (27%), followed by the Caribbean (9%). Some winners also bought caravans in the UK. Over the past 18 years, 10% of millionaires bought a caravan, creating about £7.4m in sales.

Some winners (15%) started their own business. About 9% helped other people start one, and 6% invested in or bought other people’s businesses. Businesses started or supported by winners employ 3,195 people, the study says.

Andy Logan, who wrote the report, said the effect of a win spreads further than expected. He said it changes the lives of friends and family, and it also affects the UK economy because so much money is spent in the UK. He said each win can create a ripple effect for this generation and often the next.
