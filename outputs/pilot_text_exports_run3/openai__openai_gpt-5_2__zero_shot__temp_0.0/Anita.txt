Brazil has a new funk pop star named Anitta. She became very popular by bringing music from the favelas into the mainstream. But now people are arguing about her skin colour.

Some anti-discrimination activists and writers say Anitta had to give up part of her black identity to succeed with Brazil’s mostly white middle-class audience.

The argument started after people shared old and new photos of Anitta. In the older photo, before she was famous, she looks darker. In the newer photo, taken for marketing after she became famous, she looks lighter. People do not know if this change came from skin-lightening products, cosmetic surgery, or more likely Photoshop. The photos made people talk again about whether being pale helps you get ahead in Brazil.

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote a post about what she sees as hidden discrimination in media and marketing. She said many people say they are not racist and think Brazil is a multiracial democracy, but statistics show this is not true. She said society often links white skin with being rich, nice, successful, and good, and links black skin with the opposite. She also said this kind of pressure can hurt self-esteem. She gave examples, like pop stars feeling pushed to straighten curly hair or get nose surgery.

Anitta was born Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

She later became very famous with an album and her hit song “Show das Poderosas.” The song reached number one and got 52 million views on YouTube.

Many fans like her as a pop idol with catchy songs and a strong message. Her supporters also describe her as a bridge between the mostly black and mixed-race shanty towns on Rio’s hillsides and the richer and whiter areas below.

Anitta has made baile funk less explicit. She reduced sexual dancing, gang references, and very direct lyrics. Now some people ask if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract a richer audience.

This topic is sensitive in Brazil, a country with many mixed-race people. Brazil was one of the last large countries to ban slavery. It also has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. In Brazil, colour often matters more than race and ancestry. Even though the country aims to be a multiracial democracy, skin tone is clearly linked to inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Until 2011, black or mixed-race students also spent two fewer years in school on average.

The government says this gap is shrinking because of university quotas and other affirmative action policies. But big differences remain. Most business and government leaders are white, while many low-level jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In rich areas like Ipanema and Gávea, it is more common to see black nannies pushing strollers with white toddlers than the other way around.

Colour categories in Brazil can be complicated. In the 2010 census, people who called themselves white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million said black, two million said Asian, and 0.5% said indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology teacher at the Federal University of Pernambuco, said Anitta won middle-class fans by taking a rebellious sound and making it calmer and easier for more people to accept. He said this was not racial whitening. He said it was a change in where she made her art: from the outskirts to the centre.

Other people also think the colour issue is exaggerated. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who knew Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot, but not her colour. He said it was more about makeup, hair styling, and clothing. He also said he did not think she was being pressured, because she always liked to sing, dance, and show off.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador, said the prejudice was not in the public but in music producers and media leaders. He said the public showed it could choose music for its own sake when it voted for Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, as the winner of the talent show The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for comment. But in a recent interview, she said identity should be chosen by the person. She said a powerful woman does not need to be beautiful. She said what matters is attitude, and that people can be who they want.
