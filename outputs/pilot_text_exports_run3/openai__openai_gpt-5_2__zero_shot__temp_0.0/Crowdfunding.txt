This new light could be as important as Trevor Baylis’s wind-up radio in the 1990s, before the internet. It is a cheap light that uses gravity for free power. It could replace dangerous kerosene lamps in Africa and India.

The British designer Patrick Hunt tried to get money from banks or venture capital. But it was slow and complicated. He said people did not want to take a risk because the idea was new and unproven.

So he tried crowdfunding on a US website called Indiegogo, which had just opened in the UK. In five days, he reached his goal and raised £36,200. By the end of 2012, after 40 days, he had raised £400,000.

The LED light uses a dynamo. It is powered when a 10kg bag of rocks slowly falls. The bag is tied to the light and lifted about 2m high. As it falls to the ground, it makes enough power for 30 minutes of light.

Hunt is getting ready to make the lights in China. Before making many more, he will send 1,000 lights to Africa to test the market. He hopes to later make millions.

Hunt is part of a new group of entrepreneurs using crowdfunding to get money. Another new website is InvestingZone. It matches rich people with start-up entrepreneurs.

Indiegogo does not give donors shares. Instead, projects can offer “perks” for different donation amounts. People who donated to Hunt’s light helped poorer people and also got their own light.

Danae Ringelmann, a co-founder of Indiegogo, says the “gravity light” shows how crowdfunding can test an idea. She says it is fast and fair because it is not about knowing people in a bank or a venture capital firm. It is about proving your idea to customers and fans, so they support it with money.

She also says even if an idea does not get funding, testing it can still help. You can learn sooner that it is not a good idea and save time.

Ringelmann lives in the US. She first worked as a Wall Street analyst. In 2008, she quit to help friends in the arts raise money. The website first focused on films and launched at the Sundance Film Festival that year.

Five years later, the site raises about $2m a week for new businesses. In December 2012, it added euro and sterling services and said Britain was its third biggest market. International activity has grown 41% since December.

Indiegogo has many competitors, like Kickstarter, Seedrs, and Funding Circle. Ringelmann says Indiegogo is different because anyone can start a campaign, even if the idea seems strange.

Indiegogo charges a 4% fee for successful campaigns. If a campaign does not reach its goal, users can choose to return all the money to donors for free, or keep the money but pay a 9% fee.

A British woman, Lauren Pears, raised £100,000 on the site to open a “cat café” in London. It is called Lady Dinah’s Cat Emporium. It has not opened yet. It is advertised as a place where people can sit in a comfortable wingback chair, drink hot tea, read a book, and be with a cat.

Ringelmann says venture capitalists sometimes reject ideas because they are not sure people will want them. But an Indiegogo campaign can show proof that a market exists, even before the project fully launches.

She says crowdfunding helps people test the market, the price, and the features. It can also find new ways to make money and get important feedback.

Ringelmann says ideas are common, but success comes from doing the work well. She says if you are afraid someone will steal your idea and do it better and faster, then you may not be the right person to do it. She says you need confidence, speed, and learning.

She says Indiegogo’s growth in Europe and a deal with a payment company are part of a dream to make finance open to more people. The site has more than 7,000 live campaigns.

Crowdfunding has grown as another choice besides banks. But it is not as attractive to big investors, who usually want a stake in a business. This might start to change in the UK with the launch of InvestingZone.
