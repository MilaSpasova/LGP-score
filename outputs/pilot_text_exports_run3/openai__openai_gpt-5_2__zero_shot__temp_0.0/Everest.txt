Kenton Cool usually likes to talk, but now he can hardly speak. He pushed himself hard high up in the mountains, and his voice is now rough. He is in Kathmandu, the capital of Nepal. That morning he flew down from Everest base camp.

Cool is thinking about a surprising set of climbs he finished over the last weekend. Early on Saturday morning, he reached the top of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of three main summits in the Everest “horseshoe.” This horseshoe surrounds an icy valley called the Western Cwm.

Later that same day, he climbed to the top of Everest. He reached the summit in complete darkness early on Sunday. Then he and his climbing partner went on to the top of Lhotse on Monday morning. Lhotse is the third mountain in this three-peaks challenge.

“It was a snatched opportunity,” he says. “For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. That doesn’t take away the physical achievement of what I did. I’ve set the bar at a certain level. But whoever comes along next will move the bar further and do it without ropes or bottled oxygen.”

Sixty years after people first climbed Everest, many stories look back to Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. People talk about a more innocent time, before today’s commercialism and damage to the environment. I ask Cool to look ahead and imagine what top climbers might do 60 years from now.

“I hate to think,” he says. He talks about the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. Steck left the mountain in April after what Cool calls “an altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck planned to climb Everest’s west ridge, first climbed in 1963. Then Steck planned to go down to the South Col and immediately climb Lhotse by a new route, all without fixed ropes. “Ueli had been training like a machine,” Cool says. “He’s a climber in a class all his own. He’s technically brilliant but he had also taken his physical condition to an astronomic level. It would have been amazing to see what he could have done.”

What tourism will be like is another question. One clue is a helicopter rescue by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro’s angry language helped cause the fight at Camp 2. On Tuesday, Moro flew back to Everest in a powerful helicopter to rescue a very sick climber at 7,800 metres.

It was the highest rescue ever done on Everest. It also shows how quickly helicopter flights have increased in recent years. By 2073, the mountain might even have a helipad on the South Col, bringing tourists who breathe bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing how people can rescue climbers and the many trekkers who go as far as base camp.

Mountain geographer and environmentalist Alton Byers says it is not clear if the Everest region can keep handling fast-growing tourism. He says climate change and tourism together are creating new problems for the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers in the Everest region are shrinking, and some are disappearing. This is already having a big effect. “Everywhere you go, people are talking about how there’s less water. There’s less water for agriculture and less water for all the new lodges that are getting built.”

Byers says that in the Sherpa town of Namche Bazaar, workers are building a new five-mile pipeline to bring in water. This water is needed because more tourists want showers and flush toilets. The local stream is polluted with human waste, and it does not have enough water for the town when it is very crowded in high season. “Every village is digging a pit just beyond the houses for garbage. Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world,” he says. Human waste at base camp is now handled well and taken away in plastic barrels. But Byers says these barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley, and it could leak into the region’s water.

“These problems can be solved, but we need to get serious about it,” he says. “One climber can spend $85,000 climbing Everest. And that’s fine. But at some point we’re going to have to address these other priorities. For half a million dollars a year, you could solve most of them.”

Climate change is harder. Byers works with local conservation committees to plan for climate change. They often look for new water sources or collect rainwater. Glacial lakes are growing quickly, and they could burst and flood the Sherpa homeland. This danger is always there. “There’s going to come a time when people are going to have to get out of their way.”

Changing weather is also affecting tourism. More clouds during times that are usually clear are closing Lukla Airport more often. Lukla Airport is the main gateway to the Everest region. A new road for 4x4 vehicles is being built to Lukla to make sure tourists and money can still come. But Byers worries that Nepal is building roads too cheaply, which can cause serious soil erosion and landslides.

“Everest is the icon everyone knows,” he says. “It’s the canary in the coalmine that everyone understands. It’s the perfect laboratory for figuring out how to address some of these problems, like the impacts of climate change and tourism.”
