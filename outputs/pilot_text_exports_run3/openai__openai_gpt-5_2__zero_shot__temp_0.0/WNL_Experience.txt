One warm summer evening, I decided to swim near Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland. I moved there in 2012. A woman was in the water with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty often swims with the same group of people.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Hundreds of people have swum with her, so she can seem tame. She was even in an Irish tourist ad where a girl says she wants to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty’s belly. About 20 tourists and local people watched from the pier. Soon after I got into the water, Dusty left the woman and suddenly became very wild. Later I learned she is very protective of her space when she is with someone. Her tail slapped the water hard. At first I thought she was just showing off, but then I realized she might be angry.

I tried to swim back to the pier to get out. But very quickly, Dusty crashed into me with her nose. It was very strong and very painful, and she was moving fast. I was thrown forward.

People on the pier stared at me with their mouths open. Dusty stayed next to me, still slapping her tail. That scared me the most. I thought if she hit me with her tail, I could sink.

I reached the pier, but I could not climb out because I was hurt. I felt huge fear. I shouted for help. A man reached in and pulled me onto the steps. Then another man came and said he was an orthopaedic surgeon who worked with marine injuries. He had been driving into Doolin and saw what was happening. I was very cold and very worried. I feared bleeding inside my body. He checked me and told me he could not feel signs of that, but I probably had broken bones.

Later I learned my injuries were serious: six fractures in my spine, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for several weeks. I could not work for five months and had trouble moving, with stiffness and pain. Then doctors said I had post-traumatic stress. After the near-death experience, I felt anxious and reacted strongly. I felt like people were looking at me the wrong way. Loud noises upset me. I also had some memory loss.

Three months before the accident, I had opened a health-food shop. But I had to give it up because I could not work. It was the hardest year, but now it is in the past. I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building my own osteopathy practice. I understand patients better because I have been one.

I am thankful that I am healthy, and I want to stop other people from getting hurt. People often think dolphins are always friendly. Who would expect a dolphin to attack a person? A wild animal with teeth showing looks scary, but dolphins look like they are smiling.

I am not angry at Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her space, and she is a wild animal that can act in unexpected ways. People need to know this. Many people come to swim with her and do not understand the danger. My injuries were said to be one of several that summer.

After the man pulled me out, Dusty swam away. Then she came back and floated upright near me, looking at me. We looked into each other’s eyes. I felt she was sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin. The anger was gone. The people on the pier were amazed. After that moment, my terror ended. I felt at peace with her.
