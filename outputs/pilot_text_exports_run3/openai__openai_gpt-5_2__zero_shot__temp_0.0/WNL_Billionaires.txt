A thick layer of bird droppings covers a shiny gold railing at one of Britain’s most expensive homes. Dead pigeons are inside. Mirrors are broken, and water runs through damaged walls and ceilings. This house is called The Towers. It is a £30 million mansion on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London. People have kept its damage secret until now.

The Towers is one of ten big houses on The Bishops Avenue. This street is in a very expensive part of London. These houses have been almost empty since they were bought about 25 years ago. It is believed they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. The tall columns are breaking. The swimming pools, covered with mosaic tiles, are full of broken pieces of building. Nature has taken over, and owls now live there.

Many houses on this street look the same—empty and falling apart. Lloyds Bank says The Bishops Avenue is the second most expensive street in Britain. At the same time, more people in London are struggling to buy a home because prices rise 11.2% each year. But 16 mansions on the most expensive part of the street are sitting empty. Many are behind locked gates, with metal covers on the windows and messy, overgrown gardens. Guard dogs patrol some properties.

Across the street is another empty mansion worth £18 million, with smashed windows and walls covered in anti-climb paint. Another mansion has metal grilles on the windows and was sold for £20 million.

Even so, prices keep rising. A mansion called Dryades sold for £12 million in 2007 and is believed to be worth about £30 million today. Heath Lodge is worth £13 million today. It was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000. Heath Lodge is also known as the place where fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou was murdered in 1984 by a silver bullet.

For people who struggle to pay for housing, these empty, ruined mansions can feel very upsetting. A security guard said it was frustrating to see such huge homes—big enough for dozens of people—slowly falling apart.

Rich royals from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia were some of the first to come to this road near Hampstead Heath. Iranians came after the fall of the shah. Now, Chinese buyers are coming after Russians and Kazakhs who have paid millions to own an address that estate agents say is as famous as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions were for sale for £65 million and £38 million. They promised Italian marble, lifts with leather padding, and luxury panic rooms.

But the empty homes are in bad condition. Outside, stone fountains are breaking and lawns have turned into muddy bogs. Inside one house, water drips through a large crystal chandelier onto a thick carpet that is rotting under parts of a fallen ceiling. Moss grows through broken bricks. Bathroom mirror tiles lie scattered. A swimming pool has about a foot of dirty water, and flowers are growing through the tiles. Wooden slats are pushing out from the sauna.

The most dramatic ruin is The Towers. It is a large mansion surrounded by many trees. Pigeons fill its high-ceiling rooms. Water pours through three floors, and algae has turned the walls bright green. The water drops into a huge, empty swimming pool in the basement. Unopened wooden crates marked “bullet-proof glass” show that the old owners were worried about security.

Today, very few people live full time on The Bishops Avenue. A guard outside one mansion said the owners were not there. A guard outside Royal Mansion would not say if anyone was home. At another house, a staff member warned about guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare businessman with personal wealth of £45 million, said he is one of the few people who live there all the time.

“It’s not a friendly place where you can talk over the fence,” he said. “To be honest, I have never seen what my neighbours look like. Next door, a Saudi princess spent £35 million on a new house and I’ve never seen her. There are about three houses that are lived in 24/7 and half of the properties are occupied three to six months a year. The other half, who knows if they come or not?”

These broken, expensive homes show a culture where very rich people buy British property as investments. One Hyde Park, a building of apartments in Knightsbridge, is another example. More than half of its flats are registered with the council as empty or second homes.

Even so, people on the street are talking about building £5 million apartments instead of £50 million mansions, to try to bring people back.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, wants to rebuild one very valuable site on the avenue into 20 apartments. The building would have a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

“If you build a big house and try and sell for £30m to £40m, it won’t sell,” he said. “Locals won’t buy and so you have to bring in overseas buyers.”

But using these empty properties to help solve the housing crisis still seems far away. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some of the ruined homes could become affordable housing. He said the land price would be too high.

“Very wealthy people own property there,” he said. “Sometimes they live in them and sometimes they don’t.”
