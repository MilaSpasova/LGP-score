Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is theft. He says it is like reaching into someone’s pocket and stealing their wallet. Pullman wrote this in an article for Index on Censorship. He is president of the Society of Authors. He strongly supports copyright laws. He criticizes people who download music or books without paying.

Pullman writes that the technology is very impressive, but people do not see that what they do is wrong. He says it is outrageous that people can steal an artist’s work and not be punished.

Pullman wrote the article after music industry leaders met British Prime Minister David Cameron at Downing Street. They talked about web piracy.

Pullman, who wrote the His Dark Materials trilogy, says authors and musicians often work for years in poverty and without attention. They work hard to make something audiences enjoy. But when they finally can earn money, illegal downloading can take that chance away. He says the rule is simple: if we want to enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

In the next issue of the magazine, Pullman is in a dialogue with Cathy Casserly. She is chief executive of Creative Commons. Creative Commons offers open content licences that let creators take copyright into their own hands. Casserly says many things are wrong with copyright, because it was made in an “analogue age”. She says copyright can stop people from sharing, building on, and remixing other people’s work.

Casserly says artists need to think in new ways about how they share and make money from their work. She quotes science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees. The editor, Rachael Jolley, says current copyright laws do not work in the digital age and may make consumers into criminals. She says we need new ways for artists, writers, and musicians to earn a living.

Illegal downloading is very common. Ofcom, a UK communications regulator, collected data. It suggests that in the UK, between November 2012 and January 2013, 280 million music tracks were pirated. Also pirated were 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files.

Ofcom says 18% of internet users over age 12 admit they recently pirated content. It says 9% fear getting caught. Pullman says it is still amazing how quickly people can get music as MP3 downloads, especially for people who have been building their iTunes list for some time.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron appointed Conservative MP Mike Weatherley as his adviser on the issue. A spokesman for the BPI, the record industry trade body, said Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creative producers it protects. The spokesman said they hope Weatherley and the prime minister will influence the UK’s intellectual property office.
