Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8th April, 2014, she told people about an unusual event. She received a message in a bottle, 101 years after her grandfather threw it into the Baltic Sea. People think it may be the world’s oldest message in a bottle. A museum in Germany gave it to her and is showing it to visitors.

Erdmann, who was 62, said it was a big surprise. A man came to her door and said he had mail from her grandfather. He said a message in a bottle had been found, and the name on the card was her grandfather’s name. The man was a genealogical researcher. He found Erdmann in Berlin after the letter was given to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg.

The brown beer bottle had been in the water for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it in his catch while fishing in the Baltic Sea near the northern city of Kiel. Holger von Neuhoff, a curator at the museum, said it was the oldest bottled message he had seen. He said the museum has older documents without bottles, but this is the oldest with both the bottle and the document. He said it was in very good condition.

Researchers think Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913, during a hike with a nature appreciation group. He was 20 years old then.

Most of the postcard could not be read. But the Berlin address on the front could be read. The note also asked the finder to send it to his home address.

Erdmann said her grandfather also put two stamps from that time in the bottle, so the finder would not have to pay. But he did not think it would take 101 years.

Erdmann said the message made her feel emotional. She did not know her grandfather because he died at age 54, six years before she was born.

She said she knew very little about him, but learned he was a writer. She said he was open-minded, believed in freedom, and believed people should respect each other. She said he did a lot for young people, and later traveled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful to learn where her family came from.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and traveling around the world, and she is open-minded too. She said what her grandfather taught his two daughters, her mother taught her, and she has taught it to her sons.

Even though she was happy to get the message, she said people should not throw bottles with messages into the sea today. She said the sea already has too many bottles and too much rubbish.

The message and bottle will be shown at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts will try to read the rest of the text. It is not clear what will happen to the bottle after that, but Erdmann hopes it will stay in the museum.

She said they want to add some photos with the bottle, so visitors can see the young man who threw it into the water.
