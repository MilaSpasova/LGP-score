A thick layer of bird droppings covers a gold-painted railing at one of Britain’s most expensive homes. Pigeon bones lie among broken mirrors, and water runs through broken parts of the ceiling. This house is called The Towers. It is a £30m palace on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London. Until now, people kept its terrible condition secret.

The Towers is one of ten big houses in the middle of The Bishops Avenue. This street is a key part of London’s rising property market. These houses have been almost empty since they were bought about 25 years ago. It is believed they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. Their Greek-style columns are breaking. Their swimming pools with mosaic tiles are filled with rubble. Nature has taken over so much that owls now live there.

This empty and broken scene happens along the avenue, even though Lloyds Bank says it is the second most expensive street in Britain. House prices in London rise by 11.2% a year, and more people struggle to buy a home. But 16 mansions on the most expensive part of The Bishops Avenue are empty. Many are behind locked gates. Their windows have steel bars, and the gardens are overgrown. Guard dogs patrol the grounds.

Across the street is another empty mansion worth £18m, with broken windows and walls covered in anti-climb paint. Another mansion has metal bars on its windows. It sold for £20m.

Even so, prices keep rising. Dryades, a mansion once owned by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007. It is believed to be worth about £30m today. Heath Lodge is worth £13m today. It was the place of the 1984 murder of fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou, killed by a silver bullet. Heath Lodge was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

For people who struggle to keep a home in one of the world’s most expensive cities, these empty buildings can feel very painful. A security guard on the avenue said it was upsetting to see so much space falling apart. He said the houses have many tens of thousands of square feet, enough to house dozens of people.

Rich royals with oil money from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia were among the first to come to this curved road near Hampstead Heath. Iranians came here after the fall of the shah. Now Chinese buyers are following Russians and Kazakhs. They have spent millions to get an address that estate agents say is as famous as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions were for sale for £65m and £38m. They promised Italian marble, lifts with leather padding, and luxury panic rooms.

But on the grounds of the empty mansions, stone fountains are breaking and lawns have turned into wet mud. Inside one house, water drips through a huge crystal chandelier onto a thick carpet that is rotting. Parts of the ceiling have fallen down. Moss grows through broken bricks, and mirrored tiles are spread across a bathroom. The swimming pool has about a foot of dirty, salty water, and flowers grow through the tiles. Wooden pieces bend away from the sauna.

The most dramatic ruin is The Towers. It is a grand house among acres of hornbeams, oaks, and limes. Pigeons fill its large halls. Water pours through three storeys, and the walls have turned bright green with algae. The water drops into a huge empty swimming pool in the basement. There are unopened wooden boxes marked “bullet-proof glass,” showing the past owners’ security fears.

Today, very few people live full time on The Bishops Avenue. A security guard outside one mansion said the owners were not there. Another guard, outside Royal Mansion, would not say if anyone was home. A staff member at another mansion only warned the Guardian about the guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare mogul with personal wealth of £45m, said he was one of the few permanent residents.

He said, “It’s not a friendly place where you can chat over the fence. To be honest, I have never seen what my neighbours look like. Next door, a Saudi princess spent £35m on a new house and I’ve never seen her. There are about three houses that are lived in 24/7 and half of the properties are occupied three to six months a year. The other half, who knows if they come or not?”

These broken multimillion-pound houses show a property culture where the world’s richest people treat British homes as investments. One Hyde Park, a block of flats in Knightsbridge, is another example. More than half the flats there are registered with the council as empty or second homes.

Even so, people on the avenue talk about building £5m apartments instead of £50m mansions, to bring people back.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, will rebuild one of the most valuable sites on the avenue. He plans 20 apartments with a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

He said, “If you build a big house and try and sell for £30m to £40m, it won’t sell. Locals won’t buy and so you have to bring in overseas buyers.”

But the idea of using these empty homes to help with the housing crisis still seems far away. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some of the broken homes could become affordable homes. He said the land price would be too high.

He said, “Very wealthy people own property there. Sometimes they live in them and sometimes they don’t.”
