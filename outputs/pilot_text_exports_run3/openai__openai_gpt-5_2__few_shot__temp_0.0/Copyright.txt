Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is theft. He says it is like putting your hand in someone’s pocket and stealing their wallet. He wrote this in an article for Index on Censorship. Pullman is president of the Society of Authors. He strongly supports copyright laws. He is very critical of people who download music or books without paying.

Pullman writes that new technology can look amazing, but people do not see that what they do is wrong. He says it is terrible that people can steal an artist’s work and not be punished.

He wrote the article after leaders from the music industry met British Prime Minister David Cameron at Downing Street. They talked about web piracy.

Pullman, who wrote the His Dark Materials trilogy, says authors and musicians often work for years in poverty and in secret. They try to make work that gives joy to the audience. But when they finally succeed, illegal downloading can take away their chance to earn a living. He says the rule is simple: if we want to enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

In the next issue of the magazine, Pullman is in a dialogue with Cathy Casserly. She is chief executive of Creative Commons, which offers open content licences that let creators control their copyright choices. Casserly says copyright has many problems because it was made in an “analogue age”. She says copyright can stop people from sharing, building on, and remixing each other’s work.

Casserly says artists need to think of new ways to distribute and monetize their work. She quotes science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees. The editor, Rachael Jolley, said current copyright laws do not work well in the digital age and may make normal consumers into criminals. She said we need new models so artists, writers, and musicians can earn a living from their work.

The debate is active, and illegal downloading is very large. Ofcom, the UK regulator for communications industries, collected data. It suggests that in the UK, between November 2012 and January 2013, people pirated 280 million music tracks. They also pirated 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files.

Ofcom said 18% of internet users aged over 12 admit they recently pirated content. It also said 9% fear getting caught. Pullman writes that it is still surprising how easily and quickly people can get music as MP3 downloads.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron appointed the Conservative MP Mike Weatherley as his adviser on this issue. A spokesman for the BPI, the record industry trade body, said Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creators it protects. The spokesman said they hope Weatherley and the prime minister will influence the UK intellectual property office.
