Some people have unusual ideas to make cities nicer places to live. City life can be good, but it is harder to live in a sustainable way. People in cities often face pollution, traffic, and fewer green spaces. Here are some strange solutions.

Pop-up parks
Some cities seem built more for cars than for people. A pop-up park is a simple idea: use an empty parking space, a little money, and a few plants to make a small park. The PARK(ing) project started as an art experiment in San Francisco and then spread around the world. People have also made temporary city farms and ecology demonstrations. The movement has a day of action every September.

Underground bike storage
Not everyone in a city uses a car. Bicycles are becoming more popular, but people need a safe place to keep them. A Tokyo engineering company called Giken designed an “eco-cycle” underground bicycle park that is also anti-seismic. The storage is a cylinder about seven metres wide. It goes deep underground and can hold 204 bikes. Owners can get their bike back by pressing a button. The system brings the bike above ground in about 13 seconds.

Glow-in-the-dark trees
Most people think of Christmas decorations when they think of things that glow. Dutch designer and artist Daan Roosegaarde made a “bioluminescent” plant. The experimental technology mixes DNA from glowing marine bacteria with a plant’s chloroplast genome. This creates a glow like a jellyfish. Trials are happening to make an industrial-scale version. Roosegaarde hopes it could one day replace normal street lights.

Footstep power
In West Ham in east London, many commuters and shoppers cross a raised walkway near the underground station every day. Most people do not notice that the surface feels a little springy. The rubber surface moves about five millimetres. Under it are smart tiles that capture energy from people’s footsteps and turn it into electricity to power the streetlights above. The UK company Pavegen made this system. It has also installed similar systems at London’s Heathrow Airport and other places.

Supertrees
Singapore’s Gardens by the Bay has man-made “supertrees.” They are steel frames and can be up to 50 metres tall. Flowers and ferns grow on them. Their metal tops help absorb and spread out heat. They can also collect rainwater. They help give air ventilation for two “climate-controlled biomes” (large conservatories) below. Eleven of the 18 trees also have solar panels on their “branches.”
