Kenton Cool usually likes to talk a lot. But now he can hardly speak. Climbing high mountains has hurt his voice, and it sounds rough.

He is now in Kathmandu, the capital of Nepal. That morning, he flew down from Everest base camp. He is thinking about an amazing set of climbs he finished the weekend before.

Early Saturday, he reached the top of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of three main peaks in the Everest “horseshoe.” This horseshoe is around an icy valley called the Western Cwm.

On the same day, he climbed to the top of Everest. He got there in complete darkness early on Sunday. Then he and his climbing partner kept going. They climbed to the top of Lhotse on Monday morning. Lhotse was the third peak in this three-peaks challenge.

Cool says it was a chance he quickly took. For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. He says this does not remove the physical achievement. But he thinks the next person will set a higher level, and do it without ropes or bottled oxygen.

It has been 60 years since Everest was first climbed. Many stories look back to Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. They compare that earlier time with today’s time of commercialism and damage to the environment. I ask Cool to think forward and imagine what top climbers might do 60 years from now.

Cool says he does not like to think about it. But he talks about the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. Steck left the mountain in April after what Cool calls “an altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck planned a big climb: go up Everest’s west ridge (first climbed in 1963), go down to the South Col, and then climb Lhotse right away by a new route. He planned to do this without fixed ropes. Cool says Steck trained extremely hard. He says Steck was a special climber: very skilled and also in incredible physical condition. Cool says it would have been amazing to see what Steck could do.

Tourism is another question. One example is a helicopter rescue by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro’s angry language helped cause the confrontation at Camp 2. On Tuesday, Moro flew a powerful helicopter back to Everest to rescue a very sick or injured climber at 7,800 metres.

This was the highest rescue ever done on Everest. It also shows that helicopter flights have grown very fast in recent years. By 2073, the mountain might even have a helipad on the South Col. Tourists could be brought there while breathing bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing rescue possibilities for both climbers and the many more trekkers who go only as far as base camp.

Alton Byers is a mountain geographer and environmentalist. He says it is not clear if the Everest region can keep handling fast-growing tourism. He says climate change and tourism together are creating new pressure on the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers in the region are shrinking, and some are disappearing. This is already causing big problems. Byers says people everywhere talk about having less water. There is less water for farming and also less water for all the new lodges being built.

Byers says that in the Sherpa town of Namche Bazaar, people are building a new five-mile pipeline to bring more water. This is to meet tourist demand for showers and flush toilets. The local stream is polluted with human waste. It also does not have enough water for a town that becomes extremely crowded in the high season. Byers says every village is digging a pit outside the houses for garbage. He says Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world.

At base camp, human waste is now handled well and taken away in plastic barrels. But Byers says these barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley. That pit could leak into the region’s water sources.

Byers says these problems can be fixed, but people must take them seriously. He says one climber can spend $85,000 to climb Everest, and that is okay. But he says that at some point, people must also focus on these other needs. He says that for half a million dollars a year, most of the problems could be solved.

Climate change is harder. Byers works with local conservation committees to find and plan for climate change effects. They often look for new water sources or collect rainwater. Another constant danger is that glacial lakes are growing quickly. These lakes could burst and cause floods in the Sherpa homeland. Byers says there will be a time when people will have to move away.

Weather changes also affect tourism. More clouds during times that are usually clear are closing Lukla Airport more often. Lukla Airport is the main entrance to the Everest region. A new road for 4x4 vehicles is being built to Lukla to keep tourists and their money coming. But Byers worries that road building in Nepal is being done cheaply. He says this can lead to very serious soil erosion and landslides.

Byers says everyone knows Everest. He says it is like a warning sign that people understand. He says it is also a perfect place to study how to deal with problems like climate change and tourism.
