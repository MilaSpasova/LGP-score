The lottery balls have fallen, and all six numbers match. Now it is time to buy an Audi, book a holiday in the US, and call an estate agent. An analysis says many lottery millionaires do these things.

The UK National Lottery started in 1994. Since then, it has made 3,000 millionaires. Together, they have won more than £8.5bn in total. On average, each won £2.8m. The study authors, Oxford Economics, say this money also helped make 3,780 more millionaires among the winners’ children, family, and friends.

Most winners (59%) stop working right away. But 19% keep their job, and 31% do unpaid volunteer work. The study says 98% of winners’ spending stayed in the UK. By spending money on property, vehicles, and holidays, each winner is estimated to support six full-time jobs for one year.

The winners added almost £750m to gross domestic product (GDP). They also created more than £500m in tax for the Exchequer. Most money was spent on property. Winners spent £2.72bn on their main homes, and £170m to pay off debt and mortgages.

Many winners wanted to keep having income. They spent £2.125bn on investments. They gave £1.17bn as gifts to family and friends. They spent £680m on cars and holidays.

Camelot, the operator of the UK National Lottery, paid for the study to mark the 3,000-winners milestone. The study used research from 100 winners who won £1m or more. It found that the 3,000 winners bought 7,958 houses or flats in the UK, which is 2.7 each on average. They spent £3.3bn. Most winners (82%) changed their main home, spending an average of £900,000.

Many new homes had extra features. Almost a third (29%) wanted a hot tub. A walk-in wardrobe was important for 28%. Almost a quarter (24%) chose a home behind electric gates. Also, 22% had a games room, and 7% put in a snooker table.

Big homes need help. About 30% hired a cleaner and 24% hired a gardener. A small number (5%) hired a beautician.

Audi was the favourite car for 16% of winners. Range Rovers and BMWs were also popular (11% each), as well as Mercedes (10%) and Land Rovers (5%). Winners spent £463m on 17,190 cars. The average price of their favourite car was £46,116.

Holidays were also important. Most winners (68%) chose five-star hotels abroad. The US was the top place for 27%, followed by the Caribbean (9%). In the UK, caravan sales also benefited. Over the past 18 years, 10% of millionaires bought a caravan, with sales worth about £7.4m.

Some winners (15%) started their own business. Another 9% helped other people start businesses, and 6% invested in or bought other people’s businesses. Businesses started or supported by lottery winners employ 3,195 people, the study says.

Andy Logan, the report author, said the effect of a win spreads further than expected. It changes the lives of friends and family. It also has a clear effect on the UK economy, because so much money is spent in the UK. He said each win creates a ripple effect in this generation, and often the next one too.
