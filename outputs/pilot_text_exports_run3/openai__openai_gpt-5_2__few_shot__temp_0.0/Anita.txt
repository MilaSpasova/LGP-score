Anitta is a new star of Brazilian funk music. She became very popular by bringing music from the favelas to the mainstream. But people are now debating her skin colour.

Some anti-discrimination activists and writers say she had to look less black to succeed in a market that is mostly white and middle class.

The debate started after people shared “before and after” photos. In an older photo, when she was not famous, she looked darker. In a newer marketing photo, after she became famous, she looks paler. People argue about why this happened. Some mention skin-whitening products or cosmetic surgery. Others say it was more likely Photoshop. This has brought back the question: in Brazil, do you need to be pale to succeed?

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote online about this. She said the media and marketing hide discrimination. She said many people say they are not racist, but statistics show racism is still there. She said society often connects white skin with being rich, nice, successful, and good, and connects black skin with the opposite.

Anitta’s real name is Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

She later became famous with an album and a hit song, “Show das Poderosas.” The song reached number one and got 52 million views on YouTube.

Many people like her as a pop idol with catchy songs and a strong message. Her supporters also present her as a bridge between mostly black and mixed-race shanty towns on Rio’s hills and the richer and whiter areas below.

She has changed her style. She reduced the suggestive dancing, gangsta references, and explicit lyrics of baile funk. Now some people ask if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract a richer audience. Arraes said that pop stars may feel forced to change things like curly hair or a big nose, and that this can harm self-esteem.

Race and colour are sensitive topics in Brazil. Brazil was one of the last large countries to ban slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. In Brazil, people often focus less on ancestry and more on skin colour. Even though Brazil wants to be a multiracial democracy, skin tone is strongly linked to inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Until 2011, black or mixed-race students spent two years less in school on average.

The government says the gap is getting smaller because of quota systems for university places and other affirmative action. But big differences remain. Most business and government leaders are white. Many lower-paid jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In rich areas like Ipanema and Gávea, it is more common to see black nannies pushing strollers with white toddlers than the other way around.

Colour is also hard to define. In the 2010 census, people who called themselves white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million black, two million Asian, and 0.5% indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology lecturer at the Federal University of Pernambuco, said Anitta won middle-class fans by taking a rebellious sound and making it calmer and easier for everyone to accept. He said this was not because of racial whitening. He said it was because the social space of her music changed, from the periphery to the centre.

Some people also think the colour issue is exaggerated. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who worked with Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot, but not her colour. He said the change was more about makeup, hairstyling, and clothing. He also said she was not forced. He said she always liked to sing, dance, and show off, and that this natural style helped her succeed.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador, said the prejudice is more in music producers and media executives, not in the public. He said the public showed they care about music itself when they chose Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, as the recent winner of The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for comment. But in a recent interview, she said identity should be defined by the person. She said an “all-powerful” woman does not need to be beautiful. She said she wants to show people that we can be who we want.
