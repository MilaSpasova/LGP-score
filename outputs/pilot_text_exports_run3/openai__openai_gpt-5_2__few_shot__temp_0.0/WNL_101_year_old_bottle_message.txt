Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8th April, 2014, she told people about a special moment. She received a message in a bottle. Her grandfather had thrown it into the Baltic Sea 101 years earlier. People think it may be the world’s oldest message in a bottle. A museum in Germany gave the bottle to her. The museum is now showing it.

Erdmann, 62, said she was very surprised. A man came to her door and said he had mail from her grandfather. He said a bottle had been found, and the name on the card was her grandfather’s. The man was a genealogical researcher. He found Erdmann in Berlin after the letter was given to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg.

The bottle was a brown beer bottle. It was in the water for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it in his catch in the Baltic Sea near the northern city of Kiel. Holger von Neuhoff, a curator at the museum, said it was the oldest bottled message he had seen. He said the museum has older documents, but not with the bottle. He said the bottle and the document were in very good condition.

Researchers think Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913. He was hiking with a nature appreciation group. He was 20 years old.

Much of the postcard could not be read. But the Berlin address on the front could be read. The card also asked the finder to send the note to his home address.

Erdmann said her grandfather also put two stamps from that time in the bottle, so the finder would not have to pay. She said he did not think it would take 101 years.

Erdmann said the message made her feel emotional. She did not know her grandfather because he died at age 54, six years before she was born.

She said she knew little about him, but learned that he was a writer. She said he was open-minded, believed in freedom, and believed everyone should respect each other. She said he did a lot for young people and later travelled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful because she could see where her roots came from.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and travelling around the world. She said she is open-minded too. She said what he taught his two daughters, her mother taught her, and she then taught it to her sons.

She was happy to get the message, but she said she hopes others will not throw bottles with messages into the sea today. She said the sea is already full of bottles and rubbish.

The message and the bottle will be on display at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts will try to read the rest of the text. It is not clear what will happen to the bottle after that, but Erdmann hopes it will stay at the museum.

She said they want to show some photos with the bottle. She wants visitors to see the young man who threw the bottle into the water.
