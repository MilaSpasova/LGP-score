It was a warm summer evening. I decided to swim near Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland. I moved there in 2012. A woman was in the water with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty often swims with a group of people.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Since then, hundreds of people have swum with her. This makes her seem very tame. She was even in an Irish tourist-board advertisement. In the ad, a girl says she wants to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty on the belly. It looked friendly. About 20 tourists and local people were watching from the pier. When I got into the water, Dusty left the woman and suddenly became very wild. Later I learned that she is very protective of her space when she is with someone. Her tail was moving fast. At first I thought she was just showing off. Then I understood she might be angry.

I tried to swim back to the pier to get out. But very quickly, Dusty hit me hard with her nose. It was very strong and very painful. I was pushed forward.

People on the pier stared at me in shock. Dusty stayed near me. Her tail was still moving crazily. That scared me the most. I thought if she hit me with her tail, I could go under the water.

I reached the pier, but I could not get out because I was hurt. I felt great fear. I shouted for help. A man reached down and pulled me onto the steps. Then another man came. He said he was an orthopaedic surgeon who specialized in marine trauma. He was driving into Doolin and saw what was happening. I was very cold and very worried. My biggest fear was bleeding inside my body. He checked me and said he could not feel signs of internal bleeding, but he thought I probably had broken bones. Later I learned I had six spinal fractures, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for several weeks. I could not work for five months. I had limited movement, stiffness, and pain. Then doctors said I had post-traumatic stress. After the near-death experience, I felt anxious about everything. I overreacted in a way I never did before. I felt people were looking at me in a wrong way. Loud noises became hard for me. I also had memory loss. Three months before the accident, I opened a health-food shop. But I had to give it up because I could not work. It was the hardest year, but now it is in the past. I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building my own osteopathy practice. I understand patients better because I have been one.

I am thankful that I am healthy now. I want to stop other people from getting hurt. Many people think dolphins are always safe. Who would think a dolphin could attack a person? If a dangerous animal comes at you with teeth showing, it is scary. But dolphins have a big smile.

I am not angry with Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her area, and she is a wild animal. She can be unpredictable. People need to know this. Many people come to swim with her and do not understand how dangerous it can be. People said there were several injuries that summer, and mine was one of them.

After the man pulled me out of the water, Dusty swam away. Then she came back. She was upright in the water next to me and looked at me. We looked into each other’s eyes. I felt she was sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin. The anger was gone. The people on the pier were amazed. After that moment with her, I did not feel terror anymore. I forgave her.
