Many children may not know who Steve Jobs was, and some may not even know how to tie their shoelaces. But a new report says the average six-year-old understands more about digital technology than a 45-year-old adult.

Ofcom, which checks standards in the UK communications industries, says broadband in 2000 created a generation of “digital natives”. These children were born in the new millennium. They never used slow dial-up internet. The youngest children are learning to use smartphones or tablets before they can talk.

Jane Rumble, the head of media research at Ofcom, said young people are changing the way we communicate. She said they have very different communication habits from older people, even compared with “early adopters” aged 16 to 24.

Ofcom created a “digital quotient” (DQ) test. It tested 800 children and 2,000 adults. It is not an IQ test. It measures how aware people are of digital devices and how confident they feel using them. It also checks knowledge of fast internet, 4G mobile-phone networks, and mobile apps.

Children aged 6 to 7 grew up with YouTube, Spotify, and online TV. Their average DQ score was 98. People aged 45 to 49 scored 96. Digital understanding is highest at age 14 to 15, with an average DQ of 113. After that, it slowly drops during adulthood, and it drops faster in old age.

People can now take a shorter version of the test. It gives a DQ score and advice on how to improve digital knowledge and stay safe online.

Experts say the way millennial children communicate and enjoy entertainment is so different from older generations that their choices may show the future better than the choices of young adults.

One big change is time spent talking on the phone. Twenty years ago, teenagers often talked for hours on the home phone.

Now, for 12- to 15-year-olds, phone calls are only 3% of all time spent communicating on any device. For all adults, it is 20%. For young adults, it is 9%. Today, children mostly communicate by written messages and by sharing photos and videos. Ofcom says, “The millennium generation is losing its voice.”

Over 90% of children’s device time is message-based. They chat on social networks like Facebook, send instant messages on services like WhatsApp, or use mobile-phone text messages. Only 2% of children’s time is spent on email, compared with 33% for adults.

Children aged 12 to 15 also use other media differently. A seven-day digital diary shows that live TV is only about half of what they watch. For all adults, live TV is nearly 70% of viewing. Children spend 20% of viewing time watching short video clips, such as on YouTube, or news clips shared on Facebook and other social sites. The rest is split between DVDs, streamed content on Netflix or iTunes, and recorded TV programmes.

Young adults aged 16 to 24 use almost all kinds of media a lot. But live radio and print media are almost gone from their daily habits.

Younger people are moving away from live TV and toward streaming and catch-up services. Even for adults, TV is becoming less important. TV viewing among 16- to 24-year-olds has fallen each year since 2010. In 2013, viewing fell across all age groups for the first time. Researchers think tablet computers, popular Christmas presents in 2012 and 2013, helped bring many older people online for longer. Because tablets have large screens and simple touch controls, internet access among people over 65 has increased.

James Thickett, Research Director at Ofcom, said people over 65 resisted using the internet for years. He said this has changed in the last three years, and Ofcom thinks tablets are the reason.

Britain is using more internet-connected devices in every age group. People now spend more time on screens than sleeping. A typical adult spends 8 hours and 41 minutes each day communicating or using media, including books and newspapers, and sleeps 8 hours and 21 minutes.
