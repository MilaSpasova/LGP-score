In 2005, BlackBerry brought instant messaging to mobile phones. The company was starting its best years. The iPhone did not exist yet. BlackBerry’s new ideas helped its smartphone become one of Canada’s biggest exports.

Six years later, in summer 2011, there was violence in London. It also spread to Birmingham, Nottingham, Liverpool, and Manchester. BlackBerry Messenger (BBM) helped rioters organize so well that politicians asked to shut it down for a short time. But two years later, users themselves are stopping.

People want BlackBerry phones less now. BBM is becoming quiet. Many other apps now do the same job. These include messaging apps from Facebook and Apple, and start-ups like WhatsApp and Kik (Kik is also Canadian). These apps are free to download and use. They use the internet to send text messages, pictures, voice clips, stickers, and, in WhatsApp’s case, videos. They work on most kinds of phones.

BlackBerry tried to keep users by releasing BBM on Android and Apple phones. Many people downloaded it. BlackBerry said there were more than 20 million downloads. But many people think this will not save BBM. James Gooderson, an 18-year-old student who writes a technology blog, says, “The move to bring BlackBerry to the iPhone is four or five years too late. WhatsApp has removed the reason why young people would use a BlackBerry.”

BBM says it has 80 million monthly users after its upgrade, but WhatsApp has 300 million. Other services show what BBM cannot do. Unlike Skype and Viber, BBM still does not have video or voice calls. Unlike Path, it does not share location. There is no video sharing like on iMessage. Stickers, which many kids around the world love, are missing too. Even contact and calendar sharing, which BBM had on BlackBerry phones, did not move to the Apple and Android versions.

Messaging is changing from words to pictures. Photos on Instagram bring many comments. Snapchat pictures delete themselves after ten seconds, and this creates new ways to communicate. Like BBM, these services are free for any phone with an internet connection. Yet in 2011, BBM was so powerful that people said it helped start a revolution in Egypt. During the London riots, it gave news faster than TV.

Jean-Pierre Moore, 28, said, “We could see on our BlackBerry messages where the rioters were going next; TV news would catch up four hours later.” He runs a youth club in Stockwell, south London. This area has some of the highest levels of crime and economic deprivation in Britain. Moore mostly uses an iPad now. He does not think a BBM curfew, suggested by some MPs, would have stopped the looting. “The social networking wasn’t the reason,” he says. “I know a lot of people who were out rioting. People had been angry for a long time. Mention the words ‘stop and search’ around here and you immediately have a room full of angry young men.”

The research firm Enders Analysis says nearly 80% of young smartphone owners often use a social networking app. But two-thirds use more than one. Among 16- to 24-year-olds, 60% use Facebook every day, but 46% use other services. Benedict Evans, a digital media specialist at Enders, says, “It’s a much more complex, multifaceted environment. The smartphone itself has become the platform. All of these apps plug into your phone book and your photo library. Apps rise and fall like fireworks. Some, like Instagram, last; others disappear into thin air.”

A 13-year-old called Bennett has three devices, all second-hand from family members. He keeps his BlackBerry for messaging. He uses an iPhone on wi-fi to play games. He makes phone calls on an HTC-branded Android phone. His friends still use BBM. This term, there have been four phone thefts at his school, and they were all BlackBerrys. On BlackBerry, one message can be sent to the owner’s whole contacts book, which can be several hundred people. On WhatsApp, a broadcast message is limited to 50. But for Bennett, Instagram is now a main social network. He says, “Instagram is Facebook without parents. Facebook has been taken over by the older generation. Once I saw my mum on Facebook, I deleted my account.”

For families who may struggle to pay heating bills this winter, BlackBerry still seems good because it is cheap to buy and cheap to communicate. Unlimited BBM messages are available with a second-hand device and a £7-a-month deal from T-Mobile. But people trust BBM’s privacy less now. Business people, revolutionaries, demonstrators, and rioters liked BBM partly because they believed encrypted messages on the company’s secure servers could not be traced back to the writers. Prosecutions after the riots ended that belief.

In South Kensington, outside a private school in a rich area, the older students all have Apple logos on their phones. They all use WhatsApp. For many, BBM is far in the past. “I still have a Blackberry, but I’m the only one,” says a teenager with his friends. Asked how that feels, he says, “Isolated.”
