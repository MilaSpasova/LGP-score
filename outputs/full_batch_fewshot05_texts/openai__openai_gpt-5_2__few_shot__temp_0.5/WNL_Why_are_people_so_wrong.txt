Our new survey in 33 countries shows that people often guess social facts very badly.

In Britain, people think the richest 1% own 59% of the country’s wealth, but they own 23%. In the US, people think 33% of the population are immigrants, but it is 14%.

In Brazil, people think the average age is 56, but it is 31. In Russia, people think 31% of politicians are women, but it is 14%.

In Britain, people think 43% of adults aged 25-34 still live with their parents, but the real number is 14%. In India, people who are online think 60% of the whole country has internet access, but only 19% do.

Why are people so wrong?

One reason is that many people find basic maths hard. Some people also misunderstand the questions. For example, in 33 countries, respondents say 37% of people have no religion, but the real average is 18%. This may be because people think about who practises a religion, not what people write on census forms.

Another reason is that people use quick thinking. They choose information that is easy to remember, even if it does not fit the question. For example, people often guess that rural populations are bigger than they are. They may think about how much land is rural, not how many people live there. Daniel Kahneman calls this “fast” thinking, not “slow” thinking.

People also focus too much on their own life and forget how different other people are. In India, many online people overestimate internet access because they are part of a richer, more connected group. The survey was mostly online, so in developing countries it may not fully represent the whole population. People with more education are usually more accurate, but in this study people often guess from their own situation and forget it is not typical.

People can also be wrong because of feelings. Social psychologists call this “emotional innumeracy.” This means people’s answers show what worries them, not only what is true. Worry can cause wrong beliefs, and wrong beliefs can also cause worry.

In Britain, this may help explain why people overestimate the wealth of the richest, how many young people live at home, and how many immigrants there are. People guess 25% are immigrants, but official estimates say 13%. People worry about wealth, housing pressure, and immigration, so they guess bigger numbers.

But the survey also shows issues where people are not worried enough. In many countries, people underestimate how many people are overweight or obese. In Saudi Arabia, people think 28% are, but 71% are. In Britain, people think 44% are, but the real number is 62%.

Some of the most important results are the real differences between countries. In Russia, the richest 1% own 70% of the nation’s wealth, but in New Zealand they own 18%. In Italy, half of people aged 25-34 still live with their parents, but in Norway it is only 4%. The average age is 27 in India and 47 in Japan. Only 10% of politicians are women in Brazil, Hungary, and Japan, but 44% are in Sweden.

Because the real facts are so unusual and so different, it is not surprising that people often get them wrong.
