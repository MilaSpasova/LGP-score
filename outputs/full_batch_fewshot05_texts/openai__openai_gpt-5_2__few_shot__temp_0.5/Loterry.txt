The lottery balls fall, and all six numbers match. Then some winners buy an Audi, book a holiday in the US, and call an estate agent. A study says many lottery millionaires do these things.

The UK National Lottery started in 1994. Since then, it has made 3,000 millionaires. Together they won more than £8.5bn. On average, each won £2.8m. The study says their money also helped create 3,780 more millionaires among their children, family, and friends. The study was by Oxford Economics.

Many winners (59%) stop working right away. But 19% keep their normal jobs, and 31% do unpaid volunteer work. The study says 98% of winners’ spending stayed in the UK. It also says each winner’s spending supports six full-time jobs for one year.

The winners added almost £750m to GDP and created more than £500m in tax for the Exchequer. Most of the money went to property. Winners spent £2.72bn on their main homes, and £170m to pay off debt and mortgages.

Keeping income was important, so £2.125bn went into investments. Gifts to family and friends were £1.17bn. Cars and holidays were £680m.

Camelot, the company that runs the UK National Lottery, paid for the study to mark 3,000 winners. The study used research from 100 winners who won £1m or more. It found that the 3,000 winners bought 7,958 houses or flats in the UK, about 2.7 each, and spent £3.3bn. Most winners (82%) moved to a new main home and spent an average of £900,000.

Many new homes had extra features. Almost a third (29%) wanted a hot tub. A walk-in wardrobe was important for 28%. Almost a quarter (24%) chose a home behind electric gates. Also, 22% had a games room, and 7% put in a snooker table.

Bigger homes need more care. About 30% hired a cleaner, and 24% hired a gardener. A small number (5%) hired a beautician.

Audi was the top car choice for 16% of winners. Range Rovers and BMWs were also popular (11% each). Mercedes was 10%, and Land Rovers were 5%. Winners spent £463m on 17,190 cars. The average price of their favourite car was £46,116.

Holidays were important too. Most winners (68%) stayed in five-star hotels abroad. The US was the top place to visit for 27%, then the Caribbean for 9%. In the UK, caravan sales also gained. Over 18 years, 10% of millionaires bought a caravan, creating about £7.4m in sales.

Some winners started businesses. About 15% started their own business, 9% helped others start one, and 6% invested in or bought other people’s businesses. These businesses employ 3,195 people.

Andy Logan, who wrote the report, said the effect of a win spreads more than expected. He said it changes the lives of family and friends, and it also affects the UK economy because so much money is spent in the UK. He said each win creates a ripple effect in this generation and often the next.
