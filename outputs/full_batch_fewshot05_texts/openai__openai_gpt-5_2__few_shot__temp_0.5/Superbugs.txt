Dame Sally Davies, the Chief Medical Officer for England, says antibiotic resistance is as dangerous as international terrorism. This may sound too strong, but the danger may be even bigger. Each year, many more people die because of bacterial resistance than from terrorist attacks.

It is hard to measure the worldwide effect of antibiotic resistance in all bacteria. But the World Health Organization says that, for tuberculosis alone, multi-drug resistance causes more than 150,000 deaths each year. Antibiotic resistance is not just an idea. It is happening now, and it is like a war.

In the last 100 years, how long people live has changed a lot. At the start of the 1900s, life expectancy in the UK was about 47 years for a man and 50 for a woman. This was strongly affected by the very high number of babies who died. About one third of all deaths were in children under five, mostly because of infectious disease.

Today, a child born in Britain has a better than one in four chance of reaching 100 years old. We can thank public health systems, vaccines, and antibiotics for this. These tools prevent and treat illnesses caused by microorganisms, and this is how we mainly win the fight against disease.

We have also improved survival in intensive care. This is the area where I trained. In intensive care, antibiotic resistant organisms are very common. Strong antibiotics are used often because they are needed for life-threatening illness. These drugs kill many normal bacteria. But the bacteria left behind can be tougher and can learn ways to avoid antibiotic drugs.

When I was a new doctor in the late 1990s, I learned about Methicillin Resistant Staphylococcus Aureus (MRSA). MRSA is resistant to methicillin and all other penicillins. To fight it, we had only a few unusual drugs, including vancomycin and teicoplanin. These were meant to be our last lines of defence. But antibiotic resistant bacteria became more common, and new kinds of resistance appeared. Drugs we had hardly heard of became widely used. New last-line drugs replaced old ones. We got used to this, as the arms race between us and bacteria kept growing.

But the situation has been getting worse. In hospitals and GP surgeries, we have misused antibiotics by using them too often and without enough care. Some of the worst misuse has been outside healthcare too. Antibiotics entered the food chain through agriculture, by adding anti-bacterial drugs to livestock feed. We acted as if antibiotics would work forever. We also trusted that the pharmaceutical industry would always stay ahead.

Now this is no longer true. New, more resistant bacteria have been found. The vancomycin we used to depend on for MRSA was beaten. Vancomycin Resistant Staphylococcus Aureus (VRSA) appeared in hospitals. Other bacteria learned similar ways to resist drugs. Enterobacteria, usually found in the gut, also gained vancomycin resistance.

Today, infections with very strong, highly resistant organisms are common. The pharmaceutical industry is not keeping up. Fewer new anti-microbial drugs are being produced. It is getting harder to make new drugs that work against resistant strains. For every new attack method, bacteria quickly evolve a defence. The simple solutions have already been used up. Antibiotics are expensive to develop, are taken only for short courses, and can soon stop working because resistance evolves. So the pharmaceutical industry has less reason to make new drugs to fight these bacteria.

Almost as soon as antibiotics became widely used in the 1940s, the first signs of resistance appeared. At first, these cases seemed like small curiosities. The numbers were so low that they did not seem important. But now resistance is part of everyday medicine. Less than a century after the discovery of penicillin, we are starting to lose the fight.

Since the first MRSA deaths in otherwise healthy children in the US in 1998, deaths from MRSA infection in the US each year have risen to tens of thousands. This is more than the number of deaths caused by AIDS. Resistance in hospitals is everywhere. This is a war like no other. We need a cultural change in how we prescribe antibiotics. We also need to use fewer antibiotics in farming and agriculture. And we need to persuade pharmaceutical companies to develop these less profitable drugs.

During my working life, antibiotic resistance in healthcare has changed from a rare event into a problem on an epidemic scale. If we want to avoid going back to the time before antibiotics, with many more deaths, we must act boldly. Losing the advantage we have only recently gained against microorganisms in the fight for life would be unthinkable.
