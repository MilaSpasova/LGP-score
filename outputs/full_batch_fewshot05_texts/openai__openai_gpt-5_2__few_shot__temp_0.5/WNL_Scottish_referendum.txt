David Cameron said there was a “clear result” in the Scottish independence referendum. Scotland voted against leaving the 307-year-old union with England and Wales. The “no” side won by 10.6 points. Cameron said there should be no arguments and no new vote, because the Scottish people had decided. He welcomed Scotland staying in the UK.

Cameron promised a big change in how powers are shared across Great Britain. He said Scotland should get more powers, and England should also have changes. One idea was that English MPs at Westminster should vote on English-only issues.

Earlier, Scotland’s first minister, Alex Salmond, spoke at a Scottish National Party rally in Edinburgh. He said he accepted that Scotland had not, “at this stage,” decided to vote for independence. He called the vote a “triumph for democratic politics.” He said he would work with Westminster for Scotland and the rest of the UK. He warned leaders of the three main parties to keep their promises to give Scotland more powers. He also said the campaign reached people who had not been involved in politics before.

The yes campaign had some big wins. It got 53% in Glasgow, 57% in Dundee, and 51% in North Lanarkshire. But the no side won in 28 authorities. It won strongly in places like Edinburgh, Aberdeenshire, and Borders, and also won in the Western Isles. In the final count, no won 2,001,926 votes (55.3%), and yes won 1,617,989 (44.7%).

Cameron said constitutional changes, including in Scotland, would not happen until after the general election. He said changes for Scotland would go together with changes in England.

Cameron said he was delighted Scotland had kept the UK of four nations together. He said it was now time for the UK to come together and move forward. He said there should be a balanced and fair agreement for Scotland and also for England, Wales, and Northern Ireland.

Ed Miliband, the Labour leader, said the vote showed Scotland wanted change. He said Labour would give the Scottish parliament stronger powers, and that change should also happen across England and Wales and the whole UK.

Nick Clegg, the deputy prime minister, said the vote was a new chapter for Scotland and also for wider constitutional reform. He said a vote against independence was not a vote against change. He said the UK must deliver the new powers for Scotland on time and in full.

The result also brought risks of more political trouble. Some Conservative MPs threatened to rebel against Cameron’s late promise to quickly increase the Scottish parliament’s powers while protecting its spending.

Nigel Farage, leader of the UK Independence Party, said Cameron’s plan for more powers for England did not go far enough. He said the English are 86% of the union by population and had been left out for the last 18 years. He said Scottish MPs can still vote on England-only issues in the House of Commons, and he said English people want a fair settlement.

Cameron and the Queen both planned statements to calm tensions. Cameron wanted to “cement in” the no vote by explaining how he would deepen devolution for Scotland. This included giving the Scottish Parliament in Edinburgh more power over tax and welfare.

The Queen would make a written statement, and it was understood she would focus on reconciliation. Cameron wanted to move fast so the three main UK party leaders would keep their promises from the campaign. Gordon Brown had called this “home rule” within the UK.

Ministers said it was important to act quickly to avoid a repeat of the 1980 referendum in Quebec. They said the behaviour of Ontario helped the separatist cause, which almost won in a second referendum in 1995.

The no campaign felt relief. In the final days, polls said the result was very close, after a strong rise in support for Yes Scotland.
