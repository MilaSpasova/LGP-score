For 85 years, Pluto was only a small grey shape on school maps. But on 15 July, people saw Pluto clearly for the first time. The new pictures showed big mountain ranges made of solid water ice. They are as large as the Alps or the Rockies.

The New Horizons spacecraft sent these pictures about 4bn miles back to Earth. The pictures of Pluto and its large moon, Charon, are the high point of a mission that has lasted nearly 10 years.

Alan Stern, the main scientist for the mission, said the pictures were a “home run” for the team. He said the data look very beautiful, and Pluto and Charon are amazing.

A big surprise was finding mountains in the Kuiper belt. The Kuiper belt is the solar system’s “third zone”, where Pluto is, with about 100,000 smaller icy objects. John Spencer, a mission scientist, said the mountains seem to be about 11,000ft high and several hundred miles wide.

One detailed picture of the edge of Pluto showed no craters. This may mean the surface was covered again recently by geological activity. This activity could include geysers shooting ice into the air, or cryo-volcanoes that explode with ice instead of hot melted rock.

NASA also talked about Pluto’s old status as the ninth planet. In 2006, it was changed to a dwarf planet. At the NASA press conference, they first showed pictures of the sun and the eight official planets. Dwayne Brown, a NASA spokesman, said they had brought a blurred point of light into focus.

Stern said the pictures were only the start of what scientists will learn during the next year. Scientists think the mountains are made from water ice, with a thin layer of other ices, like methane and nitrogen. Spencer said you cannot make mountains from methane and nitrogen.

Scientists think water ice is strong enough to hold up big mountains. The methane and nitrogen are only a coating. The mountains on Pluto may have formed less than 100m years ago. That is very recent compared with the 4.56bn-year-old solar system. This suggests the close-up area, about 1% of Pluto’s surface, may still be active. These are the first pictures of ice mountains outside the moons of giant planets, and scientists now wonder what process made them.

The mountains and the smooth surface suggest that geological activity is filling in holes made by asteroid impacts. Scientists think this “paving” could come from heat inside Pluto that softens rock and ice, or from snowfalls that cover the surface. The pictures are so detailed that, if the spacecraft flew over London, people could see the runways at Heathrow airport.

Pluto is far away, about 5bn km. Because of this distance, New Horizons needs hours to send one picture. It will take 16 months to send all the data from the fly-by. The team also said the heart-shaped area on Pluto will be called Tombaugh Regio, to honour Clyde Tombaugh, who discovered Pluto in 1930.

The new view of Charon showed many different kinds of land. A long area of cliffs and deep cuts, about 1,000km long, suggests the surface has cracked in many places. This could also be from geological activity. The picture also showed a large canyon about 7 to 9km deep.

Cathy Olkin, a mission scientist, said the new picture of Charon surprised the team. She said there is a lot of important science in just one picture. Scientists think Pluto is about two thirds rock, covered in a lot of ice. The surface temperature is about minus 230C. The mission cost £460m. As it moves deeper into the Kuiper belt, scientists hope it will help them understand the early solar system and how planets formed, and maybe even how Earth formed.

Andrew Coates, head of planetary science at the Mullard Space Science Laboratory, said Kuiper belt objects are the building blocks of the outer solar system. He said they are very cold, like a deep freeze, and this helps keep the history of the solar system.

In August 2015, the mission scientists will choose which of two objects to visit next. NASA says the spacecraft may keep working until the mid-2030s. Then its plutonium power will run out. It will shut down and drift toward the edge of the solar system and into deep space.

New Horizons also watched smaller parts of the Pluto system. Pluto has four other moons: Nix, Hydra, Styx and Kerberos. A first early picture of Hydra showed it has an uneven shape. Scientists estimate its size is about 43 by 33km. John Grunsfeld from NASA said the mission shows why basic scientific research is important.

He said the mission had nine years to build hopes about what it would see near Pluto and Charon. Now scientists have the first sample of the information collected at the closest point, and it is much better than they expected.

The observations also suggest Hydra’s surface is probably covered with water ice. Future pictures will show more about how this moon, and the other moon, formed billions of years ago.
