Governments in Europe want an easy answer to rising unemployment. But in the EU areas with the worst problems, more people are still without jobs.

In Elmas, a small town near Cagliari in Sardinia, the mayor Valter Piscedda thinks he has a plan. He wants to pay some residents to leave the town to look for work in other parts of Europe. The town council will pay for 10 unemployed local people to take intensive English classes, buy a cheap flight, and search for jobs abroad.

Piscedda told the Guardian that many young people come to him almost every day. They feel desperate because they cannot find work. Some still hope to find work in Elmas. Others are so discouraged that they do not want to stay and wait. They want to get work experience abroad, and life experience too. He said the council should do what it can to help those who want to go.

Italy’s economy is still weak. Sardinia, like much of southern and central Italy, has high unemployment. Italy’s National Institute of Statistics, Istat, said the jobless rate was 17.7% in the second quarter of 2014. More than 54% of people under 25 are out of work.

The programme is called Adesso Parto (Now I’m leaving). The Elmas council set aside €12,000. It will be given on a first-come, first-served basis. Applicants must be 18 to 50 years old, unemployed, and have lived in Elmas for three years. They do not need a university degree. Their yearly income must be no more than €15,000.

Some people say it is a sensitive idea, because many Italians, including many young graduates, leave Italy every year. Piscedda says he is not helping a brain drain. He believes the people who leave may return and “give me back 100 times what they were given.” He also says the plan is mainly to help the people who need help the most.

He said it is for people with no other support, like a last chance. He wants them to have dignity and not have to ask friends for money or put pressure on families that cannot help.

Earlier in 2014, the council started another plan. It gave businesses money to hire young workers from Elmas. Piscedda said they offered 20 positions, but received 120 applications.

People in Elmas have different opinions about the new plan. Alessandro Macis said there is little work in the town. He said going abroad to learn about work and other cultures can be very valuable. He gave an example of a friend’s son who went to London. He started as a waiter, then became a cook, and is learning English.

Others did not understand the plan. Consuelo Melis, who works in a local café, said it seemed strange. She asked why the town does not use the money to help people stay. On Twitter, some people also reacted with disbelief. Marco Patavino called it “the state’s admission of defeat.” Carlo Mazzaggio said institutions were “raising the white flag.”

Piscedda said he is not stopping because of online criticism. He said the critics are probably not people in need. He said he deals with people’s problems every day and must try to solve them. He said if these people had another option, they would not ask for help.

He added that the work he can create as mayor is only temporary, like cleaning a piazza or cleaning streets. He said these jobs only give a little money for a few months, and he wants to do more than that.
