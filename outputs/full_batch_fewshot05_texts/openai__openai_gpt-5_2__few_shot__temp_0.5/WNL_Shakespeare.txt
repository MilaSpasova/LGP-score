People around the world share William Shakespeare, and London also claims him. But Stratford-upon-Avon wants to spend 2016 celebrating him as their own man. Shakespeare was born in this market town in Warwickshire in 1564, and he died there 400 years ago.

Paul Edmondson, head of learning and research at the Shakespeare Birthplace Trust, says Stratford was very important in Shakespeare’s life. Some people think Shakespeare left Stratford and his family, went to London to make money, and only came back to die. But Edmondson says Stratford is where Shakespeare bought land and property, kept his library, and lived, read, and thought. Stratford plans to use the year to show the importance of “Shakespeare, the man of Stratford.”

John Aubrey, a writer who was born 11 years after Shakespeare died, said Shakespeare was not especially special. Aubrey said Shakespeare acted very well and that his plays were popular. But many people now disagree with Aubrey. The 400th anniversary of Shakespeare’s death will be marked across Britain and around the world.

Macbeth will open in Singapore, and Romeo and Juliet in Brussels. Shakespeare’s Globe is finishing the first world tour in theatre history, taking Hamlet to almost every country. North Korea is still not included. In London, they are also creating a temporary cinema with 37 screens, one for each Shakespeare play, along the South Bank.

The National Theatre, the Royal Shakespeare Company, and many other theatre companies will mark the anniversary. Some shows will be very traditional. Others will be very different, like Spymonkey’s Complete Deaths, which goes through the 74 deaths in the plays—75 if you count a fly squashed in Titus Andronicus—by stabbing, poisoning, smothering, and smashing. There will also be hundreds of lectures, recitals, international academic conferences, films, concerts, operas, and major exhibitions.

Even though Shakespeare was famous when he was alive, there are not many documents about his life. His plays might not have survived if his friends and fellow actors had not collected every piece they could find—drafts, prompt scripts, actors’ parts, and 17 plays not known in any other version—into the First Folio, published in 1623, seven years after his death.

Actor Mark Rylance has called the First Folio his favourite book in the world. Most of the surviving First Folios will be shown, including copies from the British Library and the Bodleian Library, and a damaged copy recently found in France. Other valuable documents will be shown in an exhibition at Somerset House in London, organized by the National Archives and King’s College London. These include four of Shakespeare’s six known signatures, and they are all a little different.

The exhibition, called By Me, William Shakespeare, will include his will. It will also include court papers about how Shakespeare and other actors took down a theatre north of the Thames and rebuilt it as the Globe on the South Bank. It will also show records of payments from the royal treasury for Boxing Day performances for James I and Queen Anne.

The outgoing Globe director, Dominic Dromgoole, joked that Shakespeare was a true Londoner, though he admitted Stratford-upon-Avon also has a claim. Stratford says the town made and educated Shakespeare. His old school room is being restored with a £1.4m Heritage Lottery grant, and it will open as a permanent visitor attraction.

Shakespeare bought New Place, the second best house in the town, and he died there. Literary legend says he died on St George’s Day, 23 April, the same day as his birth. Edmondson says you do not buy a house like New Place and not live there. He also says many people have underestimated how important Stratford was to Shakespeare.

Edmondson believes that after Shakespeare bought New Place in 1597, he did all his thinking there. He believes the late plays, including The Tempest, were at least planned in his library, and probably written there. The Shakespeare Birthplace Trust calls New Place “the jewel in the crown” of the 400th anniversary celebrations. But in reality, the site is now an empty space.

Shakespeare’s house was pulled down 300 years ago. The house built after it, probably using some original parts, was destroyed in 1759 by a clergyman, Francis Gastrell, during an argument about taxes. Gastrell had already cut down Shakespeare’s mulberry tree. People said Shakespeare sat and worked under it, but Gastrell was annoyed by tourists looking into his garden.

The empty space in Stratford has never been filled. But a five-year archaeology project has explored the area. News that Shakespeare’s kitchen was found in partly surviving cellars spread around the world. For the anniversary, the whole site is being shown again, with the foundations marked and the garden restored. Edmondson says that without Stratford, there would have been no Shakespeare.
