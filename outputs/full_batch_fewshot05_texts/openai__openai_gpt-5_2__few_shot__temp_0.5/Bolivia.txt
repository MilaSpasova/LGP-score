There is a big international disagreement about Bolivia. It is about whether Bolivia’s indigenous Indian tribes can chew coca leaves. Coca leaves are the main ingredient in cocaine.

Bolivia got a special exemption from the 1961 Single Convention on Narcotic Drugs. This international agreement guides global drug policy. The exemption lets indigenous people in Bolivia chew coca leaves.

Bolivia said the convention conflicted with its new constitution from 2009. The constitution says Bolivia must protect native and ancestral coca as cultural heritage. It also says coca “in its natural state … is not a narcotic”.

South American Indians have chewed coca leaves for centuries. People say the leaves give energy and may have medical benefits. People who support Bolivia praised it for protecting indigenous rights. Danny Kushlick from the Transform Drug Policy Foundation said Bolivia’s action was “inspirational and groundbreaking”. He said countries can change how they follow UN drug conventions.

But the UN’s International Narcotics Control Board (INCB) said Bolivia is harming the international drug control system. Many countries opposed Bolivia’s request, including the UK, the US, Italy, Sweden, the Netherlands, and Russia.

The UK told the UN it respects the cultural importance of coca in Bolivia. But the UK said it worries the exemption could increase coca growing and could increase coca being used for the cocaine trade. The UK said this could weaken international law and the global fight against the drug trade.

The right to chew coca leaf in the Andean region of South America was removed in 1964. At that time, Bolivia had a dictatorship and signed the convention. Bolivia had 25 years to carry out the ban. The deadline ended in 1989, and the issue has been disputed since then.

In 2011, Bolivia formally told the UN it was leaving the convention. Bolivia’s President, Evo Morales, is a former coca producer. On Friday, Bolivia joined the convention again, but with an exemption that allows coca chewing.

This is the first time something like this has happened in the history of UN drug-control treaties. Some people worry other countries may ask for changes too. Russia said the move will increase illegal cocaine and said it sets a dangerous example for other states that want a more liberal drug-control system.

In the UK, the Home Affairs Select Committee said the UK government should support Bolivia’s request. It said it is important for countries to stay inside the single convention. Bolivia’s return could be blocked only if one third or more of the 184 countries in the convention oppose it. Some people suspect the US and UK are strongly trying to persuade other countries to block Bolivia.

Nancie Prud’homme from the International Centre on Human Rights and Drug Policy criticized the coordinated opposition. She said the objections are legally questionable. She said they support an unfair and too broad rule and ignore cultural and indigenous rights that support Bolivia.

The ban on coca chewing was based on a 1950 report by the UN Commission of Inquiry on the Coca Leaf. Supporters of drug liberalization say the report was not supported by evidence. In 1949, Howard B Fonda, the head of the Commission, spoke against coca chewing before the inquiry started. He said chewing coca leaves was harmful and said his studies would support ending the habit.

In Bolivia, growing coca leaves is legal and licensed. People say this policy helped reduce cocaine production in the country. Some experts think the Bolivian model could help other countries.
