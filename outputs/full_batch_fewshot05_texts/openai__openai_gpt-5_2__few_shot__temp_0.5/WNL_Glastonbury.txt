Glastonbury Festival wants to reduce plastic water bottles. This is part of a long-term plan to become the world’s most environmentally friendly outdoor music festival.

Organizers want to stop people using single-use bottles. Each year, about one million plastic bottles are used at the 900-acre site in Somerset, and a lot of plastic is left behind.

The festival will give stainless-steel reusable bottles to 2,000 road crew and band members. More reusable bottles will be sold to people at the festival. The 140,000 ticket holders are also asked to bring reusable bottles. They can fill them at 400 drinking-water taps around the site.

Lucy Smith, Glastonbury’s green issues organizer, said the UK has very good water, but many people still want bottled water.

She said this idea comes before a plan for Glastonbury 2015. In 2015, the festival wants to replace all plastic beer glasses and plastic cutlery with reusable items. The goal is to remove the plastic waste left on the large rural site.

Environmentalists say there are about 150 million tonnes of plastic waste on the planet and in the oceans. They say it harms ecosystems and kills wildlife.

Organizers hope to make Glastonbury the world’s greenest greenfield festival. They want to copy parts of America’s Burning Man festival in Nevada. Burning Man is a “leave-no-trace” event, where people must take away everything they bring.

People going to Glastonbury are also asked to use public transport or share cars with friends. Smith said they want to be as sustainable as possible, but cleaning up after 140,000 people is hard, and they cannot put bins everywhere.

Campaigners say plastic water bottles can take hundreds or even thousands of years to fully break down. Making them also causes more harm to the environment. Millions of barrels of oil are used to make plastic bottles, and moving mineral water around the world creates more carbon emissions.

About 13 billion plastic water bottles are sold in the UK each year, but only about one in five is recycled.

Smith said festival-goers should use tap water instead of buying bottled water. The tap water comes from large underground reservoirs, not old water tanks that gave water with a lot of chlorine. The charity WaterAid will set up water kiosks around the site. They will have reusable bottles and cups, and they will offer free refills. In 2015, the kiosks, based on ones found in Africa, will also be DJ booths at night.

Organizers say almost half of the rubbish left on site was recycled in 2013. They also say there will be 15,000 recycling bins across the festival grounds in 2014.

Some critics still attack Glastonbury. They say it is becoming more corporate. One critic, Iron Maiden’s Bruce Dickinson, said he will never bring his band to Glastonbury. He called it “the most bourgeois thing on the planet.”

The weather forecast was good. The festival was expected to be sunny and dry. Experts said it would not be like 1985, the windiest year; 1997, the muddiest; or 2005, the “year of thunder”.
