A new World Health Organization (WHO) study says more than one third of women around the world (35.6%) will face physical or sexual violence at some time in their life. This violence is usually from a male partner. The report says 30% of women are attacked by a partner. It also says 38% of murders of women are done by an intimate partner.

Dr Margaret Chan, director general of the WHO, said this shows violence against women is a global health problem on a very large scale. She said health systems can and must do more to help women who face violence.

The report says the highest levels are in Africa. There, 45.6% of women will suffer physical or sexual violence. In low- and middle-income Europe, the rate is 27.2%. In high-income countries, 32.7% of women will face violence at some time in their lives.

Among women who suffer violence, 42% get injuries. This may be the first time healthcare staff notice the violence at home and can offer help. Violence badly harms women’s health. Some women come to hospital with broken bones. Others have pregnancy problems and mental illness.

The WHO made two reports: one about how common violence is, and one with guidelines for healthcare staff on how to help women. The work was done by Dr Claudia Garcia-Moreno at WHO and Professor Charlotte Watts from the London School of Hygiene and Tropical Medicine.

Garcia-Moreno said they compared data from all over the world for the first time. They looked at partner violence and sexual violence by non-partners, and how these affect health. These health problems include HIV and other sexually transmitted infections, depression, women turning to alcohol, unwanted pregnancies, and low-birthweight babies.

Violence rates are different in different regions, but Garcia-Moreno said they are too high everywhere. Data from 81 countries shows that in high-income countries, 23.2% of women will suffer physical and/or sexual violence from a partner in their lives. The global figure for women attacked by partners is 30%.

The report says more sexual assaults and rapes by acquaintances or strangers are reported in high-income countries than elsewhere. It says 12.6% of women in wealthy countries will be sexually attacked by a non-partner in their lives. This is higher than the African rate of 11.9%. But the report also says data on these crimes is not well collected in all regions.

The authors say earlier research shows that women with more education are less likely to suffer violence. Women with jobs are also less likely, but not in all regions. Watts said social norms must be addressed. She asked what society thinks is acceptable, and said in some places it is not OK, but not everywhere.

Garcia-Moreno said the numbers are a warning for everyone to pay more attention. She said people have recognized the problem more in the past decade, but it is complex. She said there is no vaccine or pill.

New WHO clinical and policy guidelines recommend training healthcare staff to recognize signs of domestic violence and sexual assault. But they do not support general screening. The guidelines say there is no case for asking every woman in a clinic if she has faced violence.

Garcia-Moreno said that if a woman comes back many times with injuries that are not explained, staff should ask about domestic violence. She said that when she was in medical school, she did not learn about this. Later, she sometimes felt something else was happening, but did not think of domestic violence. She said now she would do the interview very differently.
