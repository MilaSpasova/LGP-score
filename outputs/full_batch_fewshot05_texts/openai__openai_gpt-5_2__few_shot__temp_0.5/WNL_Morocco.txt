The Moroccan city of Ouarzazate is used to big projects. It is near the Sahara Desert. It is also a centre for Morocco’s film industry, called “Ouallywood”. Many famous films and shows were filmed there, like Lawrence of Arabia, The Mummy, The Living Daylights, and Game of Thrones.

Now Ouarzazate, called the “door of the desert”, is the place for a new big project: four solar power plants linked together. With hydro and wind power, these plants will help Morocco get nearly half of its electricity from renewable energy by 2020. Morocco also hopes to have extra electricity to sell to Europe. Morocco wants to use its deserts to become a world leader in solar power.

When the whole project is finished, it will be the largest concentrated solar power plant in the world. The first part, called Noor 1, will start working in November 2015. It uses mirrors. This technology is less common and costs more than photovoltaic panels like the ones on many roofs. But it can keep making electricity after the sun goes down.

People have known for many years that deserts have strong solar power. After the Chernobyl nuclear accident in 1986, German scientist Gerhard Knies calculated that the world’s deserts get enough energy in a few hours to cover human power needs for one year. The hard part is collecting this energy and moving it to the places where people live.

Noor 1 has 500,000 crescent-shaped mirrors. They shine in the desert. There are 800 rows, and they slowly move to follow the sun.

When all four plants are finished, they will cover an area as big as Rabat, Morocco’s capital. They will produce 580 mega-watts (MW) of electricity, enough for about a million homes. Noor 1 can produce 160MW.

Morocco’s Environment Minister, Hakima el-Haite, says solar energy could change the region in this century like oil did in the last century. But she says the project started because Morocco has urgent problems. Morocco is not an oil producer. It imports 94% of its energy as fossil fuels. This hurts the state budget. Morocco also used to pay subsidies for fossil fuels, which cost a lot. When they learned about solar energy, they thought, “Why not?”

By 2020, solar energy will be one third of Morocco’s renewable energy. Wind and hydro will each be one third too.

El-Haite says Morocco is very proud of the project. She says it is the most important solar plant in the world.

Each parabolic mirror is 12 metres high. It focuses sunlight on a steel pipe. Inside the pipe is a “heat transfer solution” (HTF). This liquid is heated to 393C. Then it goes to a heat engine, where it helps make steam. The steam turns turbines to make electricity.

The HTF is a synthetic thermal oil. It is pumped to a heat tank with molten sands that can store heat for three hours. This lets the plant give power at night. The mirrors are placed in layers to reduce damage from sand blown by desert winds.

Technicians say the Noor 2 and 3 plants, planned to open in 2017, will store energy for up to eight hours. This could lead to solar power all day and all night in the Sahara and nearby areas.

Project director Rashid al-Bayad says the biggest challenge was finishing on time and reaching the needed performance.

Even while Noor 1 is almost ready, Morocco has bigger plans outside the country. Ahmed Baroudi, a manager at Société d’Investissements Energétiques, says they are working on high-tension power lines to cover the south of Morocco and Mauritania as a first step. He says the project could reach even farther, even to the Middle East. He says the final objective given by the king is Mecca.

It is not clear if this will happen. But Morocco’s solar agency, Masen, says exporting solar power could bring stability inside countries and between countries. Morocco is talking with Tunisia. Sending energy north across the Mediterranean is still an important goal.

Masen spokeswoman Maha el-Kadiri says exporting to Europe may be possible. But first, they must build new interconnectors, because they do not exist yet. She says these new connections would not go through the existing one in Spain.

Spain has stopped new solar projects because it does not have enough interconnectors to send energy to France. The EU has a goal: by 2020, 10% of each member country’s power should be able to be sent to other countries by cable.

For now, Morocco is mainly using solar power to meet its own needs and be more independent. In the future, it could also use solar power for water desalination. Morocco is having more drought as the climate gets warmer. Officials know they are moving fast in what is the most advanced renewable energy programme in the Middle East and North Africa.

El-Kadiri says Morocco is at the “avante-garde” of solar.

About $9bn has been invested in the Noor Complex. Much of the money comes from international groups like the European Investment Bank and the World Bank. The Moroccan government guarantees the project. Energy subsidies from King Mohammed VI, which are not explained in detail, have kept the cost from being passed to energy users.

One month before Noor 1 starts, more than a thousand workers, mostly Moroccan, are still finishing the work. They are fixing electric wires, removing scaffolding, and wrapping insulation around steel pipes. They work 12-hour shifts with the Atlas Mountains behind them. They wear hard hats, safety shoes, and ear plugs.

Hajar Lakhael is 25 years old. She is an environment and security manager from Meknes. She says the building is done, and now they will see how the project works when it starts. She says it is like preparing for a big performance.

People around the world will watch with interest.
