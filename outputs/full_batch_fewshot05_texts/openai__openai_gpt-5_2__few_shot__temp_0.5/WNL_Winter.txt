Maria sits on a black plastic chair in a community centre on a cold Tuesday afternoon. She is waiting for her number to be called. She is number 34.

When it is her turn, Maria goes forward. She picks up a brown paper bag with basic food, like pasta, eggs, and corn flakes. She can also choose butternut squash or carrots for this week’s vegetables.

Maria is the 34th “client” today at the East Hampton Food Pantry. This food pantry is a community project. It is only a few streets away from some of the most expensive homes in the world.

The organizers think that by the end of the day, more than 400 families will come. They will get their weekly food parcel to help them through the cold, dark Long Island winter.

In summer, the Hamptons are a group of old ocean towns and villages about 100 miles from Manhattan. In summer, very rich people spend time there. But after Labor Day in early September, many rich people close their mansions and go back to places like Manhattan or Beverly Hills. Then the hard life of the mostly immigrant community shows more.

“The people who come here are rich and famous but we who live here are not,” Maria says. In summer she works 14-hour days cleaning mansions. In winter she can go months with no work.

Maria laughs when asked if she has enough money. “There is no work in the winter, only in the summertime,” she says. Maria, like many workers in the Hamptons, is from Latin America. “Here, lots of people live in a single room because they can’t pay the rent.”

She says some families with up to five children live crowded in basements. They still pay more than $1,000 a month in rent. “People come here looking for work but, in the winter, there is nothing.”

Maria says many friends cannot pay for heat or medicine. Many would be hungry without the East Hampton Food Pantry. It is one of several food pantries in the town.

Vicki Littman is the chairperson of the East Hampton Food Pantry. The pantry gave out more than 31,000 food parcels in 2015. She says more and more people are asking for help.

“Once Labor Day comes and the season is over and people’s hours start to be cut back, our numbers go up to about 400 families a week,” she says. “When they come to us on Tuesday, they get two to three days’ worth of food. Without us, they would struggle that much more.”

Littman says it is hard for outsiders to see poverty in a place famous for luxury. She says summer visitors are shocked when they hear about food pantries. They know the glamorous side, with galas, beaches, and mansions.

But she says many workers depend on summer jobs to survive the winter. These workers include landscapers, nannies, and waitresses. Visitors often do not notice them.

She also says some older people must choose between paying for medicine, paying bills, or buying food.

Littman says the town has lost many people in important jobs, like teachers, police officers, and even doctors and dentists. They cannot afford to live there. The food pantry board wants to do more so people can stay.

Housing is the biggest cost in the Hamptons. One of the most expensive properties in the country costs $147m. It is hedge fund manager Barry Rosenstein’s 18-acre beachfront estate at 67 Further Lane, near Maidstone Golf Club. The club is described as “the most elite, prestigious and difficult to get into” of the Hamptons clubs.

Larry Cantwell is East Hampton’s town supervisor and a lifelong resident. He says homes often sell for more than $25m. High prices at the top also raise prices for smaller apartments.

“Finding your first home is a challenge in an area like this,” Cantwell says. He says it is not only poor people. Working- and middle-class families also struggle. He says if you can find a home to buy anywhere in East Hampton for $500,000, you are very lucky.

Cantwell says more than half the town’s homes are empty most of the year. The winter population can drop to about 10,000, compared with 80,000 in August.

He says it is like “two cities.” There is a lot of wealth, but most of it is in second homes used only in summer. Many other people live there all year.

Cantwell says the community began as a farming and fishing place. It was a working-class community. Today, he says, it is almost impossible to save enough money to buy a first house while working as a farmer or fisherman. He says it is also hard for police officers, teachers, young professionals, and others to find a place to live, and many cannot afford a home.

For homeless people in the Hamptons, life can mean spending a lot of time on a bus. Different houses of worship work together so homeless people have a place to sleep in winter. Churches across the north and south fork of Long Island each take one night. About 50 homeless people are bussed between them every day.

Eddie Vallone, 22, rides that bus each night. He says people think the Hamptons are rich and have no problems. But he says there are many problems, especially drugs.

“It’s hard to really grasp — ‘OK, the summer is coming to an end. What am I going to do for the winter?’” Vallone says at Maureen’s Haven, a charity that coordinates the homeless shelter program. “I want to work but there’s no work to be done.”

Vallone cleans pools and does odd jobs on luxury estates. He says that if he saves carefully and does not impulse-buy, his summer money can last until November. “But, work doesn’t start again until May or the beginning of June.”
