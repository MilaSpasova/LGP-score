A thick layer of bird droppings sits on a gold-painted rail at one of Britain’s most expensive homes. Pigeon skeletons are on the floor among broken mirrors, and water runs through broken ceiling edges. This place is called The Towers. It is a £30m palace on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London. Its serious damage has been kept secret until now.

The Towers is one of ten mansions in the middle of The Bishops Avenue. This road is at the center of London’s fast-rising property market. These mansions have been almost empty since they were bought about 25 years ago. It is believed they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. Their Greek-style columns are breaking. Their swimming pools with mosaic tiles are full of rubble. Nature has taken over so much that owls have moved in.

This empty and broken look appears again and again along this street. Lloyds Bank says The Bishops Avenue is the second most expensive street in Britain. At the same time, more and more people in London cannot afford a home, as house prices rise by 11.2% a year. Yet 16 mansions on the most expensive part of The Bishops Avenue are empty. Many are behind locked gates. Their windows are covered with steel bars, and their gardens are overgrown. Guard dogs patrol the grounds.

Across the street is another empty mansion worth £18m. Its windows are smashed, and its walls are covered in anti-climb paint. Another has metal bars on its windows and has been sold for £20m.

But prices still rise. Dryades, a mansion that until recently was owned by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007 and is believed to be worth about £30m today. Heath Lodge was the place where fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou was murdered in 1984 with a silver bullet. Heath Lodge is worth £13m today. It was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

For people who struggle to pay for housing in one of the world’s most expensive cities, these empty, falling buildings can feel painful. One security guard on the avenue said it was very frustrating to watch so many tens of thousands of square feet of space—enough to house dozens of people—fall apart.

In the past, royals with oil money from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia were among the first to come to this curved road near Hampstead Heath. Iranians came here after the fall of the shah. Now Chinese buyers are following Russians and Kazakhs. They have spent millions to get an address that estate agents say is as famous as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions have been for sale for £65m and £38m. They promise lots of Italian marble, lifts with leather padding, and luxury panic rooms.

But on the land around the empty mansions, stone fountains are breaking and lawns have turned into muddy bogs. Inside one mansion, water drips through a huge crystal chandelier onto a thick carpet that is rotting under parts of a fallen ceiling. Moss grows through broken bricks. Broken mirror tiles are spread across a bathroom. The swimming pool has about a foot of dirty salty water, and flowers grow through the tiles. Wooden slats push outward from the sauna.

The most dramatic ruin is The Towers. It is a large mansion set among acres of hornbeams, oak trees, and lime trees. Pigeons fill its huge high halls. Water pours through three floors. The walls have turned bright green from algae. The water drops into a large, empty basement swimming pool. Unopened wooden crates marked “bullet-proof glass” show that the previous owners feared for security.

Today, very few people live on The Bishops Avenue full time. A guard walking on the pavement outside one mansion said the owners were not there. Another guard outside Royal Mansion would not say if anyone was home. A staff member at another mansion warned the Guardian about the guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare mogul with personal wealth of £45m, said he is one of the few permanent residents.

He said, “It’s not a neighbourly place, where you can chat over the fence. To be honest, I have never seen what my neighbours look like. Next door, a Saudi princess spent £35m on a new house and I’ve never seen her. There are about three houses that are lived in 24/7 and half of the properties are occupied three to six months a year. The other half, who knows if they come or not?”

These broken mansions worth many millions show a property culture where the world’s richest people treat British homes as investments. One Hyde Park, an apartment block in Knightsbridge, is another example. More than half the flats there are registered with the council as empty or second homes.

Even so, people on the avenue talk about building £5m apartments instead of £50m mansions, to try to bring residents back.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, plans to rebuild one of the most valuable sites on the avenue into 20 apartments. They would have a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

He said, “If you build a big house and try and sell for £30m to £40m, it won’t sell. Locals won’t buy and so you have to bring in overseas buyers.”

But using these empty properties to help solve the housing crisis still seems far away. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some of the ruined homes could become affordable homes. He said the land price would be too high.

He said, “Very wealthy people own property there. Sometimes they live in them and sometimes they don’t.”
