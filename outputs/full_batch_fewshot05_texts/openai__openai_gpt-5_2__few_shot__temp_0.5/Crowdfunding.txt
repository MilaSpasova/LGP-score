This new light could be a very good invention. It is like Trevor Baylis’s wind-up radio in the 1990s, before the internet. It is a cheap light that uses gravity for free power. It could stop people using dangerous kerosene lamps in Africa and India.

A British designer, Patrick Hunt, wanted money to start selling his invention. He tried banks and venture capital, but it did not work. He said getting funding was slow and complex. The idea was not proven, so nobody wanted to take the risk.

Then he tried crowdfunding on a US website called Indiegogo, which had just opened in the UK. In five days, he reached his target and raised £36,200. His campaign was very popular. In 40 days at the end of 2012, he raised £400,000.

The LED light uses a dynamo. The dynamo is powered when a 10kg bag of rocks goes down. The bag is attached to the light. People lift it to about 2m high. When it slowly falls to the ground, it makes enough power for 30 minutes of light.

Hunt is getting ready to make the light in China. He will test the market again by sending 1,000 lights to Africa. After that, he hopes to make millions of units.

He is part of a new group of entrepreneurs who use crowdfunding to get money. Another new site is InvestingZone. It matches wealthy people with start-up entrepreneurs.

Indiegogo does not give shares. Instead, people who donate can get “perks” at different donation levels. People who helped fund Hunt’s light could feel good about helping poorer people, and they also got their own light.

Danae Ringelmann, a co-founder of Indiegogo, says the “gravity light” shows how crowdfunding can work well. She says it can test an entrepreneur’s idea. She says finance can be fast and efficient, and it is not about knowing decision makers at a bank or knowing people in venture capital. It is about showing value to customers and fans, and getting them to support and fund the idea.

She also says that even if an idea does not get funding, it is still worth testing. Then you can learn faster and not waste time.

Ringelmann lives in the US. She began her career as a Wall Street analyst. In 2008, she quit and used her skills to help friends in the arts raise money. At first, the site focused on film, and it launched at the Sundance Film Festival that year.

Five years later, the site was raising about $2m a week for new businesses that were starting or growing. In December 2012, it started euro and sterling services to grow in Europe. It says Britain is its third biggest market. International activity is up 41% since December.

There are many competitors, such as Kickstarter, Seedrs, and Funding Circle. But Ringelmann says Indiegogo is different because anyone can start a campaign. No project is too wacky.

Indiegogo takes a 4% fee from successful campaigns. If a campaign does not reach its target, users can choose to return all the money to contributors for free. Or they can keep the money raised, but then the fee is 9%.

A British woman, Lauren Pears, raised £100,000 on the site to open a “cat café” in London. It is called Lady Dinah’s Cat Emporium. It has not opened yet. It is described as a place where people can sit in a comfortable wingback chair, have a hot cup of tea, read a book, and be with a cat.

Ringelmann says some campaigns are rejected by venture capitalists. They may say it is a great idea, but they do not know if the market wants it. It could be a gadget that nobody cares about. She says entrepreneurs can run an Indiegogo campaign, even before they launch the project. The campaign can be proof that a market exists. Then venture capitalists may believe there is a market.

She says crowdfunding lets you test your market, pricing, and features. It can also help you find new revenue streams and get important feedback.

Ringelmann says many people have ideas, but what matters is doing the work well. She says if you are afraid someone will steal your idea and do it faster and better, then you are not the right person to do it. She says you need confidence to move fast and to learn.

She says expanding into Europe is part of a dream to make finance more open to everyone. Indiegogo also made a deal with a web transaction provider. This will let people pay with local card services like Maestro in the UK and Carte Bleue in France, and also with PayPal. More than 7,000 campaigns are live on the site.

Crowdfunding has grown as another choice instead of banks. But big investors often want a stake in a business, not only to donate. This may start to change in the UK with the launch of InvestingZone.
