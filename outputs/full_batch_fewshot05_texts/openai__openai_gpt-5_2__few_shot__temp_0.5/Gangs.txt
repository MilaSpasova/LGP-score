Junior Smart knows a lot about gangs. He is 36 years old. He says his life has two very different parts.

When he was in his late teens, his mother died. After that, he joined a gang in south London. He says the gang helped him because he felt a big empty space in his life.

He says, “They became my new support group.” At first it was just for fun. Later it became serious and more about making money. The gang started doing crimes.

At school, Smart failed his GCSEs. Later he took them again at college and passed all of them. He got a full-time office job in administration and also worked as a DJ. But he was also making illegal money with the gang.

Later, police arrested him for serious drug crimes. He went to prison for 12 years. He says he stopped feeling “invincible” right away. He says the first night after his arrest was the biggest wake-up call of his life. He says he was living two lives, and he spent a long time trying to fix himself.

Today, Junior Smart runs a team with 12 full-time workers and six volunteers. Their goal is to help young criminals and gang members stop committing crimes. Many workers are ex-offenders like Smart. A few are still serving their sentences, but they are allowed out during the day to help because people believe they have changed. The team works with the police, the probation service, and other voluntary organizations. They help people who feel trapped and scared in violent gangs across London.

Smart says his journey from gang member to mentor began in prison. He saw that the prison system was failing many prisoners. He remembers a drug addict he became friends with who kept coming back to prison. Smart says he could not believe nothing was being done. He says many prisoners knew what they needed to change, but the prison system only dealt with the main crime.

He gives an example. One man had a £300-a-week cocaine habit. He paid for it by burglary. The man told Smart he would go into a house even when he knew people were there. Smart says the man’s drug addiction was not dealt with. Smart says it felt like a “revolving door,” with people going in and out of prison.

Because of cases like this, Smart started working as a prison “listener.” This is a prisoner who helps new prisoners and talks to them in their first days. In prison, Smart made a “little plan” for when he got out. He wanted ex-offenders to use their experience to help others avoid returning to prison. He was released early after five years. He says he then got the chance to use his model.

Smart also talks about gangs and government action. After the riots of last August, the government promised to crack down on gangs. Smart says some police work has been good, especially arresting gang members. But he criticizes the government approach. He says they think arresting a gang leader solves the problem. He agrees with a report saying it can even make things worse. He says when the leader is removed, the whole gang can become unstable.

He explains that when the top person is arrested, people fight for power. They ask who was most loyal and who had most respect. He says the gang can act more violently. He says arresting leaders often does not have a long-term effect, and it does not even give the community a break.

He says it can make streets more dangerous. In the empty space, gangs recruit and control more young people. People take sides. Other gangs may think the gang is weak and start fights between estates. He says when the leader is in prison, the leader can make new alliances. Or when the leader is released, the leader may try to take control again, and that is when violence happens.

Smart says gangs are now recruiting more in primary schools. The youngest members are called “tinies.” He says tinies can be as young as eight to eleven years old. Gangs use them to protect older members from risk. He says the youngest members often do street dealing or even stabbing.

Smart says there is not enough government money and commitment for long-term rehabilitation. He says the problems are very big, especially in the current economic climate. He says it is hard to talk to a young person who earns £300 a week through illegal work. He says it is even harder now because unemployment is high and benefits have been cut.

Still, he says his project has well over 1,000 clients and it is working. Fewer than 20% of the people who get help commit crimes again. Smart says he will not give up on anyone. He says, “I don’t think that about anybody.”
