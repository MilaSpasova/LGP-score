You cannot legally smoke a cigarette inside a bar in New Orleans, Louisiana. Many cities have banned indoor smoking, but New Orleans is different. New Orleans makes money from tourists and advertises a “let the good times roll” lifestyle. So an indoor smoking ban here may bring special problems.

The Wall Street Journal reported that since Hurricane Katrina in 2005, the New Orleans city government has tried to calm the city down a little. With support from neighbourhood groups, the city is policing bars and nightclubs more strictly. At the same time, the city is trying to create a new “noise ordinance” (a rule about music and noise).

Bar owner William Walker dislikes the anti-smoking law. He says, “This is just the wrong time for them to have pursued something like this.” He also says that making smokers go outside will make tensions worse.

Many of New Orleans’s best bars and some live music places are in quiet residential neighbourhoods. Living next to bars has been part of what makes the city charming. But lately, some people do not accept this situation, and people worry about the future of the city’s music culture.

Martha Wood lives next to a loud bar with live music. She says the bar was one reason she bought her house, so she will not complain about noise. She also manages a live-music bar. After noise problems in 2013, including complaints about loud smokers outside, her bar became one of the first in New Orleans that was legally not allowed to sell “drinks to go.” Wood says, “Now, any small infraction could get us shut down.” She says they installed a “smoke eater” machine, because they cannot open the doors at night because of the “noise.”

The Maple Leaf club chose to go smoke-free in 2014. Another club also did, after artists asked for smoke-free nights. Zalia BeVille, manager of the All Ways Lounge, says she wishes the city had let this change happen naturally instead of forcing the ban on every corner bar, even bars without music. All Ways went smoke-free in 2013 because, she says, “The horn players and the singers, they asked us for it.”

All Ways has an outdoor patio. Lost Love Lounge does not. Its owner, Geoff Douville, supports the ban. Before, he felt he had to allow smoking to keep his bar financially stable. He says he could not ban smoking by himself, because customers would go to another bar nearby. He wanted the rule to be the same across the whole city.

Some small business owners fear they will lose money. Smoker Neil Timms owns an English pub and saw a smoking ban before, in England. He says that in Coventry, every pub where he was a regular closed within a year of the UK ban that began in 2007. To avoid the same result, he is paying to build a patio.

Douville sees the ban as a business chance. He says many people avoided his bar because they did not want to smell like smoke for the next seven days, and now those people may come. He also does not worry about noise complaints like Walker does. He says no court will call a bar a “nuisance” after the city approved a smoking ban that forces smokers outside. He says he wants to see a judge deal with “no loitering” and “no smoking inside” at the same time.

Councilwoman LaToya Cantrell introduced and pushed the ban. She disagrees with Douville’s view about responsibility. She says it is the bar owner’s job to keep customers respectful outside the bar too. She says owners and bartenders should tell people they can smoke, but they must respect the community. She says the idea that the city cannot have clean air because of noise is “ridiculous.” She says it can be a win-win, with communication and partnerships between communities and businesses.

Cantrell says New Orleans is unique. She asks, “How is New Orleans different from the rest of the country?” She says New Orleans is known as “the City that Care Forgot,” but the city should say, “We care about our people.” She says the most vulnerable people are workers who breathe smoky air, and they are the backbone of the hospitality industry that drives Louisiana’s economy. She also says New Orleans is different because people can take their drink outside when they go out to smoke.

Many people worried that the New Orleans Police Department did not need another job. So the health department will handle warnings and fines. Customers are encouraged to fill out a form or call 311, and to include photos with the date and time that show illegal smoking. Neil Timms says he will follow the ban for this reason. He says he does not want someone to take a photo of a smoker and then get beaten up.

Geoff Douville says he is used to noise complaints, so he welcomes the ban. He says the neighbours who complain about noise now may be the same people who wanted the smoking ban. In the end, he shares Cantrell’s optimism. He says people will complain, but that does not mean they will win.
