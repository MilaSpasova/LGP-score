Clay Cockrell sits in his office at Columbus Circle. Across the street is 1 Central Park West. This building has the Trump International Hotel and Tower. Central Park is in front of it. Cockrell often does his popular “walk and talk” therapy in Central Park.

Cockrell used to work on Wall Street. Now he is a therapist. He spends much of his day walking in Central Park or Battery Park in Manhattan. He talks with some of New York’s richest people and gives them advice.

He said he became an expert in therapy for rich people in a natural way. He said many extremely wealthy people, “the 1% of the 1%,” feel their problems are not real problems. But he says they are real. He also said many therapists do not take these problems seriously.

What problems do America’s 1% have? Cockrell said they may feel guilty about being rich. They may feel they must hide that they are rich. They may also feel alone. Being in the 1% can be lonely. This supports the idea from F Scott Fitzgerald that very rich people are different.

Some counsellors say things got worse after the financial crisis. They also say the debate about income inequality made it worse. Movements like Occupy Wall Street and the Fight for $15 campaign increased this debate.

Jamie Traeger-Muney is a wealth psychologist. She founded the Wealth Legacy Group. She said Occupy Wall Street had important things to say about income inequality. But she said it singled out the 1% and showed them as bad in general. She also said the media is partly to blame. The media can make rich people feel they must hide or feel ashamed.

Traeger-Muney works with inheritors, people who receive a lot of money from family. She said inheritors often get a bad image in the press. She said people use many negative names for inheritors, like “spoilt brat,” “born with a silver spoon in their mouth,” and “trust-fund babies.” She said it is easy to blame the rich.

She also said she is sometimes shocked by what people say about wealthy people. She said people talk about wealth holders in a way they would not talk about other groups.

Barbara Nusbaum is an expert in money psychology. She said having a lot of money can feel very isolating. She said people’s reactions can be scary. She said people are taught not to talk about money because it is not polite. She said it can be harder to say you have money than to say you do not. It is more acceptable to say, “I am broke.” But you cannot easily say, “I have a ton of money.” She said rich people often must keep much of their life private.

Because of this, Cockrell said rich people often spend time with other rich Americans. He said this is not because they think they are better. He said it is because other rich people understand them and their problems.

The gap between rich and poor is growing worldwide. Oxfam said the richest 1% increased their share of global wealth from 44% in 2009 to 48% in 2014. Oxfam said they were on track to own more than the other 99% by 2016.

In the US, over the last three decades, the top 0.1% households increased their share of wealth from 7% to 22%. At the same time, the bottom 90% households had less wealth.

The number of extremely wealthy people is also rising. Spectrem Group research said that in 2014, the number of US households with $1m or more in assets, not counting their main home, increased by 500,000 to 10.1m. In 2007, it was 9.2m. Households worth $5m or more reached 1.3m. Also, 142,000 households were worth $25m or more.

Cockrell said that since the 2008 financial crisis, the income gap has grown. He said the situation “has gotten worse for the wealthy.” He said a main reason is that rich people may not know if friends like them or their money.

He said another billionaire does not want anything from you. But with people who have less money, it can be hard to trust the friendship. He said as the gap grows, rich people become more isolated.

These fears are real for the richest people. In 2007, the Gates Foundation worked with Boston College’s Center on Wealth and Philanthropy. They studied what it felt like to be in America’s 1%. For four years, researchers surveyed 165 of America’s richest households. Of these, 120 had at least $25m in assets. The average net worth was $78m. The study was called The Joys and Dilemmas of Wealth. It was 500 pages long. It seemed to support the saying that money cannot buy happiness.

A spouse of a tech entrepreneur who made about $80m said wealth can make it hard to connect with other people. Some Americans keep their wealth secret. Traeger-Muney said people call this “stealth wealth.” She said many people hide their wealth because they worry about negative judgment. She said when wealthy Americans talk about the special challenges of wealth, many people dismiss what they say.

She said people may say, “Oh, poor you,” and do not feel much sympathy. She said wealth is still one of the last taboos.

Cockrell has a soft, soothing voice. He said a common mistake for wealthy clients is letting money define them.

He said it is not healthy to ignore your problems. He said even if you are in the 1%, you still have problems, and they feel real to you. Even if you do not struggle for money, other parts of life can be hard. He said money is not the only thing that defines you. He repeated that their problems are legitimate.
