We often hear about animals in danger, like tigers in India, orangutans in Indonesia, and pandas. Many people love these animals and do not want them to disappear. But scientists now fear that the biggest danger to biodiversity may be closer to home. Even small animals, such as ladybirds, may be important.

Climate change, fewer animals, more humans, and many species dying out are making some scientists say we live in the “Anthropocene”. They describe it as a time when humans control ecosystems and many species are dying out.

WWF’s Living Planet Index (LPI) 2014 gave a bleak message. It showed a big drop in wildlife numbers in many species. The LPI said vertebrate animals fell by 52% over four decades. Biodiversity loss has reached “critical levels”. Some groups fell even more. Freshwater species dropped by 76% over the same time.

Scientists worry most about animals that give us important “ecosystem services”. About three quarters of the world’s food production is thought to depend on bees and other pollinators, like hoverflies. Worms break down waste and take it into the soil to make nutrients. Bats catch mosquitoes and help keep malaria rates down. A North America study said that losing pest control because bat numbers are falling could cost more than $22bn in lost agricultural productivity.

Dr Nick Isaac, a macroecologist at the Centre for Ecology & Hydrology in Oxfordshire, said the loss of common species will affect people more than the loss of rare species. He said Britain’s insects and other invertebrates may be declining as fast as vertebrates, with “serious consequences for humanity”. He also said it is not only about how many species go extinct, but also about how many animals are left in each population.

He said that 23 to 36% of all birds, mammals, and amphibians used for food or medicine are now threatened with extinction. In many places, wild animal food is an important part of the diet, especially for poor people.

Many people say the main cause is unsustainable human consumption. This damages ecosystems, causes climate change, and destroys habitats faster than people thought. And it is not only big, cute mammals we need to worry about. It is also smaller, less visible animals we depend on, like insects and worms. They may not disappear soon, but if their numbers fall, it will affect everyone. Isaac said the UK shows a similar pattern for invertebrates and vertebrates, and the effects are complex.

TV naturalist Sir David Attenborough said in 2013 that humans are a “plague on earth”. But WWF says there is still time to stop things getting worse. WWF UK Chief Executive David Nussbaum said the damage in the report should be a wake-up call. He said politicians, businesses, and people all have a responsibility to act, to protect a healthy future for people and nature.

Nussbaum said humans are cutting down trees faster than they can regrow, catching more fish than oceans can replace, taking water from rivers and aquifers faster than rain can refill them, and releasing more carbon than oceans and forests can absorb.
