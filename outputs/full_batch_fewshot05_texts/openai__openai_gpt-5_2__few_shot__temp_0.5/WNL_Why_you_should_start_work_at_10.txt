Many people do not get enough sleep. A simple idea could help: start the day later.

At the British Science Festival, Dr Paul Kelley from Oxford University said schools should not all start at the same time. He said start times should match students’ natural body clocks. This could help students think better, get better exam results, and be healthier. Lack of sleep has been linked with diabetes, depression, obesity, and a weak immune system.

In a 2014 paper, he wrote that children around age 10 naturally wake up at about 6:30am. At 16, they naturally wake up at about 8am. At 18, they naturally wake up at about 9am. Normal school start times can work for 10-year-olds, but not for 16- to 18-year-olds. For older teenagers, he said it may be better to start school at 11am, or even later. The paper says a 7am alarm for older teenagers is like a 4:30am start for a teacher in their 50s.

Kelley says it is not as simple as telling teenagers to go to bed earlier. He says the body clock is controlled by light. Special cells in the eye send information to a part of the brain. This part of the brain controls our daily rhythm over 24 hours. He says you cannot easily control this, like you cannot control your heartbeat.

Kelley also says adults could benefit from later start times. He described average sleep loss per night. For ages 14 to 24, it is more than two hours. For ages 24 to about 30 or 35, it is about an hour and a half. This can continue until about age 55, when sleep is balanced again. He says a 10-year-old and a 55-year-old naturally sleep and wake at the same time.

He says this may explain why the traditional 9 to 5 day is so common. Many bosses are in their mid-50s or older, and that schedule works best for them. He asks if workplaces should also have different start times. For example, people in their 50s could start at 8am, people in their 30s could start at 10am, and teenage interns or apprentices could start at 11am. He says this could improve performance, mood, and health, and make people less ill, happier, and better at their jobs.

He also says there might be fewer accidents because drivers would be more awake. Rush hour could be smaller if people start work and school at different times. He says a later start would help everyone, especially families. Parents often try to wake teenagers about three hours too early, and this can cause stress.

Kelley says he is 67, so he now wakes like a 10-year-old. He gets up just after six and wakes naturally. He says starting work is easier now than when he was younger.
