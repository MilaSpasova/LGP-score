A controversial auction of a Banksy mural was stopped just before it was going to be sold.

The artwork is called Slave Labour. It is a spray-painted picture of a child making Union Flag bunting. Many people saw it as a message about last year’s diamond jubilee. It was expected to sell for about $700,000 at an auction of street and modern art in Florida.

The auctioneer, Frederic Thut, owns the Fine Arts Auction Miami gallery. He had refused to say who the seller was or how the artwork got into the sale. He then announced that Slave Labour, and a second Banksy work, had been taken out of the auction.

He did not give a reason. Community leaders in Haringey, London, were very happy. They had campaigned to stop the sale of the artwork, which had been taken from the wall of a Poundland in Wood Green.

Alan Strickland, a Haringey councillor, said: “One of our two demands was that it doesn’t sell and the other was that we get it back again, so we’re halfway there.”

He said he would write to the auction house quickly to ask what happened and what will happen next. He said the people of Wood Green were pleased that their campaign in London had an effect in the US.

Claire Kober, Leader of Haringey Council, wrote to Arts Council England and to the Mayor of Miami, Tomás Regalado, asking them to help stop the sale. But it seems the gallery owners decided to withdraw the item after talking with their lawyers. The FBI would not confirm reports that they were asked to investigate.

Several hours after the auction ended, the auction house released a short statement. It said it had convinced the owners of the two Banksy works to remove them from the sale. It said there were no legal issues with selling the two Banksy lots.

About 30 possible buyers came to the auction. There were 106 items for sale in Miami’s Wynwood neighbourhood. The auction lasted three hours and other early items sold for more than their asking prices.

Critics said the auction house was selling stolen property. But Thut said the seller was a “well known collector” who was the real owner, and that the sale was legal.

Thut said his gallery received many emails and phone calls from the UK, and many were abusive or offensive. He said he still supported selling the works because it would help preserve them.

The second Banksy work was a 2007 piece called Wet Dog. It was removed from a wall in Bethlehem and was estimated to be worth up to $800,000. It disappeared from the auction house’s online catalogue at lunchtime on Saturday. But Slave Labour was still listed until the 3pm start time.

Thut said the two works came from two different owners, and neither owner was British. He said the works were important in street art and should go to buyers who care about art and preserving it.

He said he would not reveal the seller’s identity. He said the gallery only sells the art and does not control it or decide if it will be returned.

A Poundland spokesperson said the company did not know who removed the 4ft x 5ft slab from the shop wall in Turnpike Lane. Lawyers for the building owner, Wood Green Investments Ltd, would not say if the company was involved.

Banksy has not commented on the Slave Labour situation. But he has criticised people who try to sell his art. In 2011, before an auction in New York that planned to sell five of his works, he spoke against it. None of those works were sold.

Stephan Keszler, who was involved in that 2011 auction, said selling Banksy works without Banksy’s permission is acceptable. He said Banksy makes art on other people’s property without asking, so the property owner can do what they want with it.
