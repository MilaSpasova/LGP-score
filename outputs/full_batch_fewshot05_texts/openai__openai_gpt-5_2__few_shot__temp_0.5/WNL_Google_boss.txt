Piles of digital material, like blogs, tweets, pictures, videos, and official documents such as court rulings and emails, could be lost forever. This could happen because the programs needed to open them may stop working, warned Google’s vice-president, Vint Cerf. He spoke at the American Association for the Advancement of Science’s annual meeting in San Jose, California. He said we could face a “forgotten generation” or even a “forgotten century” because of “bit rot,” when old computer files become useless.

Cerf asked for something he called “digital vellum.” This would keep old software and hardware so we could open old files no matter how old they are. He said we store many parts of daily life in digital form, like email, tweets, and the world wide web, so we could lose a lot of history. He said we do not want our digital lives to disappear, so we must make sure today’s digital files can still be shown in the far future.

“Bit rot” means old digital files cannot be used anymore. Some people think access to digital content in future decades may not be as big a problem as Cerf suggests. But the warning shows something strange: people digitize music, photos, letters, and documents to keep them safe for a long time, but the programs and machines needed to read the files can quickly become unused.

Cerf said we are putting our data into what could become an information “black hole” without noticing. We digitize things because we think we are saving them, but without extra steps the digital copies may not be better, and may even be worse, than the old objects. He said that if you really care about photos, you should print them out.

Ancient civilizations did not have this problem. Text written in cuneiform on baked clay tablets or on papyrus scrolls needed only eyes to read them. But future scholars who study today’s culture may face PDFs, Word documents, and many other file types. These can only be read with special software, and sometimes special hardware too.

The problem already exists. In the 1980s, people often saved documents on floppy disks. People also played games by loading Jet Set Willy from cassette to the ZX spectrum, using a Quickfire II joystick, and keeping Atari game cartridges in the attic. Even if the disks and cassettes are still fine, the equipment needed to use them is now mostly found only in museums.

Cerf says bit rot could also destroy important political and historical documents. In 2005, American historian Doris Kearns Goodwin wrote Team of Rivals: the Political Genius of Abraham Lincoln. She used physical letters from libraries around the US to rebuild conversations from Lincoln’s time. Cerf said that today those letters would be emails, and in one hundred years it may be very hard to find them.

Cerf agrees that historians will try to save material that seems important today. But he says we often do not know what will matter until hundreds of years later. People learned new things about Archimedes after the Archimedes palimpsest was found. It was hidden under the words of a Byzantine prayer book from the thirteenth century. Cerf said we can learn important things from objects that were kept only by chance.

Researchers at Carnegie Mellon University in Pittsburgh have made progress toward a partial solution. Mahadev Satyanarayanan takes digital snapshots of hard drives while they run different software programs. These snapshots can be uploaded to a computer that acts like the old computer. This can let the computer read files that would otherwise be unusable. In a project called Olive, the researchers saved Mystery House (the 1982 graphic adventure game for the Apple II), an early version of WordPerfect, and Doom (the 1993 first person shooter game).

But new technology is only part of the fight. It may be even harder to get legal permission to copy and store software before it disappears. When IT companies close or stop supporting products, they may sell the rights, and getting approval can be very difficult. Cerf said we may need to think about preservation rights when we make rules about copyright, patents, and licensing, because we want to preserve software for hundreds to thousands of years.
