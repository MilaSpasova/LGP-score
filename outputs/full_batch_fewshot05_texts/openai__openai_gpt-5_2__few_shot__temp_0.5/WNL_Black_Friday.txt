Louise Haggerty, 56, is a hairdresser and waitress. She went to the Black Friday sales at 1am. She said she got a Dyson vacuum cleaner, but she was not sure she wanted it. She said it was very crowded and horrible inside.

Haggerty went with a friend to a 24-hour Sainsbury’s supermarket in Harringay, north-east London. They hoped to buy a cheap flat-screen TV. But many people pushed in the queue, so they could not get one. She said a woman who was second in the queue was pushed out by a group of youths. She said people behaved like animals. She said she only saw two security guards.

Haggerty wanted a Blaupunkt 40-inch TV that was reduced from £299.99 to £149.99, but she could not buy it. She then rushed to pick up a Dyson Animal Vac, reduced from £319.99 to £159.99. She said she did not even know the price and was not sure she would buy it. She said she just wanted to get something. She said some young men had three, four, or five TVs, and she thought it was not fair.

One of them was Andy Blackett, 30, an estate agent. He had two trolleys full of items. He said he got two coffee makers, two tablets, two TVs, and a stereo. He said he did not know the prices, but he knew they were bargains. His friend Henry Fischer, 19, a student, did not do as well. He said someone snatched his TV, because he was smaller.

Blackett, Fischer, and their friends drove to Sainsbury’s at 12.45am. They left Tesco’s 24-hour Lea Valley supermarket because it was “bedlam”. Blackett said Tesco was scary, so they came to Sainsbury’s instead.

At the Tesco store on Glover Drive in Upper Edmonton, more than a dozen police officers came after fights started between shoppers. People tore down cardboard barriers that were meant to hold back sale items until midnight. Tesco delayed selling its most popular items, TVs, for almost an hour, until police controlled the situation. A police officer was heard criticising the manager for not having enough security, and suggested stopping the sale. This Tesco is across the road from an IKEA store where riots happened at a midnight sale in 2005.

Police also stepped in at other stores, including Tesco in Willesden and Surrey Quays, before midnight. Greater Manchester Police said at least two people had been arrested at Black Friday sales events that morning. On Twitter they said, “Keep calm, people!” South Wales Police said staff at Tesco stores called them because they were worried about how many people came to the sales.

When TV sales began just before 1am, James Alled, 30, a businessman, bought two flat-screen TVs. He was already trying to sell one to someone further down the queue. He said he bought them for £250, and said, “I’ll sell it to you for £350, £300 cash.”

Further back, Christine Ball, 62, was angry. She said she arrived at 10.15pm, and now she was even further back. She said the people did not understand what a queue is.

Ball said she had not heard of the US-style Black Friday sales until now, like most shoppers. She came to buy her grandson a TV for Christmas. She said she wanted a normal one, about £100. In her basket she had a pint of semi-skimmed milk and a loaf of bread. She joked, “Telly, milk and bread – the necessities.”

Mel Mehmet, 23, went to Black Friday sales in 2013. She expected queues, but this time the atmosphere in Tesco scared her. She said it was crazy to have the sale at midnight, and the police should have more important things to do at night than come to sales. She said they would go to PC World in the morning, because its sale starts at 8am.
