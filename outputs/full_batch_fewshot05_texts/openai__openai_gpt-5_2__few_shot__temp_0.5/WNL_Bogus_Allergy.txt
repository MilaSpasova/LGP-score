Experts and charities say fake allergy tests are making thousands of people take treatments they do not need. Some people also put themselves or their children on poor diets. This can sometimes cause malnutrition.

Allergies and food intolerances are increasing. But many people confuse the two, and many people are diagnosed wrongly. Sense About Science made a guide with allergy specialists and charities. 

Tracey Brown, director of Sense About Science, said there is a big problem with bad information. Myths, wrong ideas from studies, and fake medicine can lead to too many or too few diagnoses. She said the costs are very high. Some people take actions they do not need, and other people do not get the help they need.

Experts also worry about restaurants and caterers. They may hear many people say they have allergies when they really have a food intolerance. Then staff may not be careful enough when a person has a real allergy.

Moira Austin of the Anaphylaxis Campaign said this is very important. If a caterer thinks someone is only avoiding a food to stop feeling bloated, they may be less careful. She said there have been deaths when people told restaurant staff about an allergy, but the meal still contained that allergen.

The guide says most allergy tests sold online or in shops are not based on science. One home test checks blood for IgG (immunoglobulin G) antibodies against foods. The guide says higher IgG levels do not mean a person has an allergy. The results are often positive even for people who do not have an allergy or a food intolerance.

The guide also says another test is wrong. This test mixes acupuncture and homeopathy and measures electric resistance in the skin while the person holds a food. Hair follicle testing is also useless, the guide says. Hair is not part of an allergic reaction, so hair tests cannot show allergy status. The guide also says people should not believe that allergies are caused by an “energy blockage” that muscle testing can find and acupuncture can cure.

Paul Seddon, a consultant paediatric allergist, said he often sees children on diets that are too limited. Parents believe the children have allergies to many foods because of “allergy tests” that have no scientific basis. He said this needs to stop.

Adam Fox, another consultant paediatric allergist at Guys and St Thomas’ Hospital in London, said he also sees patients who sent hair for testing or removed many foods from their children’s diets. He said it is hard to fix this. Children need proper diets. Often they are avoiding foods that are not harmful, which harms quality of life. A child who cannot eat wheat or drink milk cannot go to parties.

Some parents believe a child’s long-term tiredness, headaches, or eczema are caused by an allergy. It can take a long time and many tests to show this is true or false. It can feel easier to visit an alternative therapist who does one test and gives a quick answer, but the answer may be wrong.

Allergies are rising in developed countries. In the last 30 years, the percentage of children diagnosed with allergic rhinitis and eczema has tripled. Diagnosis is better now, but the increase is real. This makes more people think allergies cause their own or their children’s health problems. The guide lists myths about what causes allergies, such as E numbers in food colourings, “toxic overload,” and fast food.
