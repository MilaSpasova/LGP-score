A top energy expert says coal may become as big as oil as the world’s main energy source in the next five years. This could be very bad for the climate.

One big reason for more coal use is the large increase in shale gas use in the US.

New research from the International Energy Agency (IEA) says coal use is rising around the world, even in places with goals to cut carbon. The exception is the US, where shale gas has replaced coal. Because the US uses less coal, coal prices have fallen worldwide. This has made coal more popular, even in Europe, where the Emissions Trading Scheme was meant to reduce coal use.

Maria van der Hoeven, Executive Director of the IEA, said coal’s share of world energy keeps growing each year. She said that if policies do not change, coal will catch up with oil within a decade.

Coal is easy to find in many parts of the world, and it is cheap to take out of the ground. Because of this, coal supplied nearly half of the increase in world energy demand in the past decade. The IEA says China and India will push coal use higher in the next five years. India will likely pass the US as the world’s second biggest user. China is the biggest coal importer. Indonesia is the biggest exporter, and it has temporarily passed Australia.

The IEA’s Medium-Term Coal Market Report says the world will burn 1.2bn more tonnes of coal per year by 2017 than it burns today. This is equal to the current coal use of Russia and the US together. By 2017, global coal use is expected to reach 4.3bn tonnes of oil equivalent. Oil use is expected to reach 4.4bn tonnes by the same year.

Coal produces more carbon emissions than any other major fossil fuel. It adds a lot to climate change, especially when it is burned in old, inefficient power stations. If these power stations do not have special “scrubbing” equipment to remove chemicals, coal can also cause sulphur emissions, which are the main cause of acid rain. It can also release other pollution, such as mercury and soot.

Van der Hoeven said a high carbon price is needed to slow coal growth and support cleaner technology, like renewable power. Without that, she said only cheaper gas could really lower coal demand. This happened in the US because shale gas production has increased greatly in the past five years.

She said the US shows that a better gas market, with flexible prices and local unconventional resources produced in a sustainable way, can cut coal use, carbon dioxide emissions, and electricity bills. She said Europe, China, and other regions should pay attention.

This would mean making much more shale gas, because conventional gas is becoming harder to get in places that are easy to reach.

In Europe, the Emissions Trading Scheme was meant to reduce high-carbon power by putting a price on carbon dioxide emissions. Power companies and energy-heavy companies got a set number of permits. They had to buy more permits if they wanted to emit more than their limit. But too many permits were given out. The financial crisis and recession also reduced demand. This created a big surplus of permits and made permit prices fall sharply. At today’s prices, only a few euros per tonne of carbon, companies have little reason to choose lower-carbon fuels. So coal is becoming popular again in Europe.

This means one of the world’s main market systems for cutting greenhouse gas emissions is not reaching its key goals. Because coal use is growing fast, the risk of climate change is likely to increase.
