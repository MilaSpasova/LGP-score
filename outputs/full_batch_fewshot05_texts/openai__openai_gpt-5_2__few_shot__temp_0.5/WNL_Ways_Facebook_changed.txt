On one day in August, 1 in 7 people on Earth used Facebook. That is about 1 billion people, said founder Mark Zuckerberg. In 10 years, the social network has changed people’s relationships, privacy, business, and news. It has helped remove some governments and it has even changed the meanings of everyday words.

“A more open and connected world is a better world. It brings stronger relationships with those you love, a stronger economy with more opportunities and a stronger society that reflects all of our values,” Zuckerberg wrote when he shared these numbers.

These are some ways his company changed life, for better or worse.

1) Facebook has changed the meaning of “friend”
“To friend” is now a verb. And it is easy to “unfriend” someone on Facebook. This word means removing a person from your Facebook friends.

Facebook has also made the words “share” and “like” feel more important.

School and university reunions can feel less needed. You already know whose job is going well, who has broken up, and you have seen many photos of your old classmates’ babies. You may also know your ex is dating someone else because of romantic selfies.

But Facebook does not show different levels of friendship. A person you worked with once 15 years ago, a friend-of-a-friend you met at a stag do, or a colleague you have never spoken to in person can be listed as a “friend,” just like your closest friend, your spouse, or your mum.

Professor Robin Dunbar is known for research saying a person can only have about 150 people in their social group. He believes Facebook has not changed that yet. But he told the New Yorker he worries that, because it is so easy to end friendships on Facebook, people may stop learning how to get along.

2) We care less about privacy
There is a saying: if you are not paying for it, you are the product. Facebook follows this idea and built a whole industry from it. The surprising thing is that users know this, but they still give their information.

Pew Research Center found that many young people are willing to share personal details. 91% post a photo of themselves. 71% post the city or town where they live, up from 61%. More than half give email addresses, and 1/5 give their phone number.

Because so much of life is shared online, Facebook lets people build an image and a fanbase. In the journal Frontiers in Psychology, academics described a new idea: the “Facebook self.”

More than 80% list their interests, which helps brands target them. But many younger users do limit their profiles: 60% allow friends only.

3) Facebook has created millions of jobs, but not in its own offices
Facebook has helped create a whole job sector. Many people work indirectly to help brands use the platform.

“It is a tool like no other,” said Michael Tinmouth, a social media strategist who has worked with brands such as Vodafone and Microsoft. He said marketers can understand a brand’s consumers better than before. He said the data and analytics are extraordinary, and you can know who customers are, who their friends are, and how they interact with a brand.

Advertisers pay a lot for this. Facebook said its ad revenue rose 46% to $3.32bn.

But Facebook can also be dangerous for brands. Complaints that used to happen on the phone or in small internet forums can now be seen by hundreds of friends, or even posted on the brand’s own page. A complaint can go viral.

4) Facebook has been used to organize revolutions
The Arab Spring was sometimes called the Twitter revolution, but Facebook also changed how people organize protests and direct action. Olga Onuch at Manchester University found that Facebook was the key way to reach half of the Euromaidan protesters in Ukraine.

Onuch found that Facebook posts signalled the start of the Maidan protests soon after it was announced that Ukraine would not sign a free trade and association agreement with the EU. The posts organized action in real life, not only anger online. Mustafa Nayyem, a Ukraine activist, posted: “If you really want to do something, don’t just ‘like’ this post. Let’s meet near the monument to independence in the middle of the Maidan.”

Many people in Onuch’s research said they used Facebook to learn the truth, because they could not trust traditional media.

5) Facebook makes news, breaks news, and decides what is news
About 71% of 18- to 24-year-olds say the internet is their main news source, and 63% of users overall, according to the Pew Research Center. About 1/3 of Facebook users post about politics and government.

Many people first see journalism or breaking news on Facebook or other social media, often on mobile phones.

Users may not need to leave Facebook to read news. Instant Articles lets news stories run inside Facebook. News companies can sell ads around their articles and keep 100% of that money. Facebook can also sell ads around the article, and 70% of that advertising money from Facebook also goes to the news companies.

Facebook has also changed how journalists write stories. Many reporters now use it to find and contact ordinary people who suddenly become part of a big news story. Facebook also has its own newswire, sharing useful user reactions to breaking news, including photos and videos.

6) Users are changing Facebook
At first, Facebook was a site for students, and only elite US universities could use it.

In 2014, 56% of internet users aged 65 and older had a Facebook account. And 39% were connected to people they had never met in person.

Groups have been replaced by pages. Writing on each other’s walls is now old-fashioned. Carefully made photo albums have been replaced by quick uploads from mobile phones. More than ever, the site is a gateway not only to friends, but also to the rest of the internet.

David Kirkpatrick, author of The Facebook Effect, said we should get used to it. He told BBC Radio 4’s Today programme: “It might very well go away further down the road but something this big takes a long time to disappear. Facebook has proven its ability to change and it will continue to be a very, very major player.”
