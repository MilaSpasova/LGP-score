Nobody knows what came first in Greece: the economic crisis, or shisha. Shisha is a drug now called the “cocaine of the poor”. People agree that shisha can kill. It costs €2 or less for one hit. It has spread in Greece during Europe’s financial meltdown.

“As drugs go, it is the worst. It burns your insides, it makes you aggressive and makes you go totally mad,” said Maria, a former heroin addict. “But it is cheap and easy to get, and it is what everyone is doing.”

This drug problem puts more pressure on Athens’s health authorities. They are already struggling because of very harsh cuts.

Many homeless Greeks use shisha. They are on the streets because of poverty and despair. Addicts and officials say shisha is a type of crystal meth. It can make users very violent. The drug is often mixed with other things, like battery acid, engine oil, and even shampoo.

It is easy to find, and easy to make. This fits a society where many people see no hope.

“It is a killer, but it also makes you want to kill,” said Konstantinos, a drug addict. “You can kill without understanding that you have done it. And it is spreading faster than death. A lot of users have died.”

Charalampos Poulopoulos, the head of Kethea, Greece’s leading anti-drug centre, said shisha shows how bad the crisis is. The crisis has brought record levels of poverty and unemployment. He called shisha an “austerity drug”. He said dealers make cheap synthetic drugs for people who cannot afford more expensive drugs like heroin and cocaine.

“The crisis has given dealers the possibility to promote a new, cheap drug, a cocaine for the poor,” said Poulopoulos at a centre for addicts in Exarcheia in Athens. “Shisha can be sniffed or injected. It can be made in home laboratories, and you don’t need special knowledge. It is extremely dangerous.”

Across Greece, six years of recession have caused great harm. Depression has risen a lot. Drug and alcohol abuse has also risen a lot. Crime has increased as austerity measures cut the income of ordinary Greeks by 40%. Prostitution has also risen. It is often an easy way to pay for drug addiction.

“Desperation is such that many women agree to have sex without protection because they will make more money,” said Eleni Marini, a British-trained psychologist with Kethea. “Shisha has been linked to a very strong sex drive, but it also hurts your ability to think clearly. We are seeing more pregnancies among drug addicts who are in prostitution.” In 2012, two sex workers gave birth on the streets of Athens.

Suicides have also increased. HIV infections have spread so much that they are at epidemic levels. Drug addicts, believed to be around 25,000 people, are becoming more self-destructive. Experts say young Greeks are leading this. They are pushed aside by record unemployment. Greece has the highest youth unemployment in the EU, at 64%.

“The crisis has created a widespread sense of pessimism,” said Poulopoulos. “People who might have stopped using drugs now have no reason to stop. There is misery, and people who know they will not find work are becoming more self-destructive. In Athens, where the crisis is worst, shisha is part of that.”

Greece’s conservative-led coalition government has tried to handle the problem by pushing drug users and other homeless people out of the city centre. Police operations have cleared central streets, crowded doorways, and malls.

“But these actions only hide the problem,” said Poulopoulos. “In reality, they push these people even further out. This can push them into the hands of drug dealers, who offer them protection.”

At the same time, more people need help than ever before. But state-funded groups like Kethea have had their budgets cut by one third. This was done at the request of the “troika”: the EC, ECB, and IMF. These groups were helping keep Greece’s debt-hit economy going.

Since the crisis began in 2009, Kethea has lost 70 of its 500 staff. These cuts happened even though studies show that for every €1 spent on programmes like Kethea, the state saves about €6 in criminal justice and healthcare costs. “The cuts we have seen are a false economy, a huge mistake,” said Poulopoulos.

On the streets of Athens, where shisha has spread, many people now fear that austerity not only does not work, it kills.
