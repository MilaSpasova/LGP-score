Most of BB King’s work was blues, and much of his best work was blues. But he was always open to other kinds of music. He was friendly and skilled, and he could connect people from different musical and cultural backgrounds. Some people call him “the last of the bluesmen,” but it may be too soon to say that. Still, it is hard to imagine another blues artist having King’s influence on thousands of musicians and millions of listeners over a 65-year career.

Riley B King was born near Itta Bena, Mississippi. The “B” did not seem to stand for a name. He grew up with few chances in life, as an African-American farm worker. He slowly worked to overcome this. He learned basic guitar from a family friend. He also improved his singing with a quartet of gospel singers. In his early 20s, he moved to Memphis.

A few years later, he played often at a bar in West Memphis, Arkansas. He also became a disc jockey and hosted a show on a Memphis radio station. His name on the show was “The Beale Street Blues Boy.” This was later shortened to “Blues Boy King,” and then to “BB.” After one recording session in 1949 for a Nashville label, he started recording for Modern Records in 1950.

He got his first hit in 1952. It was a new version of “Three O’Clock Blues.” It was number one on the R&B chart for 15 weeks. After that, he had other successful songs, like “Please Love Me,” “You Upset Me Baby,” and “Sweet Sixteen.” In these and many other recordings, many of them his own songs, King built a style that was new but still part of blues history. He often praised the musicians who influenced him, and he usually named T-Bone Walker first.

He told Guitar Player magazine, “I’ve tried my best to get that sound. I came pretty close but never quite got it.” In a 2001 interview, he said, “If T-Bone Walker had been a woman I would have asked him to marry me.” He also named older blues guitarists Blind Lemon Jefferson and Lonnie Johnson, and jazz players Charlie Christian and Django Reinhardt.

King said part of his guitar style came from what he could not do. He said he started to bend notes because he could not play the bottleneck style like Elmore James and Booker White. He loved that sound, but he could not do it. He also spoke modestly about his singing style. He said it came half from crooners like Nat King Cole and half from blues shouters like Joe Turner and Dr Clayton. His favourite composer and singer was probably Louis Jordan. He honored Jordan’s music in the 1999 album “Let the Good Times Roll.”

During the 1950s, King was the top blues artist on the circuit of theatres and clubs for black audiences. He played many one-night shows. In 1956, he is said to have played 342 shows. In 1962, he tried to change this by signing with a major label, ABC. But the first records under that contract tried to make him a mainstream pop singer. Fans did not like them, and ABC also was not happy with the results.

His 1965 album “Live at the Regal” showed how strong his blues songs still were. It became an iconic album and a turning point for many younger musicians. He had more R&B hits with blues songs, including “How Blue Can You Get” and “Paying the Cost to Be the Boss.” In 1969, he reached high places on the pop charts with “The Thrill Is Gone.” No blues artist had done that for many years.

It took time for him to become popular with rock audiences. Many rock fans knew blues mostly through Chicago blues, like Muddy Waters and Howlin’ Wolf. But rock musicians who admired King helped bring attention to him. In 1969, he said that kids started telling him he was the greatest blues guitarist in the world. When he asked who told them, they said names like Mike Bloomfield or Eric Clapton. He said he owed his new popularity to these young people. He gained more rock respect with the 1970 album “Indianola Mississippi Seeds,” where he worked with Carole King and Joe Walsh.

After that, King was firmly known as “the chairman of the board of blues singers.” With his manager, Sidney Seidenberg, he began international tours to Japan, Australia, China, and Russia. He also played concerts for prisoners at the Cook County jail in Chicago and at San Quentin. These experiences led to his long work with rehabilitation programs.

In 1990, King was diagnosed with diabetes. He toured less, but he still toured enough that fans outside the US could see him every year or two. Later he did most of his shows sitting down, but his singing and guitar playing only slowly became weaker. In 2005, celebrations for his 80th birthday included an award-winning album where he worked with Clapton, Mark Knopfler, Roger Daltrey, Gloria Estefan, and others. There were also tributes from musicians like Bono, Amadou Bagayoko, and Elton John. A “farewell tour” was announced, but it was not a farewell.

In 2009, King won a Grammy award for best traditional blues album for “One Kind Favor.” In 2012, he played at a White House concert. The US President, Barack Obama, joined him to sing “Sweet Home Chicago.” King married twice and divorced twice. He had 11 children with different partners. Four other children died before him.
