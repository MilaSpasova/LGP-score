Felix Baumgartner stood at the edge of space over the deserts of New Mexico. He stopped for a moment. He only had to take a small step away from the capsule, but the drop back to Earth was 24 miles. Mission control said, “Our guardian angel will take care of you.” Then the man called Fearless Felix jumped.

About ten minutes later, the Austrian landed safely. He reached speeds up to 725mph. He broke three world records. One record was being the world’s first supersonic skydiver. He broke the sound barrier at Mach 1.24. In the control room people cheered, “We love you Felix.” His mother, Ava Baumgartner, cried. Baumgartner also set records for the highest altitude manned balloon flight and the highest altitude skydive. He raised his arms to thank his team.

He wore a special survival suit. It protected his body from very big changes in air pressure during the fall. Without the suit, his blood would have boiled and his lungs might have exploded. Later he said at a press conference, “When I was standing there on top of the world, you become so humble, you don’t think about breaking records.” He said he mainly thought about getting back alive. He also said, “Sometimes you have to go up really high to see how small you are.”

They tried twice the week before, but they had to stop. On Sunday morning the weather was good, so the mission got the go-ahead. A huge balloon carried him up into clear skies. The balloon was 30 million square cubic feet. Its skin was one-tenth the thickness of a sandwich bag. A capsule hung under the balloon. Baumgartner sat in the capsule in his suit.

At the right height, he did a checklist with his mentor, Joe Kittinger. The checklist had 40 items. Kittinger had held the record for the highest altitude manned balloon flight before.

There was a problem with a heater for Baumgartner’s visor. It did not work, and the visor could fog up. He told Kittinger, “This is very serious, Joe.” He said it sometimes got foggy when he breathed out. He also said, “I do not feel heat.” They decided to continue anyway. A record 8 million people watched because the jump was streamed live on YouTube.

The trip up took two and a half hours. During that time, they could see the curve of the Earth. The sky slowly turned black. The trip down was much faster.

Three cameras on his suit recorded the free-fall. The free-fall lasted a little over four minutes. It did not break the free-fall record for time. Then the parachute opened.

The mission succeeded, and the suit worked. This suggests astronauts might survive a high altitude disaster by leaving their craft. This is like what happened to the space shuttle Columbia in 2003. The top medical man on the jump was Dr Jonathan Clark. His wife, Laurel Clark, died in the Columbia accident. Clark now works to improve astronauts’ chances of survival in a high-altitude disaster.

Baumgartner is known for dangerous acts. He used to be a paratrooper. He has jumped off buildings and mountains. Once he jumped into a 600 foot deep cave. He did two practice free-falls for this attempt. One was from 71,000 feet in March. The second was from 97,000 feet in July 2012. But none of these matched his jump above Roswell. Roswell was chosen because it is famous for stories about UFO sightings.

He tried to break five records: the first human to break the sound barrier in free-fall; the highest free-fall altitude jump; the highest manned balloon flight; the longest free-fall; and having the largest manned balloon in history for his jump platform. The stunt took seven years to plan. Red Bull drinks sponsored it. It beat two of Kittinger’s records. Kittinger, a retired US air force colonel, had held the high altitude and speed records for parachuting. In 1960, Kittinger jumped from a balloon 19 miles above the planet. During Baumgartner’s rise, the only voice in his radio earpiece was Kittinger’s voice. Kittinger was 84.

After the jump, Baumgartner was asked what he wanted to do next. He said, “I want to inspire a generation.” He said he wanted to be in the same spot as Joe Kittinger in the next four years. He imagined a young man asking him for advice to break his record. He said the most exciting moment was standing outside the capsule “on top of the world”. He also said the most beautiful moment was when he stood at the landing area and Mike Todd, the life support engineer who dressed him in the suit, arrived smiling like a little kid.

Baumgartner said he started to feel like Todd’s son. He said Todd was very happy that he was alive. Earlier, Todd told the press conference, “The world needs a hero right now, and they got one in Felix Baumgartner.” Kittinger joked at the press conference that he wanted to give a “one-fingered salute” to people who said Baumgartner would come apart when he went supersonic.

Baumgartner said this would be his last jump. He promised to settle down with his girlfriend, Nicole Oetl. He plans to fly helicopters on rescue missions in the US and Austria.
