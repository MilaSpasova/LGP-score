Cities do not usually pick up and move. But Kiruna, a town in northern Sweden, must move so it is not swallowed by the ground.

Kiruna is an Arctic town. It has about 23,000 people. A very large iron-ore mine is eating away the ground under the town. Architect Krister Lindstedt says the town has a hard choice. If the mine stops, many people may lose their jobs. If the town does not move, it could be destroyed.

Kiruna was founded in 1900 by the state-owned mining company Luossavaara-Kiirunavaara (LK). The town became rich because of the iron ore below it. Deputy Mayor Niklas Siren says the town exists because of the mine.

Kiruna is 145km inside the Arctic Circle. Winters are very hard, with no sunlight and average temperatures below -15C. But the magnetite deposit has kept people there. The mine is now the world’s largest underground iron-ore mine. It produces 90% of the iron in Europe. This is enough to build more than six Eiffel Towers a day, and demand is still growing.

In 2004, the mining company told the town it was in danger. The mine is digging towards the city at a 60-degree angle. This will cause the ground to sink, and buildings could crack and collapse. Ten years later, cracks are starting to appear and are moving closer to the town.

Viktoria Walldin says people have lived in uncertainty for ten years. Many have paused big life choices, like buying a house, redecorating, having a child, or opening a business.

The city has now shared a master plan. Lindstedt says the town will slowly move east, like a walking millipede. Streets and squares will shift along a new main street. The plan is for Kiruna to be safely away from the mine by 2033.

A new town square is being built 3km to the east. A circular town hall is planned by Danish architect Henning Larsen. The plan also lists 20 important buildings to be taken apart and rebuilt in the new place. Kiruna’s rust-red wooden church, built in 1912 and shaped like Sami teepees, will be moved to a new park. The cast-iron bell tower will also be rebuilt near the town hall. But not everything will be saved.

Walldin talks about an old woman who passes the bench where she had her first kiss. People also care about places like the hospital where their first child was born. Many of these things will disappear.

The project is called “the most democratic move in history”. The mining company has given the equivalent of £320m to build new facilities, like a high school, fire station, community centre, library, and swimming hall. But many people worry most about where they will live and how homes will be assigned.

Lindstedt says these details are not decided yet. He says people are used to low rents and high incomes, but this will need to change. LK will pay residents the value of their homes plus 25%. Many locals say this is not enough to buy a new house at market prices.

To help set prices, the architects studied housing rentals in nearby cities for years. They also marked Kiruna homes by features, like size, gardens, and how close they are to bus stops and the city centre. They also suggested a “Kiruna Portal”, a large salvage yard where materials from old homes can be collected and reused in new buildings. But Sweden does not have a tradition of self-build, so this may not happen.

The new town will not look much like the old one. Today, Kiruna spreads out with winding streets and detached houses with gardens. The new plan has higher density, with multi-storey apartment blocks around shared courtyards and straight boulevards, where cold winds will blow.

The architects say this is a chance for Kiruna to remake itself as a model of sustainable development. They want to attract young people with new cultural buildings and ideas like a cable car over the main street. But many current residents may not be able to afford this new vision.
