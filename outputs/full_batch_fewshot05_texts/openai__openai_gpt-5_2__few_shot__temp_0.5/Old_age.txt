A new world index about ageing says Sweden is the best country for older people, and Afghanistan is the worst. It also says that being a rich country does not always mean life is better for people over 60. Sweden is number 1. Next are Norway, Germany, the Netherlands, and Canada. The index also shows some surprising results.

The US is the richest country in the world, but it is only in 8th place. The UK is 13th, so it is not in the top 10. Sri Lanka is 36th, much higher than Pakistan at 89th, even though they have similar GDP. Bolivia and Mauritius score higher than their economies might suggest. Brazil, Russia, India, and China have mixed results. Brazil and China are fairly high, but India and Russia are much lower.

Mark Gorman, director of the HelpAge International advocacy group, said that history matters. He said the top countries are not surprising, but Scandinavian countries were not rich when they started universal pensions. He said older people in Sri Lanka benefit today from good basic education and health care. He said all countries have limited resources, but when they decide how to spend money, they should also deal with the needs of old age.

The index was developed with the UN Fund for Population and Development. It covers 91 countries and 89% of the world’s older people. The report says the world is changing fast. By 2050, there may be two billion people aged 60 and over. They will be more than one fifth of the world’s population.

Ageing is happening fastest in developing countries. More than two thirds of older people live in poor countries. By 2050, this may rise to about four fifths. In France, it took 115 years for the share of older people to double from 7% to 14% (from 1865 to 1980). In Brazil, the same change may happen from 2011 to 2032, in only 21 years.

The index says the fastest ageing countries are Jordan, Laos, Mongolia, Nicaragua, and Vietnam. In these countries, the number of older people is expected to more than triple by 2050. These countries are in the lower half of the ranking. This suggests policymakers need to face ageing directly if they want to support their people well.

There are also differences between men and women. Women usually live longer than men. In 2012, for every 84 men aged 60 and over, there were 100 women. Many older women are disadvantaged because they have less paid work (so fewer savings), less power to make decisions in the family, and more risk of violence.

The report says that if countries take the right steps, ageing does not have to mean much higher health care spending. It says long-term investment in education and health care is important. Bolivia is ranked 46, even though it is one of the poorest countries. It has a national plan on ageing, free health care, and a universal pension that does not require contributions. Nepal is ranked 77. In 1995 it introduced a basic pension for people over 70 who had no other pension income. The pension has limits in value and who can get it, and coverage is uneven, but it shows how a poor country can start to reduce poverty in old age.

Basic health care started many years ago in Chile and Costa Rica, and it has helped their ageing populations. Education also helps later in life. Basic literacy is important because older people may need to deal with pension paperwork. In the Philippines, older people benefited from education reforms after independence in 1946. These reforms made elementary and high school education compulsory. Armenia also benefited from a strong education system, like other former Soviet Union countries. South Korea is 67th on the index, which is lower than expected. It did worse than similar countries by GDP per person, partly because it started a pension only recently.

The ageing index uses 13 indicators in four areas: income security, health care, employment and education, and an enabling environment. Most indicators count the same, but pension income coverage, life expectancy at 60, healthy life expectancy at 60, and psychological well-being count more. They count more because the data is better. Countries were included only if there was enough data.

Professor Sir Richard Jolly, who created the human development index, said this new index helps us understand the needs and opportunities of older people in a wider way. He said it challenges countries everywhere to aim higher and see what is possible.
