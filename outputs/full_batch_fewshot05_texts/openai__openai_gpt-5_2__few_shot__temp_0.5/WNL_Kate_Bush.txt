The last time Kate Bush went on stage, the mobile phone was still new and was being tested. Thirty-five years later, she is performing again, and the world is different.

Today, many people at concerts use phones and tablets. But Bush does not want fans to watch her show through a screen.

Before her much-wanted concerts at the Hammersmith Apollo in London, Bush asked her fans to put down their phones at her gigs. On her website, she wrote that they chose a small, close theatre, not a big venue or stadium. She said it would mean a lot to her if people did not take photos or film during the shows. She said she wanted contact with the audience, not with iPhones, iPads, or cameras. She said it is a lot to ask, but it would help everyone share the experience together.

Bush loves theatre and rich costumes. She may also want to stop fans from putting unclear videos on YouTube. This could help keep the show a surprise for the many fans who bought tickets for the 22 dates she is playing.

Bush is not the first artist to speak against phones at concerts. Many artists say phones can hurt the live music experience.

Roger Daltrey from The Who recently said it was “weird” that people go to a show but look at a screen instead of the artist on stage. He said he felt sorry for them, and that if you look at life through a screen you are not fully in the moment.

In 2013, Beyoncé told a fan to stop filming at a gig. She said he could not sing because he was too busy taping, and she told him to put the camera down.

This debate is not only about music. Some sports fans also talk about it. Recently, Dutch fans at PSV Eindhoven strongly protested wi-fi in their stadium. They held banners that said “No wi-fi. Support the team,” “You can sit at home,” and “Stand united”. Manchester United also told fans to leave “large electronic devices” at home, and this season they do not allow filming on tablets.

Jarvis Cocker has criticized people using phones at concerts, saying it can drive him “insane”. He said it seems stupid to watch something on a screen that is smaller than a cigarette packet.

Johnny Marr said in 2013 that fans miss the full live music experience when they are desperate to record it for later. He said looking through your phone is a wasted chance, and people should put their phone down and enjoy the gig. He also said that part of gigs is sharing what is happening with the people around you, and watching on a small screen is a waste of time.

The Yeah Yeah Yeahs put up a sign at one venue. It asked fans not to watch the show through a screen on a smart device or camera. It said to put it away as a courtesy to the person behind you and to the band.

This has also reached classical music. In June 2013, a famous pianist walked off stage because a fan was filming on a smartphone. Krystian Zimerman came back soon after and said: “The destruction of music because of YouTube is enormous.”

But Sam Watt of Vyclone, a phone app, disagreed. The app encourages people to film at concerts, upload the video, and then get a mixed video made from many people’s clips. He said artists like Bush are fighting a losing battle, and that filming adds to the experience instead of taking away from it.

Watt said filming is now part of concerts, and the app can create “really fantastic content”. He also said that if Kate Bush came round for a cup of tea, they could talk about it and he might be able to change her mind. He said it is important to understand that people want memories, and that telling everyone they cannot film may cause problems in the future. He said you have to accept it.
