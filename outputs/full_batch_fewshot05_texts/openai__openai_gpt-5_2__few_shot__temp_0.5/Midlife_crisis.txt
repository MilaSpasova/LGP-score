Sometimes, in a man’s life, he feels sadder and thinks more about death. He may think the best idea is to spend all his savings on a sports car.

Big and risky life changes are often linked to a “midlife crisis”. Some people say it is real. Others say it is a myth. Scientists now ask if animals can have something like this too.

An international team of scientists says they found evidence that middle-aged chimpanzees and orangutans have lower well-being. They say this drop in happiness is like a midlife crisis in apes.

The study was published in Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences. Some scientists were surprised. But the authors say the results may show that the midlife crisis comes from biology that humans share with our closest evolutionary cousins.

Alex Weiss, a psychologist at Edinburgh University, told the Guardian that many people think well-being drops in middle age. He said the team asked if this drop is not only human and not only caused by society, but could be an evolved tendency in middle-aged individuals.

Scientists from the US, Japan, Germany, and the UK asked zookeepers, carers, and other workers to fill out questionnaires about male and female apes of different ages.

The questions were about each ape’s mood, how much it enjoyed socializing, and how well it reached certain goals. The last question asked how the carers would feel about being the ape for a week. They gave scores from one to seven.

More than 500 apes were in the study, in three groups. Two groups were chimpanzees. The third group was orangutans from Sumatra or Borneo. The animals lived in zoos, sanctuaries, and research centres in the US, Australia, Japan, Canada, and Singapore.

After analyzing the answers, the researchers found that well-being went down in middle age and went up again in old age. Great apes in captivity often live to 50 or more. The lowest point was, on average, at 28.3 and 27.2 years old for chimpanzees, and 35.4 years old for orangutans.

Weiss said that in all three groups, well-being was lowest at ages that are like human midlife. He said the lowest scores were around 30 years old.

The team said the drop might happen because apes see depressed apes die younger, or because of age-related changes in the brain that are similar in humans.

Weiss agreed that, unlike men, apes are not known to make big and often disastrous lifestyle changes in middle age.

Robin Dunbar, Professor of Evolutionary Psychology at Oxford University, doubted the results. He asked what could make well-being change in this way across life. He said it is hard to see anything in an ape’s life that would create this pattern. He also said apes are not very good at thinking far into the future, and this is a big difference between them and humans.

Alexandra Freund, Professor of Psychology at the University of Zurich, was also doubtful. She said the idea of a midlife crisis is weak even in humans. She said she sees no evidence for the midlife crisis. If there is any drop in emotional or personal well-being, it is very small, and many studies do not find it at all.

But Weiss said the findings may help us understand the emotional crisis some men may have. He said that to understand the midlife crisis, we should look at what is similar in middle-aged humans, chimps, and orangutans.
