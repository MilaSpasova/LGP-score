Why do they do it? When you ask men who may become top referees in the future, they smile. This season, people have criticized referees more than before. Some former referees have even complained about standards. That is surprising, because referees strongly believe in supporting each other.

So why do it? Why spend many hours driving around the country to enforce rules, knowing some decisions will make people angry? Why try to move up to higher levels, until you might make decisions on TV in front of millions of people who watch you from many camera angles and in slow motion?

Part of the answer shows on Lee Swabey’s face after he blows the final whistle in Grimsby’s 2–1 win over Woking. The match is at level 5 in the English league system. He gets what referees hope for after a match: “Twenty-two handshakes,” he says proudly. A full set of handshakes, and a “well done” from both managers, means the highest satisfaction. He loves “the buzz” when a game goes smoothly. He says, “I wouldn’t spend so much time away from my family if this didn’t mean the world to me.”

Swabey is rated highly by Professional Game Match Officials Limited (PGMOL) because of his potential. He knew he was being watched at that match. PGMOL’s chief, Mike Riley, was there, with Steve Dunn, the coach for this level. They had notes and a stopwatch to record important actions by the officials.

A few weeks earlier, Riley, Dunn, and another former referee, Peter Jones, went to another level-5 match to watch another referee expected to progress: John Brooks. Brooks says, “I hope to have the opportunity to get promoted to the Premier League and officiate some of the top games in this country.”

However, the main thing they saw that day was how Brooks handled a difficult situation: he had to call off the match because the pitch was frozen. This is part of what he needs to learn before he is trusted with bigger games. Late postponements can cause problems, because clubs often do not want them. They may still have to pay staff who have arrived, but they will not get any gate money.

Brooks phoned his coach for advice and made the difficult but correct decision. Later, the club secretary came with envelopes to pay the officials for their time. The match fee at level 5 is £95, so it is clear these men are not doing it for the money.

Brooks, like Swabey, wants to move up. He knows he must develop a thick skin and learn how to deal with disappointments. How does he feel when he watches TV and sees a referee attacked by the public? “Erm … not great,” he says. He has been in a situation where he made a wrong decision, and it led to the deciding goal that was just offside. He says your heart sinks and you cannot stop thinking about it.

He says he sometimes wishes people understood the time and effort referees put in. He says it is easy to criticize, but referees try hard to get decisions right. Sometimes people will dislike you, and if you cannot accept that, you are in the wrong job.

The former referees say today’s referees have far more support, education, and tools than they had. Riley says that when he was young, there was no information offered to him, so he bought his own books on psychology and nutrition.

Brooks, in contrast, can call his coach. They speak weekly, talk about his matches, watch footage of key decisions, and plan how to improve. He also gets regular help from a sports psychologist, Liam Slack, and follows an exercise plan to help him handle the 11km he runs during a game.

Brooks says psychology is very important. He says they have talked about forgetting decisions and moving on. A big decision may happen in the first 30 seconds, but after that you must stay focused for the next 89 minutes, not worrying about whether you were right. He says Liam has taught techniques for letting go of a decision, and that working with the sports psychologist is important for mental toughness.

Jones says this whole approach can only help. He says he refereed in professional football, but looking back he felt like an amateur. He worked for British Telecom. He might referee at Newcastle on a Wednesday evening, and then at 9am the next morning he was in Leicester talking to customers. He might not have slept. Training was ad hoc. He says they were amateurs in a professional environment compared to now.

When they talk about technology, the three former referees all support it. Riley says they support anything that makes the referee’s job better and helps them be more effective on the field. The aim is to make fewer mistakes. Jones says a bad decision can stay with you for a long time—“the rest of your life,” he jokes.
