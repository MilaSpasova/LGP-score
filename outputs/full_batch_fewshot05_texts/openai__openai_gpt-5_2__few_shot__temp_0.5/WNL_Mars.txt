Many people have doubts about Mars One. Mars One is a very big private plan to send humans to live on Mars from 2025. Joseph Roche is also doubtful, but he is different from most critics because he is on the shortlist to be an astronaut.

Roche is an astrophysicist at Trinity College Dublin. In February, Mars One said he was one of 100 people still in line for the mission. He wrote in the Guardian that he has serious doubts about whether Mars One can work.

Roche says the selection process “was not rigorous enough” compared with normal astronaut programs. He also says the Dutch Mars One team is too naive to think they can succeed on their own with the supposed $6bn mission. He says they should accept it is very unlikely to happen.

Roche says Mars One should be more open and transparent. He also says Mars One does not work enough with the scientific community. He thinks Mars One should admit this plan is not realistic and should instead support other space missions that are more likely to succeed.

Roche also worries about how Mars One advertised a “top-ten” list of candidates. He says this list was not about who was most likely to be chosen. He says it was based on “supporter points,” such as points from buying official merchandise. Roche says these points do not affect selection. They only show how much money each supporter has given to Mars One.

Mars One’s timeline says it will send a lander and a satellite to Mars in 2018, then a rover in 2020, and cargo missions starting in 2022. Humans would start arriving in 2025. Crews of four would go every two years to grow the settlement. They would not return to Earth.

In February, Gerard ’t Hooft, a Dutch Nobel Prize winner in physics and a supporter of the project, said he did not think this timeline was realistic. He said it would take longer and cost more. He said: “You have to put a zero after everything.”

Roche also spoke to Medium, a US blogging platform that has also expressed serious doubts about Mars One. Medium reported that Mars One’s claimed 200,000 astronaut applications were actually 2,761.

Roche told Medium more about the selection process. He said he has never met anyone from Mars One in person. At first, Mars One said there would be regional interviews with travel, interviews, and tests over several days. But then Mars One made candidates sign a non-disclosure agreement to be interviewed. After that, the plan changed to a ten-minute Skype call.

Roche told the Guardian he did not want to do more interviews because he did not want to sound too negative about space travel.

In his Guardian piece, Roche says he loves scientific progress, and that is why the bold Mars One idea attracted him. He says Mars One was never likely to solve the money and technology problems on its planned schedule, but it was interesting to hear a new idea.

He also says he enjoyed the public debate about the ethics of future missions. He says that if a one-way mission to Mars ever became possible, he would volunteer. But he also says he does not think a one-way mission will happen in his lifetime.
