The UN says: if the world cut food waste by 25%, there would be enough food to feed all people who are malnourished. Each year, 1.3bn tonnes of food is wasted. That is about one third of all food made. About 45% of fruit and vegetables are wasted, 35% of fish and seafood, 30% of cereals, 20% of dairy products, and 20% of meat. At the same time, 795 million people have severe hunger and malnutrition.

Some countries are trying to reduce food loss. For example, new laws in France make supermarkets give unsold food to charities. The UN says food waste is one of the biggest problems for food security. Estimates say that by 2050, food production must be 60% higher than in 2005 to feed a growing world population. If we waste less food, we will use fewer resources.

Food loss and food waste happen in different ways. In developing countries, there is a lot of “food loss”. This is waste by accident, often because of bad equipment, transport, and infrastructure. In wealthy countries, there is less accidental loss but more “food waste”. This is when people throw away food because they buy too much, or when shops reject food because it does not look perfect.

In developed countries, people and shops throw away 30% to 40% of the food they buy. In poorer countries, only 5% to 16% is thrown away. A 2011 report says that in Europe and North America, each person wasted 95 to 115kg of edible food each year. In sub-Saharan Africa and south and south-east Asia, each person wasted only 6 to 11kg.

Robert van Otterdijk from the UN Food and Agriculture Organization’s Save Food programme says food waste is almost not found in the developing world. He says food waste happens where people can afford to throw food away. He gives a statistic: consumers in industrialized countries waste 222m tonnes a year, almost the same as the total net food production of sub-Saharan Africa, 230m tonnes. He also says food losses are very common in developing countries because conditions are underdeveloped, from production management to transport and distribution.

Food loss and waste also hurt the environment. The carbon footprint of food that is made but not eaten is estimated at 3.3 gigatonnes of CO2. If food waste were a country, it would be the third biggest emitter of greenhouse gases, after the US and China. About 1.4bn hectares, close to 30% of available farm land, is used to produce food that is later wasted. More surface and groundwater (“blue water”) is used to make wasted food than any single country uses for farming, including India and China.

Van Otterdijk says climate change is linked to production and consumption being out of balance with what Earth can provide. He says food production is one of the biggest sectors in the world, and if one third is made for nothing, it has a huge impact on land, water, energy, and greenhouse gas emissions.

The biggest food wasters are the US, Canada, Australia, and New Zealand, where consumers waste 39% of the food they buy. Europe follows, where about 31% is thrown away. In the UK, 15m tonnes of food is lost or wasted each year. British consumers throw away 4.2m tonnes of edible food each year. That means 11.7% of food bought is wasted when it could be avoided. This costs each family about £700 a year, or almost £60 a month.

In the UK, the foods most often thrown away are bread, vegetables, fruit, and milk. By weight, bread is wasted the most: 414,000 tonnes, which is 22.4% of all bread bought. By percentage, lettuce and leafy salads are wasted the most: 38%, or 64,000 tonnes.

For meat, poultry is wasted the most by both percentage and weight. People waste 13% of the edible parts (not including bones). For fruit, melon is wasted the most by percentage: more than a quarter of each melon is thrown away.

The UK has improved in the last ten years after a strong campaign to reduce waste. Van Otterdijk calls it “one of the great successes”. From 2007 to 2012, avoidable food waste from UK homes fell by 21%, from 5.3m tonnes to 4.2m tonnes, mostly because people knew more about the problem. Also, less uneaten food goes to landfill. In 2000–01, only 14% of household waste was recycled or composted. By 2011–12, composted food rose to 43%.

Van Otterdijk says there is strong and continuing interest in food waste, and this helps grass-roots campaigns around the world grow. He says much more must be done, and public and private sectors must help. He says that if progress continues, maybe after ten years there will be important results worldwide.
