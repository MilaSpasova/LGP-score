The view from the visitors’ centre at the south edge of Doñana National Park in southern Spain is very impressive. For bird scientists, it is like a dream. The park has 200,000 hectares of salt marsh. It is very important for birds in western Europe. Many birds from Britain stop here each year when they fly from Africa. These birds include house martins, swallows, cuckoos, and warblers. Doñana is a UN World Heritage Site. It also has some of Europe’s rarest birds, such as the Spanish imperial eagle. Some rare animals live there too, including the highly endangered Iberian lynx.

The landscape is beautiful and full of life. But it is also fragile. In 1998, a dam at Los Frailes mine, 45 km to the north, broke. Almost two billion gallons of polluted, very acidic water, mixed with metal waste, flowed into the park. The toxic waste rushed down the Guadiamar river and over its banks. It left a thick metal layer on a large area of land. After this, workers collected more than 25,000 kilos of dead fish. Nearly 2,000 adult birds, chicks, eggs, and nests were killed or destroyed.

This was Spain’s worst environmental disaster. Cleaning it up cost €90m. Spain then decided to spend another €360m, including some EU money, to restore the area. In the 1950s and 60s, parts of the land had been drained to make rice and cotton fields. Now, some of this farmland is being changed back into wetland.

This work has been expensive but hopeful. Still, Doñana is under pressure from modern life. There are plans to build an oil pipeline through Doñana. Other developers want to expand nearby tourist resorts. New hotels and golf courses would need more water, which would lower the local water table. Also, silt from nearby farms is blocking the channels that run across Doñana.

But conservationists are most worried about a recent decision by the Andalucian government. It wants to reopen the Frailes mine, which nearly destroyed Doñana in 1998. Laurence Rose of the RSPB says Doñana is Europe’s most precious bird sanctuary. It is important for local birds and for birds that travel between Africa and Britain and other parts of north-west Europe. He says Doñana already has many threats, and bringing back the cause of the old disaster is extremely worrying.

It may seem strange to reopen the mine after spending so much money to restore Doñana. But the local economy helps explain it. Spain’s bank crash in 2008 hurt the region badly. Unemployment in some parts of Andalusia is now over 30%. Reopening the mine would give more than 1,000 jobs.

Vicente Fernández Guerrero, secretary-general of Innovation, Industry and Energy for Andalusia, said the area needs these resources. He said mining could help local people keep living there. He also said this has been a mining area since Roman times.

Fernández also said the mine licence would require only modern mining methods. These methods would avoid making poisonous wet waste. The licence also says the Canadian company that ran Los Frailes cannot bid for the contract. Even after 16 years, the two sides still disagree about compensation. Fernández said the best technology would be used. He said liquid would not be used, and the tender states this clearly.

Some local people support reopening the mine, but many people oppose it. The workers’ union UGT supports reopening, and one road sign had rude words written on it about UGT. Carlos Dávila, from the Spanish Ornithological Society in Doñana, was also very worried. He said it was a very bad idea. He said they also said the mine was safe in 1998, and then Spain had the worst ecological disaster in its history.

Dávila and others are also worried about eco-tourism in Doñana. A lot of money has been spent on eco-tourism in recent years. For example, at the restaurant Dehesa de Abajo, people can eat or drink among trees where storks and black kites are nesting.

Almost every visitor had a camera, a long lens, or binoculars. People can make money from tourism linked to Doñana’s birds. This is not surprising, because the place is special. The sky is huge over the very flat land. Birds of many kinds fill the air, and sometimes the road. During my visit, a stork stood calmly in front of our car until it decided to fly away.

Rose said Spain does not have the public money it had 16 years ago. He said a new toxic spill today would cause much more damage. Dávila agreed. He said that after the disaster, Spain understood how important this place was, and worked hard to clean it up and protect it. He said now Spain seems to be forgetting that lesson, and it is very depressing.
