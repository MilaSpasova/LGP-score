Get fit in four minutes sounds too good to be true. But Dr Izumi Tabata has a training plan that is very hard. You work as hard as you can for 20 seconds. Then you rest for 10 seconds. You repeat this eight times. After it, many people are breathing very hard and are covered in sweat.

Tabata saw this before. He remembers using this system with his university students in the early 1990s. After four minutes they were very tired. But after six weeks, they saw results and were surprised.

In the early 1990s, Tabata studied Japan’s speed skating team with the coach Irisawa Koichi. They noticed that short, very hard exercise could work as well as hours of medium exercise. Tabata then did a simple experiment with students.

One group rode a stationary bike for one hour, five times a week, at a steady speed. The other group did a ten-minute warm-up on the bike, then four minutes of Tabata intervals, four times a week. They also did one 30-minute steady session with two minutes of intervals.

After six weeks, the Tabata group trained only 88 minutes a week. Their anaerobic capacity went up by 28%. Their VO2 max went up by 15%. The other group trained five hours a week. Their VO2 max went up by 10%, but their anaerobic capacity did not improve. Tabata also says they measured bigger heart size after three weeks of the protocol. He also says there is research coming that shows it lowers the risk of diabetes in humans, something they already showed in rats.

This plan is not easy. You cannot do it slowly while reading a magazine. You must do very hard bursts on a stationary bike or rowing machine, or do sprints or fast bodyweight exercises. It is like doing a 100m sprint, but doing it eight times, with only a 10-second break.

Tabata says the rule is all-out effort at 170% of your VO2 max. If you feel fine after, you did not do it right. The first three rounds may feel easy, but the last two will feel extremely hard. The original plan aimed for eight rounds, but some people could only do six or seven.

A commenter on the exercise forum T-Nation says that if you do it correctly, you should “meet God”. The commenter says most people cannot do it correctly.

Tabata does not fully agree. He says everyone can do it, but beginners should start with educated trainers. This helps them work at the right intensity for them. He says he will soon publish research showing that doing the program only twice a week still gives important health benefits.

Another finding that will be published soon, which Tabata calls “rather significant”, says the protocol burns an extra 150 calories in the 12 hours after exercise, even at rest. This happens because of excess post-exercise oxygen consumption. So it can help people get fit, and it can also burn fat.

It may seem surprising that this plan is still mostly used by serious athletes and musclehead groups. This may change because Tabata made a deal with Universal Studios. This will create a network of instructors and a DVD range released near the end of the year. Tabata says he did this because he often sees people on YouTube doing it wrong, because they do not understand how intense it must be.

Should everyone add this plan to their fitness regimens? Richard Scrivener, a former Assistant Strength and Conditioning Coach at Northampton Saints Rugby Club, says the benefits are clear. But Tabatas should be added to training, not replace a sport or main training method. He says runners need running economy, which comes from skill and running many miles. But runners could reduce long runs and total mileage by using Tabata training. This can reduce stress on the skeleton and let joints rest and recover, especially for people with small pains or past injuries. Then they may get more benefit from long runs when they do them.

People who train in the gym can do three strength sessions and three Tabatas a week. Other people can build up slowly, session by session and week by week. But it will not get easier, because each session needs maximum effort. That is the hard idea of the protocol: it does not stop, and it works.
