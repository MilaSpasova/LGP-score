New numbers suggest that more than one million British workers may be on zero-hours contracts. This means businesses may use these contracts much more than people thought.

The number comes from a survey of more than 1,000 employers by the Chartered Institute of Personnel and Development (CIPD). After a week of criticism of many organizations, including retail chains and Buckingham Palace, people again asked the government to investigate. On these contracts, workers may get no guaranteed work or pay each week. They often get no holiday pay or sick pay, and may have to ask before taking another job.

The CIPD found that 38% of people on zero-hours contracts say they work full-time, usually 30 hours or more each week. One-third of voluntary sector employers use these contracts, and one in four public sector organizations use them.

The new numbers also raise questions about official data. The Office for National Statistics increased its estimate by 25%, to about 250,000 UK zero-hours workers.

Peter Cheese, the chief executive of the CIPD, said there should be a closer look at what a zero-hours contract means, the different types, and clearer guidance on good and bad use. He said this should include both the advantages and disadvantages for businesses and workers.

Sports Direct was criticized after it was reported that about 20,000 of its 23,000 staff are on these contracts. Other organizations were also named, including Cineworld and Buckingham Palace, which uses them for 350 summer workers. Pub group J D Wetherspoon has 24,000 staff, or 80% of its workforce, on these terms.

Vidhya Alakeson from the Resolution Foundation said that if around one million people are on zero-hours contracts, it is a large part of the workforce and not just a small problem. She said the government needs a deep review that looks at the size of the issue and how these contracts affect workers’ rights, ability to earn money, and well-being.

Unions and anti-poverty groups say some employers pressure workers to sign these contracts to avoid duties and cut benefits.

Dave Prentis from the union Unison said most workers are on these contracts because they have no choice. He said they may give flexibility to a few people, but employers have more power and it is hard for workers to complain.

Workers on zero-hours contracts often find out their hours only when weekly or monthly rotas are made. But they are expected to be ready for extra work at short notice. They should get holiday pay based on the hours they work, but they do not qualify for sick pay.

The National Trust said it uses zero-hours contracts for many seasonal workers. It said it gives them the same pay and benefits as full-time staff, in proportion to hours worked, but it needs some workers to be more flexible. A spokeswoman said the organization depends on the weather, and zero-hours contracts help it change staff rotas quickly.

Politician Chuka Umunna said these contracts should be used only in special cases. He said some workers like the flexibility, but many feel insecure and do not know when work will come.

Some people say the flexibility of zero-hours contracts may have helped the UK avoid higher unemployment during the economic downturn. CIPD research also says only 16% of zero-hours workers report that their employer often does not give them enough hours each week.

The CIPD also said 17% of private sector employers use zero-hours contracts. This is lower than the voluntary sector (34%) and the public sector (24%).

The industries most likely to have at least one worker on a zero-hours contract were hotels, catering and leisure (48%), education (35%), and healthcare (27%).
