In the Arctic part of Canada, summer days are very long. Bright sunlight covers the tundra for more than 20 hours each day. Many people like this after the long, dark winter.

But for the small and growing Muslim community in Iqaluit, Nunavut, these long days are hard during Ramadan. During Ramadan, Muslims usually do not eat or drink from sunrise to sunset.

Abdul Karim is 29 years old. He moved from Ottawa to Iqaluit in 2011. He has used the local Arctic sun times for fasting. This year, he eats at about 1:30am before sunrise. He ends his fast at about 11pm when the sun sets. He said he has not fainted. He said he would stop only if fasting hurt his health. He also joked that, because of his large body, he does not think fasting will hurt him.

Near the end of Ramadan, Muslims around the world still focus on community work, prayer, and thinking deeply. But in Iqaluit and other Arctic Muslim communities, the very long days have changed how people fast.

Most Muslims in Iqaluit follow the Ramadan timetable used in Ottawa, about 1,300 miles to the south. Muslim scholars have said that Muslims in the far north can follow the timetable of Mecca or the nearest Muslim city. Even so, this still means fasting about 18 hours each day, said Atif Jilani, who moved from Toronto to Iqaluit in 2015. He said the days are long, but it is easier to manage.

Many people in the community, about 100 people, break their fast together. They meet at the city’s new mosque. It was finished in February, when temperatures went as low as -50C with wind chill. They eat nightly suppers there. They eat traditional foods like dates and goat or lamb curry, while the sun is still bright outside.

This also happens in other northern mosques in Canada during Ramadan. Muslim communities must deal with Canada’s special geography.

In Yellowknife, in the Northwest Territories, there are about 300 Muslims. Nazim Awan, president of the Yellowknife Islamic Centre, said people have different choices for fasting. Exceptions are made for people who are pregnant or ill. He said some people might want to fast for 23 hours, but others can follow the times of nearby cities, or follow the times of Mecca and Medina.

In recent years, many people there have chosen to follow the Ramadan timetable of Edmonton, in Alberta. Some people, including Awan, follow the times of Mecca. Awan has two young children, including a 12-year-old who recently started fasting. He wants to help his son by using the more manageable Mecca timetable, about 15 hours of fasting, instead of about 18 hours in Edmonton. He said that if he followed Yellowknife or Edmonton times, his son might say he was insane.

In Inuvik, a small town about 125 miles north of the Arctic Circle, there are about 100 Muslims. They also follow Edmonton’s timetable. Ahmad Alkhalaf said they currently have 24 hours of sun, with no sunrise or sunset.

Alkhalaf said this Edmonton schedule was already used in 2001, when he moved from Toronto to Inuvik, a town of 3,500 people. His first Ramadan there was in December. At that time there was no sun, and it was dark all day and night. So they used Edmonton time.

Alkhalaf said it can feel hard in your mind to follow the clock instead of what you see outside. People should break the fast at dusk, but in the Arctic they sometimes eat while the sun is still up. He said it is not usual to have iftar, the meal that ends the fast, when the sun is up.

In Inuvik, many people are Inuit. The Muslim community tries to balance Ramadan with local culture and traditions. The iftar meal includes dates and rich curries, and also local game like reindeer. The reindeer is prepared according to Islamic law. Alkhalaf said they make soup or curry, but use reindeer instead of beef.

In Iqaluit, as Muslims prepare for the end of Ramadan, some say that Ramadan in 2016 was one of the harder years because it happened during some of the longest days of the year. This was especially true for people like Karim, who followed the local sunrise and sunset. But Karim said it will be easier in future years because of the lunar calendar. Ramadan will later come during winter. In Iqaluit in winter, the sun rises and sets within only a few hours each day. Karim said he will follow those hours too.
