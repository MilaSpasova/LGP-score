A day started with new early-morning police raids at the Baur Au Lac hotel in Zurich. It ended with 16 football officials being charged in the US for corruption. This group included five current or former members of FIFA’s executive committee. They included the former Brazilian federation chief Ricardo Teixeira and his successor, Marco Polo Del Nero, who recently stepped down from the FIFA executive committee.

The US Department of Justice accused 16 people of fraud and other crimes. It described several kickback schemes in a new 240-page indictment, which replaced the one from May 2015. This brought the total number of people charged by the US to 27. It also mentioned 24 unnamed “co-conspirators,” including former FIFA executive committee members. US Attorney General Loretta Lynch said the “betrayal of trust” was outrageous and the corruption described was unacceptable.

On the same day, FIFA’s executive committee hoped to show new reforms during a corruption crisis. Swiss police arrested Juan Ángel Napout, the president of the South American football confederation, and Alfredo Hawit, the head of the North and Central American and Caribbean football body. Hawit replaced Jeffrey Webb in May 2015, after Webb was arrested in the US operation that put FIFA into crisis and led to the fall of Sepp Blatter. Webb’s predecessor, Jack Warner, was also arrested in May.

The Swiss Federal Office of Justice said the arrested men were being held until extradition. It said the US requests suspected them of taking bribes of millions of dollars. Webb and Luis Bedoya, a former FIFA executive committee member from Colombia, were among those who pleaded guilty in the US. Lynch said that since May, eight people had pleaded guilty, and five of them were not named in the original indictment.

So far, 11 current and former FIFA executive committee members have been charged in the investigation. The investigation says there were $200m in bribes. Most were kickbacks from TV and marketing contracts, and some were linked to FIFA’s development programmes. The last three presidents of CONCACAF and Conmebol have all been charged.

Lynch warned that people who still hope to avoid the investigation will not escape. Teixeira, who was once the son-in-law of former FIFA president João Havelange, was charged together with Del Nero and with José Maria Marin, who was charged in May.

In May 2015, 14 men were charged, and four more guilty pleas were entered. A few days later, Blatter won a fifth term as president, but later agreed to step down as the crisis grew. He was then temporarily suspended together with UEFA President Michel Platini, over an alleged £1.3m “disloyal payment” to Platini. Both could be banned for life if the FIFA ethics committee finds them guilty when the case is heard in December.

Other people charged on Thursday included Rafael Salguero from Guatemala, who left the executive committee in May; Eduardo Deluca, the former South American Confederation Secretary General; Manuel Burga, Peru’s former football federation president; and Carlos Chaves, Bolivia’s football president, who was already in jail in Bolivia.

Lynch said the Department of Justice wants to end the serious corruption among leaders of international football. She said this was because of the size of the schemes, how wide the operation was, and because this behaviour goes against international principles.

The acting FIFA President, Issa Hayatou, did not comment on details of the arrests. But he said neither he nor FIFA was corrupt. Speaking to the media for the first time since he took the role in September 2015, after Blatter was suspended, Hayatou said the crisis was caused by only a few people. He said, “FIFA is not corrupt. We have individuals that have shown negative behaviour. Do not generalize the situation.” He also said many people have been in FIFA for 20 or 30 years and have not been accused of anything.
