Europe will be the first place in the world to make car makers do “real-world” emissions tests. This is part of the fight against air pollution. The new rules will test cars while they drive on real roads and in traffic, not only in clean laboratory conditions, as now.

The European Commission Vice President, Frans Timmermans, approved these tests. The tests aim to enforce a limit of 80mg of nitrogen oxide per kilometre. Researchers say only one car out of 16 meets this level. Other countries, like China and Korea, are also thinking about real-world emissions tests, and they will watch what happens.

Pollution from diesel engines, such as nitrogen oxide, carbon monoxide, and particulates, is believed to cause at least one quarter of the 29,000 pollution-related deaths each year in the UK. This number may rise when the committee on the medical effects of air pollutants publishes what it calls “strengthening evidence” about harm to public health from nitrogen oxide emissions later in 2015.

The current lab test, the “New European Drive Cycle,” is about 25 years old. Technology in the car industry has moved ahead. Studies say lab results can be changed easily. For example, testers can tape up doors and windows to reduce air resistance, drive on unrealistically smooth roads, and test at very high temperatures. Campaigners also say car makers use tricks like programming cars to switch into a low-emissions mode when the front wheels spin and the back wheels do not move, as in lab tests.

Lucia Caudet, a Commission spokesperson, said the Commission is finishing a proposal for a new testing procedure. It will allow proper assessment of vehicles in real driving. The proposal still needs approval from other commissioners and a technical committee. An EU source said they do not expect major internal problems.

Greg Archer, clean vehicles manager for Transport and Environment, said air pollution kills 400,000 citizens each year partly because car makers cheat diesel car tests. He said the new real-world driving test is an important step to reduce urban air pollution. He said EU states should support the Commission’s proposals and ignore complaints from car makers that the rules are too tough.

Research from the International Council on Clean Transportation in 2014 says real nitrogen oxide emissions from cars are seven times higher than the 80mg/km standard. Some models were 22 times above the recommended limit. Only one car out of 16 met the 80g target. About one third of all nitrogen oxide pollution comes from road transport, mostly diesel. In cities, the share can be as high as 64%, according to European Environment Agency figures.

Campaigners say the car industry tried to delay changes to test cycles. Industry groups deny this and say they need five years for technical and economic reasons. Cara McLaughlin, a spokesperson for the European Automobile Manufacturers Association (ACEA), said Real Driving Emissions (RDE) is a new regulation. She said it will require big changes to emissions control hardware and may happen during a vehicle’s production lifetime. She also said ACEA accepts that RDE will apply to new types of cars from September 2017.

In an unusual step, ACEA sent Timmermans its own draft regulation, after EU representatives agreed a regulation to implement nitrogen oxide limits with stronger road trials and strict monitoring of exhaust fumes. ACEA’s draft would have covered fewer pollutants and delayed the phased introduction until 2020. It would have cut test distances from 1,300m to below 700m, raised minimum temperatures from -7C to -3C, and used more rural roads. Archer said lobbyists were heavily involved and called it an attempt to subvert the law-making process.

After Timmermans seemed to reject the ACEA proposal, the regulation will now go to commissioners for a rubber stamp and to member states for final changes, before an expected start in September. By 2017, the first real-world car emissions tests are expected to begin seriously. A similar debate is already expected about testing CO2 emissions for cars at the end of 2015. Other countries will also have similar debates, so many people will watch the EU’s new emissions tests closely.
