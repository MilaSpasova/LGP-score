During an important day at Liverpool’s Anglican Cathedral, for the families of the 96 people who died at Sheffield Wednesday’s Hillsborough football ground, one phrase was heard again and again: the truth. This word was badly misused in a headline in The Sun newspaper. We now know that South Yorkshire Police gave The Sun stories that were false and shocking, to move blame away from the police and on to innocent victims.

Margaret Aspinall, whose son James (18) died at what should have been a happy day out at an FA Cup semi-final between Liverpool and Nottingham Forest, said the families had to fight for 23 years for the truth. Aspinall, Chair of the Hillsborough Family Support Group, said their loss would never go away, but she was “delighted” by the clear and “profound” apology from David Cameron for the terrible failings at Hillsborough.

The Hillsborough Independent Panel looked at 450,000 documents from the police, Sheffield Wednesday, and other responsible groups. It produced a 395-page report. The report said officials failed badly, and it cleared the victims and football supporters.

Some facts about the disaster, and how the police later tried to change the blame, were known before. But the families said the cover-up was much deeper than many people understood. The panel said there was a deliberate police campaign to avoid responsibility and to blame the supporters.

The panel said South Yorkshire Police, in a planned campaign led by Chief Constable Peter Wright, spread a story that drunken supporters or supporters without tickets caused the disaster. The victims had blood tests for alcohol. The panel said this was “an exceptional decision” with “no rationale”. When alcohol was found, the police then checked whether the victims had criminal records.

The report, mainly written by Professor Phil Scraton of Queen’s University, Belfast, and agreed by all eight panel members, said there was “no evidence … to verify the serious allegations of exceptional levels of drunkenness, ticketlessness or violence among Liverpool fans”.

The report said that even while the families’ nightmare was beginning, Wright met the police federation in a Sheffield restaurant to prepare “a defence” and “a rock-solid story”. The Secretary of the South Yorkshire Police Federation branch, Constable Paul Middup, said at the meeting, before Wright arrived: “The Chief Constable had said the truth could not come from him, but had given the secretary a totally free hand and supported him,” and many senior officers also supported him.

This meeting happened just four days after the disaster. It was also the day The Sun printed the headline “The Truth” above lies given to it by four senior South Yorkshire police officers. Middup was pushed to continue the police campaign that attacked Liverpool supporters for supposed drunkenness and bad behaviour, and to get “togetherness” across to the force.

The panel supported the claim made in parliament that senior officers changed the statements of junior officers in an organised way, like a “black propaganda unit”. The statements, used as official police accounts in the later inquiry, were changed. Criticism of the police was removed, and supporters’ supposed bad behaviour was emphasised. The panel found that 116 of 164 statements were changed “to remove or alter comments unfavourable to South Yorkshire police”.

The police said they only removed “conjecture” and “opinion”, but the panel said it went further. Scraton said: “It was done to remove criticism of the police.”

This propaganda did not persuade the first inquiry. By August 1989, it ruled that the police stories about fan drunkenness and misbehaviour were false, and it criticised the police for making these claims. The panel report also said Sheffield Wednesday’s ground was unsafe in important ways. It said the Football Association chose it for the match without even checking if Hillsborough had a valid safety certificate. It did not.

In this unsafe situation, the main cause of the disaster was police crowd control, led by an inexperienced Chief Superintendent David Duckenfield. Outside the ground, 24,000 Liverpool fans had to go through only 23 turnstiles. Duckenfield then ordered a large exit gate to be opened, and many people were allowed in. The inquiry called his biggest mistake the failure to close the tunnel that led to the already overcrowded central area of the Leppings Lane terrace.

The inquiry established this, but the police repeated their claims at the later inquest. The inquest process was affected by the coroner’s decision not to hear evidence about what happened after 3.15pm. This left out an emergency response that the panel said was chaotic. The panel’s finding that 41 of the 96 people who died might have been saved if the police and ambulance service had done their jobs properly was very serious. Aspinall said this was hard for the families to think about.

After the panel’s report, the Attorney General will consider asking the High Court to cancel the inquest verdict of accidental death and hold a new inquest. There may also be prosecutions, even after all these years, of Sheffield Wednesday, South Yorkshire Police, and Sheffield City Council, which failed to oversee the safety of the football ground.

Trevor Hicks, President of the HFSG, whose teenage daughters Sarah and Victoria died in the crush, said: “The truth is out today. Tomorrow is for justice.”
