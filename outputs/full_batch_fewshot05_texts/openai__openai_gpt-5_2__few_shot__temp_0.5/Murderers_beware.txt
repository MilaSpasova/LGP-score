The inventor of a new computer system for autopsies says it could make it impossible to do a “perfect murder” in the future. This system is being used more and more in hospitals in Europe.

The method is called “Virtopsy”. Scientists at the University of Zurich first developed it. In a normal autopsy, doctors cut the body with a scalpel and make a Y-shaped cut on the chest. With Virtopsy, doctors can look at the body in 3-D on computer screens instead.

Michael Thali, Director of Zurich’s Institute for Forensic Medicine and one of the inventors, said Virtopsy could change criminal investigations. He said a virtual autopsy can help find every piece of evidence.

Virtopsy uses images from MRI, CT, and surface scans of dead bodies. Together, the machines are called a “Virtobot”. The technique can find injuries like lesions and blows that are often missed in a traditional autopsy. It can also find air pockets, heart attacks, and even cancer.

Richard Dirndorfer, a pioneer of DNA analysis in criminology and a developer of Virtopsy, told the German science magazine PM that Virtopsy could one day replace the autopsy. He said it could happen slowly, like DNA analysis slowly replaced blood group analysis.

Computer images let doctors look deeply inside the body. The method has already found haemorrhages and fractures that normal autopsies did not find. At first, the goal is to use it together with the traditional autopsy.

Dominic Wichmann, a specialist at Hamburg’s University Hospital, told Spiegel that it can help forensic scientists plan autopsies better. Criminologists from many countries have traveled to Switzerland in recent years to see the new method.

The US TV show CSI has shown Virtopsy twice. In one story, it proved that a victim was killed by a bullet through the cheek. In another story, Virtopsy on a murdered man allowed his wish to be cryogenically frozen, because his body stayed intact.

Scientists have worked on this method for decades. At first they worked in a cold lab on a university campus, and people laughed at them. Later, a donation from a rich ophthalmologist helped the project grow. Even then, some people rejected it because they thought it would harm the traditional skills of forensic scientists and pathologists. But younger scientists often see it as a tool to support traditional methods, and maybe replace them one day, though probably not completely.

Lars Oesterhelweg, Deputy Director of the Institute of Forensic Medicine at the Charité Hospital in Berlin, said they still need the normal cut to study things like the colour of the blood, the thickness of body fluids, or smells.

He also said the new method helps when the cause of death is not clear. It allows third opinions, new checks of investigations, and reopening of cases.

Scientists said families of the dead are often against autopsies because they damage the body. They are much more willing to accept this method because it does not cut the body.
