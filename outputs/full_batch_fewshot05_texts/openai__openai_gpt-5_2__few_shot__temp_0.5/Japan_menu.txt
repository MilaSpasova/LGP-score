The problem started with a fake “scallop” dish. Now a menu scandal has spread to some of Japan’s most famous hotels and department stores. It may hurt the international reputation of Japanese food.

One luxury hotel chain said it lied about where ingredients came from. After that, Japanese media reported new cases almost every day.

The scandal began when the Hankyu-Hanshin hotel chain in Osaka said it had written false descriptions for dozens of menu items. This happened at some of its restaurants from 2006 to October 2013. It affected about 78,000 diners.

One big example was a red salmon “caviar” dish. It was not salmon eggs. It was the cheaper eggs of the flying fish.

The company president, Hiroshi Desaki, went on TV and said he would cut his own pay by 20% and other executives’ pay by 10%. This did not calm angry customers.

A few days later, Desaki resigned. He said the group had “betrayed our customers,” but also said: “We never had the intention to deceive them.” Later, one head chef refused a medal of honour he was supposed to receive from the government.

The company has refunded more than 10,000 customers. It has paid about 20m yen so far. The total cost may reach 110m yen.

People called this Japan’s version of the UK horsemeat scandal. Like in the UK, nobody got sick from eating food with wrong labels. But people are still very angry.

For example, some customers thought they ate expensive kuruma shrimps. Later they were told they actually ate cheaper black tiger shrimps.

At first, one case got little attention. The Prince Hotel in Tokyo admitted a problem after a diner wrote in a blogpost. The diner said a “scallop” dish contained a similar but cheaper shellfish.

The hotel investigated and corrected more than 50 menu items at dozens of restaurants. This report made Hankyu-Hanshin and other hotels admit they also tricked diners who paid high prices for premium ingredients.

The Hotel Okura chain, whose guests have included Barack Obama, admitted many problems. It said it injected beef with fat to make it juicier. It also wrongly called tomatoes organic.

The chain said: “We deeply apologize for betraying the expectations and confidence of our clients.”

More examples kept appearing: orange juice from cartons sold as fresh; Mont Blanc desserts with Korean chestnuts instead of French chestnuts; bought chocolate cream sold as home-made; imported beef sold as high-end wagyu.

Even the government’s top spokesman, Yoshihide Suga, spoke about it. He said these cases appeared one after another, and wrong labels caused consumers to lose trust. He said: “These are clearly cover-ups.”

The scandal happened at a bad time. Japan is trying to get South Korea and other countries to end bans on food imports after the Fukushima nuclear accident. Also, UNESCO is thinking about adding Japanese cuisine to its intangible cultural heritage list.

One local newspaper used the headline, “Japan’s proud food culture in tears.” The Yomiuri Shimbun said it was “astonished by the industry’s lack of morals.”

The newspaper worried the scandal could harm “brand Japan,” including products and services. These are praised by foreign countries and tourists for safety and security.

Experts said the global financial crisis in 2008 made luxury hotels cut costs. At the same time, they tried to attract diners with detailed menu descriptions.

Hiroshi Tomozawa, a hotel and restaurant consultant, told Kyodo News that menu descriptions were made to match customers’ desire for brand products. When hotels could not get the ingredients named on the menu, they used food from other places instead.

The hotels and restaurants may not face legal action. Menus are not covered by the agricultural standards law or a new food labelling law planned for 2015. The only legal tool is a law that bans misleading descriptions of goods and services.

The biggest problem for the industry may be Japan’s careful and demanding consumers. In a 2009 online restaurant guide poll, 72% said where the food comes from was the most important factor when choosing dishes. After that, people cared about calories and nutrition details.
