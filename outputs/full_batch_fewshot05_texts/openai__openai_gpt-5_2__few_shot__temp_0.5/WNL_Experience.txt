It was a beautiful summer evening. I decided to go for a swim near Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland. I moved there in 2012.

A woman was in the water with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty swims often with a group of people, and she seems close to them.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Since then, hundreds of people have swum with her. Many people think she is completely tame. She was even in an Irish tourist-board advertisement, where a girl says she wants to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty on her belly. It looked safe and friendly. About 20 tourists and local people were on the pier, watching.

Just after I got into the water, Dusty left the woman and became very wild. Later I learned that she is very territorial when she is with someone. Her tail was flapping hard. At first I thought she was only showing off, but then I realized she might be angry.

I tried to swim to the pier to get out. But very quickly Dusty crashed into me with her snout. It was very strong and very painful. I was pushed forward fast.

People on the pier stared at me with their mouths open. Dusty stayed beside me, still flapping her tail. That scared me most. I thought, if she hits me with her tail, I could go under the water and die.

I reached the pier, but I could not get out because I was hurt. I felt terrible fear. I shouted for help. A man reached in and pulled me out onto the steps.

Then another man came. He said he was an orthopaedic surgeon who specialized in marine trauma. He was driving into Doolin and saw what was happening.

I was very cold and very worried. I did not know how bad my injuries were. My biggest fear was internal bleeding. He checked me and said he could not feel signs of internal bleeding, but he thought I probably had broken bones.

Later I learned my injuries were serious: six spinal fractures, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for several weeks. I could not work for five months. I had limited movement, stiffness, and pain.

Later I was diagnosed with post-traumatic stress. The near-death experience made me anxious and made me overreact. I felt people were looking at me in the wrong way. Loud noises became hard for me. I also had memory loss.

Three months before the accident, I had opened a health-food shop. But I had to give it up because I could not work. It was the hardest year of my life, but now it is in the past.

I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building my own osteopathy practice. I understand my patients better because I have been a patient too.

I am thankful that I am healthy. I also want to stop other people from getting hurt. People often think dolphins are always kind and safe. Who would think a dolphin could attack a person? If you see a fierce animal coming with its teeth showing, it is scary. But dolphins have a wide smile.

I am not angry at Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her territory, and she is a wild animal. She can be unpredictable. People need to know this. Many people come to swim with her, and they do not understand how dangerous it can be. Reports said there were several injuries that summer, including mine.

After the man pulled me out, Dusty swam away. Then she came back. She stayed upright in the water near me and looked at me. We looked into each other’s eyes. I felt she was sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin, and the anger was gone.

The people on the pier were amazed. After that short moment, my terror ended. I felt at peace with her.
