At a primary school in Stirling, when children hear the words “daily mile”, they put down their pencils and go outside to run laps around the school field. For three and a half years, all pupils at St Ninian’s Primary have walked or run one mile every day. They can do it at different times in the day. They seem happy to do it. Even though childhood obesity is rising across the UK, none of the children at this school are overweight.

The daily mile has helped the children’s fitness, behaviour, and focus in class. Because of this, many nursery and primary schools across Britain are also doing it. They ask pupils to leave their desks and spend 15 minutes walking or running around the school or a local park.

Elaine Wyllie, the headteacher of St Ninian’s, said she gets at least two emails a day from other schools and local authorities asking how they do it. She said it is amazing to think children across the country are running every day because of what they started.

Figures from the Health & Social Care Information Centre say that one in ten children are obese when they start school at age four or five. In summer 2015, a study found that schoolchildren in England are the least fit they have ever been. Many primary schools have noticed the benefits of the daily mile. Schools in London, Gateshead, Wales, and other parts of Scotland have started it. Other schools planned to start it in the 2015–6 school year. In Stirling alone, 30 schools have started it or will start it.

Wyllie said it is a simple idea to help children get fit, and it is free and easy. She said the most important thing is that children enjoy it, or it would not last. She said they come back looking bright and healthy. At St Ninian’s, teachers take pupils out during lessons to a special path around the playing field when it fits the timetable. Only ice or very heavy rain stops them.

The full benefits are not known yet. Researchers from Stirling University have started a study to find clear evidence of physical, thinking, and emotional benefits. Dr Colin Moran, who leads the study, said the children at St Ninian’s do not seem to have obesity problems, seem happier, and get ready for lessons faster. He said there are many stories about the benefits, but there are no scientific facts yet. The study will compare St Ninian’s pupils with children at another school in Stirling that has not started the daily mile.

Kevin Clelland, a primary school teacher from Leeds, visited St Ninian’s and then told his colleagues it was a great idea. He said it is simple but has a big effect. He said his school wants to improve pupils’ fitness beyond the two hours of PE they are expected to teach. His school is now building a track.

Active Cheshire, a group for sports and fitness in Cheshire and Warrington, will take senior local authority staff to Scotland to see the results of the daily mile. They hope to introduce it in all 450 schools in the region if a pilot works.

Paralympian Tanni Grey-Thompson, chair of ukactive, a UK not-for-profit health body for physical activity, said children need 60 active minutes every day. This can be in lessons, on the walk to school, or in the playground. She said it is great to see the daily mile growing. She said it shows leadership from education to improve children’s fitness and behaviour, and to help schools, teachers, parents, and young people. She also said sitting still is harmful, and moving helps children learn skills for life.

The Scottish government also supports it. A spokesperson said PE learning is improved by ideas like the daily mile. They said it can help parents teach healthy habits from a young age. They said they are pleased that many Scottish schools are taking part or planning to do so.
