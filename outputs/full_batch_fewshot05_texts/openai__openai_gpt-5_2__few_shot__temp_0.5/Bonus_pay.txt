Male bosses get bonuses that are about twice as big as women’s bonuses for the same job. Over a whole working life, this can mean men get £141,500 more than women.

New figures from the Chartered Management Institute (CMI) show that in 2012, men in UK management jobs got an average bonus of £6,442. Women got £3,029.

In the highest jobs, female directors got bonuses of £36,270 in the past 12 months. Male directors got £63,700.

These numbers made equality campaigners ask for action.

Ann Francke, the CMI chief executive, said managers should take this issue seriously. She said workplaces should have more flexibility, less “masculine” culture, focus more on results than time in the office, and be more open about performance and rewards. She said fixing this would improve companies and help people at work.

Dr Ruth Sealy from Cranfield School of Management said this is not surprising. She said bonuses can be given in a way that depends on personal choice, and the process should be more open.

Some statisticians said the data may be affected because women may work in jobs where bonuses are less common. But the bonus difference may be making the UK pay gap worse. The government says the pay gap is closing, but full-time men still earn 10% more than women.

Maria Miller, the Minister for Women and Equalities, said the CMI figures show women still lose out at work. She said the pay gap is closing, but there is still more to do.

She said the government has made pay secrecy clauses illegal. It has given tribunals power to make employers who break equal pay laws do equal pay audits. It has also signed 120 companies up to the Think, Act, Report scheme to improve how companies recruit, promote, and pay women. She also said the government has tried to help people balance work and family by introducing shared parental leave and the right for all employees to ask for flexible working.

Big companies like Tesco, BT, Unilever, and the law firm Eversheds are in Think, Act, Report. After nearly two years, the scheme has 120 supporters, up from 54 in November 2012.

CMI data also gave some support to the idea that the pay gap is getting smaller. The difference between average salaries of male and female bosses fell from £10,060 in 2012 to £8,502 in the newest CMI figures.

But CMI warned not to compare the 2012 and 2013 samples directly. Both asked about 40,000 managers, but they were not the same people.

A smaller group of 17,000 managers was followed for several years. It showed that men’s total pay is rising faster than women’s for the first time in five years. Men’s total pay rose 3.2%, and women’s rose 2.8%, including salary and bonus.

At the highest level, male directors’ earnings rose 5.3% in the past 12 months. Female directors’ earnings rose 1.1%.
