Since he was told he is HIV positive, Moses King, 48, has had one big problem. He could deal with the shame that many people in Liberia feel about HIV. He could also get antiretroviral medicine. The Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis and Malaria provided it, and the Liberian government gave it out. But King and his family, with six children, could not get the right food. He is a subsistence farmer. He grew vegetables and bought rice. But meat and fish were too expensive in Liberian markets. These foods are important sources of protein, but he could not get them.

King said, “Subsistence farming allowed us to survive, but we had so many problems. We could not get any protein, and we were not getting the nutrients we needed to sustain ourselves.”

Pate K Chon is a counsellor who works with people with HIV in Liberia. She had an idea to help. Years earlier, she watched a documentary about a fish farm in Thailand. She thought Liberia could do something like that. People with HIV could have work and also get a steady source of protein.

Chon said, “I saw this film about fish in a cement pool and I thought it was a good idea.” Chon was diagnosed with HIV in 1992. She said many people she helps cannot get a balanced diet with enough protein. She said fish is a clean source of protein. She said it does not cause health problems like other sources, and it can be farmed.

Chon started building a pool to raise fish. Chon is the founder of a faith-based NGO. In June 2012, she met John Sheehy. He is a “strategic philanthropist” and the founder of a consultancy called Emerging Business Lab. He raised money for the non-profit fish farm in the northeast of Monrovia, the capital of Liberia. He also learned about fish farming. He took an online course through Cornell University and talked to other fish farmers in Africa.

Sheehy said, “I raised the money and built the farm, learned the proper tank layout and water flow system.” He said much of what he learned was self-taught. He said he would like to write a manual and share it with other people.

Now the project is called the Grow2Feed Liberia Fish Farm. It has 12 tanks. When the tanks are full, each tank will have 5,000 fish. This can provide up to 200,000 fish per year. It serves a community of 1,200 people, mostly HIV positive, including King and his family.

The farm also uses waste from the tanks. The waste is collected and used to water crops. This also gives food and income to the community. The community has 503 women, 206 men, and 346 children.

Sheehy said community members live near the farm and agreed to join the co-operative. Many people work on the fish farm or take care of the crops. What they get back is fish, when the fish are fully grown after 90 days. They can eat the fish. They can also sell fish in the market to get money to buy other basic foods.

Sheehy said the fish farm helps people with HIV return to society. Now they trade in the market every week.

Liberia’s demographic health survey says 1.5% of Liberia’s 3.5 million people are HIV positive. Of those, 60% are women or girls. Shame and unfair treatment still happen. Because of this, about half of cases in the country do not get treatment.

Good nutrition is very important for people with HIV. Research shows they need much more protein. This helps stop their health from getting worse and helps healthy growth. Chon said, “Nutrition is one of the key things if you are taking antiretroviral drugs.” She said the drugs are toxic. If you do not have food, the drugs can make you very sick. But food in Liberia is very expensive. She said people buy expensive imported rice, even though they should grow it in Liberia. She also said fish is hard for most people to afford.

Sheehy says Grow2Feed is the first co-operative fish farm in Liberia that works to help an HIV-positive community. The project has interested the Liberian government. It has also interested the UN Food and Agriculture Organization. This organization has worked with Grow2Feed to provide training. It will also support teaching at the University of Liberia.

Fish farming experts say fish farming could grow a lot in Africa. Paul White owns the HydroFish fish farm in Ivory Coast. It produces 3,000 tonnes of fish each year. He said, “Fish farming is absolutely viable in Africa.” He said much of the fish in the market is frozen fish imported from China. He said the quality is so low that it could not enter Europe or America. He also said there has been too little investment and too little knowledge about producing fish, but this is changing now.

Some critics do not trust farmed fish. They say farmed fish can be inbred and can have high levels of toxins. But Sheehy said good practice can reduce these problems. He said much farmed fish is inbred, which can cause problems. But he said Grow2Feed uses local fish from Liberia, not fish from another region. This means they can keep using local fish to bring in new broodstock. He also said they do not use lakes that are closed off, where fish can take in toxins. He said they can control the environment in tanks. They test the water and watch it all the time.

Sheehy hopes to copy this model across Liberia and the region. He said a rice-growing co-op in Sierra Leone asked if Grow2Feed could do this on their land, to feed their workers. He also said there has been interest from Nigeria and Central America. But he said they are 100% non-profit and will not lose their social justice focus.
