Cathal Redmond was swimming near the Greek coast. Colourful fish swam past him. He thought he took great photos with his first underwater camera. But on land, the pictures looked brown and unclear. He took the photos while holding his breath. He thought he did not have enough time to set up the shots. He wanted a little more time to film the fish in their natural home.

He decided to make this extra time possible. He invented the Express Dive. It is a refillable air storage device that you hold in your mouth. It lets users swim underwater for two minutes. It is meant to sit between snorkelling and scuba diving. Snorkelling is limited, and scuba diving lets you breathe underwater, but it needs heavy and expensive equipment. The prototype looks like a mix of a scuba mouthpiece and a water bottle.

Redmond is 27. He says he wanted people to do more underwater. Instead of 30 seconds holding their breath, he wanted them to do a bit more. In 2006, the Irish designer finished a scuba-diving course. He loved breathing underwater and watching fish. But he did not enjoy all the gear, like fins, a weight belt, a wetsuit, an air tank, a mask, and other equipment.

He says he felt that about 50kg was strapped to him. Going into the water felt strange because he was used to staying on the surface. He says scuba diving can be limiting. You can stay underwater longer, but you must plan your whole day. You carry 20kg to 50kg of gear. You cannot just walk on the beach and decide to go in. Planning is very important, and you must relearn many things.

Redmond made the Express Dive during his final-year project for his product design degree at the University of Limerick. The device has two main parts. Above the surface, it uses a fan to pull in air through a vent in the mouthpiece. The air moves around a motor-driven turbine. It is pushed through valves and stored in an attached tank. A light flashes green when the tank is full. When it stops taking in air, the vent closes. When the user dives, the air goes back through the mouthpiece.

The light is in front of the user’s eyes. It works like a health bar. It changes from green to red when the air is low. The device stores enough air for two minutes. It takes about the same time to refill. Redmond says the main challenge was making it small enough to hold between the teeth and out of the mouth, even with the compressor, batteries, electronics, and other parts.

The electrical parts are protected from water inside the casing. The device is recharged by inductive power transfer. This uses an electromagnetic field, like pads that wirelessly charge mobile phones. This means there are no exposed wires.

Redmond says the mouthpiece feels like a snorkel. He says it is like an extra set of lungs. The user takes a deep breath and then uses it underwater. The prototype is made from high-density foam, aluminium, and silicone. Parts of it have been tested. Redmond says the motor can compress two minutes of air into the unit. He also says it can be held well in a diver’s mouth. He has not yet tested it on a diver who is fully underwater for two minutes. But he says that with enough testing, he can make a working device that will not put swimmers in danger.

Redmond was recently runner-up for the International James Dyson Award. This will give him £5,000 to develop the project more. He says early signs suggest the device may cost £280. It will likely weigh from 1kg to 3kg, depending on the safety features needed.

Some people may think two minutes of air is only a small improvement on snorkelling. Redmond says it could make a big difference underwater. He says a typical swimmer can hold their breath for about 40 seconds underwater. He says two minutes is not a lot of time, but it is much longer than 40 seconds.
