It is hard to complain when you can walk in the sunshine on Rio’s Copacabana beach and then watch a World Cup match. England fan Anthony McDowell from Liverpool did not feel negative about the tournament in Brazil. He said, "The place is lovely. The people are great. There’s a party atmosphere." He added, "The only thing that could be better is the England team."

McDowell and about six friends were among many supporters from all over the world. They turned the beach into a party area with national colours and chants. Some people danced, some took photos, and some drank. Most people walked and talked about football and waited for the next game on the nearby FanFest big screen. The last time so many people were here in the daytime was when the pope was visiting.

The mood was happy and mostly peaceful. This was very different from the protests, transport chaos, and stadium problems before the World Cup. Now that the football has started, many visitors want to enjoy it. Brian Hill, a retired chief executive from Sunderland, said, "If I had known, when I started planning, how complicated and costly it would be, I wouldn’t have come. But, now that we’re here, it’s great."

The trip has had problems. Hill travelled for more than 20 hours to reach Rio, and then had long delays on the road from the airport at 6.30am. His son, Andrew, had his sunglasses stolen soon after he sat on the beach. They were also surprised that many bars do not have big screens for the games. But they said they loved the atmosphere, even if the organisation was difficult. They also said the tournament has started very well on the pitch.

Robin van Persie’s diving header for the Netherlands against Spain excited many people. There were also many goals: 28 in the first eight games, almost three times as many as at the same stage in South Africa in 2010. On the streets, most fans were from nearby countries. Latin-American teams won every game they played up to the time of writing.

So far, the World Cup has not been as bad as the worst Doomsday stories, but it still has problems. Stadiums were finished late and, in some cases, not fully finished. But there were no reports of structural problems or trouble getting into the stadiums since the kick-off.

As in past World Cups, tickets have been a problem. Many seats were empty at several games, including the Netherlands against Spain. FIFA spokesman Saint-Clair Milesi said only 48,000 of the 51,900 seats were filled. FIFA is also investigating security problems after Chilean fans set off fireworks during their win over Australia.

The Globo newspaper listed problems in the 12 host cities. Almost all had worse traffic than usual. The worst transport problems were in Natal, where bus drivers have been on strike since 12 June. In Salvador, some trips took five times longer than usual. Jecilda Mello, president of the residents’ group the Association of Friends of the Historic Centre, said, "Traffic was already bad but this week it is chaotic."

Protests became smaller after the opening day. On that day, small demonstrations happened in several cities, and police used tear gas and pepper spray. Since then, the main security problem has been small theft and fans getting too excited. Police used pepper spray to break up an unplanned street party of Argentinian fans after they blocked roads.

Because Brazil is so big, people had very different experiences. The tournament made only a small difference in São Paulo, South America’s most populous city. But far away in Manaus, the remote Amazonian city where England played Italy, visitors said World Cup fever was strong, with decorated streets and flags on many cars.

FA chairman Greg Dyke said the change in mood was clear. He said they had a very warm welcome in Manaus. He also said it was strange to spend so much on a stadium with no one to play in it. He said they were in São Paulo for four or five days before the opening match, and until the last day it did not feel like there was a World Cup. He said it was weird.
