James Bond is the star of one of the world’s longest-running and most successful film series. There are 23 movies. The series has made more than $6bn at the global box office. Bond shows no sign of slowing down.

The Bond brand is stronger than ever after Skyfall. Skyfall broke records. When it was released in 2012, it became the biggest film ever at the UK box office. Skyfall has total earnings of $1.1bn. It is now in ninth place on the all-time list of biggest earners.

People were very interested when more details about the 24th Bond film were announced, including its official title, Spectre. Many people asked if Spectre can repeat Skyfall’s success. They asked if Skyfall will stay as the highest point for Bond movies, or if Spectre can continue the winning run.

Charles Gant, film editor for Heat magazine, says the signs look good. He says Skyfall was a smart move. It felt modern and also retro. It pleased Daniel-Craig-era fans, who are newer to the series. It also brought back older fans who missed the classic style and may have lost interest in the last few films. Gant says the new title is already a good sign. He thinks Spectre shows they want to keep older fans from different ages, and also keep younger viewers.

The early signs are that Eon Productions is trying hard to make another big hit. Eon Productions was founded in 1961 to make Dr No. It is behind all the “official” Bond films. The key creative people from Skyfall are working on Spectre too. Daniel Craig has been signed at least until Bond 25. The same writers have written the script.

Many Bond fans are most hopeful because director Sam Mendes is back. Mendes is mainly known for character films like American Beauty and Revolutionary Road. With Bond, he helped the series reach new heights. Gant says Mendes pleased both modern and traditional Bond audiences. Mendes also brought in top actors like Ralph Fiennes. Gant says actors like Fiennes need to be happy with the director.

Skyfall almost doubled the box office of the film before it, Quantum of Solace. Quantum of Solace made $586m worldwide when it was released in 2008. Nobody expects Spectre to double the numbers like that, but Eon will not relax.

The Bond series was not in trouble before Mendes and Craig arrived. But near the end of Pierce Brosnan’s time, the series felt a bit stale. The films had already moved far away from the Ian Fleming originals. There were no longer many real Bond stories left to adapt. The series was missing energy.

This kind of change has happened before. The Bond series has had ups and downs, stillness and renewal. It often changes around the lead actor. The actors were first Sean Connery, then George Lazenby, Roger Moore, Timothy Dalton, Brosnan, and now Craig. Each new actor was chosen because of the state of the series, and some choices worked better than others.

Lazenby made only one film. Dalton made two films: The Living Daylights and Licence to Kill. These came out in the late 80s, when 007 films were overtaken by stronger, slick Hollywood action movies. Gant says the last Roger Moore films, and then Dalton’s films, did not excite audiences much. Brosnan brought a commercial rise. Craig took the series to new levels. Gant also says the early Bond films were very successful, sexy, and exciting, and there was little else like them.

Skyfall made more money than all other Bond films. But some films from the 1960s were almost as strong when you adjust for inflation. The 1965 film Thunderball is only slightly below Skyfall. Goldfinger and You Only Live Twice did better than the other Craig films. The 1970s films The Spy Who Loved Me and Live and Let Die also did better than the other Craig films. By this way of counting, Licence to Kill is the worst-performing Bond film. Moore’s last film, A View to a Kill, is second to last.

Even so, the Bond brand has stayed very powerful for decades. Eon had to fight off other companies that tried to use Bond’s popularity. Because of a strange situation with rights, other versions of Casino Royale (in 1967) and Thunderball (made as Never Say Never Again, in 1983) were released to compete with Eon’s films. There were legal fights for many decades. Eon now has full control of both books.

The rights to Casino Royale were sold separately by Fleming in 1955. These rights later went to Eon in 1999. This happened because of an agreement between Eon’s backers, MGM, and the rival studio Sony. This cleared the way for the 2006 version.

Thunderball had a dispute because of writer-producer Kevin McClory. He helped Fleming plan the original story. He claimed ownership of the novel for many years. Because of this, he produced Never Say Never Again. After McClory died in 2006, his family later made a settlement with MGM and Eon’s parent company in November 2013.

The first big excitement for Bond lasted through the 60s and into the 1970s. But the series almost stopped after Licence to Kill did badly. Goldeneye, Brosnan’s first Bond film, brought a strong renewal. This was not only because there was a new actor, but also because of the people making the film.

After a six-year break, Eon chose Martin Campbell as director. He was an experienced British director. He created one of the most complex stunts in Bond history. In the famous opening of Goldeneye, Bond falls freely into the cockpit of a pilotless small airplane. This helped bring new energy and a modern feel to the series. A very successful Goldeneye video-game spin-off also reached a new generation. This helped people feel that Bond was no longer stale and old-fashioned.
