Introduction
Did you know that in the UK there is no law that says restaurants must give tips to staff?

This was discussed in a government review published in May 2016. Workers, employers, and customers shared their views about tipping. After the review, ministers said they wanted to change the rules so low-paid workers get the money customers leave for them. The government announced a two-month consultation on ideas they said would stop unfair treatment.

The review said that some waiters lose up to 15% of tips that are paid by credit or debit card, because of an “administration fee”. In some restaurants, the government saw evidence that waiters had to pay their own cash if they did not collect enough tips to cover these fees. The government said it would think about banning employers from charging an admin fee or making other deductions.

The government also said customers should know that tips are optional. It asked for a clearer system, with restaurants showing their tipping policy clearly.

We asked waiters around the UK what they think about tipping (including how much tip money they get and if it is fair) and what could make it better.

1. Rodri, 37, London: “Around 50% of a waiter’s income is tips”
Average tips: £60 per eight-hour shift
I think they treat waiters best in ... the US

Rodri says things became fairer after a tipping scandal in summer 2015. At that time, dozens of restaurants were found to be taking some of the staff’s tips for administration fees. But he still hears bad stories from new workers at some chains.

He has heard of restaurants not giving chefs a share of the tronc (a system used to share tips). Some restaurants take 4% of all sales to pay the chefs and as an admin fee. So if you sell £1,000 of food, the company takes £40. This happens no matter how much you get in tips. If a table has a £100 bill and does not tip, the waiter must pay £4 from their own money. Rodri says this creates a culture of taking money and can lead to worse service.

He usually gets about £60 in tips per shift. On a good night it can be over £100, and on a bad night about £40. He says about 50% of a waiter’s income is tips. He thinks restaurants should have to publicly explain exactly how tips are handled. He says the US is good because everyone tips.

2. Elle, 22, Edinburgh: “We never know whether it’s fair”
Average tips: £20 per eight-hour shift
I think they treat waiters best in ... France

Elle works three part-time zero-hours jobs in catering and hospitality. Her day job is in a cafe where the small team works both front of house and in the kitchen, so all tips go into one pot and are shared equally.

Her main evening job is in a restaurant where staff do not get tips, but they are paid minimum wage plus £2.50 extra per hour. She says this extra pay is meant to cover average tips when tips are shared with kitchen staff. Her third job is events catering, and nobody tips there.

Elle says many customers tip by card, and staff do not see how much is tipped. So they do not know if they are getting a fair share. She says the system seems better in France. People do not tip much, but waiting tables is seen as a real job with job security and decent pay.

3. Ashley, 22, London: “Tips go towards customer breakages”
Average tips: £10-15 per eight-hour shift
I think they treat waiters best in ... Australia

Ashley works evenings in a pub in south-east London and does day shifts at a local restaurant. In both places, all tips are collected and shared at the end of the night. Some money from the tip jar is used to pay for breakages caused by staff or customers. If someone leaves early, they often do not get tips, because the tips are shared just before the pub closes.

Ashley says it is very unfair that tips are shared, especially if one worker does not do their job well. Also, if someone works very well, they still do not get what they earned. Ashley thinks it is unfair that tips are used to pay for breakages made by customers. Ashley says managers should pay for broken glasses and plates without taking tips.

Ashley says they might make around £20 in tips in a shift but only get £10-15 after sharing. Tips can make a big difference to their money. Sometimes Ashley earns over £50 in one night, almost double for that day, but never gets more than £30 after sharing. Ashley relies on tips because they are paid £7 an hour.

Ashley would prefer a good basic wage, like in Australia, so workers do not have to depend on tips.

4. Tom, Manchester: “A big night of tips can help pay the rent”
Average tips: £40 per eight-hour shift
I think they treat waiters best in ... Italy

Tom says that where he used to work, floor staff kept 80% of cash tips and 40% of card tips. The other 20% of cash tips went into a pot for commis waiters and the service bar. The other 60% went into a tronc system, which helped increase kitchen pay. Tom is not sure if this was to reach the agreed salary or to give bonuses.

Tom says it is hard to say how much he earned per shift, but maybe about £40, with £20 from card tips. He says tips can change your weekly money a lot. Sometimes people needed a big night of tips to pay the rent.

Tom says tipping is done well in Italy. People usually do not add a service charge. Instead, they often round up the bill. For example, if a meal costs €19, they leave a €20 note and do not ask for change. He says people respect the staff, and many people make a career in waiting, which shows in their experience.
