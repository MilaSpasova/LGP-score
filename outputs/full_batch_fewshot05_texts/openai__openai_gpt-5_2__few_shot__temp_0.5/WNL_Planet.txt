A new scientific study says that if climate change is not stopped, the temperature rise will be very severe.

The lead scientist said that if we do not cut greenhouse gas emissions, Earth will warm by at least 4C by 2100. This is twice the level that the world’s governments say is dangerous.

The study says that as the planet warms, fewer clouds will form. Clouds reflect sunlight back into space. With fewer clouds, less sunlight is reflected, and temperatures rise even more. Scientists have long been unsure about how clouds affect future warming.

Professor Steven Sherwood from the University of New South Wales in Australia led the work. He said the study explains what controls the cloud changes and makes the lowest warming estimates much less likely.

Sherwood said 4C would probably be catastrophic, not just dangerous. He said it could make life very difficult, or even impossible, in much of the tropics. He also said it would guarantee the eventual melting of the Greenland ice sheet and some of the Antarctic ice sheet. This would raise sea levels by many metres.

Other scientists said the research is a big advance. It cuts in half the uncertainty about how much warming comes from higher carbon emissions. The study was published in the journal Nature. Hideo Shiogama and Tomoo Ogura from Japan’s National Institute for Environmental Studies said the cloud explanation was convincing. They agreed it suggests future climate change will be greater than expected. But they said more work is needed to make temperature predictions even narrower.

Scientists measure how sensitive Earth’s climate is to greenhouse gases by estimating how much the temperature would rise if CO2 in the air doubled compared with pre-industrial levels. On current trends, this could happen within 50 years. For two decades, estimates have ranged from 1.5C to 5C. The new research narrowed this range to 3C to 5C by studying clouds, the biggest cause of uncertainty.

The researchers checked that computer climate models show cloud formation correctly. These models are the only tool scientists have to predict future temperatures. When water evaporates from the oceans, the vapour can rise over nine miles and form rain clouds that reflect sunlight. Or it can rise only a few miles and then sink without making clouds. In the real world, both happen. Models that include both processes predicted much higher future temperatures than models that include only the nine-mile-high clouds.

Sherwood said climate sceptics often criticize climate models. He said models are not perfect, but the bigger mistakes are in models that predict less warming, not more. He also said sceptics point to a “hiatus” in temperatures since the end of the 20th century. But he said there is growing evidence that this “hiatus” is not seen in other parts of the climate system and is almost certainly temporary.

Global average air temperatures have risen more slowly since a high point in 1998, which was caused by El Nino. But observations show that greenhouse gases are still trapping more heat. Over 90% of this extra heat is going into the oceans. Also, a study in November 2013 said the “pause” may mostly be an illusion because there are not enough temperature readings from polar regions, where warming is greatest.

Sherwood said his team cannot fully rule out temperature rises at the lower end of predictions. But he said that for much lower warming to happen, there would need to be a major missing ingredient that we have no evidence for now. He said this is possible but would require a lot of faith. He added that warming of at least 4C by 2100 will strongly affect the world and the economies of many countries if we do not urgently start to curb emissions.
