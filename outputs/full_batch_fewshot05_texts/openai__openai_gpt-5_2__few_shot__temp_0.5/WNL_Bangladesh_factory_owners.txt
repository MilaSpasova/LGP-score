An international agreement to make clothing factories in Bangladesh safer may face legal action. Some factory owners want money back for the cost of closing factories and doing repairs.

Some repair work may take months. Factory owners say they cannot pay workers while the factory is closed, and also pay for big repairs to make buildings safe. These repairs started after the Rana Plaza building collapsed in Dhaka, Bangladesh, in 2013. In that accident, 1,138 people died.

Every month, hundreds of Bangladeshi clothing factories are checked for fire safety and building problems. This is under the Accord on Fire and Building Safety in Bangladesh. More than 170 international brands support it, including Primark and Marks & Spencer. International trade unions also support it, including IndustriALL.

The owner of a Dhaka factory called Softex Cotton has threatened legal action against the Accord. His factory was closed because of building problems. He is asking for a reported $100m in compensation.

Another factory owner said that if a factory closes, even for a few months, it can lose orders and close forever. He said there is “no such thing as temporary closure.” He also said the Accord did not clearly say who must pay when factories close, and there is no clear process for these costs.

Jenny Holdcroft, policy director at IndustriALL, said the agreement helps factories keep orders during closure. She said brands promised to keep orders with suppliers for two years.

Holdcroft said the Accord has found 12 factories that need major work so far. She said many may only need partial closure, so production can continue on other floors. She also said the Accord legally requires brands to make sure workers are paid during closures. She said the agreement did not clearly say who makes the payments, so factory owners who can afford repairs and worker pay will contribute.

Holdcroft said payment was always going to be negotiated. She said brands do not want to pay if rich factory owners keep profits and do not spend money on their factories. She said some disruption was expected, and without disruption there would be no change. A spokesman for the Accord said talks about payments, and even legal action, will not stop the safety work.

But pressure on the Accord has increased. A rival safety group, the Alliance for Bangladesh Worker Safety, is supported by US retailers including Walmart and Gap. The Alliance set aside $5m to help pay workers for up to two months while repairs are done in buildings it found need improvement. So far, it has found five factories in four buildings where production had to stop.

Shaidullah Azim, a director of the Bangladeshi Garment Manufacturers and Exporters Association, said this created confusion. He said the Alliance is sharing workers’ salaries with factory owners, and he asked the Accord to do the same so workers can be helped.
