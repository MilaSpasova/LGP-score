More and more people want to find dark places to see the night sky. On Black Fell, above Kielder Water in Northumberland, a group of people wait in a car park near a strange wooden building. This is Kielder Observatory. It is part of a new kind of tourism in Britain: people travel to see stars. The people waiting are lucky. Many more people wanted to come, but only a small number can enter.

Inside the observatory, near a woodburner and under low lights, the founder and main astronomer, Gary Fildes, talks to his team and volunteers. They hope to see the northern lights, but Fildes thinks it is unlikely. So they plan to point their strong telescopes at Jupiter and Venus. Later they will look for stars like Capella and Betelgeuse. People may also see the International Space Station.

Fildes tells his team to talk with visitors, entertain them, and inspire them. He plays music, like Pink Floyd, the Jam, and the Pogues. He says that by 9.30 the sky will look great.

Fildes is 49 and is a leader in astrotourism in the UK. A big change came in 2013. The national park with Hadrian’s Wall, plus Kielder Water and Forest Park, about 1,500 sq km, got Dark Sky Park status. It was the only one in England. Dark Sky Parks are rare. A 2013 Star Count showed that only 5% of people in the UK can see more than 31 stars on a good night.

The International Dark-Sky Association (IDA), based in Tucson, Arizona, gives this status only to places that work hard to stop light pollution. They must also show that their night skies are dark enough. In Northumberland Dark Sky Park, it is so dark that Venus can make a shadow on the ground.

Duncan Wise works for the Northumberland National Park Authority. He helped lead the campaign for dark-sky status after the Council for the Protection of Rural England said the area was one of Britain’s most peaceful places. Wise said people often look only up to the horizon, but we should also look at what is above it. He and others worked for years on the IDA application. They collected many light readings and worked with local councils, park groups, and community groups. They made a plan for outside lighting that affects new buildings in the area.

Their work paid off. About 1.5 million people visit Northumberland each year, and many now know it has Dark Sky status. A worker at a car-hire firm in Newcastle says many people come to see the sky now. They come in autumn and winter, when it is darkest. This helps B&Bs because they now get customers all year. Some local hotels give guests night-vision torches and put out deckchairs at night. Hotels can also get a badge that says they are “Dark Sky Friendly” if they have some astronomy knowledge.

Wise says Northumberland should do more with this special resource. He thinks the region needs a couple more observatories so visitors will see what they came for. A £14m national landscape discovery centre will include an observatory when it is finished in a couple of years.

Fildes has bigger plans. He wants to build Britain’s first “astrovillage”. It would have the largest public observatory in the world, a 100-seat auditorium, a 100-seat planetarium, a one-metre aperture telescope, and radiomagnetic and solar telescopes. The project would cost many millions of pounds. It would also have a hotel and would bring 100,000 people a year. That is four times more than the number who can use the observatory now. Fildes does not say much about who supports the plan, but he believes it will be real by 2018.

But Northumberland has competition. Galloway Forest Park in Scotland also has Dark Sky Park status. Exmoor got Europe’s first International Dark Sky Reserve status in 2011, which is one level below Dark Sky Park. After that, many local businesses started offering stargazing breaks and safaris. The UK also competes with northern Chile, which has more than a dozen tourist observatories and some of the clearest skies in the world. Teide National Park in Tenerife is also becoming a big astrotourism place.

Why do people want to look up at the sky? The media help. TV presenters like Brian Cox have brought in a new group of stargazers. Wise says Brian Cox has made astronomy easier for people to understand. Technology also helps. Apps like Stellarium can turn smartphones into small planetariums. Fildes thinks people are starting to value what is above them. He says that if you could build a visitor attraction from nothing, nothing is better than the universe.
