Wales will be the first country in the UK to use an opt-out system for organ donation. This means people will be treated as if they agree to donate their organs after death, unless they choose to opt out.

The Welsh Assembly voted for this policy. It lets hospitals assume that a person who dies wanted to donate, unless the person had officially registered an objection.

The final stage of the bill passed by 43 votes to eight, with two people not voting. Some religious groups objected for moral reasons. Some people also worried it could make grieving families feel more upset.

The Welsh Health Minister, Mark Drakeford, said this is an important day for Wales and for the 226 people in Wales waiting for an organ transplant. He said Wales is taking action to increase organ donation and give hope to people waiting for a transplant.

Drakeford said family refusal is a major reason there are not enough donations. Often, families refuse because they do not know what their loved one wanted.

He said families still have an important role. The goal is to respect the wishes of the person who died. Relatives or friends can object if they believe the person would not have wanted to donate.

He said that when families know the person wanted to donate, they usually agree. He said the new law will help make people’s wishes clearer and increase agreement to donation.

The law is planned to start in 2015, and Drakeford said the rest of the UK will watch with interest.

The issue is controversial. Opponents worry that the need for kidneys and hearts could lead to the wishes of the dead person and the family being ignored. But ministers say there will be safeguards.

Some people may not register their opposition. Because of these worries, the government said families would have a bigger role. Relatives will have a clear right to object, so they can show the person would not have wanted to be a donor.

Wales made this change because there are not enough organs. Drakeford said about one person each week dies in Wales while waiting for an organ.

About one third of people in Wales are on the organ donor register. But in surveys, well over two thirds say they are happy to be donors. Drakeford said many people just do not get around to registering, and the new system aims to reach these people.

The new law would apply to people over 18 who have lived in Wales for at least one year before they die. The donated organs could go to people in Wales or to anyone in the UK.

Doctors are pleased. The British Medical Association has supported an opt-out system for a long time. It is worried about the growing number of people who need transplants.

The number of young donors fell a lot after seatbelt laws began. In recent years, there have been big efforts to increase the number of people who carry an organ donation card, and there has been success. Hospitals have also improved how they organise transplants, including important talks with relatives when the dead person’s wishes are not known. But there are still not enough organs.

Some religious groups strongly oppose the plan. They say it could cause more distress for bereaved relatives. People from the Muslim Council of Wales and the South Wales Jewish Representative Council have shown concerns. The Archbishop of Wales, Barry Morgan, said donation should be a gift of love and generosity. He said that if organs can be taken unless someone has clearly registered an objection, it is not an expression of love, but more a medical use of a body.
