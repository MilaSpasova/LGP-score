Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is theft. He says it is like putting your hand in someone’s pocket and stealing their wallet. In an article for Index on Censorship, Pullman, who is president of the Society of Authors, strongly supports copyright laws. He criticizes people who think it is OK to download music or books without paying.

Pullman writes that new technology can look exciting, but people may not see that what they do is wrong. He says it is shocking when people steal an artist’s work and do not get punished.

He wrote this after music industry leaders met British Prime Minister David Cameron in Downing Street to talk about web piracy.

Pullman, the writer of the His Dark Materials trilogy, says authors and musicians often work in poverty and are unknown for many years. They work hard to make something that audiences enjoy. But when they finally might earn money, illegal downloading can take that away. He says the rule is simple: if we want to enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

In the next issue of the magazine, Pullman writes in a dialogue with Cathy Casserly. She is the chief executive of Creative Commons, which offers open content licences that let creators control copyright in a different way. Casserly says many things are wrong with copyright, which was made in an “analogue age”. She says copyright often stops people from sharing, building on, and remixing other people’s work.

Casserly says artists need to think of new ways to share and make money from their work. She quotes science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees. The editor, Rachael Jolley, says current copyright laws do not work well in the digital age and may turn consumers into criminals. She says we need new ways for artists, writers, and musicians to earn a living.

The debate is active, and illegal downloading is very large. Ofcom data suggests that in the UK, between November 2012 and January 2013, 280 million music tracks were pirated. Also pirated were 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files.

Ofcom says 18% of internet users over 12 admit they recently pirated content, and 9% say they are afraid of getting caught. Pullman says it is still surprising how easy and fast people can get music as MP3 downloads, especially for people who have been building their iTunes list for some time.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron appointed the Conservative MP Mike Weatherley as his adviser on this topic. A spokesman for the BPI, the record industry trade body, said Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creative producers it protects. The spokesman said they hope Weatherley’s influence, and the prime minister’s support, will affect the approach of the UK’s intellectual property office.
