Loneliness is now a big topic. The Office for National Statistics says Britain is the loneliest country in Europe. British people are less likely to have close friends or know their neighbours than people in other EU countries. Many also have no one to depend on in a crisis. Research by Professor John Cacioppo at the University of Chicago says loneliness is very bad for older people’s health. It is twice as bad as obesity and almost as big a cause of death as poverty.

But these studies often miss loneliness in young adults. In 2010, the Mental Health Foundation said loneliness was a bigger worry for young people than for older people. People aged 18 to 34 were more likely than over-55s to feel lonely often. They also worried more about being alone and felt depressed because of loneliness.

“Loneliness is a known problem for older people, and there are day centres and charities to help them,” says Sam Challis from the mental health charity Mind. “But when young people reach 21, they are too old for youth services.” This is a problem because loneliness is closely linked to mental health. It is linked to more stress, depression, paranoia, anxiety, addiction, and cognitive decline, and it is a known factor in suicide. In a new essay, Paul Farmer of Mind and Jenny Edwards of the Mental Health Foundation say loneliness can both cause mental health problems and happen because of them.

What can young people do about loneliness? Dr Grant Blank at the Oxford Internet Institute says social media and the internet can help, but they can also cause problems. They help when they let us talk to loved ones far away. But they are not good when they replace meeting people in person. Challis says people show a perfect version of their life online, and we start to expect lives like those in the media. When we compare our lives with others’ “perfect” lives, we may stop seeing people.

Meditation methods and apps like Headspace are popular, and people often suggest them for mental health problems. But they may not help loneliness, because they can make us sit alone with our thoughts. Challis says it may be better to look at the real reasons you are lonely first, and ask what is stopping you from going out and seeing people.

A 2013 University of Michigan study on social media found that Facebook can reduce life satisfaction. But technology can help if it helps you meet new people. If you cannot go outside, the internet can still bring comfort. Maddy Matthews, 19, is a student with a two-month-old daughter. Since the birth, she rarely sees her university friends, and her partner works most evenings. In the first days, she stayed up late to feed her baby and worried she was doing something wrong. She says posting on Mumsnet helped her feel less alone.

Helplines can also help, at least for a short time. One in four men who call the charity Samaritans mention loneliness or isolation. Get Connected is a free private helpline for young people. They can ask for help with emotional and mental health issues linked to loneliness. Mind’s website also has support services that can remind you that you are not alone.

At work, it can help to tell your employer how you feel. John Binns advises businesses on mental health and well-being. In 2007, he went to hospital for stress-related depression and took two months off work. He felt he had no one to talk to, and he was not close enough to colleagues for them to notice changes in his behaviour. Being more open with his employer and colleagues made it easier to go back to work. He says colleagues can be more supportive than you expect. His colleagues reached out, invited him to lunch, and told him that things had not changed much since he left.

Small talk at the office may seem useless, but it can protect us from the emotional and mental effects of work stress. Rick Hughes from the British Association for Counselling and Psychotherapy (BACP) says that if you connect with your team, you might feel stressed but not alone.

Nicky Forsythe, a psychotherapist and the founder of Talk for Health, says we often think our social networks are not important, but they are very important for our well-being. She says the most important thing is to have a regular time and place to think about your life and to have a kind, understanding listener.

Counselling can help you learn skills like empathy. The BACP website lets you search for counsellors near you. Hughes says that talking about a problem can help, and sometimes you need to talk to someone neutral, not a friend or family member. Most universities offer this kind of counselling to students, and many have group sessions that focus on loneliness.

Recent research suggests loneliness is killing older people. Because the population is ageing, we should try to reduce isolation before it is too late. In a new report, Ruth Sutherland of Relate says getting older does not have to mean getting lonelier. But she says this depends on building good relationships earlier in life.
