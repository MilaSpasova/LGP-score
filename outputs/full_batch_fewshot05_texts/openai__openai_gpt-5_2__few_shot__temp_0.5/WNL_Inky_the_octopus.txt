An octopus escaped from the National Aquarium in New Zealand. It got out of its tank, slid down a 50-metre drainpipe, and went into the sea.

The octopus was named Inky. He is a common New Zealand octopus. The lid of his tank was left a little open by accident. Staff think that at night, when nobody was there, Inky climbed out of the glass tank and moved across the aquarium floor.

Rob Yarrell, the national manager of the National Aquarium of New Zealand in Napier, said octopuses are famous for escaping. He said Inky was not unhappy or lonely, because octopuses live alone. He said Inky was very curious and wanted to see what was outside.

One idea is that Inky moved three or four metres across the floor and then went into a drainpipe that led straight to the sea. The pipe was 50 metres long and opened into Hawke’s Bay, on the east coast of New Zealand’s North Island.

Another idea is that Inky squeezed into an open pipe at the top of his tank. This pipe went under the floor to the drain. Yarrell said he was very surprised when he came in the next morning and the tank was empty. He said the staff felt sad. He also said Inky often surprised them. Yarrell has not started a search for Inky.

Reiss Jenkinson, an exhibits keeper at the aquarium, said he is sure nobody took Inky. He said he knows octopus behaviour well. He has seen octopuses on boats slip through bilge pumps. He also said security at the aquarium is too tight for someone to take Inky.

Octopuses have no bones, so they can fit into very small spaces. They have been filmed going through gaps as small as coins. People also think octopuses are very intelligent and can use tools.

At the Island Bay Marine Education Centre in Wellington, one octopus often went to another tank at night to steal crabs, and then returned to its own tank. Another octopus there, Ozymandias, was thought to have broken a world record for opening a jar before it was released into the ocean.

Inky came to the National Aquarium a number of years ago. A local fisherman found him caught in a crayfish pot. Yarrell said Inky was scarred and rough-looking, with shorter limbs. He said Inky had been living on a reef and fighting with fish, so he was not in good shape.

Yarrell said Inky is about the size of a rugby ball and was unusually intelligent. He said Inky was friendly, curious, and popular with visitors. The aquarium has another octopus named Blotchy, but Blotchy is smaller than Inky. Yarrell said Inky had more personality.

The aquarium does not plan to add more security because Inky was a one-off case. But the staff now know more about what octopuses can do. The aquarium is not looking for a new octopus, but if a fisherman brought one, it might take it. Yarrell said there is always a chance Inky could come back.
