A new tiger count says Nepal has more tigers now than at any time since the 1970s. This gives conservationists hope. Tiger numbers have been falling across south Asia for 100 years, but they may still be saved.

The government survey says Nepal now has 198 wild royal bengal tigers. This is a 63.6% increase in five years. Maheshwar Dhakal, an ecologist with Nepal’s Department of National Parks and Wildlife Conservation, said this is very encouraging.

The count used photos from more than 500 cameras. The cameras were in five protected areas and three wildlife corridors. More than 250 conservationists and wildlife experts worked on the survey. It cost about £250,000. Dhakal said India did a similar survey. The results from both countries will be published later in 2013. India has about 1,300 tigers in several very large protected areas, and it needs a few more months to finish its data.

Nepal promised to double its tiger population by 2022. It wants to grow from 121 tigers in 2009, when the last full tiger count happened.

In Asia, rising prosperity has raised the prices of tiger skins and body parts used in traditional Chinese medicines. International gangs pay poor local Nepali large amounts of money to kill tigers. The skin and bones go to middlemen. These middlemen can cross the weak border to India easily, where the main dealers are.

A big problem is that some senior officials work together with mafia networks in this trade. Experts say tiger numbers rose because national parks are policed better, anti-poaching work is stronger, and tiger habitats are managed better. In Nepal, forests cover 29% of the land. But experts say Nepal must do more to protect tiger habitat and the animals that tigers hunt, so tigers have enough space and food.

As tiger numbers increased, conflicts with villagers also increased. Park officials said tigers killed seven people near national parks in 2012, compared to four in 2011. Villagers want more protection. Krishna Bhurtel, a village headman in Chitwan, said the government should also make plans to protect people from tigers. In Chitwan, wildlife authorities caught a tiger after it killed two people, including a villager pulled from his bed in May.

Thousands of tigers once lived in forests in Bangladesh, India, and Nepal. Now there are about 3,000. This is a 95% drop over a century. In Nepal, Chitwan National Park has the most adult tigers, with 120. Bardiya National Park has 50, and Shukla Phanta Wildlife Reserve has 17.

Diwakar Chapagain, who leads a World Wildlife Fund Nepal unit that watches wildlife trade, said tiger skins are wanted in Tibet. Rich people there use them as festival clothes.

In Nepal, kings once stood on tiger skins in front of stuffed tigers for special events. Some rich Nepali have tiger heads on the walls in their living rooms. Tiger bones are also in high demand for traditional Chinese medicines. Chapagain said this illegal trade makes a lot of money and can bring in thousands of dollars, which is a serious threat to tigers.
