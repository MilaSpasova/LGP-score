In 2010, the old city in Srinagar was very dangerous. Police only went there with body armour. Violent separatists wanted an independent Kashmir. Big uprisings happened there, and more than 100 people died.

Now things are different. Tourists walk in the same streets and buy food like mutton tikka and rogan josh. The Nowhatta mosque, where young people once gathered after Friday prayers to throw stones at security forces, may become part of an official walking tour about heritage, crafts, and markets. Near Dal Lake, many houseboats are booked months ahead. Visitors take photos in the gardens by the lake. Until the snow melted, nearby ski resorts were also full of rich Russians.

In 2002, only a little over 27,000 tourists visited the Kashmir Valley because they feared the anti-Indian insurgency, which has killed up to 70,000 people. In 2012 so far, almost one million holidaymakers have visited, and more than 23,000 came from outside India. But fewer than 150 were Britons. One reason is the UK Foreign Office travel advice, which warns people and lists recent security incidents.

Omar Abdullah, the UK-born Chief Minister of Jammu and Kashmir, asked the British High Commission in Delhi to soften the advice, but it did not change. He said this is frustrating. He said that because of the travel warning, people’s insurance may not be valid if they visit. The last well-known case of foreign tourists being murdered in J&K was in 1995. Then, six westerners, including two Britons, were kidnapped by Al-Faran, a Kashmiri militant Islamist group. Only one person escaped alive.

Abdullah said British people have died in other places, but the UK did not stop travel there. He mentioned 9/11 and travel to New York, and deaths in Spain and in Bali. He also said Indians have died in London, but Indians still travel there. He said there is no reason to treat Jammu and Kashmir, or even Srinagar, as especially unsafe.

Germany made its travel advice less strict in 2011. Germany said foreigners are usually not direct targets in clashes. Many people thought this was a bold move because it was less than a year after the 2010 troubles ended. On 15 August there was a national holiday for 65 years of Indian independence. In the past this day could be dangerous in the state, but this time there was no trouble.

Abdullah said tourists are safe if they take normal precautions. For example, he said they should not go trekking near the border between the Indian- and Pakistani-controlled parts of J&K. Syed Ali Shah Geelani, leader of a pro-Kashmiri independence party, strongly disagrees with Abdullah on politics. But they agree about tourism. At the start of summer, Geelani wrote an open letter to tourists and pilgrims. He said that no matter their faith, language, or region, they share a bond of humanity. He said tourists are honoured guests, and locals should respect and protect them.

Some visitors may feel uneasy about having fun in a place where people have very high anxiety and mental health problems. But locals in Srinagar strongly support tourism. Amjid Gulzar, 26, said Abdullah should look for truth and reconciliation, and also bring foreign visitors. He said without tourism the economy will be in chaos. He welcomed the one million tourists this year, but said Kashmir needs to attract more big-spending visitors, especially foreigners.

Gulzar said they need better infrastructure: better roads and reliable electricity. He also said tourists need more to do in the evening. He said the city does not even have one cinema, and there is not enough to do after dark.

Some people asked if tourists will feel welcome. In June, a local Islamic group gave a “dress code” for foreign tourists. Abdullah said no one expects tourists to cover their faces. He said it was probably about short shorts and sleeveless vests. He said the main idea was to respect local culture and dress appropriately.

Abdullah said he is pushing tourism because he needs to help the economy. Jammu and Kashmir has serious money problems after more than two decades of turmoil. The state gets only £72m each year in taxes, but salaries for 500,000 public employees cost £155m. Abdullah said he needs more money quickly. For now, he is careful but happy to see tourists return. He said one million tourists does not mean everything is normal. But he feels satisfied that people can come, enjoy themselves, and go home.
