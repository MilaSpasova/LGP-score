Researchers were shocked not by how hard the challenge was, but by how much people tried to avoid it. The task was simple: sit in a chair and do nothing, only think.

Some people found this so boring that they chose to give themselves mild electric shocks to stop the boredom.

In 15 minutes alone, about two-thirds of the men pressed a button to give themselves a painful shock. Under the same conditions, about one-quarter of the women did this. Scientists think this may be because men often look for stronger feelings than women.

Psychologists at Virginia and Harvard Universities wrote a report about why many people find it hard to do nothing. In more than 11 studies, they found that people did not like being left alone with their thoughts. This was true no matter their age, education, income, or how much they used smartphones or social media.

Timothy Wilson, who led the work, said the results may not be because of modern life or phones and social media. Instead, these things may be popular because people feel a strong need to do something rather than nothing.

In the first experiments, students were taken one by one into a plain room. They had no phones, no books, and nothing to write with. They were told to sit and think. They had to stay seated and not fall asleep. They were told they would be alone for six to 15 minutes.

After time was up, students said they did not enjoy it. They found it hard to focus, and their minds wandered. Even when they were given time to plan what to think about, it did not help.

The researchers tried the experiment again with people at home. The results were similar, but people felt even worse. Some cheated by getting up or checking their phones.

To see if this was only true for students, the scientists studied more than 100 people aged 18 to 77 from a church and a farmers’ market. They also did not like being alone with their thoughts.

Then the most surprising result came. The researchers wanted to see if people would choose something unpleasant instead of nothing. Students were given the choice to give themselves a mild electric shock.

Earlier, the students had rated how unpleasant the shocks were, and also rated other unpleasant things, like looking at pictures of cockroaches or hearing a knife rubbing on a bottle.

After a demonstration, every student chosen for the test said they would pay to avoid mild electric shocks.

But during the thinking time, 12 of 18 men gave themselves up to four shocks. Six of 24 women also shocked themselves.

The scientists wrote in Science that it was striking that being alone with their thoughts felt so bad that many people gave themselves shocks they had said they would pay to avoid.

Jessica Andrews-Hanna at the University of Colorado said many students might shock themselves to feel better during a boring lecture. But she said we need to know more about why people in Wilson’s study pressed the button.

She asked readers to imagine the situation: a person sits in a chair with wires on their skin and a button that gives a harmless but uncomfortable shock. The person is told to sit and keep busy only with their thoughts. As time passes, the person’s mind may return to the shock and wonder: was it really that bad? She also wondered what the experimenters were really looking for, and suggested it might be curiosity.
