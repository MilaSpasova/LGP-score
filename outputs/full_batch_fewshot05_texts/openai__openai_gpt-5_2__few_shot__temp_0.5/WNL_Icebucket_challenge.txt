Some famous people have said no to the Ice Bucket Challenge, like David Cameron, Barack Obama, and Pamela Anderson. Others have done it, like George W Bush, Benedict Cumberbatch, and Stephen Hawking. The challenge raised $100m for a US motor-neurone-disease charity and £4.5m for a British one. It also raised money for charities in Hong Kong and Australia. But soon there was a backlash.

Some people said the videos were about vain celebrities showing their bodies. Others said people were having fun but not donating. Some complained it wasted water. These complaints came from writers, animal-rights groups, and environmentalists. The US Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS) Association also got criticism because top staff were paid six-figure salaries.

The challenge is still growing. Here is how it works: a person speaks on camera about the charity, then pours a bucket of icy water over their head (or a friend does it). Then they name three people. Those people must do the same or donate.

It started in the US in July. Some people say it began on a golf course, others say on a baseball field. It first appeared on mainstream American television on 15 July. The ALS Association tried to patent the phrase “Ice Bucket Challenge”, but stopped after criticism. Spokeswoman Carrie Munk said they understood the public’s concern and were withdrawing the trademark applications. The ALS has not yet answered criticism about its high costs and wage bills.

By coincidence, 31 August to 5 September was World Water Week. International delegates came to Stockholm to discuss the world’s water crisis. WaterAid asked people to use recycled water from bathtubs or garden butts, or to use sea water.

Douglas Graham, fundraising director of the MND Association, said the backlash was expected, but the money was a wonderful surprise. He said they did not expect it, but donations suddenly started. The money is a big help for a small charity that supports people with a serious disease. There is no cure, and it kills five people a day in the UK.

Pamela Anderson, a former Baywatch star and animal-rights activist, wrote an open letter to the ALS Association. She said she could not support its record on animal experiments. Some US stars refused because of California’s drought. Matt Damon used water from his toilets, and said much of the world has less clean drinking water. Verne Troyer used milk for environmental reasons. The challenge was also blamed for a water shortage on the Scottish island of Colonsay after its 135 inhabitants joined in.

In Australia, TV anchorman Lincoln Humphries said “no, thanks” and later apologized. He said people should not waste fresh water and ice. He listed charities that help communities in desperate need of money. He said people who have more than they need should donate to people who need it most.

Another criticism was that small charities could not handle so much money. The MND Association disagreed. Graham said they could cope. He said they pay for world-class research to find the causes and, in the end, a treatment or cure. They also give care and support to 3,500 people. He said the disease gets worse very fast and is expensive to manage. He said over 50% die within two years of diagnosis. He said it is heartbreaking to see people decline in only a few months.

For many people close to the disease, the awareness is as important as the money. Graham said it is priceless. Normally, the MND Association gets about 300,000 hits a year on its website. On one recent day, it got 330,000.

Graham said they could not have created this success on purpose. He said all charities are good causes, and some people may want to donate to a different one. He said that in 2013, British people gave £62bn to charity, and people should be proud. He said the windfall was wonderful for them. He said they would meet in the next few weeks to decide the best way to spend it, and every penny would be used for good.
