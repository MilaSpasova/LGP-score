Young people in Cuba are going in large numbers to use a new internet service. It is the first known free, open service on the communist island. A small cultural centre in Havana has become a rare place with free wi-fi. The service is provided by the well-known Cuban artist Kcho. His spokesman said the state phone and internet company, Etecsa, approved it.

The internet there is very slow, especially when many people come. But in Cuba, only about 5% of people are thought to have full internet access. So a place that is free and has no restrictions is being called something never seen before.

People are excited to read international news, talk with family overseas, and use Facebook and Twitter. Adonis Ortiz, 20, said, “I come as often as I can,” while video-chatting with his father in the US. He has not seen his father in nine years.

Relations between the US and Cuba are slowly improving. This may bring American tech companies like Google and Apple to Cuba when they are allowed. Meanwhile, Cuba has put a high-speed fibre-optic cable under the sea from Venezuela. Internet users also have some access to Chinese equipment.

Another estimate says one quarter of Cubans have internet access. But this often means they use a limited Cuban intranet. It has only some websites and limited email.

Kcho has let the public use his personal internet connection. Some people think he may have been chosen to make this change look acceptable, as the government slowly accepts the internet.

Kcho has close ties to the Cuban government. He said the Ministry of Culture approved what he is doing. He said he wants Cubans to learn about the internet. He told the Associated Press, “It is expensive but the benefit is tremendous. I have something that is great and powerful. I can share it and I am doing so.”

Kcho’s real name is Alexis Leiva Machado. He became famous worldwide for his paintings, sculptures, and drawings after he won the grand prize at an important art biennial in South Korea. He is preparing for the Havana biennial in May. He was born on one of Cuba’s faraway islands. He is known for modern art with simple, seaside, and patriotic themes.

In the centre’s courtyard, many young people sit all day in wicker chairs under a white cover, or outside when it is full. They use laptops and tablets, or stare at their smartphones.

Cuba has some of the lowest internet connections in the world. Dial-up accounts are tightly controlled. Home broadband is very rare, except for foreigners who pay hundreds of dollars each month. This is in a country where the average salary is $17 to $20 a month. Kcho is thought to pay $900 a month to provide the free wi-fi.

Since 2013, the authorities have opened hundreds of internet salons. One hour online costs $4.50. The speeds are much lower than at Kcho’s studio, which is around 2mbps. A 2014 report by Akamai Technologies found average speeds of about 10.5mbps in the US and 23.6mbps in South Korea. The world average was about 3.9mbps.

Because dozens of people use Kcho’s wi-fi at the same time, the signal becomes weaker. One user said he sometimes comes in the middle of the night when nobody is there, and then it is unbelievably fast.
