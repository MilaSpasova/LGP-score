Google has mapped many places, like the world’s highest mountains, the ocean floor, the Amazon rainforest, and even parts of North Korea. But it has mostly avoided the cold and harsh Arctic.

Now Google is starting a major new mapping project in the far north. It wants to help millions of web users understand life on permafrost. Google flew a small team to Iqaluit, the largest town in Nunavut, Canada. They brought very warm winter clothes, laptop computers, and an 18kg backpack camera with a telescopic pole.

With help from an Inuit mapping expert, and followed by curious local people, the team walked for four days to collect photos and information. This will let people in Iqaluit have Street View like people in big cities around the world.

Iqaluit has about 7,000 people. It will appear on Google Street View in July 2013.

Aaron Brindle, the project leader, said that in Toronto he takes it for granted that maps show everything in the right place, but for a long time that was not true in the north.

In many places, Google maps streets using a special camera on top of a car. But in Iqaluit, the team had to walk on snowy roads and on little-known trails. Some trails are made of ice and disappear during the short summer.

The team also made a path along part of a 15km dead-end road called the Road to Nowhere. They did this even though there were warnings about polar bears and other animals.

Mayor John Graham said the Google mappers were chased, in a friendly way, by excited local people, also called Google stalkers.

Graham said the project follows earlier explorers. He mentioned Martin Frobisher, an English explorer who sailed into the bay in 1576 while searching for the Northwest Passage. He also mentioned a 1941 flight by Captain Elliott Roosevelt, a reconnaissance officer and the son of the US President. That flight helped the area get chosen for a military airbase. This led to the founding of the modern town of Iqaluit, which is the seat of government and also a transport and communications hub for Nunavut.

One early problem for Google was getting enough basic data to improve its existing map. The satellite map was fairly accurate, but Iqaluit has grown quickly because of a mining boom. Arif Sayani, the town’s Director of Planning, said Google was missing one road built in the past year.

Another problem was addresses. Many businesses and homeowners get mail at the post office, not at their home address. If Google used PO box addresses, many places would look like they were all located at the Canada Post building in the centre of town.

About 30 Inuit elders, business owners, and high-school students came one night to help fix these problems. They used a laptop computer and learned how to make sure their homes, shops, and meeting places were placed correctly on the map.

The project is not only for fun or charity. Sayani, 32, said Iqaluit can use the maps to promote the town to people who want to visit or move there. It may also make planning decisions faster as the town grows.

Google tested this kind of mapping last summer in Cambridge Bay. Cambridge Bay is a smaller Nunavut town with about 1,500 people. It is 1,700km west of Iqaluit and one time zone west.

The online images from Cambridge Bay show gravel roads and muddy puddles. But they do not show what life is like when the land is usually covered in snow. That is one reason Google chose to travel to Iqaluit in March, which is less friendly weather.

Brindle said he hopes the work will continue in other northern towns. But shipping and airfares in the huge Arctic are expensive, and this may limit Google’s plans.

Google has not chosen the next northern place yet. Brindle said the company might send a high-tech backpack camera and depend on volunteers to do the mapping.

Brindle said, “I’m hoping that three, four, five years from now we’ll look back and see a very different map of Canada’s north.”
