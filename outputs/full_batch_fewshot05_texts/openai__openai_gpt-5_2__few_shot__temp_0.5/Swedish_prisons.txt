People around the world often say Swedish prisons are modern and fair. But some people ask if they are too easy.

In November 2013, Nils Oberg, the head of Sweden’s prison and probation service, said four prisons would close. This was because the number of prisoners fell in an unusual way.

Crime did not go down, but the number of prisoners did. From 2011 to 2012, Sweden had 6% fewer prisoners. Now there are a little over 4,500 prisoners. A similar drop was expected in 2013 and 2014. Oberg said he was surprised, but he hoped it was because prisons are helping people change and not commit crimes again.

Kenneth Gustafsson is the governor of Kumla Prison, Sweden’s most secure prison. It is 130 miles west of Stockholm. He says the prison service is very different from when he started in 1978. But he does not think it has become too soft. He says prisoners were treated well before, maybe too well. After some big escapes in 2004, Sweden put more focus on security.

Even with more security, Sweden still tries to be humane. Prison sentences rarely go over ten years. People with life sentences can ask a court after ten years to change the sentence to a fixed time, usually about 18 to 25 years. Sweden was the first country in Europe to use electronic tags for convicted criminals. Sweden also tries to avoid short prison sentences when possible by using community measures, because these can reduce reoffending better.

In Sweden, about 30% to 40% of people reoffend within three years. This is about half the rate of the UK. One reason may be that in Sweden, a person is responsible for crimes from age 15. In the UK, children aged 10 to 17 and young people under 21 have the highest reoffending rates. In Sweden, no one under 21 can get a life sentence. Sweden also tries hard to keep young offenders out of prison.

Another reason for fewer prisoners may be strong support after prison. Sweden’s probation service is a government agency. It supervises people on probation and also must provide treatment programmes for drug, alcohol, or violence problems. The service is helped by about 4,500 lay supervisors. These are volunteers who befriend and support offenders.

Gustafsson says that in 2013 and 2014, the justice department planned to focus on young offenders and men convicted of violent behaviour. Sweden has had programmes for drug addiction for many years. Now it is also making programmes to deal with aggression and violence.

A former prisoner, Peter Soderlund, now runs a social enterprise called X-Cons Sweden. He served almost three years of a four-year sentence for drug and weapons offences and was released in 1998. He got help from a new group called Kris (Criminals’ Return Into Society), run by former prisoners.

Soderlund says Kris and X-Cons have one main goal: to help people return to society after prison. He says one difference is that X-Cons lets people join even if they are still taking addiction medicines. He says he was treated well in Osteraker Prison, but he knows not all prisons were like that. He met many people who needed help. With X-Cons, they meet people at the prison gate, help them find housing, and give them support.

Gustafsson says Sweden believes in rehabilitation, but is not naive. Some people will not or cannot change. But he believes most prisoners want to change, and prisons should help them. He says change may take more than one prison sentence.

He also says prison alone is not enough. Rehabilitation often needs probation and the wider society. Prisons can give education and training, but after prison people still need housing and jobs.
