Seeing co-workers and other people use an e-cigarette is now common. Some people ask if vaping has reached its highest point. Some statistics suggest that vaping by smokers and recent ex-smokers may already be going down. Big e-cigarette companies will watch these numbers closely, because they have spent millions to promote e-cigarettes.

In 2014, the health charity Action on Smoking and Health (ASH) said that adult use of e-cigarettes in Britain tripled. It rose from about 700,000 users in 2012 to 2.1 million in 2014. E-cigarettes do not contain tobacco. They make vapour, not smoke.

But numbers from the Smoking Toolkit Study, supported by the Department of Health, suggest vaping may be losing interest. Vaping among smokers and ex-smokers rose until the end of 2013, when about 22% were vaping. It stayed about the same during 2014, then fell to 19% in the last quarter of 2014. Early signs suggest it fell again in 2015. Professor Robert West from UCL’s Department of Epidemiology and Public Health said this drop is statistically significant.

Smokers are the main group for e-cigarette companies, because seven out of ten vapers are smokers. Only about 1% of people who have never smoked have tried an e-cigarette. West said the number of people who use e-cigarettes while still smoking is going down. He also said there is no clear growth in long-term ex-smokers or never-smokers using e-cigarettes.

The UK pattern seems different from the US, where vaping has been promoted strongly and some reports said it was growing. But West said the US data may be unclear, because it may mix people who tried an e-cigarette once with people who vape regularly.

Experts say worries that vaping will become popular with young non-smokers may be wrong. The ASH study found that only 1.8% of children are regular users. E-cigarettes seem most popular with adults trying to quit smoking. James Dunworth, director and co-founder of ecigarettedirect.co.uk, said vaping by smokers has levelled off, but use has increased a lot since May 2011. He said customers are happy, and better hardware is improving the experience and helping people switch from traditional cigarettes.

West said e-cigarettes act like a stronger nicotine patch. He said they are more popular than nicotine patches, and may or may not work better. He said one-third of quit attempts use e-cigarettes, making them the most popular way to stop.

Hazel Cheeseman, director of policy at ASH, said it is too early to say vaping has peaked. She said the market may not have grown in the UK for about a year, but the number of people using e-cigarettes to help them quit does not seem to be falling. She said using an e-cigarette is safer than smoking, some people find them helpful for quitting, and there is little evidence that they increase smoking among young people.

The European Commission (EC) is looking at higher taxes on e-cigarettes, which could affect how popular they are. A new EC tobacco directive starts in 2016 and will limit nicotine levels in e-cigarettes to below current levels. This may mean vapers need to use them more to get the same hit, which could make e-cigarettes more expensive.

West said lawmakers should see e-cigarettes as a tool to help people stop smoking, and not regulate them the same way as cigarettes. He said some local authorities and organisations treat e-cigarettes like cigarettes and ban them in public places and outdoors. He said this can damage public health messages and can stigmatise e-cigarette users.
