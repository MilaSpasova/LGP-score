New research says noise from passing ships may disturb animals like killer whales and dolphins more than people thought. The loud underwater sound can make it harder for them to communicate and to find prey.

People have known for a long time that the low rumble from ships can disturb large whales. But US researchers found that ship noise also stays at medium and high frequencies. This includes 20,000Hz, where killer whales, also called orcas, hear best.

This noise may stop killer whales from communicating and using echolocation. Echolocation means they use sound to bounce off things like prey and find where it is. Dolphins and porpoises, which also use high frequencies, may have the same problem.

The findings suggest the noise could affect the endangered killer whales near shipping lanes. Only 84 killer whales forage up the US west coast and into Puget Sound.

Scott Veirs of Beamreach led the research. He said even a small increase in sound may make echolocation harder for whales. This is worrying because their prey, chinook salmon, is already scarce. He said hearing a small click from a salmon is very hard, and it is even harder when there is a lot of noise.

The researchers used underwater microphones. They measured the noise from about 1,600 individual ships as they passed through Haro Strait in Washington State. The study lasted two years. It recorded sounds from 12 types of vessel, including cruise ships, container ships, and military vehicles. Ships passed through the strait about 20 times a day.

Some ships are quieter than others. On average, the noise next to ships was 173 underwater decibels. This is like 111 decibels in air, about as loud as a rock concert. Whales are usually not right next to ships, so they would hear about 60 to 90 decibels, like a lawnmower or a vacuum cleaner.

Veirs said scientists have already identified effects of underwater noise on baleen whales, the group that includes the largest animals on Earth. But the new research shows danger for smaller whales, dolphins, and porpoises too. He said ships were seen as low-frequency noise, like trucks or trains. Most noise is low frequency, but the ocean’s background noise also rises at high frequencies. This could be a big problem and needs more study.

A noisy ocean can cause more problems. Whales may need to stay closer together to hear each other. If they cannot find prey as well, they may use up extra blubber. This is a problem because blubber can hold manmade pollutants. These pollutants can be toxic to whales if they are released into the body.

Veirs said more work is needed to see how much the noise is harming whales and how to make ships quieter near the cetaceans. He said it should be easy to reduce noise pollution. He said military ships are quieter, and this technology could be moved to the commercial fleet. Another way is to slow down. Reducing speed by six knots could cut noise intensity by half.

Some whale species, like humpbacks and blue whales, have improved as whaling has declined. But other whales are still in danger for many reasons. The US federal government recently protected nearly 40,000 square miles of the Atlantic to try to avoid losing the critically endangered North Atlantic right whale, with only 500 individuals left.

In Europe, killer whales have dangerously high levels of banned polychlorinated biphenyl (PCB) chemicals in their blubber. Scientists are still trying to find out if pollutants caused the deaths of five sperm whales that were stranded on the east coast of Britain in January 2016. Around Australia, whales face more danger from ship strikes and oil and gas drilling, and Japan has recently said it will resume whaling in Antarctic waters.
