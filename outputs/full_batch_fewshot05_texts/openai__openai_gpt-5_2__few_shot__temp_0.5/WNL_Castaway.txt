Two people on an island saw a small fibreglass boat on a faraway Pacific island. They went to look. Inside they found a very thin man with long hair and a beard. He said he had been drifting at sea for 16 months. He said he started from Mexico, more than 12,500km away.

The man wore only old underpants. He said he was in a 7.3-metre fibreglass boat. The engines did not have propellers. He said he left Mexico for El Salvador in September 2012. He also said a person who was with him died at sea a few months earlier.

Ola Fjeldstad, a Norwegian student working on Ebon Atoll in the Marshall Islands, said the man was not in good condition, but he was getting better. The man said his name was José Ivan. He said he stayed alive by catching turtles and birds with his hands. He spoke only Spanish, so the details were not clear. There was no fishing equipment on the boat, but there was a turtle inside when the boat came ashore. Fjeldstad said the boat was very scratched and looked like it had been in the water for a long time.

The islanders took the man to the main island of the atoll to meet the mayor, Ione de Brum. The atoll is very remote and has only one phone line and no internet. The mayor contacted the Ministry of Foreign Affairs in Majuro, the capital of the Marshall Islands. Ministry officials said they were waiting for more information and expected the man to go to the capital.

The government airline’s only plane that can land at Ebon is being repaired, and it will not fly for several days. Officials may send a boat to get the man. Fjeldstad said the man was staying at the local council house and a family was feeding him. He had a basic health check and had low blood pressure. But he did not seem to have any life-threatening problems. He could walk with help from men on the island. Fjeldstad said they gave him a lot of water and he was getting stronger.

Fraser Christian, who teaches sea survival in Dorset, said the story, if true, would be amazing but not the only one like it. He said it is possible to catch turtles or small fish by hand, because turtles can come close to a small boat to shelter under it. Christian tells people who must eat turtles to start with the eyes, which have “lots of fluid”, and then the blood.

Christian said the biggest dangers were too much sun and not enough water. He said, “no water, no food,” because you need water to digest protein. If there is no rain for a few days and you cannot collect rainwater, you can die. He also said some people can survive better than others. He said the mind is very important, and it depends on a person’s life and how used they are to hardship.

Survival stories in the Pacific Ocean are not rare. In 2006, three Mexicans were found drifting in a small fibreglass boat near the Marshall Islands. They said they survived for nine months on rainwater, raw fish, and seabirds. They also said they kept hope by reading the Bible. But Cliff Downing, who teaches sea survival to sailors, said he did not fully believe the new story. He said there are many dangers that would make such long survival very unlikely, and he would want to know more.
