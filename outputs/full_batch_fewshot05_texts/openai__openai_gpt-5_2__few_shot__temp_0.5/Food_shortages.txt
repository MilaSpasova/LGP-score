Top water scientists gave a very strong warning about the world’s food. They say that by 2050, people may need to eat almost only vegetarian food to avoid very serious food shortages.

Today, humans get about 20% of their protein from animal foods. The scientists say this may need to fall to only 5% by 2050. This is because about two billion more people may be alive then.

A report by Malik Falkenmark and other researchers at the Stockholm International Water Institute (SIWI) says there will not be enough water on today’s farmland to feed about nine billion people in 2050 if diets keep moving toward western-style eating.

The report says there will be just enough water if animal-based foods are limited to 5% of total calories, and if food trade can reliably help areas that do not have enough water.

This warning comes as Oxfam and the UN get ready for a possible second global food crisis in five years. Prices for basic foods like corn and wheat have risen almost 50% on world markets since June. The rise was caused by strong droughts in the US and Russia, and weak monsoon rains in Asia. More than 18 million people already face serious food shortages in the Sahel.

Oxfam says the higher prices will badly hurt developing countries that buy a lot of food from other countries, including parts of Latin America, North Africa, and the Middle East. Food shortages in 2008 led to civil unrest in 28 countries.

The scientists say eating vegetarian food is one way to save water and grow more food, in a world where the climate is more unstable. Food high in animal protein uses five to ten times more water than a vegetarian diet. One third of the world’s farm land is used to grow crops for animals. Other ways to feed people include cutting food waste and increasing trade from countries with extra food to countries with shortages.

They also say that 900 million people are hungry, and two billion people are malnourished, even though food production per person keeps increasing. Since 70% of all available water is used in agriculture, growing more food for two billion more people by 2050 will put more pressure on water and land.

The report is released at the start of the yearly world water conference in Stockholm, Sweden. There, 2,500 politicians, UN bodies, non-governmental groups, and researchers from 120 countries meet to talk about world water supply problems.

The scientists say competition for water will get stronger, between farming and other needs. The UN says food production must rise by 70% by mid-century. The report says this will put more pressure on water that is already strained. At the same time, more water is needed for world energy demand, which is expected to rise by 60% over the next 30 years, and to make electricity for the 1.3 billion people who do not have it now.

The report says overeating, poor nutrition, and waste are all increasing, and future food production may be limited by lack of water.

“We will need a new recipe to feed the world in the future,” said the editor, Anders Jägerskog.

Another report from the International Water Management Institute (IWMI) says the best way to protect millions of farmers from food insecurity in sub-Saharan Africa and south Asia is to help them buy small pumps and simple technology. It says this is better than building costly, large irrigation projects.

Dr Colin Chartres, the Director General, said the world’s poor often depend on farming for their living and already suffer from not enough water. He said they are harmed when they depend on a weak global food system.

He said farmers in developing countries are using and gaining from small, local water solutions more and more. The report says these methods could raise crop yields by up to 300% and add tens of billions of US dollars to household income across sub-Saharan Africa and south Asia.
