People call Enric Duran the “Robin Hood of the banks.” He took many loans, worth almost 500,000 euros, and he did not plan to pay them back. He gave the money to projects that supported alternatives to capitalism.

After 14 months in hiding, Duran said he was not sorry, even though he could go to jail. In a Skype interview from a secret place, he said he was proud. He said the money created chances for people. He said it helped start a movement and build a strong network of groups.

From 2006 to 2008, Duran took 68 loans from 39 banks in Spain. He gave the money to social activists. He paid for speaking tours against capitalism and for TV cameras for a media network. He said these movements had ideas but did not have enough money or good ways to communicate. He also said the system depended on endless growth and could create money “out of nothing.”

He said taking the loans was his way to protest and show this problem. He began carefully. He first applied for a few credits using his real information. The banks said no, but he wanted to learn what they asked for.

Later, the former table-tennis coach made a complicated system of accounts, payments, and transfers. He said he kept learning. By summer 2007, he learned how to make the system work. He applied for loans using the name of a fake TV production company. He said he then got many loans. The total was 492,000 euros.

Duran was arrested in Spain in 2009 after six of the 39 banks accused him. He spent two months in prison. Then he was released after paying 50,000 euros for bail. In February 2013, when he could face up to eight years in prison, he chose to run away instead of going to trial. He said he did not accept the authority of the court system.

He said his actions were part of a worldwide debate about the economic crisis. At that time, the anti-capitalist movement became more visible. Many people in Spain were looking for other options because the system had badly harmed their lives.

He said people in Spain today might understand his actions more. But he also said they would not be needed now. He said the anti-capitalist movement has grown from a small group to a movement supported by thousands of Spaniards. He pointed to popular movements such as the Indignados.

Duran said the movement has had success and can now support itself. He said they can now create resources. He said, a bit ironically, that this is what banks say credit is for: to help you move forward and become independent.

Duran is now also focusing on Spain’s justice system by promoting restorative justice. He said some people in Spain believe banks do not work and think he does not owe anything. He said he has already done his work. But he also said some people do not agree with him, and he thinks he should answer them.

He said he might offer something back to banks. He said he learned many things from getting bank loans through fraud for years. He said he could share ideas about good practices and bad practices, for the general public and for bank workers.
