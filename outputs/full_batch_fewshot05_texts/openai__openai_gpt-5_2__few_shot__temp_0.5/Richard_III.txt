At a press conference at Leicester University, people shared new details about Richard III. They spoke not only about who the man was in a car park with a twisted spine, but also about his horrible last moments and the humiliating treatment of his naked body after he died.

There were cheers when Richard Buckley, the lead archaeologist, said the team was sure “beyond reasonable doubt” that they had found Richard III, the last Plantagenet king. His spine was bent by scoliosis. His body was also twisted more so it could fit into a quickly dug grave in Grey Friars church. The hole was a little too small.

Scientists and researchers explained why they were sure. They used DNA tests. They used computer imaging to look at the bones in great detail. Genealogists found a distant relative with matching DNA. Academics also read old writings about the king’s death and burial.

Philippa Langley from the Richard III Society said, “What a morning. What a story.” She had pushed the project for years, even when many people did not believe it. She also helped find money from around the world when funds were running out.

People have started to design a new tomb in Leicester Cathedral, about 100 yards from the dig site. There will be a ceremony to place him in his new grave there, probably next year. Leicester’s Museums’ Service is also planning a new visitor centre in an old school building that looks over the site.

Richard died at the Battle of Bosworth on 22 August 1485. He was the last English king to die in battle. The researchers showed new details about how he died. A slide showed the base of his skull cut by one terrible blow. They think it came from a halberd, a medieval weapon. It had a very sharp iron axe blade, about two kilos, on a wooden pole. It was swung at him from very close range. The blade probably went several centimetres into his brain. Jo Appleby, an expert on human bones, said he would have become unconscious at once and died very soon after.

This injury fits old stories that he died in close fighting, after he was knocked off his horse. Shakespeare wrote the line: “A horse! A horse! My kingdom for a horse!” Another sword cut also went through the bone and into the brain and could have killed him too. But many other injuries happened after death. This suggests a grim ritual on the battlefield and on the way back to Leicester, when his body was stripped, mocked, and cut.

One very bad injury was a stab through his right buttock and into his pelvis. This was after death. It could not have happened while he wore armour that protected his lower body. It suggests the story is true that his naked body was carried back over a horse and abused on the way. Bob Savage from the Royal Armouries, a medieval weapons expert, said it was probably not a war weapon. It was more like a sharp knife or dagger that a workman might carry.

Michael Ibsen, a furniture maker born in Canada, was shown to be a descendant of Richard’s sister. He heard the confirmation on Sunday and listened quietly. He said, “My head is no clearer now than when I first heard the news. Many, many hundreds of people died on that field that day. He was a king, but just one of the dead. He lived in very violent times and these deaths would not have been pretty or quick.”

Mathew Morris first found the body, in the first hour of the first day of the dig. He did not think it was the king. A mechanical digger was still removing the tarmac from the council car park. Years of research by local historians and the Richard III Society had pointed to this car park as the likely place of the lost Grey Friars church. The priests there had claimed the king’s body and buried him in a quickly dug grave, probably still naked, but in a place of honour near the high altar. Morris saw the leg bones just under the soil, and they were covered again.

Ten days later, on 5 September, more digging showed Morris had found the right place. He returned with Lin Foxhall, head of the archaeology department, to dig out the body. Morris said they did it the usual way. They lifted the arms, legs, and skull first, and moved slowly to the torso. Only when they saw the twisted spine did he think: “My word, I think we’ve got him.”

Langley believes Richard was the true king, the last king of the north, and a brave leader. She thinks he was hurt by very strong propaganda made by the Tudors and by Shakespeare. There is also the dark story of the princes in the Tower. Even in Richard’s time, people talked about it. The child Edward V and his brother Richard were said to be illegitimate when Richard III took the throne. They were put in the Tower of London and were never seen alive again. It is not certain that bones found at the Tower centuries later are theirs, but there may be more DNA work to do there.
