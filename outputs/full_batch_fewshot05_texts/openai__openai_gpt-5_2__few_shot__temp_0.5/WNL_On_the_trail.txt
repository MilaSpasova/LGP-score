Robert Mys_ajek stops on a muddy mountain path. Between two paw prints, the scientist finds what he wants. “Droppings!” he says happily. Wolves are so hard to see that even their faeces is important for a tracker.

But wolves are easier to find now. There are about 1,500 wolves in Poland, and the number has doubled in 15 years. Wolves, along with the brown bear, the lynx, and the wolverine, are Europe’s last large predator carnivores. Conservationists from Britain, Germany, and the Netherlands come to Poland to learn how the country has protected wolves.

In the black faeces, Mys_ajek sees bits of bone and hair. “It ate a red deer,” says the University of Warsaw biologist. He says that in his lab he can learn many things about the wolf, such as its diet, gender, age, health, and family connections.

DNA tests show that Polish wolves travel far. One wolf even reached the Netherlands, where it was hit by a car. Wolves need a lot of space. Mys_ajek says an average territory for a Polish pack is 250 sq km.

Student Alex Entwisle, 23, asks if wolves could ever return to Scotland. He is on a field trip from his college in the UK to southern Poland. The animal science students spend the day looking at droppings and paw prints in the Beskidy mountains in the Polish Carpathians. They discuss bringing wolves back to the British Isles for the first time since the 18th century.

Mys_ajek visited the Scottish Highlands in 2015 with a British charity, the Wolves and Humans Foundation. He answered questions from villagers about Poland. He says a big difference is that people in Poland eat pork, and there are not many sheep. He says a similarity is that there are many wild animals: 300,000 red deer and more than 800,000 roe deer. Poland also has about 200,000 wild boar, and they damage farmers’ cereal crops. He says wolves help with this problem.

Mys_ajek says wolves can move up to 30 km during one hunt. He says the Beskidy pack is strong, with eight or nine wolves. This year they recorded five cubs, two yearlings, and two adults. They track wolves with motion-activated forest cameras and by following prints in mud and snow. In each family group, only one pair has babies, once a year. All pack members help care for the young.

Mys_ajek is confused by wolves’ bad reputation. He asks why people say “lone-wolf gunman” and why stories like Little Red Riding Hood exist. He is interested in wolves, which stayed wild 33,000 years ago while others became domestic dogs.

He says wolves are not pets. He believes only scientific reasons can protect Europe’s wild carnivores, especially wolves. He says natural predators balance the ecosystem. They control plant-eating animals, which helps trees grow tall so birds can nest.

He says shooting deer is only a partial solution. He explains that when predators are present, herbivores feel fear and do not stay all day eating soft grass by rivers. They move away to avoid predators. This helps plants grow.

Poland banned wolf hunting in the western Carpathians in 1995 and across the whole country in 1998. Now wolf packs live in almost all major forests. Wolves live near people instead of being kept behind fences like in African safari parks.

The Polish government pays compensation when wolves kill livestock. Mys_ajek advises farmers on building electric fences. He also helped bring back two deterrents that wolves seem to fear: red bunting hung around sheep pens, and the bark of the fluffy white Tatra Mountain Sheepdog.

Poland’s wolves have also been helped by slow infrastructure development. In 1989, when communists gave up power, Poland had only one motorway. Big road projects started after Poland joined the European Union in 2004, and these projects required wildlife impact studies. Poland now has one of the highest densities in the world of overhead crossings and underpasses for wild animals.

People’s attitudes changed too. Mys_ajek says hunting was cultural for many years. In 1975, there were fewer than 100 wolves in Poland. From the 1950s, the authorities encouraged wolf hunting and paid a reward for killing a wolf equal to a month’s salary. He calls it carnage.

Mys_ajek says wolves are doing much better now, but their situation is still fragile. Packs move across borders, and wolves are still hunted in Russia, Lithuania, Belarus, Ukraine, and Slovakia.

He says Poland’s government, elected in October 2015, is hostile to wolves. He says the Environment Minister, Jan Szyszko, is a hunter. Poland has 120,000 licensed hunters, and they have influence in parliament. He says hunters claim wolves are a pest and that there are 4,000 wolves in Poland, but he says this number is false and based on an unscientific count. He says the government could undo progress.

He says it is hard to defend wolves. It is not like telling politicians that wolves bring many tourists, because wolves avoid people and have a very sensitive sense of smell.

The 12 British students leave the Polish Carpathians without seeing a wolf. They only have photos of paw prints and droppings. Entwisle thinks Scotland cannot copy Poland’s success. He says wolves would help the environment because there are too many deer, but it would not work because of roads and sheep. He says there would be problems with farmers. He says Britain had its industrial revolution too long ago and damaged nature. He says people in Britain prefer predators to be far away and to watch them on television.
