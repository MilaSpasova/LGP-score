Passing clouds
Flying is fun because you can see clouds close up. Clouds look light, but they hold a lot of water. A small cumulus cloud can hold about 500 tonnes of water. Water is heavier than air, so you may ask why clouds do not fall. Clouds do fall, but very slowly. The tiny drops sink very slowly. An average cloud would take a year to fall one metre.

On cloud nine
Many people call clouds “fluffy” or “black and nasty”. But weather scientists (meteorologists) know more than 50 types of clouds. They use shape and height to name them. They put them into groups with numbers from one to nine. Cloud nine is a huge, tall cumulonimbus cloud. So “on cloud nine” means you feel very happy, like you are on top of the world.

Around the rainbow
A plane is a great place to see a rainbow. A rainbow happens when sunlight hits raindrops. From the ground we see a bow because the Earth blocks part of it. From a plane, the rainbow can be a full circle. When the plane flies over clouds, the plane’s shadow can look like it is in the middle of the circle.

Mr blue sky
Sunlight is white and has all colours in it. When light goes through air, some light is scattered by gas molecules. Blue light scatters more than other colours. So the sky looks blue.

There’s life out there
From an airplane window, we usually see clouds and other planes. But the air also has many bacteria. Over cities, scientists have found as many as 1,800 different types of bacteria. They can reach twice the cruising height of a plane.

Turbulence terror
Turbulence can make even experienced flyers feel sick. It can be small bumps or sudden big drops. But no modern airliner has ever crashed because of turbulence. People can be hurt, and sometimes killed, if they are not wearing a seat belt, or if loose luggage hits them. But turbulence will not knock the plane out of the sky.

In-flight radiation
When body scanners came to airports, people worried about radiation. But the radiation from a scan is the same as what passengers get in one minute of flight. The Earth is always hit by cosmic rays, which are natural radiation from space. At higher altitude, the effect is stronger.

You can’t cure jet lag
The world has time zones. On long flights, local time is different from your body time, and this causes jet lag. You cannot cure it, but you can reduce it. You can eat bland food for 24 hours before travel, drink lots of fluids, and live on the time at your destination as soon as you get to the plane.

Supersonic 747s
Many of us have traveled faster than sound. High in the atmosphere there are jet streams. On trips from the US to Europe, a temperature inversion can make a corridor of air move as fast as 250 mph. If an airliner flies at 550 mph and enters this jet stream, its speed can become 800 mph. This is faster than the speed of sound, which is 740 mph.

Flying through time
Time zones make it feel like we travel through time. But special relativity says flying also causes real time travel. The effect is very tiny. If you crossed the Atlantic every week for 40 years, you would only move 1/1,000th of a second into the future.

Terrible tea
If your tea tastes bad, it is not the cabin attendant’s fault. To make tea well, water should be just under 100°C when you pour it on the leaves. On a plane, this cannot happen. During flight, water cannot go above 90°C. So you may want to choose coffee.

I can’t hear my food
Airline food is known for having little taste. This may not only be because of the cooking. A plane is very noisy, and there is evidence that loud noise makes food taste less savoury.

Needle in a haystack
It may seem strange that Malaysian flight MH370 could vanish without a trace. But finding a missing plane is like finding a needle in a haystack. The plane knows where it is, using GPS and inertial tracking, but this information is not sent elsewhere in real time. This would be possible. Ships at sea have had tracking since the 1980s. The problem is not technology, but that there is no law that requires it.

Volcanic fallout
Volcanoes can cancel flights. Ash particles are like glass. They can melt in the hot engine, then harden on the rotors. Rules that stop flights in ash clouds can be annoying, but flying in the ash is clearly dangerous.

The wing myth
For many years, people taught the wrong reason for why wings lift planes. In fact, almost all lift comes from Newton’s Third Law of Motion. The wing shape pushes air down. When air is pushed down, the wing gets an equal push up. This lifts the plane.

Forget electric planes
Some very light, experimental planes use electricity. So people think big electric airliners will come soon. But that will not happen any time soon. Plane fuel holds a huge amount of energy. Batteries are much less efficient. To get the same energy as one tonne of fuel, you would need 100 tonnes of batteries. A 747 uses 150 to 200 tonnes of fuel. Unless batteries become much more efficient, electric airliners will not fly.

Beware the vortex
Pilots often wait before take-off. This lets the air settle after the last plane. A plane’s wing tips make spinning air called vortices. These can take two or three minutes to disappear. If the next plane takes off too soon, the fast-moving air can make it hard to control. The delay gives the air time to recover from these small whirlwinds.

The doors aren’t locked
Plane doors do not need to be locked. If you watch a door open, it moves in a strange way. It must first move inward, then it can be moved out of the way. After take-off, there is soon a big difference in air pressure between inside and outside. This pressure pushes the door firmly into place. To open it, you would have to pull against the air pressure. Human muscles are not strong enough.
