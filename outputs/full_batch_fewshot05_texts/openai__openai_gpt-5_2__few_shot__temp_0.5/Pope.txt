He became Pope in 2005. He said he was “a simple, humble worker in God’s vineyard”. On a grey, cold, windy Monday in February, Pope Benedict XVI ended his time as Pope in a similar way. He was like an old worker who could not ignore the pain in his back and could not trust the strength in his arms.

This Pope was very traditional, so he spoke in Latin. He was the first German pope in modern times. He gave the exact time of his resignation. He told the cardinals in the Vatican: from 28 February 2013, at 20.00, the seat of Rome, the seat of Saint Peter, would be empty. Then the people with responsibility would need to call a conclave to choose a new pope.

One person there was a Mexican church leader, Monsignor Oscar Sanchéz Barba, from Guadalajara. He was in Rome to get the date for a canonization where he had an important role. He said they were all in the Sala del Concistoro in the Apostolic Palace. After the Pope gave the date for the canonization, 12 May, the Pope took a sheet of paper and read from it.

Sanchéz Barba said everyone was left without words. The cardinals looked at each other. They had just heard the man they believe speaks for God on earth say he was leaving the job.

Angelo Sodano, the Dean of the College of Cardinals, spoke next. He seemed to have known before. He said they had listened with confusion and could hardly believe it. He then promised the cardinals’ loyalty and devotion. At the end, the Pope blessed the people there and left. Sanchéz Barba said it was very simple. Then everyone left in silence. There was deep silence and sadness.

John Thavis, who reported on the Holy See for 30 years, said he had felt the Pope might resign. He planned his return to Rome from the US because of this. Another Vatican watcher said this was true. Thavis said that in a long interview in 2010, published as Light of the World, Benedict had said it could be right to leave if he was no longer able to do the job. Thavis thought about when the Pope might choose to resign. Benedict had finished his series of books and many projects had started. Also, there were no events in his calendar that he personally had to attend. Thavis thought the most likely date was 22 February, the Feast of the Chair of St Peter. But he was wrong.

A message from the Vatican soon after the announcement said the Pope’s decision was brave. It said modern medicine can keep men alive long after they still have the strength to run a huge global organization. Thavis agreed. He said it was brave that the Pope chose to leave while he was not sick, and that he was leaving because he was tired, not because he was seriously ill. But some people asked if that was the full story. They wondered if the Pope knew more about his health than the Vatican had said.

Benedict explained his reasons. He said he thought about his body and his mind. He said that to lead the Church and proclaim the Gospel, a person needs strength of mind and body. He said his strength had become worse in recent months, and he had to accept that he could not do the work properly.

Other ideas and rumours were expected in the Vatican in the next days and weeks. The text compares this to what happened after Pope John Paul I died in 1978, 33 days after he was chosen. Some people already guessed that something about Benedict’s past was going to become public. The Vatican was expected to reject these ideas strongly. But the text says it is easy to see why people ask questions, because what Benedict did was extremely important.

Outside St Peter’s Basilica, Julia Rochester from London, who said she was a lapsed Catholic, was still thinking about what the resignation meant. She asked, “If you’re God’s chosen one, how do you choose not be chosen?” The text says many practising Catholics will ask their priests this question. In his first speech as Pope, Benedict said he did not feel fully fit for the task. He said the Lord can work even with weak tools. The text suggests that at some point in the last eight years, he stopped believing that.
