Donald Trump walked onto the stage and said something surprising. He told the crowd that his campaign only matters if he wins.

At a rally in South Carolina, Trump said, “Unless we win, it doesn’t mean a damn thing.” At that time, he had been first in the opinion polls for four months in a row. He also joked that he wanted the election to be next Tuesday. About 11,000 people were there. He needed this kind of support to keep growing into March 2016 to win the Republican nomination, and then to win the general election in November.

These moments of doubt did not last long. Trump quickly went back to speaking with confidence. He often said he was a “winner.”

Many people wondered if Trump could really keep going and win. This was especially true after several controversies, because his lead in the polls still stayed strong. Many people believed that these kinds of events would have ended most other politicians’ campaigns.

First, he upset prisoners of war by doubting the heroism of Vietnam veteran John McCain because McCain was captured. Then, in the first TV debate, he insulted Fox News moderator Megyn Kelly because she asked difficult questions.

Trump also insulted Mexican immigrants to the US. He said a Black Lives Matter protester who was violently pushed out of a rally deserved to be “roughed up.” He seemed to mock a New York Times journalist for his disability. He also falsely accused Muslim Americans of cheering the 9/11 attackers.

Trump said the political media made these incidents seem worse than they were. He said 70% of the media are “scum.” But he did not take back his words. He told the crowd in South Carolina that he could have said, “Oh, I misspoke,” but he does not like doing that.

Some rivals hoped that Trump’s supporters would get tired of what critics call his constant attacks, especially on minorities. One poll showed his support among Republicans dropped by 12 points. However, at 31%, he still led.

Other opponents said Trump did not have a clear political ideology. Bobby Jindal said, “He is an egomaniac; he’s a narcissist. He’s not a conservative, he’s not a liberal – he believes in himself.” Jindal said this shortly before dropping out of the race.

Liz Mair, a Republican strategist who was organizing an anti-Trump fundraising committee, said some comments that the liberal media call mistakes may actually be planned. She said people who dislike Trump and his policies should actively try to hurt his campaign, not just wait for him to destroy it himself.

Trump was not only about shocking comments. He often said he focused on three main issues: immigration reform, free-trade deals, and Barack Obama’s national security policy. These had become key issues in the election.

Trump’s plans included deporting every undocumented immigrant in the US and making Mexico pay for a border wall. He said it would be “a real wall” and “a very tall wall.” These ideas might sound unrealistic, but they may have harmed the campaign of Jeb Bush, who supports immigration reform.

So what could stop Trump? Opponents said one hopeful sign was that polls far from election day are often wrong by about eight percentage points, because many people have not decided yet. Among Republicans, the polls suggested Trump had about 25-30% of the vote.

It would be hard to change the minds of Trump’s strongest supporters. For another candidate to catch him, that person would need to attract Trump’s supporters without losing their own supporters. Polling experts said this was unlikely, because it is hard to do “Trump” better than Trump, or to copy him without losing support.

This was seen in answers to one question: “Are there any of these candidates you would definitely not support for the Republican nomination for president?” While 20-30% said they would support Trump, another 20-30% said they definitely would not.

Steve Deace, a well-known conservative in Iowa, said Trump’s behavior was “both a good and a bad thing.” It creates loyal supporters who like his style, but it also makes it hard for him to gain support from more people.

Republican pollster Frank Luntz said Trump speaks for voters who feel they finally have someone speaking for them. Luntz said Trump says what they are thinking, and the more outrageous he is, the more they agree. He said they also like that Trump says things other politicians would not say.

Many regular supporters at Trump’s crowded events felt this way. Sandra Murray from Dubuque, Iowa, said she likes the way he speaks. She said the country is a “huge mess” and Trump could be the man to fix it.

Another supporter gave a simpler reason: Trump is not afraid of anybody or anything, and that is “pretty cool.”
