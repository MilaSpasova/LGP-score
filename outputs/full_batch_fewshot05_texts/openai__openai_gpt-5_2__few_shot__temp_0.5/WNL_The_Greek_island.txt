Agios Efstratios is a very remote island. Banks, the government, and much of the modern world have forgotten it. The mobile phone network cannot use data there. There is no ATM and no machine for credit cards.

Before Greece had big financial problems, life on the island was OK. People did their banking at the post office. In summer, the few rooms to rent were full. Visitors came because they heard from other people about the empty beaches, clear sea, and fresh seafood.

But the island still uses cash. So when banks across Greece closed, it hurt the island badly. People have had to travel for nine hours to the nearest big island to get cash. Greek visitors say they cannot get enough money to come. Mayor Maria Kakali said tourist numbers are down 80% this year. She said even people born on the island who now live in Athens are not coming.

Kakali pushed the government and a big Greek bank to promise an ATM within weeks. But she worries it will be too late for this tourist season. Tourism is much bigger than the other two ways people earn money: fishing and agriculture. She said they have almost no bookings for August.

This winter may be a little better because 50 workers will stay in the village to expand the harbour. But a bigger problem is coming. The government says it will end a tax break that islands have had for many years.

The lower sales tax was made to help island communities. It helped because everything had to be brought in from outside, and tourism was cheaper. Tourist islands like Mykonos fear they will not compete well with Turkey without the tax break. For Agios Efstratios, the danger is even bigger. Kakali said that if they must pay a 23% tax, they will all die on the island.

Food and fuel already cost more than on the mainland. The island has only three shops and two restaurants, even in summer. There is not a single official hotel. Provatas Costas, a 58-year-old fisherman, said it is expensive because goods like milk and bread change hands three or four times before they arrive, and each middleman takes profit.

For Agios Efstratios and its nearest large neighbour, Lemnos, the crisis came at a very bad time. For years, they were less known because ferries were slow and not reliable. In 2015, the government finally gave the ferry contract to a new, efficient company. Many new visitors started to come, but then bank controls began.

Atzamis Konstantinos, a travel agent in Lemnos, said it started as the best season in 30 years and, in one week, became the worst. He said he used to earn €15,000 a month as a captain of petroleum tankers and other large ships, but he came back because he missed Lemnos. He said he always loved the island and always wanted to return.

Lemnos has many wild beaches where you can be almost alone, even in summer. It has a small nightlife and many cultural sites. It is the eighth largest island in Greece, so it is set to get the first round of tax increases in autumn 2015. But it is less wealthy than many smaller islands. Lemnos has just over 3,000 beds for visitors, compared with tens of thousands on Rhodes. Local councillors say they will fight the tax rise, but they do not know how. Lemnos Mayor Dimitris Marinakis said people have suffered economically in recent years, and will suffer more. He said when there is not enough money, people buy less, and the whole economy goes down.

The crisis has been especially hard for people under 40. Many have two or three jobs and still cannot afford a family. Young people fear many of their friends will leave, like the earlier exodus when their grandparents’ friends went to America, Australia, and Canada. Katerina Fikari said she feels lucky to work for the local government in Lemnos. She asked what the future is for their generation, and said that if you work only to pay bills, it is hard to have dreams.

Mayor Kakali warned that if taxes go up, even more young people will leave. She has used her year in office to improve education, to keep the island attractive for families. Agios Efstratios is one of the smallest islands, so it has until 2017 before the tax rise would start. Kakali hopes Greek politics may still save the island from the rise. If not, she plans to go to Athens to tell the government what the tax rise would cost.

Kakali said the government does not pay much attention to the islands of the north Aegean. She said she would take all the school children to the gates of parliament to say: “There is still life in these islands”.
