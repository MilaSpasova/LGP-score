He liked the things many millionaires like. He owned a gold and silver Rolex and many expensive cars. He also bought some modern art. But this Chinese businessman, who had several companies and a very large villa near Madrid, had almost no money in the bank. This made Spanish authorities interested.

His name was Gao Ping. He had lived most of his adult life in Spain. He was the main supplier for 4,000 Chinese bazaars in Spain. But the authorities suspected he was not paying taxes on clothes, furniture, and other goods he imported from China.

In 2012, police raided his warehouses. They found piles of cash. There were many €100, €200, and €500 notes, tied with elastic bands. About €12m was taken away in trolleys. This was the largest cash seizure ever by Spanish police. The gang, with Gao as the alleged leader, is accused of laundering up to €300m a year. They are also accused of selling counterfeit goods and toys with fake safety marks. A government prosecutor said Gao’s illegal business was so big it harmed Spain’s competitiveness. Gao is on bail, and the case has not yet gone to trial.

Police have long worried about €500 notes. They are small and easy to move, but worth a lot. People who dodge taxes, launder money, and drug barons often prefer them. €1m in €500 notes can fit in a small laptop bag. The same amount in €50 notes would need a small suitcase. Some cash mules have folded the notes into plastic pellets and swallowed them. A safer way is to hide the notes inside a car chassis.

The UK stopped distributing the €500 note in 2010. It said demand was “almost entirely for criminal purposes”. In 2009, Italy’s central bank warned the notes were widely used by mafia money launderers and terrorists. Other countries have limited their own high-value notes because of links to organized crime. Canada stopped its $1,000 note in 2000 after advice from law enforcement officers.

Today, many people use electronic payments and contactless cards. So more people ask if printing these notes still makes sense. Peter Sands, the former head of Standard Chartered Bank, has called for ending high-value notes, including the €500, the $100, the 1,000 Swiss Franc note, and the £50. In a report for the Harvard Kennedy School of Government, he said high-value notes help “bad guys” with tax evasion, financial crime, terrorist finance, and corruption. He said criminals could switch to smaller notes, or to gold or diamonds. But these are bigger and easier to track, so criminals are more likely to get caught.

At a conference in London about terrorist financing, Europol head Rob Wainwright asked the European Central Bank (ECB) to review whether it should keep making and using these notes. He said they make it easier for criminals and terrorists to hide their business and pay for illegal activities. Europol says the purple €500 note is 30% of the total value of all euro notes in circulation, even though most people have never seen one.

The €500 note started in 2002, when the euro began. It replaced the 1,000 Deutschmark, the 10,000 Belgian franc, and the 500,000 Italian lira. Some European countries liked high-value banknotes. Pia Hüttl, a fellow at the Bruegel think tank, says this preference has existed for a long time. She says people believe cash costs less and is accepted everywhere.

Cash is still very common in Germany and Austria. More than half of all transactions there use cash. Hans-Jürgen Papier, the former president of Germany’s constitutional court, said limits on cash go against personal freedom. The tabloid newspaper Bild started a petition to defend cash, including the €500 note, saying “hands off my cash”.

Police are not so sure. There are many reports about suspicious bundles of cash. In one case, two men went into a bank and tried to deposit €200,000 in torn and muddy €500 notes. In the same week, €1.3m in €500 notes was found hidden in a suitcase with a false bottom. But suspicion alone is not enough to keep people in custody. Jennifer MacLeod, a specialist in Europol’s financial intelligence group, said it is often impossible to get enough proof for judges that suspicious cash is linked to crime. She said it is even harder because of time limits and weak cooperation and information sharing.

Europol wants central banks to take more responsibility for strange patterns in €500 note use. For example, Luxembourg issued more than twice its annual GDP in banknotes in 2013, even though it is one of the least cash-using countries in Europe. Europol asked Luxembourg’s central bank to explain. MacLeod said Luxembourg replied that it only issues the notes people request, and it does not know why there is demand. She said she is surprised a central bank does not feel responsible here.

This may be changing. Mario Draghi, head of the ECB, said he does not want the money the bank earns from issuing notes to be “a comfort for criminals”. Other ECB leaders, like Yves Mersch, say there is no evidence about criminal use of the €500 note. But because fears about terrorism are higher, this argument may not persuade people now. EU finance ministers asked policymakers to look at “appropriate restrictions” on high-value notes and report back by 1 May 2016.
