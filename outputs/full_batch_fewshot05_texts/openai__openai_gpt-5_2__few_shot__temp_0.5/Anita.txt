Anitta is a new star in Brazilian funk music. She became very popular by bringing favela music to more people. But now people are arguing about her skin colour.

Some anti-discrimination groups and writers say the music industry made her look less black so she could sell to a mostly white middle-class audience.

The argument started after people shared old and new photos of Anitta. In an older photo, before she was famous, she looked darker. In a newer photo for marketing, after she became famous, she looks lighter. People do not know if this happened because of skin-lightening products, cosmetic surgery, or Photoshop. The big difference made people talk again about whether you need light skin to succeed in Brazil.

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote online about hidden discrimination in media and marketing. She said many people say they are not racist and think Brazil is a multiracial democracy, but statistics show big inequality. She said society often connects white skin with being rich and successful, and sees black skin as the opposite.

Anitta’s real name is Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

She later became very famous with an album and a hit song, “Show das Poderosas.” The song reached number one and got 52 million views on YouTube.

Many people love her as a pop star with catchy songs and a strong message. Her supporters say she connects the mostly black and mixed-race shanty towns on Rio’s hills with the richer and whiter areas below.

She also changed her style. She reduced sexual dancing, gang references, and very explicit lyrics from baile funk. Now some people ask if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract a richer audience. Arraes said this can push pop stars to change their hair or get nose surgery, and it can hurt self-esteem.

Race and colour are sensitive topics in Brazil. Brazil was one of the last big countries to end slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. In Brazil, colour often matters more than race and ancestry. Even though Brazil says it wants to be a multiracial democracy, skin colour is strongly linked to inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Until 2011, black or mixed-race students also spent about two fewer years in school on average.

The government says the gap is getting smaller because of university quotas and other affirmative action. But inequality is still very clear. Most business and government leaders are white, while most low-level jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In rich areas like Ipanema and Gávea, you are more likely to see black nannies pushing strollers with white children than the opposite.

Colour is also hard to define. In the 2010 census, people who called themselves white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million said black, two million said Asian, and 0.5% said indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology lecturer at the Federal University of Pernambuco, said Anitta became popular with the middle class by making a rebellious style of music calmer and easier for everyone to accept. He said this was not done by racial whitening. He said it was a change in the social place where she made her art, from the periphery to the centre.

Some other people also say the colour issue is too big. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who worked with Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot, but not her colour. He said it was more about makeup, hairstyling, and clothes. He said he did not think she was forced. He said she always liked to sing, dance, and show off, and that this natural way is why she is successful.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador, said the prejudice was not in the public, but in music producers and media executives. He said the public showed they care about music itself by choosing Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, as a recent winner of the show The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for a comment. But in a recent interview, Anitta said identity should be defined by yourself. She said a powerful woman does not need to be beautiful, but can be powerful because of her attitude. She said her work tries to show that we can be who we want.
