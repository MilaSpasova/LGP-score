In South Africa, two mothers found out they are raising each other’s daughters. The babies were accidentally switched at a hospital in 2010.

One mother wants to fix the mistake and take back her biological child. The other mother does not want to give up the girl she has raised. This has created a serious legal problem.

A lawyer, Henk Strydom, said the swap is a tragedy. He said it may not have a happy ending. The mothers cannot be named because of a court order.

Both women gave birth at Tambo Memorial Hospital in Boksburg, east of Johannesburg, on the same day in 2010. At the time, nobody noticed anything was wrong.

In 2013, one mother, who is 33 and unemployed, went to court to get child support from her ex-partner. The man said he was not the father. A DNA test showed he was not the father, and also that the child was not the mother’s biological child. She was very upset and did not know what to do.

Later, she met the other mother. Since December 2013, the two mothers have had counselling together, arranged by the hospital. They also met their biological daughters.

Strydom said his client saw similarities between herself and her biological child. He said it was very hard for her. She is raising a child who is not biologically hers, while her own child is with someone else.

The woman became unhappy with the process. She went to the children’s court to try to get custody of her biological child, but the other mother refused. Strydom is representing her for free. The woman also has one older child.

Strydom said it is a tragedy. He said he understands why the other mother does not want to give up the child, because it is already four years later.

The High Court in Pretoria asked the University of Pretoria’s Centre for Child Law to investigate what is best for the children. Under South African law, the best interests of the children are most important. The centre must report back within 90 days.

Strydom said he does not know what the court will decide. He said it is very hard to fix such a mistake after four years. He said the longer it takes, the more painful it can be, and someone will not be happy with the result.

He said he and his client do not want to sue the hospital or the government health department now. They are helping with the case and providing counselling.

The Centre for Child Law will interview the mothers and fathers, and other people who have a close relationship with the girls. The children and mothers will have full clinical assessments and may see a psychologist.

Karabo Ngidi, a lawyer at the centre, said what happens must be best for the children. She said biology matters, but it is not the only thing.

She added that the families are Zulu, so Zulu tradition, culture, and customary law will also matter. It is also possible that the ex-partner of the mother who started the legal action could be the biological father of the other girl.

This is not the first baby-swap case in South Africa. In 1995, two mothers got money after their sons, born in 1989, were accidentally switched at a Johannesburg hospital.

Other cases happened in other countries. In 2009, in Oregon in the United States, Dee Ann Angell and Kay Rene Reed found out they were mixed up at birth in 1953, when a nurse brought them back after bathing. In 2013, in Japan, a 60-year-old man who was swapped at birth was given compensation. He was raised in a poor family and became a truck driver. His biological siblings, and the boy raised in his place, went to private secondary schools and universities.

Bruce Laing, a clinical psychologist in Johannesburg, said a baby swap can have very strong long-term effects and can be very frightening and traumatic. He said parents might sometimes feel resentment toward a child who is not biologically theirs, and they may keep thinking, “What if?”
