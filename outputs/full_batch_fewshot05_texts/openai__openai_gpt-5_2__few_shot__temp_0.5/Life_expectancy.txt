A girl born today in the UK will live to almost 82 years old on average. Her brother will live to 78. In Andorra they would live longer (85 and 79). In the US they would live a little less (81 and 76). In the Central African Republic they would live much less (49 and 44).

In most places in the world, people are living longer. Some countries, such as Lesotho, are exceptions because of HIV and violence. The best news is about young children: small children are much less likely to die than they were 40 years ago. Deaths of children under five fell by almost 60%, from 16.4 million in 1970 to 6.8 million in 2010.

This helps explain a big project led by the Institute of Health Metrics and Evaluation (IHME) in Seattle. Over the past five years, nearly 500 researchers worked to measure the global burden of disease. If we know how many children die and what causes their deaths, the world can use its efforts and resources to keep them alive. The large database also gives many lessons. It can help global organizations and governments care for people better, for example by focusing again on diet, dealing with alcohol, and continuing work against HIV in Africa.

The Lancet published seven papers from this work. The project is large and has caused debate. IHME used some new methods. When there are no death records or medical files, they sometimes use verbal autopsies. This means they decide the cause of death by interviewing the family.

One result surprised many people. In 2012, IHME said 1.2 million people die from malaria each year. This is about twice as many as people thought before. The biggest increase was in adult deaths. Many people believed malaria mostly kills children under five.

Dr Christopher Murray, the IHME Director and a founder of the Global Burden of Disease project, said doctors are taught that in places with malaria, adults become partly protected. He said IHME first agreed with that common view, but then changed as they used more data. He said African doctors write in hospital records that many adults are dying of malaria. But he added that the fever could be from another cause. These findings have led to more studies.

Margaret Chan, the Director General of the World Health Organization (WHO), officially welcomed the IHME study. But some WHO staff are careful. Colin Mathers, a senior scientist, said they must be very careful when checking if the figures are valid, and they need evidence. Dr Ties Boerma, Director of the WHO Department of Health Statistics and Informatics, said people should understand that some numbers are very different, and the WHO cannot quickly accept every academic publication with new numbers. Mathers also said IHME’s work is stimulating because it pushes the limits. Murray said one main message was very fast change in the leading causes of death, faster than expected.

Because people have fewer children and live longer, the average age of the world’s population rose in one decade, from 26 to almost 30. The change was very strong in Latin America. Murray said Brazil and Paraguay had life expectancy below 30 in 1970 and almost 64 in 2010. That is a 35-year rise in the mean age of death over four decades. Murray said the speed of change in places like Brazil is so fast that many institutions are not ready for it.

A second theme is a change outside Africa. Many places are moving away from communicable diseases and the common causes of mother and baby deaths. They are moving toward “lifestyle” diseases, like heart disease, stroke, diabetes, and cancer. Some of these also have strong genetic triggers. Murray said this change is especially clear in Latin America, the Middle East, and south-east and even south Asia.

A third finding, Murray said, was a surprise. Many people now live longer but not healthier lives. Disability is widespread and has a big cost for people. Murray said the main causes of disability are not the same as the causes of death. These causes include anxiety and depression, arthritis and lower back pain (found in every country), anaemia, loss of sight and hearing, and skin disease. There is also substance abuse. Murray said the rates for these problems are not going down over time, and there is no progress in reducing them.
