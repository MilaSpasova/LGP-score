The British government is preparing for many lawsuits. People say they were put in prison and treated badly in the last years of the British Empire. A High Court in London said three old Kenyans from the Mau Mau rebellion can sue for money.

On Friday, the court did not accept the government’s argument that too much time had passed since the 1950s, so a fair trial was not possible. In 2011, the same judge, Mr Justice McCombe, also rejected the government’s argument that the three people should sue the Kenyan government, because Kenya became independent in 1963.

Human rights activists in Kenya say more than 5,000 of the more than 70,000 people held by British colonial officials are still alive. Many of them may sue the British government. The ruling may also help victims in other places sue for crimes during colonial times.

The Foreign Office said the decision could have big legal effects, and it plans to appeal. A spokesman said the normal time limit for a civil case is three to six years. He said this case has gone on for over 50 years, and many key people are dead and cannot tell their side.

The three people who won on Friday were Paulo Muoka Nzili, 85, Wambugu Wa Nyingi, 84, and Jane Muthoni Mara, 73. Their case took three years in court. Their lawyers said they suffered very serious violence, including castration, beatings, and severe sexual attacks. A fourth person left the case. A fifth person, Susan Ciong’ombe Ngondi, died two years ago at age 71.

In Nairobi, Nyingi and Mara heard the news by mobile phone. They were sitting quietly with supporters in a hot garden. When they heard, they were very happy. They hugged, danced, and raised their hands to the sky to pray.

Nyingi said he was held for about nine years. He said he was beaten until he lost consciousness, and he still has marks from leg chains, whipping, and caning. He said he wanted the truth to be known, and that people should get compensation so they can start to forgive the British government. Mara said she was very happy. She said she would tell her four children: “I won.”

In 2011, McCombe said there was a lot of evidence that torture may have been organized and repeated. On Friday, he said a fair trial was possible. He also pointed to thousands of documents that appeared in 2011, after the Foreign Office admitted it had a secret store of colonial files.

During the case, the British government tried to stop the claims. The claimants’ lawyer, Martyn Day, called those efforts morally wrong. The government’s lawyers accepted that the three Kenyans were tortured by colonial officials.

Day said the government admitted the three Kenyans were badly tortured, but used legal arguments for three years to avoid responsibility. He said victims from places such as Malaya, Yemen, Cyprus, and Palestine will read this decision carefully.

Some people watching closely are veterans of the Eoka insurgency in Cyprus in the 1950s. One has already met the Mau Mau lawyers. People from Cyprus could use British records and also files from the International Committee of the Red Cross in Geneva. Those files stay secret for 40 years and then become public. The Red Cross recorded hundreds of torture cases in Cyprus. Reporters at the time called British questioners HMTs, meaning Her Majesty’s Torturers.

There may also be claims from Malaysia. Many people were detained during a 12-year war that began in 1948 against communist fighters and their supporters. Relatives of 24 unarmed rubber plantation workers killed by British troops are now in British courts asking for a public inquiry. Former prisoners in Aden may also have claims. But because Aden is now part of Yemen, British lawyers may find it hard to contact people there.
