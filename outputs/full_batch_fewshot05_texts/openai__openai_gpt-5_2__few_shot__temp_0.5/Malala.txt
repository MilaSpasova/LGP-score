In October 2012, the Taliban sent a gunman to shoot Malala Yousafzai. She was riding home on a school bus. They wanted to stop her and end her campaign for girls’ education.

Nine months later, after many surgeries, Malala spoke at the United Nations on her 16th birthday. She answered them strongly. She said, “They thought that the bullet would silence us. But they failed.”

Her 16th birthday was unusual. She did not have cake and candles. Instead, she sat in a main UN chamber, in the central seat usually used by world leaders.

She listened as Ban Ki-moon, the UN secretary-general, called her “our hero, our champion.” Gordon Brown, the former British prime minister and now UN education envoy, told her happy 16th birthday and said these were words the Taliban did not want her to hear.

The event was called Malala Day. It ended an extraordinary four years for Malala, a girl from Mingora in Pakistan’s Swat valley. People first noticed her more after she wrote a blog for BBC Urdu about trying to get an education while the Taliban were becoming more powerful.

By age 11, she showed strong determination. She called Richard Holbrooke, the US special representative to Pakistan, and asked him to use his influence against the Taliban’s attacks on girls’ education. By age 14, Archbishop Desmond Tutu supported her for the International Children’s Peace Prize. By age 15, she became the youngest Nobel Peace Prize nominee in history.

But the world’s attention brought danger. She received death threats. On 9 October 2012, after a meeting of Pakistani Taliban leaders, a gunman was sent to remove what they called the “symbol of infidels and obscenity.”

After the shooting, Malala had many operations in Pakistan and the UK. Doctors fitted a titanium plate on her left forehead and a cochlear implant to restore her hearing. She now lives with her family in Birmingham. She does what the Taliban tried to stop: she goes to school every day.

At the UN, she said, “I am not against anyone.” She said she was not there for personal revenge against the Taliban or any other terrorist group.

She said she would answer violence with words, not bullets. “I do not even hate the Talib who shot me,” she said. “Even if there is a gun in my hand and he stands in front of me, I would not shoot him.”

She spoke with confidence. She had an injured eye and the left side of her face drooped a little. She also wore a white shawl that belonged to Benazir Bhutto, the former prime minister of Pakistan, who was targeted by extremists and did not survive.

Malala said extremists are afraid of books and pens. She said the power of education frightens them. She said they are afraid of women and the power of women’s voices.

She talked about an attack in June on a hospital in Quetta, the capital of Baluchistan, and about female teachers being killed in Khyber Pakhtunkhwa. She said this is why schools are being blasted every day: because extremists are afraid of change and afraid of equality.

She gave a different view of Islam from the Taliban’s. She said they think God would send girls to hell for going to school. She said terrorists misuse the name of Islam and Pashtun society for personal benefits. She said Islam is a religion of peace, humanity, and brotherhood. She said Islam says education is not only a right for each child, but also a duty and responsibility.

Her words brought a strong response. The “Stand with Malala” petition asks for education for the 57 million children in the world who do not go to school. It got more than four million signatures, with more than one million added shortly after her speech.

At the start of her speech, Malala said she did not know where to begin and did not know what people expected her to say. But she did not need to worry.
