People from all over Rwanda, and even from parts of nearby Burundi, travel to the southern town of Butare. They go to a small shop called Inzozi Nziza (Sweet Dreams). They come to try something many have never eaten before: ice cream.

This is Rwanda’s first ice-cream shop. Customers can buy ice cream in sweet cream, passion fruit, strawberry, and pineapple flavors. They can add fresh fruit, honey, chocolate chips, or granola on top. The shop also sells black tea and coffee.

The shop has signs that say “ice cream, coffee, dreams”. The manager, Louise Ingabire, says the shop uses people’s curiosity and is “changing lives”.

Ingabire says ice cream is important. Some Rwandans like it, but it is still new. She says they need to tell more people that they will enjoy it.

The shop has helped Ingabire. She says she did not have a job before and stayed at home. Now she has a plan for the future. She earns money and can give some to her family.

Butare has 89,600 people and is 135 km south of Kigali, the capital. It is home to the National University of Rwanda. Inzozi Nziza is now a popular place for tired students who want something cool and different.

Kalisa Migendo, a 24-year-old agriculture student, says the shop brings people together. He says that if you want to go out and talk to a friend, a girl, or a boy, you come to Inzozi Nziza for ice cream.

Most ingredients come from Rwanda. The milk comes from a depot in nearby Nyanza. The vanilla beans and cocoa are brought in from other countries.

The theatre director Odile Gakire Katese opened Inzozi Nziza. She met Alexis Miesen and Jennie Dundas, who started Blue Marble Ice Cream in Brooklyn, New York. Together they made a partnership and opened the shop in 2010.

Miesen says Katese believed an ice-cream shop could help people feel better by rebuilding spirits, hopes, and family traditions.

At first, Miesen and Dundas owned the shop together with the staff. The shop is a cooperative and non-profit. They did not set financial targets. After 18 months, they gave their shares to the women, who had shown they could run the business well.

Fatuma Ndangiza, deputy chief executive of the Rwanda Governance Board, says the shop’s success is not unusual. She says small businesses are mostly managed by women. But in big business, where companies compete for big tenders, there are very few women. She says women are new to big business. She also says there are more women entrepreneurs now, in and outside Kigali.

Ndangiza says ice cream is still new in Rwanda, but she likes the idea of the shop. She says it needs many skills and it needs people to change their thinking, because selling and eating ice cream is not part of their culture. She says it is interesting to innovate, bring this to the market, and make it.

The Butare shop employs nine women. In their free time, they practice with Ingoma Nshya. This is Rwanda’s first and only female drumming group. Katese started it ten years ago.

The drummers are Hutu and Tutsi women. Some survived the 1994 genocide, when almost a million Tutsis and moderate Hutus were killed.

Some drummers are widows, and some are orphans. Others were affected by the killings in different ways.

Ingabire says that in the past, Rwandan women were not allowed to drum. Many people thought drums were too heavy for women to carry. But she says drumming brings unity.

Ingabire says her father, two siblings, and many cousins were killed in the genocide. She says some of them are survivors, and some knew someone who was killed. She says when she drums with the group, it gives her power because they are still alive.

Inzozi Nziza is shown in a documentary film by Rob and Lisa Fruchtman, who are brother and sister film-makers. The film is called Sweet Dreams. It tells how the women have built a hopeful future after the genocide, and it also shows the female drummers.

The film has been shown in more than a dozen countries, including the US, the UK, and several African countries. It premieres in Rwanda in 2014. Lisa Fruchtman, who won an Academy Award for film editing in 1984, says the film is about resilience, hope, bravery, resourcefulness, and the power to change your own life.
