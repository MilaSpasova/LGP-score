A large scientific study says organic food has more antioxidants than regular food. Antioxidants are linked to better health. The study also says organic food has lower levels of toxic metals and pesticides. The researchers say that eating organic fruit and vegetables could be like adding one or two extra portions of the recommended “five a day”.

The team was led by Professor Carlo Leifert at Newcastle University. They say the differences are “statistically significant”. They found that many antioxidants were higher in organic food, by 19% to 69%. The study looked at fruits, vegetables, and cereals.

The researchers say the extra antioxidants could equal one to two of the five daily portions of fruit and vegetables. They say this would matter for nutrition if the link between these compounds and health benefits is confirmed.

Some experts disagree. Tom Sanders, a professor of nutrition at King’s College London, said the study shows some differences, but he is not sure they matter for health. He also said Leifert’s work has caused controversy before. Sanders added that organic cereals have less protein than conventional crops.

The results come from an analysis of 343 peer-reviewed studies from around the world. Helen Browning, chief executive of the Soil Association, said the study shows that farming methods can change the quality of food.

Plants can make antioxidants to protect themselves from pests. Organic crops may have more antioxidants because they are not protected by chemical sprays. The scientists also say other reasons may matter, like organic plants being bred to be tough and not being given too much artificial fertilizer.

Leifert and his team say many antioxidants have been linked to a lower risk of long-term diseases, including cardiovascular diseases, neurodegenerative diseases, and some cancers. But they also say there are no long-term studies yet that show health benefits from eating a wide organic diet.

The researchers found higher levels of cadmium, a toxic metal, in conventional crops. They also found pesticide residues on conventional crops four times more often than on organic food. The EU and an organic farming charity funded the research.

The research may be criticized. Using many studies could let some poor-quality studies affect the results. The team did extra checks and said removing weaker studies did not change the result much. Also, the cadmium and pesticide levels in conventional food were still below legal limits. But the researchers say cadmium can build up in the body over time. They also say pesticide limits are set for each chemical separately, not for many chemicals together.

Another criticism is that differences could come from climate, soil, or crop types, not from organic farming. The researchers say that using many studies should reduce these effects. The biggest argument is about health benefits. A major review in 2012 of 223 studies found little evidence that organic food is much more nutritious. It said the research does not strongly show that organic foods are significantly more nutritious than conventional foods.

Smaller studies in 2009, including one by the UK Food Standards Agency, also found little difference. The 2012 study did say organic food might help people avoid pesticide residues. Sanders said the new study does not change his view. He said what matters most is eating fruit and vegetables, not whether they are organic or conventional. He also said people buy organic as part of a lifestyle, and they like that it is grown without chemicals and not by big business.

Opinion polls show why people buy organic food. 55% say healthy eating is a reason. 53% say they want to avoid chemical residues. Other reasons are the environment (44%), taste (35%), and animal welfare (31%). Browning said the research supports what people believe about organic food. She said other countries have supported organic food for a long time, and she hopes this study will increase support in the UK and bring it closer to the rest of Europe.
