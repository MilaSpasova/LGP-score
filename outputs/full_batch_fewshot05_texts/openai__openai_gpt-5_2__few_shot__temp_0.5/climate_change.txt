A new, very detailed report says low-income countries will be hit hardest by human-caused climate change in the next 100 years. They may have slowly rising sea levels, stronger cyclones, hotter days and nights, more changing rain, and bigger and longer heatwaves.

A big United Nations (UN) report in 2007 said temperatures could rise 6°C or more by the end of the century. Scientists now think this is not likely. But they still expect land and sea temperatures to keep rising this century. Temperatures might reach 4°C above today’s level. This could destroy crops and make many cities too hot to live in.

The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) report says that as the world gets warmer, tropical and subtropical areas will see big changes in yearly rainfall.

East Africa may get more short rains. West Africa may get heavier monsoons. Burma, Bangladesh, and India may get stronger cyclones. Other parts of southern Asia may get heavier summer rains. Indonesia may get less rain from July to October. But coastal areas near the South China Sea and the Gulf of Thailand may get very extreme rain when cyclones reach land.

The report says it is almost certain that, over time, global rainfall will change. High-latitude areas, such as parts of Europe and North America, are expected to get more rain. But many dry and semi-dry subtropical areas will likely get less rain. In wet tropical areas, very heavy rain events will likely become stronger and happen more often.

The authors also say monsoons may start earlier, or not change much, but may end later. This would make the monsoon season longer.

Scientists and writers in developing countries welcomed the report, saying it matches what they already see. Saleemul Huq, director of the International Centre for Climate Change and Development in Dhaka, said climate change is real and stronger than before. He said people already see the effects in Bangladesh and across south Asia, and many developing countries face climate change now.

Scientists have also reduced their estimates of sea-level rise. Depending on future greenhouse gas emissions, average sea level will rise 40–62 cm by 2100. But the rise will be different in different places. Many millions of people in big developing-world cities, including Lagos and Calcutta, are at risk.

The report suggests that weather disasters are more likely in a warmer world. The total number of tropical cyclones worldwide may go down or stay about the same, but cyclones may become stronger, with stronger winds and heavier rain.

Life in many cities in developing countries could become almost impossible because city temperatures are already higher than in nearby countryside. The report says much higher heat could shorten the growing season in some parts of Africa by up to 20%.

Dr Camilla Toulmin, director of the International Institute for Environment and Development, says climate models are not strong enough yet to predict effects in small local areas. But she says it is clear that everyone is vulnerable in some way.

The charity Oxfam said world hunger will get worse because climate change will hurt crops and disrupt incomes. Oxfam suggested the number of people at risk of hunger could rise by 10% to 20% by 2050, and daily calories per person could fall around the world.

Oxfam said climate change is already harming progress in the fight against hunger, and it will likely get worse. They said, “A hot world is a hungry world.” They also warned that if the rest of the 21st century is like its first decade, people will soon face climate extremes far beyond normal human experience.
