On a normal day, Starbucks stores in the UK are busy places. People use them for business meetings, reading groups, and to get coffee in the morning. But Starbucks may need to be careful.

The protest group UK Uncut plans to take action in dozens of Starbucks branches in the UK. They want to turn the stores into crèches, refuges, and homeless shelters. They want to show what they say are Starbucks’s tax avoidance tactics.

UK Uncut announced this on the same day that a Starbucks executive was questioned by the House of Commons Public Accounts Committee. The committee asked why Starbucks paid no corporation tax in the UK during the past three years. This happened even though senior US managers said the company was making money in Britain.

Starbucks’ Chief Financial Officer, Troy Alstead, will try to improve the company’s reputation. The company is still facing criticism.

Some MPs said HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC) officials were too close to big businesses. Conservative MP Richard Bacon said about Starbucks: “It smells. And it doesn’t smell of coffee – it smells bad.”

UK Uncut says there is a link between government cuts and tax avoidance by big international companies. They say some cuts affect women in particular.

Sarah Greene, a UK Uncut activist, said refuges and rape crisis centres could face cuts unless companies paid their fair share of tax. HMRC estimated that about £32bn was lost to tax avoidance in 2011.

Greene said the government could raise billions of pounds for important services by clamping down on tax avoidance.

UK Uncut became well known after sit-ins at Vodafone stores, Topshop, and Fortnum & Mason. They turned to Starbucks last month after a Reuters investigation. Reuters said Starbucks had paid only £8.6m in corporation tax since starting in the UK in 1998, even though total sales were £3bn.

UK Uncut campaigner Anna Walker said they want to show how cuts affect women. She said they will focus on changing Starbucks stores into services that are being cut by the government, like refuges and crèches. She said Starbucks is a good target because it is on many high streets, so people can take action locally.

Other international companies have also been criticised in the UK. Amazon, eBay, Facebook, Google, and Ikea were said to pay little or no corporation tax even with big business in Britain. But YouGov’s BrandIndex said Starbucks had the biggest damage to its image.

Starbucks says it pays the right amount of tax. Chief Executive Howard Schultz said Starbucks has always paid taxes in the UK. He said that in the last three years the company paid more than £160m in different taxes, including National Insurance, VAT, and business rates.

Margaret Hodge, who leads the Public Accounts Committee, said last month that Apple, eBay, Facebook, Google, and Starbucks had avoided nearly £900m of tax. Prime Minister David Cameron said he was not happy with the situation. He said HMRC should look at it carefully, and that these businesses should invest in the UK but also pay fair taxes.

A Starbucks spokeswoman said tax law can be very complex, but Starbucks respects and follows tax laws and accounting rules in all 61 countries where it works, including the UK. She said Starbucks shared facts about its UK tax practices on its website.

She also said Starbucks helps the UK economy in other ways. She said the company spends hundreds of millions of pounds with local suppliers on milk, cakes, and sandwiches, and on store design and renovations. She said that when indirect jobs are included, Starbucks’ wider economic impact on the UK is more than £80m each year.

She said Starbucks hopes UK Uncut will respect the wellbeing of employees and customers. She said Starbucks creates jobs and apprenticeships and pays its fair share of taxes in the UK.
