In the past, not sleeping much was seen as a badge of honour. It showed you were busy and important. People said that sleeping was for weak people. But now things are changing. Arianna Huffington’s book, The Sleep Revolution, is a New York Times best-seller. She tells people to sleep more and to “sleep their way to the top”.

At the same time, the sleep industry is growing fast. Many start-ups are changing where, when, and how we sleep, and how much we pay for it. Rich customers can go to luxury hotels for “sleep retreats”. For more than $1,000, you get dinner and a movie about sleep. If you stay at home, you can buy new products. These include a mattress cover with a sensor that tracks your sleep ($249) and a brainwave-monitoring sleeping mask that helps you nap more efficiently ($299).

Sleep is now part of company life too. Some companies already have nap pods. Huffington says nap rooms in offices will become “as common as conference rooms” in the next two years. So why did this happen? Why did sleep suddenly become so fashionable?

Getting enough sleep fits a lifestyle where paying $10 for green juice and $34 for a SoulCycle class is normal. Also, more people now measure their lives with wearable technology. We watch our bodies and try to make them more efficient. Sleep becomes another set of data to track. Huffington says sleep does not just rest you; it restores you. She says sleep is the best performance enhancer. Getting eight hours of rest has become a status symbol.

How does Arianna Huffington get her eight hours? One reason is that she has “nine or so” assistants, according to a New York Times profile. She calls them her “A-Team”. They run her errands, plan her travel, and load The Huffington Post on her computer in the morning. The Times says most of the A-Team can only do the job for about 12 months because it is so taxing. The low pay also means many of them take second jobs. In simple terms, they do not sleep much so that Huffington can sleep, and can sell books about it.

Getting enough sleep is not only about choosing to go to bed on time. It also depends on where you live and what life is like for you. Many studies show that poor people are more likely to sleep badly. It is hard to sleep if you worry about safety or do not have enough to eat. It is hard to sleep if you are one of the 15 million American shift workers with irregular hours. Research also shows a black/white sleep gap. One study says white people sleep an average of 6.85 hours, while African Americans sleep an average of 6.05 hours. African Americans also have lower quality sleep. Researchers say this is partly because of the stress of discrimination.

Who gets the most sleep and the best quality of sleep in America? Wealthy white women. That is probably the same group Huffington is aiming her book at. Huffington calls her push for sleep a “revolution”, but it is really a rebranding. The real sleep crisis is not mainly because a few rich people think sleep is a waste of time. It is because the 99% often cannot afford the time and conditions needed for good sleep.

Sleep is popular right now, but this may not last long. Sleep can improve performance, but it is not very efficient. The real goal is to stop the effects of sleep deprivation so people can work well with less sleep. The military is leading this research. In 2008, the Pentagon published a report called “Human Performance”. It looked at a future where soldiers could perform at their best with only a couple of hours’ sleep. The report says: “Suppose a human could be engineered who slept for the same amount of time as a giraffe (1.9 hours per night). This would lead to an approximately twofold decrease in the casualty rate. An adversary would need an approximately 40% increase in the troop level to compensate for this advantage.” The report also looks at the effects of ampakines, a class of drugs that modulate neurotransmitters in the brain, to remove the effects of sleep deprivation.

In the end, humans may find a way to get rid of sleep. Spending a third of your life unconscious would not be a luxury anymore. It would be something only poor people are forced to do. Then we may need a very different kind of sleep revolution.
