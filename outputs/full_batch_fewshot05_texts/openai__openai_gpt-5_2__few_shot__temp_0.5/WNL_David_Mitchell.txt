David Mitchell is a British writer. His books are often judged by critics. He says it is a relief that his newest work will not be seen by anyone until 2114. He finished it at 1am one Tuesday morning, just before a car took him to the airport for a flight to Norway.

Mitchell is the second writer in the Future Library project by Scottish artist Katie Paterson. For this project, 1,000 trees were planted in 2014 in the Nordmarka forest near Oslo. Margaret Atwood was the first writer. In 2015, she gave a manuscript called Scribbler Moon. Each year for the next 100 years, one writer will give a text. Nobody will read these texts until 2114. In 2114, the trees will be cut down to make paper, and the 100 texts will be printed.

Each year, the writer’s name is announced. The writers are chosen by Paterson and a group of experts while Paterson is alive. The writer walks to a place in the forest above Oslo. There is a short ceremony, and the writer gives their manuscript.

Mitchell said the project gives hope. He said it suggests civilization might still exist in 100 years. He said many things make people feel doomed, but the Future Library shows another possible future. He hopes there will still be trees, books, readers, and civilization.

Mitchell said writing this book felt freeing because he will not be alive to face what people think of it. But he also worried. He said he is between Margaret Atwood and, later, other brilliant writers, so his work should be good. He said he would look foolish if people opened it in 2114 and it was not good.

Mitchell was shortlisted for the Man Booker prize for his novels number9dream and Cloud Atlas. He said he usually “polishes and polishes” his writing, and even over-polishes. This time was different. He wrote until the last moment. He said the first two-thirds were polished, but he did not have time to polish the last third. He said that was liberating.

Paterson asked the writers to write about imagination and time, in any direction they want. At a ceremony in the woods in Norway, near the 1,000 trees, Mitchell revealed only the title of his manuscript: From Me Flows What You Call Time. The title comes from a piece of music by Japanese composer Toru Takemitsu. Mitchell said only that the work was more substantial than he expected.

In the forest, it rained sometimes, and Mitchell used an umbrella. Around him were the young pine trees. He read the audience, children and adults, a short story and the poem A slumber did my spirit seal by William Wordsworth. The ending, “Rolled round in earth’s diurnal course / With rocks, and stones, and trees”, seemed to fit the place. The forest had blueberry bushes. It will be cared for during the next 98 years, before it is used for the Future Library manuscripts.

Mitchell also wrote that it seems unlikely that his writing will matter to people in the future. But he said it is also humble to work hard on a manuscript when nobody can praise you and say they liked parts of it.

Mitchell’s manuscript is now sealed. It will be kept with Atwood’s in a room lined with wood in Oslo’s new public library, which will open in 2019. A group of experts will watch over it until it is printed. Mitchell said it now feels as far away from him as a coin dropped in a river.
