Canadian tennis player Frank Dancevic criticized the Australian Open organizers. He said they made players play in “inhumane” heat.

Dancevic collapsed during the second set of his first-round match against France’s Benoît Paire. The match was on uncovered court six at Melbourne Park. The temperature went up to 41C. He passed out for about a minute.

He said the conditions were dangerous. He said the heat made him hallucinate. He said: “I was dizzy from the middle of the first set and then I saw Snoopy.”

He said it was not fair to the players, fans, or the sport when people are pulling out of matches or passing out. He said he had played many five-set matches, and passing out after about a set and a half because of heat stroke was not normal.

He said many players complained it was too hot. He said the tournament keeps going and keeps putting matches in this heat.

Other players agreed. British number one Andy Murray said they should look at it more closely. He said one bad thing could happen. He said it looks terrible when players, ball kids, and people in the stands collapse. He said the conditions around 2.30–3pm were very tough. He said he did not know if it was safe, and he mentioned issues in other sports with players having heart attacks.

Caroline Wozniacki said she put a water bottle on the court and it started melting a little under the plastic.

John Isner, who retired from his first-round match because of a right ankle injury, said it felt like an oven.

Defending champion Victoria Azarenka said it felt very hot, like dancing in a frying pan.

The tournament used its “extreme heat” plan for women’s matches on Tuesday. It allowed an extra ten-minute break between the second and third sets. But under a rule change for 2014, the decision to stop matches is now up to the tournament director, Wayne McKewen.

Organizers do not only use the Celsius temperature. They prefer the Wet Bulb Globe Temperature, which also includes humidity and wind. They said the temperature peaked at 42.2C in the early evening on Tuesday. They said the conditions never reached the level where play would be stopped.

McKewen said they must reach a minimum level and expect it to stay that way for a reasonable time. He said that did not happen. He said it was hot and uncomfortable, but the humidity was relatively low, so play continued.

Dancevic got medical help and then played again. He lost 7–6, 6–3, 6–4. He said he was close to stopping. He said he was not tired, but his body temperature felt too high.

Earlier, a ball boy collapsed during Milos Raonic’s 7–6, 6–1, 4–6, 6–2 win over Daniel Gimeno-Traver on uncovered court eight. The tournament then made shorter work times for ball boys: 45-minute shifts.

China’s Peng Shuai said the heat made her cramp and vomit. She needed help leaving the court after losing 7–5, 4–6, 6–3 to Japan’s Kurumi Nara.

Officials said the health risks were not as big as people thought. They said most matches finished without medical calls.

Tim Wood, the tournament’s chief medical officer, said a few players had heat sickness or discomfort, but none needed serious medical care after finishing their match.

Many players followed Roger Federer’s view. He said the conditions were hard but the same for both players. He said it is a mental thing, and if you cannot deal with it, you stop.

Dancevic disagreed. He said it is not only about fitness. He said some players’ bodies can handle heat better than others. He said it is dangerous. He said that an hour and a half after his match, he still could not pee.
