To tourists, Amsterdam still seems like a liberal city. The Labour Mayor recently said the city’s coffee shops that sell marijuana would stay open, even with a new national law against drug tourism. But Amsterdam may lose its tolerant image because of a plan to send very troublesome neighbours to “scum villages” made from shipping containers.

The Mayor, Eberhard van der Laan, says this new policy costs £810,000. He says it will protect people who suffer abuse and homophobia from harassment. The camps would house antisocial tenants for three to six months. People call them “scum villages” because the idea is similar to a plan from Geert Wilders, a far-right populist. Last year he said “repeat offenders” should be “sent to a village for scum”.

The Mayor’s spokesman, Bartho Boer, says the plan is not against freedom. He says they want to defend the liberal values of Amsterdam. He says the plan is to protect people, including gay and lesbian people, and people who stand up to violence and then get harassed. Boer says the plan is not for normal neighbour problems, like loud music on Saturday night. It is for people who are very violent and frightening, where a victim is harassed again and again.

People judged guilty of causing “extreme havoc” will be evicted. They will be placed in temporary, very basic homes, including shipping containers changed into small homes in industrial areas. Boer calls it a “living container”. These units have showers and kitchens, and they have been used for student housing. Boer says this is so the tenants are not “rewarded” with better housing.

Dutch newspaper the Parool says that in the 19th century, troublemakers were sent to villages in Drenthe and Overijssel, and these quickly became slums. But Boer says the city has learned from the past and will not put the antisocial tenants together.

Boer says “scum houses” is a better name than scum villages, because they do not want to put more than one of these families in the same area. After up to six months in these houses, in different places around the city, the tenants will get permanent homes. The city expects to move about ten families a year into this programme. It starts in 2013.

The temporary homes will have strong police control. The tenants will also be able to see doctors, social workers, and parole officers. Boer says they will be supported so the same problems do not happen again in the next home.
