People call it the “Richie Rich Club”, and it may soon get even richer. A report says India’s richest people will make their total wealth four times bigger between now and 2018. It says hundreds of thousands of new business owners and people who inherit money will become multimillionaires. The survey is based on interviews with 150 very rich people. It comes at a time when business confidence in India seems to be coming back.

In recent years, growth has been slow, prices of basic food have gone up, and the currency has become weaker. But in May 2014, the Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) won a big election victory. They promised to make the weak economy stronger. Even with the slowdown, the Kotak Mahindra Bank report says there are almost one sixth more Indians worth more than $3.75m than in 2013. Murali Balaraman, a co-author, says cities are growing fast, the middle class is growing, there are many more opportunities, and the political situation has improved in recent months.

Together, India’s rich people own assets worth $1tn. That is about one fifth of all the wealth in the country. The report says total wealth in India may reach $4tn by 2018. It also says this will create three times as many multimillionaires.

A growing luxury market serves both the new rich and the old rich. Balaraman says rich people want to show their wealth in a quiet way, and buying luxury goods is a good way to do that. Abhay Gupta, the CEO of Luxury Connect, says the market for very expensive goods and experiences will “only get bigger”. He says there is a large group of people who want to be like the very rich, and they copy what the rich do.

The report says cars are one of the most popular luxury items. In 2009, rich people showed off SUVs made in India, but now only foreign cars get attention. Mercedes sales in India rose 47% in 2013. BMW has launched a new $200,000 model in Delhi.

But India’s very bad roads and traffic limit demand. In 2013, Lamborghini CEO Stephan Winkelmann said the traffic and roads in India “are not so suitable” for $450,000 sports cars. Lamborghini sells two models in India: the Gallardo and the Aventador. The Aventador has a top speed of 217mph. Winkelmann said Lamborghini buyers in India are younger than buyers in Europe, and a typical buyer is in his 30s. Still, the most popular investments are real estate (mostly in India) and jewellery.

India’s super-rich often surprise people around the world with very expensive spending. Mukesh Ambani, India’s richest man, built the world’s most valuable home in Mumbai. The building is a 27-storey tower with helicopter pads, indoor cinemas, and more than 600 staff. It is worth about $1bn.

A three-day wedding for the niece of Lakshmi Mittal, a UK-based steel tycoon worth about $16bn, was reported to cost $80m. Hundreds of guests were flown to Barcelona. The ceremony and party were held in a museum there.

The Kotak Mahindra report says buyers of luxury goods who want to feel special are becoming more demanding. One person ordered nine cases of Japanese whisky that cost over $750 a bottle for a wedding reception. The report says the whisky was attractive because guests would not know how to buy the same drink in India. Another big spender bought matching pairs of Louis Vuitton bags, then cut up half of them to make clothes that matched her accessories.

Traditional weddings are also changing quickly. Gifts like silver plates, dried fruit, or sweets used to be sent with wedding invitations. Now they are being replaced with gifts from top western designer brands. Gupta says, “These days, it’s Rolex watches and Louis Vuitton bags.”

Almost half of new very rich people live in smaller cities. Many give large amounts to charity. However, the report says charity giving has not grown as fast as the total wealth of these very rich people.

Balaraman says having more rich people will not cause social tensions, because a big gap between rich and poor is an “accepted norm” in India. He says people know some are rich and some are poor, and they continue with their lives.
