Scientists took DNA from the wisdom tooth of a hunter-gatherer in Europe. This helped them learn about modern humans before farming began.

The man lived in Spain about 7,000 years ago, in the Mesolithic period. Tests on his genes suggest he had blue eyes, black or brown hair, and dark skin.

He was probably lactose intolerant, so he could not digest milk well. He also probably had more trouble digesting starchy foods than later farmers.

Farming brought humans and animals closer together. Because of this, people likely developed stronger immune systems to fight infections from animals. But the man’s DNA showed he already had some gene changes that help the immune system fight bacteria and viruses. Some of these gene changes still exist in modern Europeans.

Carles Lalueza-Fox led the study at the Institute of Evolutionary Biology in Barcelona. He said many of his earlier ideas were wrong.

The research started after cavers found two skeletons in a deep cave in the Cantabrian Mountains of northwest Spain in 2006. The two bodies were men in their early 30s. The cool cave kept the remains very well preserved.

Carbon dating showed the remains were about 7,000 years old. This was before farming spread into Europe from the Middle East. The time also matched objects found at the site, including reindeer teeth with holes that were worn on clothing.

The scientists studied the better preserved skeleton. After several failed tries, they reconstructed the man’s full genome from DNA in the root of a third molar. This was the first complete genome from a modern European who lived before the Neolithic revolution.

The results surprised the scientists. Lalueza-Fox expected light skin, but the genes suggested much darker skin. He said the man was darker than any modern European, but the exact shade is not known.

Another surprise was the man’s blue eyes. Scientists had thought the change for blue eyes happened later than the changes for lighter skin. These results suggest blue eyes came first in Europe, while lighter skin was still developing during Mesolithic times.

Because of this, artists may need to change how they draw these people. Lalueza-Fox said many pictures show hunter-gatherers with light skin, not dark skin with blue eyes.

The team compared the man’s genome with the genomes of modern Europeans. They found the closest match was with people in northern Europe, especially Sweden and Finland.

The immune system gene changes suggest that farming in Neolithic times did not cause all the immune changes seen in Europeans today. Some changes are older. Lalueza-Fox said they still do not know which pathogens affected these people.

Martin Jones, a professor at Cambridge University, said the immunity genes were the most striking result. He said there is a common story that moving from hunting and gathering to settled farming was bad for health at first. He said many things could have caused this, such as living close to other humans and animals, having fewer kinds of food, and less clean water. He said the study points to the role of pathogens before farming, and that is interesting.
