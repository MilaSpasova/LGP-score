Flappy Bird
Be careful what you wish for, especially when you want to invent something new. Recently, Dong Nguyen made the mobile game Flappy Bird. He removed it from app stores. He said the success ruined his simple life. The game was downloaded more than 50 million times. It was making him about £30,000 each day from advertising. On Twitter he said, "I cannot take this anymore."

He was not sorry in the same way as someone who makes a rifle, but he is one of many inventors who later wished they had not made something that became a problem.

The labradoodle
The labradoodle is not a monster. It is cute. But after Wally Conron first made it in the 1980s, many crossbreed dogs were bred and marketed in a bad way. He told the Associated Press, "I’ve done a lot of damage," and, "I’ve created a lot of problems. There are a lot of unhealthy and abandoned dogs out there." Conron created the labradoodle while working for the Royal Guide Dog Association of Australia. He wanted a dog for a blind woman, because her husband was allergic to dog hair. He did not expect the labradoodle, and other poodle-cross dogs, to become so popular. Many of these other poodle-cross dogs have health problems.

The AK-47
Six months before he died in December 2013, Mikhail Kalashnikov wrote to the head of the Russian Orthodox Church. Kalashnikov designed the AK-47 assault rifle. He wrote that his "spiritual torment" was "unbearable." He asked if he was responsible for people’s deaths because his rifle killed people, even if they were enemies. He wrote this when he was 93 years old.

Electronic tagging
The electronic tag was first planned in the 1960s. It was meant to track former prisoners at schools and workplaces, and to reward good behaviour. The inventors were Bob Gable and his brother Kirkland. Later they were horrified that the tag became a tool for control and punishment. Kirkland Gable told the Guardian in 2010, "It’s not pleasant," and said he was not in control of everything.

Pepper spray
In 2011, police used pepper spray on peaceful protesters at a University of California campus. One scientist who helped develop pepper spray in the 80s spoke against this. Kamran Loghman told The New York Times, "I have never seen such an inappropriate and improper use of chemical agents."

The office cubicle
In the late 60s, a new kind of office was introduced. It was designed to give workers privacy and to increase productivity by giving more work space. Instead, companies used it to pack employees into smaller spaces. It became a symbol of uniformity and soulless work. The inventor, Bob Propst, said in 1997 that "the cubiclizing of people in modern corporations is monolithic insanity."
