The room is very small and looks like a classroom. The walls are made of corrugated iron. On the walls there are school charts, like letters of the alphabet and a map of Bangladesh.

But it is hard to study there. There is loud hammering all the time. There are also chemicals in the air. They hurt the throat and make the eyes sting. Still, the children who study in this three-square-metre room are lucky. They do not have to work in the factories across the street.

For 14 years, SOHAY has worked in slum areas of Dhaka to help working children go to school. SOHAY is a grassroots NGO. It is funded by the Global Fund for Children and Comic Relief. It helps children who work in dangerous places, like aluminium and plastic factories, and tanneries.

This classroom is one of 23 urban development centres that SOHAY has set up in the capital. The centres teach children and prepare them for primary school. The classes help children catch up. After the children start primary school, they can come back to the centres to do homework. Other children there can help them.

Alamin is 10. He used to work in a plastic factory, but now he goes to one of the centres. His father sells things on the street. His mother is a part-time domestic worker. They are happy he is now in school and not doing dangerous work. His friend Rabi says he wants to forget his time in the factory. “I like school,” he says.

SOHAY’s programme manager, Mohammed Abdullah al-Mamun, says the centres try to make the community support education and change how people think about children. SOHAY also runs sessions for parents and employers to stop child labour. It also offers skills training to help families earn more money.

Mamun says it is very hard to bring working children into formal education. Their bodies and minds are not like other children’s. After they stop working, it can be hard for them to make friends and get used to school. It is also hard to help them stay in school, because many of these children drop out. He says schools need to be more understanding and caring.

Zhorna Akter Sumayya is 7. She has two older brothers. Both work: one in a restaurant and one at a local club. She first learned at a SOHAY centre, and now she goes to a state primary school. Her family lives in the slum. Her parents need the money her sons bring home. Her father works in a rickshaw garage and her mother is a domestic worker. But they really wanted their daughter to go to school.

In 2015, SOHAY helped 1,540 children leave dangerous work and helped 2,125 vulnerable children go to school. These vulnerable children were in danger of starting work. About 780 more children were preparing to enter school in 2017. SOHAY is also helping 635 children who work in dangerous conditions learn their rights under Bangladesh’s 2010 child labour elimination code of conduct. This policy wanted to end all child labour by 2015, but it did not reach that goal.

Bangladesh’s Labour Law of 2006 bans work for children under 14. But UNICEF says 4.7 million children under 14 work in the informal sector. UNICEF also says 1.3 million children aged five to 17 work in hazardous industries.

Sadia Nasrin runs Sonjag, another grassroots NGO in Dhaka. She says it was hard to send children to school when families got no payment for the children’s lost work time. So Sonjag started working closely with the slum communities where the children live.

Sonjag talked with community members about why school is important. It chose community volunteers who wanted to change children’s lives. It formed groups with social workers, community leaders, mothers, young volunteers, and local government.

Nasrin says these groups are very important. They encourage employers to let children leave work for two to three hours a day to attend school. They also try to make the workplace safe for children. She says the biggest people who can create change are the community members.

Nasrin adds that people in slums face eviction, police raids, and being forced to move. She says the national plan of action for children does not recognize what street children need. She also says laws are limited.

If children do not start school at age five, there is little time to help them. Mamun says that after children pass school age, it is very hard to get them into school. Without education, children may just wait and then start dangerous work. SOHAY says it is working to stop this flow into child labour.
