A senior US researcher warns that young people may lose the calm sounds of nature. He says people often block out the sounds around them.

Kurt Fristrup is a senior scientist at the US National Park Service. He says background noise is getting louder in some places. This noise can make people stop noticing nice sounds like birds singing, water flowing, and trees moving in the wind. These sounds can often be heard even in cities.

He says the problem is worse because many people listen to music with earphones. Then they do not listen to birds and other nature sounds. Nature sounds can also be covered by traffic, music, and other loud noise.

Fristrup calls this “learned deafness.” He says people are training themselves to ignore what they hear. He says humans are born able to hear sounds from hundreds of metres away, but this ability may be lost by the next generation. He also warns that people may lose good listening habits.

For the past ten years, the US National Park Service recorded sound levels at more than 600 sites across the US. These include Yosemite in California, Yellowstone, and Denali in Alaska. Every place had some noise from human activity, like planes flying over, motorbikes, motorboats, or tour buses.

Fristrup’s team used these national park recordings and similar data from cities. They made a model of noise levels across the US. They predict noise pollution is growing faster than the US population. They also predict it more than doubles every 30 years.

Fristrup says it is not surprising that people use earphones or noise-cancelling headphones to create a quieter place for themselves.

He says louder background noise affects hearing like fog affects vision. When there is fog, you cannot see far. In the same way, when noise is high, you notice only a small area of sound around you. He says even in cities there are birds and other things to enjoy. There can be rich natural sounds to listen to, but this is being lost.

He says people quickly get used to changes in their environment, including louder noise. Over time, he fears people will accept much worse conditions than they should. They may also forget how quiet the world could be. He says if finding peace and quiet becomes too hard, many children will grow up without this experience.

Other scientists at the same meeting reported health benefits from listening to nature sounds. Derrick Taff is a social scientist at Pennsylvania State University. He described early experiments. They suggest that listening to recordings from national parks, like waterfalls, birds, and wind, helped people recover from stressful events.

In one experiment, people came to Taff’s lab and gave a speech without preparation. Researchers judged them from behind a one-way mirror. Taff measured their heart rate and the stress hormone cortisol before and after the speech. People calmed down faster when they listened to nature recordings than when the same recordings also included sounds from road traffic, aeroplanes, or normal conversation.

Taff says natural sounds are very important to people. He says they are one main reason people visit protected areas. People want to hear quiet, birds, wind, and water. He says we may be losing this because people listen to iPods all the time. But he believes the public still likes these sounds. He advises people to visit protected areas and hear what they are missing.

Scientists are not sure why nature sounds calm people. But Fristrup thinks evolution may be part of the reason. Over millions of years, people may have linked calm nature sounds with safety. He thinks natural soundscapes may tell our brains it is safe and there is no predator nearby. He says noisy soundscapes may trouble us because we have lost the ability to listen for danger.
