For a traditional navigator like Tua Pittman from Raratonga in the Cook Islands, a canoe is much more than transport. He says, “The canoe is our island, the crew members are the community, and the navigator is the leader.” He also says it works the other way. “An island is our canoe, the community are the crew members, and the politicians and leaders are the navigators.” He explains that navigation is not only about traveling from one place to another using the stars, the moon, the sun, and birds. He says it is also about being a good leader and guiding people in life.

It was a very busy week for the crews of four sailing canoes after they arrived in Sydney for the start of the World Parks Congress. Tua’s trip began in the Cook Islands on 25 September. First they sailed to Samoa, then Fiji, Vanuatu, and the Gold Coast, and then south to Sydney. About 100 crew members joined at different parts of the trip. They wanted to travel using only traditional navigation. But sometimes they had to use modern navigation equipment so they could reach Australia in time for the Congress.

The official name of the trip is the Mua Voyage. It is a partnership between the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) Oceania Regional Office and five Pacific Island countries: Samoa, Tonga, New Zealand, the Cook Islands, and Fiji. The main goal of the 6,000-nautical-mile (11,000km) trip was to bring a special message to the World Parks Congress.

Part of the message said they see signs of too much use of the ocean. They do not see fish and other sea animals like before, in the same size, variety, or number. They see foreign fishing fleets in their waters, trying to take resources quickly. Their coral reefs, and their mangrove and wetland areas where sea life begins, are disappearing. They said the ocean is huge, but it is not endless. They said growing world populations and unsustainable development are making the ocean less able to support life.

Even with this urgent message, much of the last days of the marine part of the Congress were spent trying to set a new target for how much of the ocean should be protected in marine sanctuaries. The IUCN said that in 2013, not even 3% of the world’s oceans were in marine protected areas. Less than 1% of that was “no take,” where people cannot remove anything. This was despite a target of 20–30% no-take areas set by the last World Parks Congress in South Africa in 2003.

Professor Callum Roberts from the University of York helped set the 20–30% target in 2003. But he said it was not enough. He said the IUCN should lift the target from 30 to 33%. He said new research supports raising the target. He warned that reducing efforts now would be disastrous for the oceans.

After difficult talks, the Congress delegates passed a motion that changed the goals for global marine management. The IUCN’s new official position is to urgently increase the ocean area that is managed well and fairly by 2030. This should be done with connected systems of marine protected areas (MPAs) or other conservation measures. It should include strictly protected areas of at least 30% of each marine habitat, and it should address both biodiversity and ecosystem services.

Tua Pittman was very happy that a strong decision about the oceans passed at the Congress. He said it felt like a big reward for their hard work to come and be heard. He said it was a step in the right direction. He also said that while tools like the sun, moon, and stars do not change, damage to the environment is becoming clearer when traveling across the Pacific.

He said he is 55, and in his lifetime it has already become harder to catch fish in the open ocean. He also said pollution is getting worse, especially near big cities like Sydney. He said climate change is already seriously affecting Pacific Islanders. He said decisions by big countries can have two, three, or four times the impact on small countries than on large developed nations. He said many people do not even know where their islands are, but decisions made far away can affect their homes.

Tua said the Mua Voyage was very hard to organize. It took years of preparation and navigation planning. It was important to the voyagers that the world listened and acted. He said leaders of wealthy countries need to think more like traditional navigators, who know their vessels are very small in a huge sea. He said politicians must choose a different route to avoid the ecological crises starting to hit small island nations. He said, “The world needs to find a different path.”
