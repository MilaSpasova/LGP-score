If we ignore big disaster movies with volcanoes, hurricanes, and earthquakes, two main natural things can still destroy a city slowly, or even make it disappear: water and sand.

If climate change were making Earth colder, we could add ice. A huge glacier can destroy a city as it moves. In 2013, there was a rare “ice tsunami” in Canada at Ochre Beach. Wind pushed a wall of melting ice from Dauphin Lake onto the shore, and it smashed every house in its way.

But Ochre Beach was unusual. In most places, melting ice is dangerous because it raises sea levels. A hundred years ago, Venice used to flood about ten times a year. Now its lowest point, Piazza San Marco, which is only three feet above sea level, floods about 100 times a year.

Rising sea levels are not the only cause. In many places, the land is also sinking. In Venice, the ground under the city became more compact because of industry nearby. This lowered the city by 20 cm between 1950 and 1970. Ho Chi Minh City in Vietnam is sinking about 2 cm a year. Jakarta is worse: it is sinking 10 to 20 cm each year. In the past three decades, it has sunk about four metres, so its 40-year-old seawall works less and less well.

Jakarta has pumped out a lot of groundwater to support its people. Because of this, the land above is drying out and sinking, making a bowl shape. Rivers that used to flow through the city to the sea had to be moved, because now they cannot flow uphill.

Many plans exist to save Venice. Ho Chi Minh City and Jakarta are taking the problem seriously. But Miami is different. There, politicians will not admit the city has a serious environmental problem.

Miami has three problems. It is less than ten feet above sea level. More tropical storms are flooding the city. And it is built on porous limestone, which lets rising seawater soak in. The water then fills the city’s foundations and comes up through drains and pipes. This pushes sewage up and pollutes fresh water. Miami could one day become impossible to live in.

In the Maldives, people on whole islands now want to leave their homes. The capital, Male, has 153,379 people and is only four feet above sea level. With Japanese investment, it built a ten-foot sea wall that cost $63 million. But in the long term, only stopping sea levels from rising will save Male and the other islands.

The sea is not the only danger. Rainfall is also harming cities. Recently, Detroit had an entire summer’s worth of rain in one morning. Its old sewer system could not handle it. Detroit became poorer after America’s car industry collapsed, and it is struggling to build a better system. Drinking water is also affected because Detroit is very vulnerable to water-borne pathogens.

In Africa, desertification is making the Sahara Desert spread south by 30 miles per year. This threatens settlements in northern Mauritania. Around the trading and religious centre of Chinguetti, the desert has grown by more than 260 acres in the past 20 years. Chinguetti’s population has fallen from 20,000 in the mid-twentieth century to only a few thousand now. Trading has almost stopped because sand is filling the streets.

In the same way, the resort city of Rancho Mirage in California, near Palm Springs, may have to be left within the next decade. This problem is not only about global warming. It is also about people expanding too much while resources are limited. California’s plan to farm the desert made sense when the population was about half a million in 1870. Now California has 38 million people. They own 32 million vehicles and expect lawns and golf courses.

In Rancho Mirage, daily water use is more than 200 gallons per person. This is causing a drought made by people. A 25% cut in water use has started, but it may not save the resort, which is surrounded by sand and dust. In the long term, some desert cities in California may need to be abandoned, while people move to and strengthen other cities.

People leaving is the final sign that a city is falling apart. When a city is not cared for, nature takes over fast. Hashima Island in Japan became a concrete city in 1887 because of coal under the sea. In 1974, when coal was almost gone, the mine closed and Hashima was abandoned. Now sea spray is making its concrete walls fall down.

Fire is also a growing danger for cities in America. After severe storms, forest fires cause the most damage. Between 1953 and 2014, America declared 800 major fire disasters. A new report from the USDA Forest Service shows more urban areas that are very at risk from wildfire.

In Australia, long droughts are making trees burn easily. Some resorts in Victoria and several suburbs of Melbourne are on a list of the state’s 52 most vulnerable bushfire places. A forest fire may not destroy a whole city at once, but many fires over time could make a city impossible to live in. Leaving a city is one of the strongest causes of city collapse.

Many cities are losing their fight against nature. Can we name the most vulnerable big city in the world? Natural events are very hard to predict, but Male looks especially at risk. Even if its sea wall keeps working, the islands around the Maldives capital will disappear before too long. And if they disappear, Male loses its reason to exist.
