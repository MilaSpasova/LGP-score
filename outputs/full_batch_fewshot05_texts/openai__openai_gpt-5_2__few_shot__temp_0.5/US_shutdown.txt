The International Monetary Fund (IMF) asked Washington to fix its money problems. This was after the US almost failed to pay its debts, and many federal workers went back to work after a 16-day government shutdown.

US President Barack Obama said the country must stop “governing by crisis.” The IMF leader, Christine Lagarde, also asked for more stability.

She said the US should reduce uncertainty about government spending and taxes. She said the US should raise the debt limit in a longer-lasting way. She also said the US should approve a 2014 budget. She said the US should replace the “sequester” with slow, gradual steps that would not hurt the economic recovery. She also said the US should make a balanced, complete plan for the next few years.

A deal written in the Senate was passed by the House of Representatives only hours before the deadline to extend the debt limit. The deal gave almost nothing to the conservatives who had helped push the country close to a new financial crisis.

The World Bank said it was relieved. Its president, Jim Yong Kim, said the world had avoided a “potential catastrophe.” He asked leaders in all countries to focus on policies that help economic growth, jobs, and opportunity for everyone.

Stock markets in Japan, China, Hong Kong, and South Korea first showed relief after Republicans gave up their failed attempt to weaken Obama’s healthcare reforms. But overall, markets in Asia and Europe did not react strongly. Traders seemed to expect another fight in Washington early in 2014.

Standard & Poor’s estimated the shutdown cost the US $24bn. China’s Xinhua News Agency said Washington put other countries’ dollar assets at risk. China is the US government’s largest creditor.

Obama signed the new law shortly after midnight on Thursday to prevent a debt default. There was a split among Republicans in the House of Representatives. The bill passed easily in the Senate with support from both parties. It was a temporary fix. It funded the government until 15 January and raised the debt ceiling until 7 February.

Obama said he did not expect another bitter budget fight and shutdown in 2014. He said he hoped the deal would “lift the cloud of uncertainty” over the country. He said he would sign the agreement immediately. He also said he hoped next time it would not happen at the last moment.

After a Wednesday night press briefing, a journalist asked Obama if the crisis would happen again in a few months. Obama answered, “No.”

Earlier, Republican senator Mike Lee spoke strongly and suggested more trouble. He said the media asked, “Was it worth it?” He said it is always worth it to do the right thing. He added, “This is not over.”

But the deal was very bad for Republicans. None of their goals were reached, and polls showed voters mostly blamed them for the crisis.
