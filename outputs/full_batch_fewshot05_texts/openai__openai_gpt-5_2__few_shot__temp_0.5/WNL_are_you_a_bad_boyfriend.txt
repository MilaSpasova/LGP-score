Dan Sullivan was late to breakfast with the Guardian. Police stopped him while he was riding his motorcycle.

Sullivan says he works too much. He misses dinner dates. He forgets to buy presents.

Like many people in Silicon Valley, the 27-year-old venture capitalist made a start-up to help with this. It is called BetterBoyfriend.me. For about $70 a month, it sends a gift every month to a girlfriend or wife.

Sullivan works in finance. He has been quietly testing the service. About 350 boyfriends have signed up. The service makes about $17,000 each month. Many members are his Harvard and investor friends. They include venture capitalists, founders, and workers at companies like Apple, Google, and Facebook. Their girlfriends have been getting gifts for the last year.

Each month, members choose one gift from a list of seven. The gifts can be chocolate, tea sets, or manicures. The package is sent to the boyfriend with no brand name and no label.

Sullivan tests ideas by buying Facebook ads to see what happens. BetterBoyfriend.me grew fast. Sullivan said it solved a problem many people have.

BetterBoyfriend.me was not his first idea. For a few days, he thought about a dog-sharing idea. He said he saw rich men who looked unhappy while walking dogs. He said a dog is only valuable 5% of the time. He asked: what if someone babysits the dogs, and other people can rent them?

Another start-up is called InvisibleBoyfriend. It lets users design a believable social partner. This partner is always available to talk. The result is a “boyfriend” who can text all day.

Founder Kyle Tabor started it to help users stop unwanted attention. But he found that some people became attached to their algorithmic partners. He said many users want companionship through conversation, not just “proof” of a relationship.

For Sullivan and Tabor, one surprise was the real relationships they formed with customers. Sullivan says he now feels like a relationship consultant for the boyfriends.

Sullivan says he made mistakes. At first, the packages had receipts with his name, Dan Sullivan. One boyfriend wrote, “Listen, dude, she’s not mad but Cynthia found out.”

About 50% of the women who get BetterBoyfriend.me packages know Sullivan is involved. Sullivan says it is connected to age. He thinks that after people have been married a while, they keep fewer secrets.

Over the year, Sullivan says he got to know the boyfriends well. Once, they even delivered to a hospital delivery room. He says the important thing is that his relationship is with the boyfriend.

When he first started the company, he put tags that said BetterBoyfriend.me on flower bouquets. Then he went to Dolores Park in the Mission District.

He looked for couples and gave the bouquet to the girl. The boyfriend did not like that. Sullivan said it made sense, so he changed his plan. He started giving the flowers to the boyfriends.
