Race engineer
A race engineer works between the driver and the mechanics.

Typical salary: New graduates start at £25,000 to £30,000. After a few years, they can become junior engineers and earn more than £40,000. Senior race engineers earn £50,000 to £90,000. Promotion can lead to six-figure salaries.

What the job involves: Race engineer Jamie Muir says a race engineer is like an interpreter between the race-car mechanics and the driver. The engineer listens to the driver’s feedback, studies the data, decides the best car set-up for top performance, and tells the mechanics what to do.

Qualifications: You usually need a university degree, often in automotive or mechanical engineering, or motorsport technology. Hands-on experience is essential.

To succeed as a race engineer, you need to handle pressure.

Worst thing about the job: The long hours. Chris Aylett, CEO of the Motorsport Industry Association, says race engineers work 24/7.

Ethical hacker
Typical salary: A newly qualified hacker can expect a minimum salary of £35,000 to £50,000. At team-leader level, pay is £60,000 to £90,000.

What the job involves: A company pays an ethical hacker to break into its computer system to test how strong its security is against a real attack.

Qualifications: You do not always need a computer science degree. The industry accepts people with many different qualifications and skills.

To succeed as an ethical hacker, you need to love technology and details. You also need strong analytical skills and you should enjoy solving difficult problems.

Worst thing about the job: Testing a new customer’s network and finding that they have already had a data breach.

Bomb-disposal diver
Typical salary: In the private sector, you can earn up to £100,000 by working two months out of every three.

What the job involves: You go down to the sea bed and look for unexploded ordnance, such as bombs, shells, grenades, and landmines. Then you either collect the weapons safely or destroy them safely.

Qualifications: To dive offshore, you must have diving-inspection and medical-technician qualifications. To safely dispose of bombs, you also need an explosive ordnance disposal qualification and years of experience.

To succeed as a bomb-disposal diver, you need to stay calm under stress. You work alone deep underwater, with no visibility. You also need to be okay living in small, crowded spaces.

Worst thing about the job: You should expect to be away from home at least six months each year.

Social engineer
Typical salary: On average, between £50,000 and £80,000. Graduates start at £25,000, but pay can rise quickly with qualifications and experience.

The job: A company pays a social engineer to try to trick employees into giving secret information. This can help the engineer get access to sensitive company data or the company’s computer network.

Qualifications: Social engineers usually have a degree in IT. Knowledge of psychology is useful. A background in marketing, teaching, and customer service can also help.

To succeed as a social engineer, you need the confidence to lie convincingly and the skill to fit in almost anywhere. You also need strong personal ethics and you must understand the law.

Worst thing about the job: People may misunderstand your work. Social engineers are not spies, but most people think they are.

Power-line helicopter pilot
Typical salary: £65,000.

The job: You fly a helicopter very close to high-voltage power lines. This lets the power company inspect the lines with a camera and find possible faults.

Qualifications: You need a private helicopter pilot licence, a commercial pilot’s licence, and about 2,000 hours of low-level flying experience in the type of helicopter the company uses.

To succeed as a power-line helicopter pilot, you need a steady hand and a calm head. Pilots may fly beside the power line only 20 feet away and just 30 feet above the ground.

Worst thing about the job: Robin Tutcher, chief helicopter pilot for Western Power Distribution overhead-power network, says there are no negatives.

Private butler
Typical salary: £60,000 to £90,000.

The job: A private butler can be asked to do many tasks, from managing clothes to driving and caring for pets. Usual duties include managing other staff, serving meals, running errands, looking after guests, booking restaurants, keeping the house secure, housekeeping, cooking, and anything else the household needs.

Qualifications: You do not need specific qualifications. You can take a course at the British Butler Academy or the British Butler Institute.

To succeed as a butler, you need to truly enjoy looking after other people.

Worst thing about the job: The hours are long and the schedule can change, so it is hard to have family life. Sara Vestin, director of the British Butler Academy, says butlers can also feel isolated, have cultural differences with their employer, and sometimes have to work for people who are not always nice.
