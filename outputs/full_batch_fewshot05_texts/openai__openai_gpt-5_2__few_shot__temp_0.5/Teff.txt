At Addis Ababa airport, visitors see pictures of teff. There are photos of golden grains, tiny red-brown seeds, and men sitting around a very large pancake. Big signs say: “Teff: the ultimate gluten-free crop!”

Ethiopia is one of the world’s poorest countries. Many people worry about having enough food. But Ethiopia is also the home of teff, an old and very nutritious grain. Teff is now appearing more often in health-food shops and supermarkets in Europe and America.

Teff seeds are very small, about the size of poppy seeds. They have a lot of calcium, iron, and protein. Teff is naturally gluten free. People can use teff flour instead of wheat flour to make bread, pasta, waffles, and pizza bases. Like quinoa, teff has strong nutrition and could bring new markets in the west.

In Ethiopia, teff is very important. About 6.3 million farmers grow it. Teff fields cover more than 20% of all farm land. People grind teff into flour and make injera, a soft, sour flatbread that is basic in Ethiopian food. Teff is also part of many religious and cultural events. In Ethiopia and nearby Eritrea, people eat together around big pieces of injera. They use the injera like a spoon to pick up stews. They also feed each other to show loyalty or friendship. This tradition is called gursha.

For many years, teff was not well known in the west, except in diaspora communities. Now more people want traditional crops, and the health-food and gluten-free markets are growing. Some people call teff Ethiopia’s “second gift to the world”, after coffee.

Sophie Kebede is an entrepreneur in London. She owns a UK company that specializes in teff. She says she was very surprised when she learned how nutritious teff is. She says, “I didn’t know it was so sought after. I am of Ethiopian origin; I’ve been eating injera all my life.”

But higher demand for “ancient grains” does not always help poor communities. In Bolivia and Peru, some reports say quinoa trade raised incomes. Other reports talk about malnutrition and land conflicts, because farmers sell all their crop to meet western demand.

In Ethiopia, the growing middle class also wants more teff. Prices have gone up, so the poorest people cannot afford it. Many small farmers now sell most of what they grow to people in cities.

The government says this can raise incomes in some rural areas, but it can also hurt nutrition. Teff is the most nutritious grain in the country. Estimates say city people may eat up to 61kg of teff a year, but rural people eat about 20kg. Rich people mostly eat the more expensive magna and white teff. Poorer people usually eat red and mixed teff, which is less valuable. More than half also mix teff with cheaper grains like sorghum and maize.

The Ethiopian government wants to double teff production by 2015. In a 2013 plan, it says teff could help in school meals and emergency aid, and could reduce malnutrition, especially for children and adolescents.

Ethiopia’s economy is growing fast, but it is still on the UN list of least-developed countries. About 20% of children under five are malnourished or have stunted growth.

The government’s Agricultural Transformation Agency wants to increase how much teff farmers can harvest. It plans to develop improved teff varieties, and new planting methods and tools. It also wants to reduce losses after harvest.

Since 2006, the government has restricted teff exports. It does not allow exports of raw teff grain. It only allows exports of injera and other processed products. But this might change. The government says the goal is to have enough teff for people in Ethiopia and also a strong export market.

Mama Fresh is a family company that sells injera to top restaurants and hotels in Addis Ababa. It also ships injera to Finland, Germany, Sweden, and the US, mainly for diaspora communities. But the company also wants customers in the gluten-free market. It plans to double exports to America in 2014. It will also start making teff pizzas, bread, and cookies.

David Hallam works at the UN’s Food and Agriculture Organization. He says these products often pass through many people before they reach shops like Sainsbury’s. Each step has profit, and small farmers may not be able to bargain well with big traders. He says quinoa is a warning that exports can bring both good and bad results for poor countries.

Regassa Feyissa is an Ethiopian agricultural scientist and a former head of the National Institute for Biodiversity. He warns that if Ethiopia is not careful, growing more teff for export could push out other important crops. He also says efforts to increase production might help businesses more than small farmers.

Because little Ethiopian teff is sold on the world market, farmers in the US have started growing it. Farmers in Europe, Israel, and Australia have also tried it.

Kebede says she buys teff from farms in southern Europe, but she would rather buy it from Ethiopia. She says teff is natural for Ethiopians, so Ethiopians should supply it. She asks why Ethiopian farmers cannot benefit from this grain.
