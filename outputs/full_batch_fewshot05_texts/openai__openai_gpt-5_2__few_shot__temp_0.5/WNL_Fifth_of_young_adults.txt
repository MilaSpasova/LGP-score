People sometimes call it “the hotel of mum and dad”. During the housing crisis, many young adults are staying in their family home until they are at least 26. About one in five are not paying any money to live there.

A survey found that the number of adults living at home is different in different parts of the country. It is just under 9% in the East Midlands. It is more than double that in London, where house prices and rents are highest. Many people paid some money, but the survey found that 20% paid nothing.

Young adults often have low wages, and rents have reached record highs. Many people want to buy a home, but they cannot save because rent costs so much. Research from the homeless charity Shelter showed that half of renters could not save any money for a deposit. A quarter could save only £100 or less each month. Interest rates are very low, so mortgages are cheaper than before, but the best mortgage deals are still for people with large deposits.

Because of this, more young adults are going back to live with their parents to save money. The survey found that 28% were living at home to save for a deposit. But it also found that 30% were not saving any money.

A spokesman for the company that did the survey said the “hotel of mum and dad” is staying open longer than many people expected. High rent costs and deposits, or the need to save for a mortgage deposit, mean some children have to wait before leaving home. For some, moving out may never be possible.

Michael Day, 30, lives with his parents in Bristol. He says he is stuck between paying high rent and saving for a mortgage deposit. In Bristol, rent for a one-bedroom home is between £500 and £800 a month. Buying a similar home would cost about £130,000. He said renting can cost more than a mortgage, but you need a big deposit to get a mortgage, so it becomes a “vicious circle”.

Day does not want to live with strangers, so he has fewer choices. At home, he pays a small rent to help with bills. He keeps the rest of his pay from his job at a candle retailer. He plays golf at county level. He says that instead of saving, he spends his extra money on golf and holidays. He said he will need to save for a long time. Because it has been so hard, he has been going on holiday and enjoying it.

Sue Green, from Saga, a business that sells insurance and products to people over 50, said many parents did not plan for their children to live with them into their late 20s or 30s. She said most parents are happy to let them stay rent-free because it may help them buy a home sooner. She said children who do not pay rent may help in other ways, like buying food, buying takeaways for the family, or doing small jobs at home.

Angus Hanton, co-founder of a thinktank called the Intergenerational Foundation, said older generations were “the architects of the housing crisis” and young people should not be blamed for living at home. He said that since the 2008 downturn, average incomes for under-30s have fallen by about 20%. He said rents and car insurance have never been so high. He said mortgage lending rules have become stricter for young people but not for older buy-to-let investors, who push young people out. He said student-fee debt is rising quickly, and many jobs are zero-hour or short-term contracts, which makes young workers feel like second-class citizens. He said people should stand up for young people’s interests so they can afford to build their own lives.

Jenna Gavin, 29, lives in Southport, Merseyside, in the home where she grew up. She moved out for one year for university, but she has lived with her parents since then. She works nearby as a medical receptionist, so she wants to stay in the area. Renting a one-bedroom flat would cost more than £420 a month before bills, which would take a lot of her income. She said she does not want to rent and then have nothing at the end. She said she has looked at buying and talked to mortgage advisers, but she cannot borrow enough to buy a home.

Gavin is trying to save, but it is hard to save enough. She said even a 5% deposit is a lot of money, and she would like to pay more than that. Her parents are happy not to charge her rent. She said they want her to save, and she helps in other ways by bringing in food and doing things around the house.

Gavin gets along with her parents and has her own space in a bedroom she moved into when she was 14. She said she always thought she would have her own home by the time she was 30. She said she does not think that will happen next year, but she hopes that in a couple of years she will have moved out.
