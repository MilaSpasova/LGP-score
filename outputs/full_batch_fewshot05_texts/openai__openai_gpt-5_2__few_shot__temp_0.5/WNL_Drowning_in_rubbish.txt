Some cities have pigeons. Lima has black vultures, also called gallinazos. They fly in circles above the city in groups. They also sit on famous buildings, like old churches and old buildings in the city centre. Many people think their wrinkled heads and small eyes remind them of the parts of Lima they want to forget: neglect, poverty, and dirt.

Vultures like dead and rotten things. Now the city is trying to use this as something good. Environmental authorities are giving the birds GoPro cameras and GPS trackers. The birds have a new job: helping find places where people throw rubbish illegally.

Samuel is one of 10 certified disease-free birds in the project. His species is Coragyps atratus. With a tracker on him, he is released above the city. He finds secret rubbish dumps and records the GPS location on a live map.

His trainer at Lima’s Huachipa Zoo, Alfredo Correa, says he admires them. He says they can eat dead animals because their metabolism protects them from viruses and bacteria. He also says they have some of the strongest gut flora in nature.

The project is a team effort between USAID and the Peruvian Environment Ministry. It is trying to solve Lima’s rubbish problem. Other birds have names like Capitan Huggin, Capitan Fenix, and Capitana Aella. A joking video uses a dramatic voice. It says the vultures fight sickness and disease while people calmly ignore the danger.

The project has a serious message. Lima has almost 10 million people, but only four landfills. Because of this, there are many illegal dumps. The Environment Ministry says one fifth of the rubbish ends up in these places. Dirty water from the waste pollutes Lima’s main water source, the Rimac river. It also pollutes the Chillon and Lurin rivers, which flow into the Bay of Lima.

The environmental agency OEFA says three poorer neighbourhoods have the most illegal dumped rubbish, even though they have only 12% of Lima’s people. These areas are Villa Maria del Triunfo (39.4%), Villa El Salvador (25.3%), and El Agustino (18.3%).

Part of the problem is unpaid taxes. Many residents, especially in the barrios, do not pay. So some of the 43 district municipalities do not have enough money for basic services like rubbish collection.

This also means that when vultures find illegal rubbish, no one may clean it up. Javier Hernandez, the project director, says they share the GPS locations with the municipalities. He says it is the municipalities’ job to collect the rubbish and try to change people’s habits.

The project also wants citizens to be “vultures on the ground.” People should report illegal dumping, make less waste, and recycle. Some residents are doing this. They post photos of illegal dumps on the Twitter feed and Facebook page.

The idea started at the 2014 United Nations Climate Change Conference in Lima. Hernandez says they wanted ways to involve both authorities and citizens, to build environmental awareness. The artist Cristina Planas put huge vulture heads on top of 25 dead palm trees in a wetland reserve in the south of the city. Rich residents hated it and thought it was ugly. But Planas asked citizens to “adopt a vulture” to support conservation and recycling.

Planas said people are a little scared of how the vulture looks. But she said the vulture is sacrificing himself for people. She said the vulture is recycling, in the last place where people threw their rubbish.
