In the market square in Rjukan, there is a statue of the town’s founder. He was a famous Norwegian engineer and industrialist named Sam Eyde. The statue looks north across the square at a very steep mountain.

Behind the statue, to the south, is another steep mountain. It is the 1,800-metre peak called Gaustatoppen. Between these mountains, in the narrow Vestfjord valley, is the small town of Rjukan. Eyde built the town in the early years of the last century. He built it for workers in his factories.

Eyde used the power of the 100-metre Rjukanfossen waterfall to make hydroelectricity. At that time, it was the world’s biggest power plant.

But Eyde could not change where the sun was. Rjukan is deep in a valley and surrounded by high mountains. So the town and its 3,400 people are in shadow for half the year. From late September to mid-March, it is not fully dark in the day, but it is not bright either. Rjukan is three hours north-west of Oslo.

Now there is something new. On the mountain opposite, 450 metres above the town, there are three big mirrors. They are solar-powered and computer-controlled. They follow the sun in the sky. They reflect sunlight down to the square and make it bright.

“It’s the sun!” says Ingrid Sparbo. She lifts her face to the light and closes her eyes. She is a retired secretary and has lived in Rjukan all her life. She says people get used to the shade. But she says this sunlight feels warm, not only for the body but also for the mind.

Two young mothers bring their children into the square. An older couple sit on a new bench on a freezing day. Children smile. Many people take photos. A shop assistant, Silje Johansen, says it is “awesome. Just awesome.”

Electrical engineer Eivind Toreid is more careful. He is pushing a buggy. He says it is strange: it is not real sunlight, but it is very similar, like a spotlight.

Heidi Fieldheim says she heard about it on the radio. She says it is much more than she expected, and it will bring a lot of happiness.

In the Nye Tider café, across the road, sits the man who made this happen. He is Martin Andersen, a 40-year-old artist. He moved to Rjukan in the summer of 2001.

Andersen got the first idea for an artwork called the Solspeil, or Sun mirror, when September was ending. He says he walked with his young child in a buggy every day. Every day, they had to go farther down the valley to find the sun. By 28 September, the sun disappears completely from Rjukan’s market square. It does not come back until 12 March.

During these long months, Andersen says they could see blue sky above and the sun high on the mountain slopes. But to feel the sun, they had to go out of town. He says it is sad if people must leave a town to feel the sun.

Twelve years after his first idea, a German company that works with CSP, concentrated solar power, brought the mirrors by helicopter. There are three glass mirrors, each 17 sq m. They stand high above the market square. Andersen says it took longer than they imagined.

The mirrors really work. Many local people were against the project at first. They signed petitions, wrote letters, and made a Facebook page. They thought it was a vanity project and a waste of money. But now many of them seem to accept it.

Nils Eggerud says he was strongly against it. He thought the money could be used for other things, like extra carers for old people, better schools, cycle paths, or fixing rural roads. He also worries about future maintenance costs and who will pay them. But he says it feels nice to stand in the light, and you can see it on people’s faces.

Rjukan’s mayor, Steinar Bergsland, has an office with a view of the square. He is more interested in the benefits than the cost.

Bergsland says there are already more visitors at this time of year. Shopkeepers say they are earning more money too. A hi-tech company may move to Rjukan because it likes the new technology on the mountain and the publicity.

Bergsland says the mirrors are a powerful symbol for Rjukan. With government grants and a donation from a local business, the town had to pay only 1m krone – £100,000 – of the total 5m-krone cost.

He says that compared with the town’s 650m krone yearly budget, 1m krone was not much. He says the mirrors give the town a better chance to raise money for better schools and more nursing care. He says you can see the happy faces outside, and now that it is real, people love it.
