After a vote in the European Parliament, new EU rules on tobacco came closer.

The new rules would put big health warnings on cigarette packs. The warnings would cover 65% of each pack, almost two-thirds. Now the warnings cover at least 30% on the front and 40% on the back.

The EU would also ban menthol and other flavours from 2022.

MEPs decided that most electronic cigarettes (e-cigarettes) do not need the same rules as medicines. This was bad news for the UK government.

Health officials and the e-cigarette industry in Britain want to understand what the vote means. For example, they want to know if e-cigarette companies will face the same rules on sponsorship and promotion at sports events as tobacco companies.

The Department of Health (DH) said it would not comment on advertising until it studied the decisions. The DH said it was happy about tougher action on tobacco, including the ban on flavoured cigarettes and stricter rules for health warnings. But it was disappointed that MEPs rejected the plan to regulate nicotine-containing products (NCPs), including e-cigarettes, as medicines. The DH said it still wants these products to be regulated as medicines.

The DH said smoking in England is at its lowest level since records began: 19.5%. It said it still wants to reduce smoking further.

The UK e-cigarette industry mostly welcomed the vote. It said it was already talking with the Advertising Standards Authority. It also said banning all advertising would not be sensible.

MEPs said e-cigarettes should be treated as medical products only if companies say they can prevent tobacco smoking. The government’s main medicines regulator criticized this.

By 2014, an estimated 1.3 million people in Britain were using these products. Some people want e-cigarettes to be treated like nicotine gums, patches, and mouth sprays that help smokers quit. But the industry says the costly licensing process could push alternatives to tobacco off shop shelves.

The UK government has delayed a decision on standardized packaging like Australia’s. It says it needs evidence that this reduces tobacco use.

The votes were the first reading of a draft tobacco directive, which could become law in 2014. Next there will be talks with the EU Council of Ministers.

The Medicines and Healthcare Products Regulatory Authority had asked manufacturers in June 2013 to use voluntary regulation. It still hopes for compulsory rules across Europe. A spokesman said the process is not finished and there will be more negotiations. He said the UK still believes medicine-style regulation of NCPs is best for public health.

Linda McAvan, a Labour MEP for Yorkshire and the Humber, said children start smoking, not adults. She said World Health Organization figures show worrying rises in young smokers in some member states. She said tobacco companies must not target young people with gimmicky products, and cigarette packs must have strong warnings.

Martin Callanan, a Conservative MEP for North East England, said forcing e-cigarettes off shelves would be crazy. He said e-cigarettes have helped many people stop smoking more harmful cigarettes.

Katherine Devlin, president of ECITA, said the decision not to support medicines regulation was very important, and that it was now off the table.

British American Tobacco said the larger warnings were more than needed to inform consumers about smoking risks. It also said banning menthol cigarettes would increase demand for black-market goods.
