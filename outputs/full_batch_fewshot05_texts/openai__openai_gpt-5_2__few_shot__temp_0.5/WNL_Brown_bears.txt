A new study says big predators are doing well in Europe. Forests and even suburbs have the sounds of large animals. The study says brown bears, wolves, and lynx are growing in number, or staying stable. This is surprising because some people feared these animals would die out as humans increase and use too many resources. The study was published in Science.

Brown bears, wolves, the Eurasian lynx, and wolverines live in almost one-third of mainland Europe (not including Belarus, Ukraine, and Russia). Most of these animals live outside nature reserves. This suggests that people’s attitudes are changing and that large-scale conservation is working. These species were heavily hunted for a long time in human history.

Bears are the most common large carnivore in Europe. There are about 17,000 bears, 12,000 wolves, 9,000 Eurasian lynx, and 1,250 wolverines. Wolverines live only in the far north of Scandinavia and Finland. In mainland Europe, only Belgium, Denmark, the Netherlands, and Luxembourg (like Britain) have no breeding populations of at least one large carnivore species. The lead author and other conservationists said the wide spread of these animals in crowded places suggests the British countryside could also support big predators.

Guillaume Chapron, from Sweden’s University of Agricultural Sciences, and other researchers found that wolves sometimes live in suburban areas. In some places, up to 3,050 people live in one square kilometre. This is higher than the population density of Cambridge or Newcastle. On average in Europe, wolves live on land with 37 people per sq km. Lynx live where there are 21 people per sq km, and bears where there are 19 people per sq km. The Scottish Highlands have nine people per sq km.

Chapron said, “In order to have wolves, we don’t need to remove people from the landscape.” He and his colleagues said the return of big predators shows a “land-sharing” model can work. This is different from keeping people and predators apart by fencing off “wilderness” areas, as in North America and Africa.

Chapron also said living together can bring conflict. He said it is important to manage conflict, keep it low, and solve the problems it causes. He said wolves can be difficult neighbours. He also said the main conflict is often between people who disagree about predators. He said these animals raise hard questions about how we should use the land.

The researchers said the “land-sharing” approach could work in other parts of the world too. They said it has worked in Europe because of political stability, growing numbers of prey animals like wild deer, and money for non-lethal ways to protect farm animals, such as electric fences. This helps stop farmers from shooting predators. Chapron said the most important reason is the EU Habitats Directive. It has forced member states to protect and bring back rare species.

Chapron said that without the Habitats Directive, the recovery might not have happened. He said it shows that if people want to protect nature, and if political will becomes strong laws like the Habitats Directive, then wildlife protection can succeed.

The recovery was welcomed by the author and commentator George Monbiot. He is launching Rewilding Britain, a new charity. It aims to encourage the return of wild landscapes and extinct species.

Monbiot said it is good that numbers are rising, but Britain is very unusual. He said Britain has lost more large mammals than any country except Ireland. He said that apart from the accidental return of boar, Britain has done almost nothing. Meanwhile, in much of Europe, bears, lynx, and wolves are coming back. He said this is a huge change after centuries of persecution.

The survey found Eurasian lynx living permanently in 11 population groups across 23 European countries. Only five of these were native populations. This suggests reintroduction has worked. Monbiot said support is growing for bringing lynx back to the Cairngorms in Scotland.

Monbiot said that if it works in the rest of Europe, there is no reason it cannot work in the UK. He pointed out that bears and wolves live within an hour of Rome. He said there is no demographic reason why wildlife cannot return in a similar way in the UK.
