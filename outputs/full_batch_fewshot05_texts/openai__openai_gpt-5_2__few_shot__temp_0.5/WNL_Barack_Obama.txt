Barack Obama told young people to not be negative and to talk with people who have different beliefs if they want to change the world.

On the last day of his final visit to Britain as US president, Obama spoke to 500 young leaders at a town hall meeting in London. He said they should not think there are forces they cannot control. He repeated a quote from JFK: problems are made by people and can be solved by people.

He spoke to A-level students and UK–US exchange students in a Q&A session. He said they have good tools to make a difference. He told them to reject pessimism and cynicism, and to believe progress is possible. He said progress is not guaranteed and needs struggle, discipline, and faith.

Obama said young people have faced hard times and big changes, including 9/11 and 7/7. He said there is also a lot of information and Twitter, with a steady stream of bad news.

The audience cheered when he was introduced. He talked about his policies, including healthcare and education.

He told them to talk with people who have different political beliefs. He said meeting people who disagree can help you compromise.

He also said social attitudes are changing. He said it is not fast enough, but they should keep pushing, and that change is partly because of brave young people.

When asked about his legacy, Obama said he was proud of his healthcare reforms. He also spoke about the US response to the 2008 financial crisis and said, “Saving the world from great depression – that was quite good.”

He also listed diplomatic deals with Iran and the response to the Ebola crisis as important moments. He said he was proud and had stayed true to himself.

He told young people not to give up if problems like poverty are not solved after five years. He quoted Dr Martin Luther King, saying the moral universe is long but bends toward justice.

When asked about the Transatlantic Trade and Investment Partnership (TTIP), he said globalization should not mean shutting off from the world. He said it was important to pay attention to workers’ rights.

Before Obama arrived, Tanya Williams, a community engagement officer, told the Guardian she loved Michelle, but also liked Obama. She said it was exciting to hear someone who has changed so much and inspired many people who did not vote before.

Oliver Sidorczuk, 26, said people were very excited to hear Obama. He said he wanted to ask about electoral rights and ask if Obama would join their campaign to ask David Cameron for automatic registration.

Obama ended the session by taking a question from a young Sikh Londoner. The young man asked about racial profiling at airports and being mistaken for a Muslim.

Obama said that even though some people have “crazy ideology,” pluralism is important. He said he visited a mosque a few months before and said America’s greatest allies are American Muslims who are well integrated and economically well off.

Furqan Naeem, a campaigner from Manchester, said he visited the States through the US embassy on the community leaders programme. He said he saw important work the president did to celebrate America’s diversity and bring communities together.

Kenny Imafidon, the managing director of the youth organization ClearView Research, said the meeting was a great opportunity. He said he would remember Obama’s message about meeting people with different politics and making compromises. He also mentioned what Obama said about being a good leader and finding great talent.

Later, Obama met Jeremy Corbyn. Corbyn said they had an “excellent” 90-minute discussion. Corbyn said they talked about challenges in postindustrial societies, the power of global corporations, the increasing use of technology, and its effects. Corbyn said Obama’s comments about Britain’s membership of the EU were discussed briefly.

After the meeting, Obama joined Cameron to play golf at the Grove in Chandlers Cross, Hertfordshire. He ended the day at a dinner with the prime minister and the US ambassador, Matthew Barzun, at the ambassador’s residence, before travelling on to Germany.
