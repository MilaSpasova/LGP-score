Valdevaqueros is a beach in southern Spain. It is still clean and natural. Above the golden sand, many kites pull surfers over the waves. The beach has only a small access road. Camper vans from Germany, France, Italy, and Britain park there. Windsurfers and kitesurfers come because the wind is strong.

For many years, this beach was very different from the built-up beaches in Torremolinos and Marbella. But on 29 May, the local council in Tarifa approved a plan to build a tourist complex next to the beach. It would have 1,400 hotel rooms and 350 flats. Environmental and conservation groups say it will hurt the homes of protected animals. Many councillors say the main reason is jobs. Tarifa has 18,000 people, and 2,600 are out of work. Spain is in a very bad economic crisis, and people worry about the future of the euro.

Sebastián Galindo, a councillor from the Socialist Party, said fishing is dying because fleets are being taken apart and there are fewer fish. He said tourism is the only way out, if it is sustainable. The Socialist Party is in opposition in Tarifa, but it voted with the ruling People’s Party to approve the project.

Tarifa’s mayor, Juan Andrés Gil, did not comment. Galindo said the plan follows environmental rules. The complex would be 800 metres from the coast. The law says it must be at least 200 metres away. The law was made to stop ugly building like the mass tourism building in the 1960s and 1970s.

People against the complex say Spain already has a million empty homes, so it does not need more housing. But last week the central government suggested selling homes by giving non-Spaniards residency permits if they buy property worth at least €160,000.

The Socialist opposition in Madrid attacked this idea. Galindo said it is unfair to migrant workers who came to Spain during the boom years, many from Morocco. Morocco is only 14 km away and can be seen from Tarifa. He said the plan helps rich people more than the workers who helped Spain.

Surfers worry that new buildings in Valdevaqueros will make the famous Levant wind weaker. They also think the new resort will not attract the usual package holiday families. Henning Mayer, from Augsburg in Germany, has come there for 20 years. He said it is not really a family place. He also said that 10 years ago people talked about a new highway, but it did not happen, so he thinks building new hotels will be very hard.

Tarifa is at the southernmost tip of Spain. It is a key place between Africa and Europe, where the Mediterranean Sea meets the Atlantic. Campaigners say it is also very important for animals that migrate.

A campaign to save the beach started only hours after the council vote. It has a Facebook page. Groups supporting it include Greenpeace, the World Wide Fund for Nature, and the Spanish branch of Birdlife. Noelia Jurado, who works with multimedia, said it is like putting a shopping centre in the middle of the Alhambra. She also said the resort would be near the ancient Roman town of Baelo Claudia, and it might even cover unknown Roman ruins.

The Andalusian College of Geographers also opposes the plan. In an early study on its website, it said “free areas,” including car parks, could overlap part of Alcornocales National Park. The geographers also say the site enters two wildlife protection areas. One area in Valdevaqueros has two kinds of bats that are in danger.

Raúl Romeva, a member of the European Parliament and Vice-President of the Greens group, said money is again coming before planning laws and European environmental rules. He also said the place does not have enough water. Tarifa already has water shortages in summer in hot Andalusía. In another case, the Andalusía Supreme Court supported an appeal against plans for a different complex in the region. That other plan would have had golf courses, hotels, and luxury homes. It failed because of lack of water.

Some local people also ask why the resort must be built 10 km away, instead of on empty land near Tarifa’s old centre. The centre has white walls, narrow streets, and a 10th-century Moorish castle. Cristóbal Lobato has worked at the same beach bar in Tarifa for 30 years. He said he and other food workers agree with the complex if it creates jobs, but they do not want it to help only a few people. He said if it was in the centre of Tarifa, visitors could go to shops, tapas bars, and restaurants.

Biologist Aitor Galán, who does environmental impact studies, pointed to one of only two seaside breeding places for vultures in Europe. He said in most of Europe it would be protected, but here people want to cover it with buildings. He said they want to turn it into Benidorm, but people come for the wildlife and the wind. He said builders and mayors are using the crisis and unemployment to justify a lot of destruction.
