More people are now against Western Australia’s shark cull. Thousands of people went to beaches across Australia to ask the state’s premier to stop it. RSPCA Australia and Richard Branson, the owner of Virgin Atlantic, also spoke against it.

The policy catches and kills sharks longer than three metres. It started after what the state government called an “unprecedented” number of shark attacks on Western Australia’s coast. A 35-year-old surfer was killed in November 2013. He was the sixth person to die from a shark attack in two years.

But the Shark Attack File says Australia has had about one shark-related death each year on average for the last 50 years.

Kate Faehrmann from the Sea Shepherd Conservation Society spoke at a protest in Perth. She said the policy will not work. She said drumlines are not selective. They can catch and kill sharks of any size, and also dolphins, turtles, and other animals.

Thousands protested at Cottesloe Beach in Perth and Manly Beach in Sydney. Hundreds protested at Glenelg in south-west Adelaide, and at beaches in Victoria and Queensland. Faehrmann said the protests showed many Australians want sharks protected. Surfer Anthony Joyce, who once had his foot caught in a shark’s mouth, said killing sharks will not make a difference overall.

The state government has not said how many sharks have been killed so far. There are reports that some sharks smaller than three metres were released after being caught on drumlines. Drumlines are floating drums fixed to the sea bed, with bait on hooks under them.

Conservationists say there is no evidence the cull will reduce shark attacks on people. They also say no earlier cull used only drumlines. Researchers at the University of Western Australia say the recent shark attacks may be linked to Western Australia having the fastest-growing population in Australia, not to more sharks.

Richard Peirce from the UK charity Shark Trust said the cull will not work and could bring more predators near the coast. He said leaving drumlines in the water overnight could attract other predators, because hooked animals may be injured.

Worldwide in 2012, there were 80 unprovoked shark attacks, and seven were fatal. In comparison, humans kill nearly 100 million sharks each year.

RSPCA Australia said the cull is not justified. It said there is no evidence the attacks increased because there are more sharks. It said it fits better with changes in population and human behaviour, because more people are in the water.

Richard Branson told Fairfax Radio the policy could have the opposite effect from what was intended. He said Colin Barnett, the Western Australia Premier, may have thought it would help tourism, but Branson said it could stop people from coming to Perth and the nearby countryside. He said it may only make people worry for no good reason.
