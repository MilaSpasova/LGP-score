Do you want your child to be good at sport, join the school team, and maybe one day compete in the Olympics or the World Cup? A study says the month your child is born may matter. It found that school children born in November, or in October, are often fitter than other children in the same class.

The study said children born in November and October were fitter, stronger, and more powerful than children born in the other months. Children born in April or June were often the least fit. Dr Gavin Sandercock from the Centre for Sports and Exercise Science at Essex University said autumn-born children had a clear physical advantage.

The research tested 8,550 boys and girls aged 10 to 16 from 26 state schools in Essex. They were tested between 2007 and 2010. The tests measured stamina, handgrip strength, and lower-body power. The results showed that birth month could make a significant difference in heart fitness, muscle strength, and ability to speed up. These things are important for sport.

Children born in November were the fittest overall. They had the most stamina and power, and they were the second strongest. Children born in October were almost as fit. They were the strongest and third for power. Children born in December were close behind.

Sometimes the differences were big. Sandercock said a boy born in November could run at least 10% faster, jump 12% higher, and be 15% more powerful than a boy of the same age born in April. The paper said that choosing top athletes can depend on very small differences.

The study was published in the International Journal of Sports Medicine. When the three fitness scores were added together, children born in April were the least fit, then children born in June. Sandercock said this could mean some children do not get chosen for school teams.

The findings suggest an “autumn advantage.” Children born early in the school year are already known to do better in school, and now they also seem to do better in sport. Sandercock said this was not only because these children are older in the school year, because the fittest children were not the tallest or the heaviest.

The authors think vitamin D may explain it. Vitamin D is the “sunshine vitamin.” They think mothers of autumn-born children may get more sunlight in summer near the end of pregnancy.

John Steele from the Youth Sport Trust said the quality of a child’s first experience of PE and sport at school can be very important. He said a good start can improve skills like agility, balance, and coordination, and can help children enjoy physical activity.

UK Sport could not say if many of the 1,300 athletes it supports across 47 sports were born in November and October. Natalie Dunman, its head of performance pathways, said the differences were seen in teenagers in junior competitions, but they disappeared in adult competitions. She said many factors make a champion, and their work did not show that birth month is a key factor.
