Kenton Cool usually likes to talk a lot. But now he can hardly speak. Climbing high mountains has made his voice rough. He is now in Kathmandu, the capital of Nepal. That morning he flew down from Everest base camp.

Cool is thinking about an amazing set of climbs he finished the weekend before. Early on Saturday morning, he reached the top of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of three main summits in the Everest “horseshoe”. This horseshoe surrounds an icy valley called the Western Cwm.

Later that same day, he climbed to the top of Everest. He reached the summit in complete darkness early on Sunday. Then he and his climbing partner went on to the top of Lhotse. He reached Lhotse’s summit on Monday morning. Lhotse was the third mountain in this three-peaks challenge.

“It was a snatched opportunity,” he says. “For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. That does not take away the physical achievement of what I did. I’ve set the bar at a certain level. But whoever comes along next will move the bar further and do it without ropes or bottled oxygen.”

Sixty years after Everest was first climbed, many stories look back to Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. They talk about a more innocent time, before modern commercialism and environmental damage. I ask Cool to look forward and imagine what top climbers might do 60 years from now.

“I hate to think,” he says. But he talks about the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. In April, Steck left the mountain after what Cool calls “an altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck planned a very hard trip. He wanted to climb Everest’s west ridge, first climbed in 1963. Then he wanted to go down to the South Col. After that, he wanted to climb Lhotse right away by a new route. He planned to do all this without fixed ropes. “Ueli had been training like a machine,” Cool says. “He’s a climber in a class all his own. He’s technically brilliant, and he had also taken his physical condition to an astronomic level. It would have been amazing to see what he could have done.”

Tourism is another question. One clue is a helicopter rescue by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro’s angry words helped cause the confrontation at Camp 2. On Tuesday, Moro flew back to Everest in a high-powered helicopter. He rescued a very sick climber at 7,800 metres.

This was the highest rescue ever done on Everest. It also shows the fast growth of helicopter flights in recent years. By 2073, the mountain could even have a helipad on the South Col. Tourists could arrive there while breathing bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing rescue options for climbers and for the many trekkers who go as far as base camp.

Mountain geographer and environmentalist Alton Byers says it is not clear if the Everest region can keep handling fast-growing tourism. He says climate change and tourism together are creating new pressure on the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers in the Everest region are shrinking, and some are disappearing. This is already having a big effect. “Everywhere you go, people are talking about how there’s less water,” he says. “There’s less water for agriculture and less water for all the new lodges that are getting built.”

Byers says that in the Sherpa town of Namche Bazaar, workers are building a new five-mile pipeline. It will bring water for the growing number of tourists who want showers and flush toilets. The local stream is now polluted with human waste. It also does not provide enough water when the town is very crowded in high season. “Every village is digging a pit just beyond the houses for garbage. Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world,” he says. Human waste at base camp is now handled well and taken away in plastic barrels. But Byers says the barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley. This pit could leak into the region’s watercourses.

“These problems can be solved, but we need to get serious about it,” he says. “One climber can spend $85,000 climbing Everest. And that’s fine. But at some point we’re going to have to address these other priorities. For half a million dollars a year, you could solve most of them.”

Climate change is different and harder. Byers works with local conservation committees. They try to find and plan for the effects of climate change. Often they look for new water sources or start rainwater harvesting. Fast-growing glacial lakes are another danger. These lakes could burst and flood the Sherpa homeland. “There’s going to come a time when people are going to have to get out of their way,” he says.

Changing weather is also hurting tourism. More clouds in times that are usually clear are closing Lukla Airport more often. Lukla Airport is the gateway to the Everest region. Nepal is building a new road for 4x4s to Lukla to keep tourists and their money coming. But Byers worries about the fast spread of roads in Nepal. He says many roads are built cheaply, and this can cause serious soil erosion and landslides.

“Everest is the icon everyone knows,” he says. “It’s the canary in the coalmine that everyone understands. It’s the perfect laboratory for figuring out how to address some of these problems, like the impacts of climate change and tourism.”
