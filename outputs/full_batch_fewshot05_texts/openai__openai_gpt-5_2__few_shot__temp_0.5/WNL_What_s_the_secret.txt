How long can you hold your breath? I am trying it now. The first 30 seconds are easy. At 45 seconds I want to stop, but I keep going. For a short time it feels easier. But after one minute, I feel I cannot last much longer. My heart beats fast. I breathe out a very small amount of air, and this helps. In the end I stop, breathe out, and then take a big breath in. I keep breathing hard for a few more breaths, and my husband asks what I am doing. I reach 1 minute and 12 seconds. I am proud of myself.

Holding your breath is very important in some sports, especially freediving. In 2006, I made a BBC programme about the lungs for a series called Don’t Die Young. I met Sam Amps, the captain of the UK freedive team. At a pool in Bristol, she taught me simple exercises to help me hold my breath longer while swimming underwater. By the end, I could hold my breath for about 90 seconds, long enough to swim one width of the pool. Sam could swim three widths easily. She could hold her breath for five minutes while swimming.

I asked how she did it. She breathed very slowly for several minutes before each dive, then took one big deep breath before going under. She also said training helped her fight the strong need to breathe.

Some people say humans can hold their breath because we once lived in water during evolution. They also say humans can slow the heart and the body’s energy use to hold the breath longer. People have also linked other human features to this idea, like having little body hair, body fat under the skin, and walking on two legs. But this “aquatic ape hypothesis” is not supported by scientific evidence, and it is considered fiction.

Humans are not the only land mammals that can hold their breath. It is hard to test this in other mammals because they do not do what you ask. Studies also show that on land, the heart rate does not drop when you hold your breath. In cold water, it is different: when the face gets cold, the heart rate becomes slower in most people. But this does not prove humans came from an aquatic ape, because many air-breathing animals show the same effect. This slower heart rate is part of what is sometimes called the “mammalian diving reflex”. These body changes can help with diving, but they can also help an animal not drown.

Even if our breath-holding is not very special compared with other animals, it is now useful in one area of medicine. Radiotherapy for breast cancer uses radiation aimed very carefully at the tumour. It can take several minutes, so it is usually given in short bursts between breaths. But if a patient can keep her chest completely still for several minutes, the full dose can be given in one go, in the right place. Most people cannot hold their breath for much more than one minute. But doctors at University Hospital Birmingham did careful experiments. They found that if patients breathe oxygen-rich air before holding their breath, they can hold it for about five and a half minutes.

The reason seems to be the diaphragm. The diaphragm is the main muscle for breathing. When you breathe in, it tightens and pulls down, so your chest gets bigger and air goes into your lungs. When you hold your breath, you keep the diaphragm tight. Breathing oxygen-rich air and lowering carbon dioxide before holding your breath may help because it makes the diaphragm get tired more slowly. Also, breathing out a little air can let the diaphragm relax a bit and help you hold your breath longer, like it did for me. In the end, the diaphragm sends signals that are too strong, and you must breathe again.
