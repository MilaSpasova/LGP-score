The normal eight-hour workday is finished. The most important work is done. Many workers would think about going home now.

But for millions of Japanese employees, leaving work and being home for dinner can lead to people saying they are not loyal.

After many years of overwork, the Japanese government is thinking about a new law. The law would require workers to take at least five days of paid holiday each year.

Japanese employees have the right to about 18.5 days of paid holiday a year on average. This is only two days less than the world average. There is a minimum of ten days, and there are also 15 one-day national holidays. But in real life, few people take all their days. The labour ministry says they usually take only nine days out of 18.5. Many British workers think a two-week summer holiday is a basic right. In Japan, some workers see a four-night vacation in Hawaii as too self-indulgent.

Parliament will debate this plan in the current session. It comes after companies started encouraging employees to take naps at work to improve performance.

By the end of the decade, the government hopes the new law, if it passes, will push Japanese workers to act more like workers in Britain and France. British workers use about 20 days of paid leave on average. Workers in France take about 25 days.

Japan’s strict work culture helped make it an economic superpower. People around the world respected Japanese workers for strong commitment to their companies. But this often left little time for anything else.

Japan has a low birth rate, and the population may fall quickly. Some people blame this partly on couples not having enough time to start families. More employees get sick from stress, or even die from karoshi, which means death from overwork.

Studies suggest longer hours do not always make people more productive. But many workers still keep working long hours, influenced by the bubble years of the 1980s.

About 22% of Japanese workers work more than 49 hours a week. In the US it is 16%, and in France and Germany it is 11%, based on Japanese government data. South Korea is worse: 35%.

Erika Sekiguchi works about 14 hours a day and gives up many paid holidays. She is not even an extreme case. She is 36 and works at a trading company. In 2014, she used eight of her 20 paid vacation days. Six of those days were counted as sick leave. She said, “Nobody else uses their vacation days.”

She faces a common problem in Japan. If she takes time off to rest, people may criticize her. They may say she is leaving more committed co-workers with extra work.

Yuu Wakebe is a health ministry official who manages policy on working hours. He says he works 100 hours of overtime a month. He blames strong pressure to match co-workers, hour for hour. Wakebe said paid vacations are a worker’s right. But he also said working in Japan involves a lot of “volunteer spirit.”

Fear of being left out at work is blamed for more stress illness, early death, and suicide. Official data says about 200 people die each year from heart attacks, strokes, and other karoshi events caused by very hard work schedules.

Prime minister Shinzo Abe is not known for taking long vacations. Still, he has criticized unreasonable demands by companies that are trying to survive in a difficult global market. Abe recently said Japan’s work culture “falsely beatifies long hours”.
