Brazil had one of its biggest nights of protests in many years. More than 100,000 people went into the streets across the country. They were angry about strong police actions, bad public services, and the high costs for the World Cup.

Big protests happened in Rio de Janeiro, São Paulo, Brasilia, Belem, Belo Horizonte, Salvador, and other places. They began peacefully, but some later turned into fights with police. Some people also set cars and buses on fire.

The protests grew quickly. Earlier, smaller protests were about higher bus prices. People said the police reacted too strongly, using rubber bullets, tear gas, and violent beatings.

The rallies happened at the same time as the start of the Confederations Cup, a test event for the World Cup. Many different groups joined. They were upset about rising costs, poor public services, expensive spending on big sports events, low health care standards, and worries about inequality and corruption.

In Rio, most protests were peaceful. But some police were hurt near the city’s legislative assembly. At least one car was turned over and burned. Windows were broken at some bank offices and notary offices.

Later that night, a large crowd started several fires outside the legislative assembly. They broke windows and wrote graffiti on the walls, including “Revolution”, “Down with Paes, down with Cabral [the mayor and state governor]”, and “Hate police”. Police inside used pepper spray and maybe more. The Guardian saw one protester unconscious and bleeding badly from a wound in the upper arm.

The protesters had many different reasons. Oscar José Santos, 19, said: “We are here because we hate the government. They do nothing for us.” He was with hooded youths from the Rocinha favela.

Nadia al Husin said: “I’m an architect but I have been unemployed for six months. There must be something wrong with this country.” She held a sign asking the government to do more for education.

In Brasilia, at a much smaller rally, protesters broke through police lines and entered the high-security area of the national congress. Several climbed onto the roof.

In Belo Horizonte, police fought with protesters who tried to get past a barrier around a football stadium. The stadium was hosting a Confederations Cup match between Nigeria and Tahiti.

In Porto Alegre, protesters burned a bus. In Curitiba, protesters tried to force their way into the office of the state governor. There were also rallies in Belem, Salvador, and other places.

In São Paulo, large crowds gathered again. The week before, São Paulo had the worst clashes and the main claims of police violence. Early reports said this time the marches were peaceful.

Social networks helped spread the message. In São Paulo, where estimates were between 30,000 and 100,000 people, some carried signs saying, “We come from Facebook.”

Many protesters were young, and for many it was their first very large rally. Student Thiago Firbida said: “My generation has never experienced this. Since the dictatorship, Brazilians never bothered to take over the streets. They did not believe they had a reason to. But now Brazil is once again in crisis, with a constant rise in prices, so people are finally reacting.”

People compared the protests with rallies in Turkey and other places. A few protesters wore Guy Fawkes masks, linked with Anonymous and the Occupy Wall Street protests.

People called the protests the “vinegar revolution”, because police arrested people for carrying vinegar to reduce the effects of tear gas. They also called it the “20-cent revolution”, because of the bus price rise, and Passe Livre, because of the demand for free public transport.

Some people said the protests felt not like Brazil, but also freeing. Deli Borsari, a 53-year-old yoga instructor, said: “Our politicians need to see the strength we have as one people. Brazilians tend to be too nice sometimes – they enjoy partying rather than protesting – but something is changing.”

After much news about the costs of new and repaired stadiums, the Confederations Cup became a main focus of the protests. Before the first match in Brasilia, riot police pushed crowds away. Video showed scared Japanese supporters running away with their children. People heard shots, maybe rubber bullets or tear gas.

Another protest march, near Rio’s Maracana Stadium, also faced a strong police response.

Most rallies seemed peaceful at first, until they met the security forces. These forces are mostly organized by region.

President Dilma Rousseff accepts the protests, her aides said. Helena Chagas from the president’s office said: “The president believes peaceful protests are legitimate and proper for a democracy, and that it is natural for young people to demonstrate.”

However, the president was booed at the opening ceremony of the Confederations Cup. With the economy doing badly and social unrest growing, she faces a serious political challenge now and in 2014, when Brazil will host the World Cup and also hold a presidential election.
