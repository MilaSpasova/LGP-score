James Hamblin is a senior editor at the American magazine The Atlantic. In his series “If Our Bodies Could Talk,” he tried a challenge: he stopped showering for a time. He also took fewer showers, and when he did shower, he did not use shampoo or soap. He wanted to see what happens when we clean our bodies too much.

He found something many people have seen too. When we clean our skin a lot with soap and body wash, the skin tries harder to return to balance. Showering can remove the skin’s natural oil and bacteria. Hot water and cleaning products can make this effect stronger.

After a shower, your skin can feel tight and dry. This is because natural moisture has been washed away. Our skin also has millions of helpful bacteria, like our gut does. Frequent rubbing and scrubbing can kill these bacteria. When the bacteria grow back, the ones that cause bad smell may grow more. So showering too often may make you smell more.

When you stop using soap and stop showering so often, your skin may go through an early adjustment period that feels unpleasant. After that, the skin often returns to balance, makes less oil, and healthy bacteria grow again.

In the end, Hamblin learned what other people who avoid soap and showers have said for years: the human body can work well on its own. Dermatologists also say that using less soap can help acne and eczema.

Taking fewer showers can also help the environment. The average shower lasts seven minutes and uses 65 litres of water. That is 65 litres of clean drinking water mixed with soap and sent down the drain every day, sometimes more than once.

Clean water is becoming more important, especially as California faces another summer of drought. Clean water is one of the world’s most valuable resources, and it may soon be in short supply. There is also waste from many body wash bottles. These are good reasons to be more natural.

If this idea makes you uncomfortable, remember this: many people who reduce washing still use deodorant if they need it. Everyone’s natural smell is different, and it can change because of diet, how much water you drink, and exercise. Also, washing hands with soap is still recommended to reduce the spread of infectious diseases.

You do not need to stop all showers at once like Hamblin. But between washing all the time and never showering, there is a simple choice: reduce. Skip some showers, use less soap, and let the healthy bacteria grow.
