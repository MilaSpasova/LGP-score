The customer next to you in the line looks normal. But she is not holding a shopping list. You see handwritten notes about how the store looks and how clean it is. She is timing how fast the line moves on her phone. You might even see a small camera in her purse. You may have just seen a mystery shopper.

In the UK, about 50,000 mystery shopping trips happen every month, says the Mystery Shopping Providers Association. More people now spend money online, so the need for mystery shoppers is growing. Simon Boydell from Marketforce says that people who go into a real store want good service and an experience they cannot get online. Marketforce has more than 300,000 mystery shoppers on its list. He says clients want to check how well stores give this experience.

Jill Spencer from ABa says they send each shopper to different store locations and change them, so shoppers do not go back to the same shop within three months. Each day, shoppers often spend up to eight hours visiting five to ten stores. Then they spend another hour or two writing detailed reports about the visit. For this, they can earn up to £155 a day. They also get money back for petrol and hotel stays, and they are paid for car depreciation. Shoppers may drive up to 20,000 miles a year. Video mystery shoppers, who film visits with a hidden camera in a buttonhole or handbag, can earn more, about £300 a day.

Shoppers are usually paid back for what they buy in the stores. They may also be allowed to keep the products. Laura, a 50-year-old mystery shopper from Devon, says she is usually given £5 to £20 to spend in each store, to check the service at the till. She has been paid to visit about 7,000 shops since 2001. The purchase usually must match what the retailer wants her to check. She says she is given a story to follow, like buying from a certain department or a new product range. But inside those rules, she can often buy what she wants and keep it.

Like many full-time mystery shoppers, Laura is self-employed. She takes jobs from ABa and other companies when they are available. She usually earns £30,000 to £40,000 a year, not counting the free things she gets. She says the extra benefits make it enough to live on. But she does not do it because she loves shopping. She says she hates shopping now, and even buying a pint of milk feels painful.

Still, she likes it when she goes back to a store she checked before and sees it has improved. She says it must be because of her feedback, or they would not pay her for it. She says some retailers she visits win awards for customer service, and she thinks mystery shoppers help with that. She feels she is helping all shoppers.

But regular mystery shopping jobs that pay like Laura’s are rare. Competition is very strong. She keeps her job secret, and even her friends and family do not know who she works for. Laura is not her real name.

It is estimated that more than 500,000 people have registered as mystery shoppers in the UK. But only 10% or less get regular work each month. This has caused pay to drop a lot. Val, a 51-year-old former mystery shopper, says that before, you got a fee, money back for what you bought, and mileage. Now you often only get some help towards the purchase. She worked for 40 mystery shopping companies for almost 20 years. She stopped completely three years ago because she had bills to pay and very few jobs paid what she thought was acceptable.

Now, mystery shopping companies often use free items to motivate workers. Boydell says Marketforce shoppers usually get a couple of pounds for a visit as a small payment. At most, they pay £15 to £25, plus money back for something like a meal for two or a hotel stay. He says they do not employ shoppers directly, so they do not have to pay minimum wage.

Laura says she would go on a cruise for nothing. But she thinks companies that pay only a small fee for jobs like traveling to a restaurant and eating a meal are using people. She says she will not do those jobs anymore. But many people will. Hannah, a 41-year-old City lawyer, has done nearly 500 visits for the Mystery Dining Company in her free time with no pay and no travel expenses. As a “platinum diner,” she is often chosen for special jobs. She gets £200 meals at Michelin-starred restaurants in Mayfair, five-star hospitality at Ascot, and overnight stays at boutique hotels.

But Hannah says there is no free lunch. After each visit, she usually spends two to four hours writing detailed reports. She writes about the food quality and about specific talks with staff. She must be able to name staff, or describe them “subtly.” The hardest part is that she must remember everything while eating, because she cannot openly write notes.

She says there is a lot to remember, and sometimes it makes the experience less enjoyable. She is expected to give feedback while it is fresh, so she has had to get up at 5am to write a report before work. She says it is hard, and you must be focused, clear in your writing, and very careful about details.
