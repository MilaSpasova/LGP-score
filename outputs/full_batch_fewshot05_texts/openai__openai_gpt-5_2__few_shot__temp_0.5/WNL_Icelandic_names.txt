In Iceland, a girl can have many Icelandic names, like Aagot, Arney, Ásfríður, Baldey, Bebba, Brá, Dögg, Dimmblá, Etna, Eybjört, Frigg, Glódís, Hörn, Ingunn, Jórlaug, Obba, Sigurfljóð, Úranía, or Vagna. But she cannot be named Harriet.

Tristan Cardew said the situation is silly. He and his Icelandic wife, Kristin, are fighting a decision by the National Registry in Reykjavik. The registry will not renew the passport of their 10-year-old daughter, Harriet, because it does not accept her first name.

The registry also does not accept the name of Harriet’s 12-year-old brother, Duncan. Until now, the children travelled with passports that called them Stúlka and Drengur Cardew, which mean Girl and Boy Cardew. Cardew said the authorities are now following the law closely. The law says no official document will be given to people who do not have an approved Icelandic name.

Because of this, the family from Kópavogur could have missed their holiday in France. They asked the British embassy for an emergency UK passport, which should let them travel.

Names are very important in Iceland, a country with about 320,000 people. The phone book lists people by first name. This is because most Icelandic last names show who your father or mother is. For example, the children of Jón Einarsson might be Ólafur Jónsson and Sigríður Jónsdóttir.

The law says that the names of children born in Iceland must be sent to the National Registry within six months, unless both parents are foreign. If the name is not on an approved list of 1,853 female and 1,712 male names, the parents must ask a group called the Icelandic Naming Committee.

About 5,000 children are born in Iceland each year. The committee gets about 100 requests and rejects about half. This is based on a 1996 law meant to protect the Icelandic language. The rules say names must be able to take Icelandic grammar endings, must not go against the Icelandic language system, and must be written with normal Icelandic spelling rules.

In practice, this means names with letters not in Iceland’s 32-letter alphabet, like “c”, are not allowed. Names that cannot take the endings used in Icelandic grammar cases are also often rejected. Cardew said this was the problem with Harriet.

In recent years, more people have criticized these naming laws. In 2013, Blær Bjarkardóttir Rúnarsdottir won the right to use her name, instead of “Girl”, after a court said the earlier decision broke the Icelandic constitution. The former mayor of Reykjavik, Jón Gnarr, called the naming law “unfair, stupid and against creativity”.

The Cardews could solve Harriet’s problem by giving her an Icelandic middle name. But Cardew said it is too late and too silly. He asked, “Are they saying they don’t want us here?”
