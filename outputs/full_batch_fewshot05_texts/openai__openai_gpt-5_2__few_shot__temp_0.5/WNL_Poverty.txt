A major report warns that up to 1 billion people could still be in extreme poverty in 2030. This may happen unless countries focus on inequality and deal with social, economic, and cultural forces that stop people from escaping poverty or push them back into it.

The report is by the Chronic Poverty Advisory Network (CPAN). It says some people rise above the poverty line of $1.25 a day, but then fall back when shocks happen. These shocks can include drought, illness, and insecurity or conflict.

Using household surveys, the report found that in parts of rural Kenya and in South Africa, 30% to 40% of people who escaped poverty later fell back into it. In some areas of Ethiopia, this rose to 60% between 1999 and 2009. Even in countries doing well, like Indonesia and Vietnam, the share has been 20%.

The report gives examples. Amin, 61, from rural Bangladesh, saw his living situation slowly get worse. This was due to his and his wife’s illnesses, the cost of his son’s marriage, his father’s death, and the loss of goods such as fishing nets. Lovemore, 74, from Zimbabwe, became one of the poorest people in his village. He lost his job as a car-park attendant because of poor health, and he took in his five grandchildren after his daughters died.

Andrew Shepherd, a research fellow at the Overseas Development Institute, a partner of CPAN, and the lead author, said governments must make sure people who escape poverty stay above the poverty line for good. He said many families face “two steps forward, one step back.” He also said governments should not think the work is finished just because a person’s income reaches $1.25.

A UN high-level panel looking at a new plan after the Millennium Development Goals said ending extreme poverty by 2030 was possible. But this report says “more of the same” will not reach zero.

Extreme poverty fell from 1.9bn in 1990 to 1.2bn in 2010. Still, the report says progress in the next 15 years will be harder. It says big gains in China are unlikely to happen in the same way in other places. It also says climate-related shocks and deep poverty in parts of sub-Saharan Africa will slow progress.

The report asks for a change in how people think about poverty. It says we should focus more on the chronically poor, meaning people who are poor for many years or their whole lives. It also says we should do more to stop impoverishment, meaning people falling into poverty.

Shepherd said governments have often helped people cross the poverty line because that is easier. But they have avoided the harder work of dealing with chronic poverty.

The report says poverty reduction so far has helped the chronically poor less than it has helped people who were already close to the poverty line. It says reaching zero will not be possible unless policies put people in chronic poverty first.

It sets three connected goals, called a “zero tripod”: tackle chronic poverty, stop impoverishment, and support people so they can stay out of poverty. It also suggests three policies, which need very large global investment.

First, social assistance, which is a safety net, to help the poorest people reach a decent living standard. One example is Ethiopia’s productive safety net programme. It allowed thousands of vulnerable households to get through drought without cutting spending on education. Second, education from early childhood to the start of work, to help people escape poverty and stay out of it. The report says education is also a “portable” asset. Third, economic growth policies that help the very poorest people, so they benefit from national growth.

Shepherd said economic growth is important, but it must create decent jobs, not insecure low-paying jobs. The report mentions India’s rural employment guarantees scheme, which has raised wages in cities as well as rural areas. It also mentions South Africa extending the minimum wage to groups such as domestic workers.

The report says these actions need money. It says countries will need stronger tax systems and more tax income. It also says aid will be needed for start-up costs for social assistance, universal health coverage, and education, including scholarships for the poorest children. Shepherd said many developing countries spend less than $500 per citizen each year. He said Nigeria, even with oil wealth, spends only $650 per capita.

The report says conflict and environmental disasters must be dealt with. It mentions crises in Syria, South Sudan, and the Central African Republic. It says governments must reduce the risk of conflict and build inclusive peace settlements.

The report says making “reducing (income) inequality” a goal after 2015 would help. It says lowering or controlling inequality makes economic growth reduce poverty faster. It argues that if inequalities affecting the poorest people are addressed—such as access to land, labour markets, and power between men and women—then two goals are helped at once: reducing chronic poverty and reducing inequality.

The authors urge governments to make an inclusive national development plan and work with civil society. They say the poorest people should be represented in politics. They also say governments should address difficult social norms, such as dowries and witchcraft, that add to extreme poverty. They say this may mean strongly challenging the current way things are done.
