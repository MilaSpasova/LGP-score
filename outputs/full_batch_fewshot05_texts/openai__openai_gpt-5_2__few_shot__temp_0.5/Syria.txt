It is hard to know exactly where the noise comes from, but everyone in Damascus can hear it. Day and night, you hear the dull thuds and booms of artillery, rockets, or planes hitting rebel positions. The war is getting closer to Syria’s capital. But a little more than two years into the Syrian crisis—the longest and bloodiest of the Arab uprisings—many people in Damascus have started to ignore the sound of death and destruction. This has become the new normal.

Over the weekend, men smoked water pipes in a park with palm trees. Children played near the flowers. Couples talked on benches. At the same time, a loud blast could be heard a few miles away. Smoke rose between the minarets of a nearby Ottoman-era mosque. No one seemed to notice.

"Actually you do get used to it after a while," said George, an IT technician from a village on the coast. "But you never know exactly what they are hitting." Later, people often learn from video clips posted by opposition media outlets on YouTube.

The background noise is even more upsetting because the government tries hard to act like life is normal. "As you can see, everything here is fine but we have to hit the terrorists, these extremists," an army officer said. An official said his way home has been attacked by rebels in Daraya. He said, "If I was afraid, I would just shut my door and stay inside. I have to work and I am not afraid. If I don’t defend my country, who will?"

In private, ordinary citizens speak differently. In the center of town, a shopkeeper said sadly that his baby daughter cries when she hears shelling. Zeina, a student in her twenties, is afraid she is getting used to suffering—and maybe to danger too. "In the beginning, when there started to be explosions, I used to have nightmares," she said. "Now I can sleep through anything."

The danger is also getting closer to home. In Sabaa Bahrat Square, which was supposed to be the safest part of Damascus, a car bomb exploded. It left a blackened concrete front, broken windows, twisted metal, and damage to the Syrian Central Bank next door.

Notices of mourning for two of the 15 victims—Muhammad al-Sufi and Manal al-Tahan—are on a wall across the street. Scruffy militiamen with machine guns walk around the square. The square is often used for televised pro-regime rallies, with civil servants brought in large numbers to chant slogans under huge banners of President Bashar al-Assad.

That bombing was not the worst in Damascus as the situation has gotten worse. In February, 80 people, including schoolchildren, reportedly died near the ruling Ba’ath Party headquarters in Mazraa. The crater is still there. It is marked by a very large patch of new asphalt on the main road going north. "I live nearby but luckily I wasn’t there," said Munir, a university lecturer.

Mortar bombs, fired from rebel-held areas that are now close enough to reach the city, have become a scary new thing. These bombs killed 15 students in a university cafeteria on 28 March. The target was thought to be a government building.

Security has become tighter since the very bad bombing of the national security crisis cell in July 2012, when four of Assad’s most senior aides were killed. Concrete blast barriers, often painted in the Syrian flag’s black, red, and white, now protect many official buildings, not only military or defense places. The Iranian Embassy in Mezze, with a turquoise mosaic front that looks like a glimpse of Isfahan or Shiraz, now looks like a fortress. "The regime did manage to set up a ring of steel round Damascus," a foreign diplomat said. "But for whatever reason the perimeter is starting to be punctured and that brings home the reality of the war."

Because of all this, moving around the city is difficult, unpredictable, and slow. This is another part of the new normal in a nervous city. Checkpoints on main roads force cars into lines for ID checks and bag searches. Soldiers use handheld explosive detectors to try to stop future bombers. Only drivers with an official security clearance can use special fast lanes to avoid the long wait.

Many people keep thinking about one question: will there be a battle for Damascus—the world’s oldest continually inhabited city, as guidebooks say—like the battle that has badly damaged Aleppo? Some parts of the city already feel like a war zone. The richest hotel feels empty, although many rooms are used as offices by international agencies because the crisis is getting worse. Blue helmets and flak jackets are piled on Persian carpets in a decorated reception room. White UN vehicles are parked behind blast barriers outside. The streets become empty soon after 9pm.

Some people think the fight for Syria’s capital is coming, but not yet—maybe in the summer, some predict, when the rebels have strengthened their gains in the south. Others say neither side is likely to win clearly and they hope for a political solution forced from abroad. But few people here expect things to get better.
