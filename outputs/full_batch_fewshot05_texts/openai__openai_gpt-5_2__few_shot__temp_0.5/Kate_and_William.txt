On Tuesday, the Duke and Duchess of Cambridge won the first step in a privacy case. A French magazine was banned from selling or using again photos of the couple at a private chateau in Provence.

But the case is not finished. French prosecutors must now decide if they will start a criminal case against the magazine editor and the photographer or photographers. The photos showed the duchess sunbathing topless while on holiday in the south of France.

A court in Nanterre, near Paris, ordered the gossip magazine Closer to give the digital photo files within 24 hours. The court also stopped the magazine from sharing the photos again, including on its website and tablet app.

The ruling only applies to Mondadori Magazines France, the publisher of Closer. It must pay €2,000 in legal costs. The magazine will face a €10,000 fine for each day it does not follow the order. The couple did not ask for damages.

The court said the photos were very intrusive. They were taken at a private home, partly showing the couple naked, and the couple could reasonably think they were protected from people passing by.

The judges also said that in the future, every photo published in France by Mondadori would bring a €10,000 fine for each breach.

However, the ruling talks only about the 14 photos that were already published. The editor of Closer suggested she has other, more intimate photos.

St James’s Palace said the couple welcome the judge’s decision. A source said they believed the law was broken and they had a right to privacy. Maud Sobel, their lawyer in Paris, called it a wonderful decision and said they had been vindicated.

The couple also want a criminal case for breach of privacy. The prosecutor must decide who to charge, because the complaint names “persons unknown”. The couple are believed to want action against the editor of Closer, which published the photos on Friday, and whoever took the pictures at the chateau. The chateau belongs to Lord Linley, the son of the late Princess Margaret.

Paris police started a preliminary investigation on Tuesday. In French law, breach of privacy can lead to up to one year in prison and a €45,000 fine.

The couple hope this will send a message and stop paparazzi in the future.

Their lawyers did not ask for Closer magazines to be taken off shop shelves.

On Saturday, the Irish Daily Star published the photos. Its editor was suspended on Monday night while an internal investigation happens. On Monday, the Italian celebrity magazine Chi, also owned by Mondadori, released a special issue with 26 pages of the photos.

The couple’s lawyer, Aurélien Hamelle, said it was important to block the very intimate photos of the duchess without her bikini top. He said she is a young woman, not an object.

Delphine Pando, speaking for Closer, said the legal action was an exaggerated response. She said the magazine could not control resale because it did not own the original photos.

On eBay, people were selling copies of Closer. One sold for £31.01. eBay later removed all listings after strong feedback from its community.
