Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8 April 2014, she told people about a special moment. She received a message in a bottle. Her grandfather had thrown it into the Baltic Sea 101 years earlier. People think it is the oldest message in a bottle in the world. A museum in Germany gave it to her and is showing it now.

Erdmann is 62. She said it was very surprising. A man came to her door and said he had mail from her grandfather. He said a bottle had been found, and the card inside had her grandfather’s name. The visitor was a family history researcher. He found her in Berlin after the letter was given to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg.

The bottle was a brown beer bottle. It had been in the water for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it in his catch in the Baltic Sea near the city of Kiel. Holger von Neuhoff, a museum curator, said this was the oldest bottled message he had seen. He said the museum has older documents without bottles, but this bottle and document together are the oldest right now. He said it is in very good condition.

Researchers think Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913. He was hiking with a nature group. He was 20 years old then.

Much of the postcard cannot be read. But the Berlin address on the front can be read. The writer also asked the finder to send the note to his home address.

Erdmann said her grandfather also put two stamps in the bottle, so the finder would not have to pay. But he did not think it would take 101 years.

Erdmann said the message made her emotional. She did not know her grandfather because he died at age 54, six years before she was born.

She said she knew little about him, but learned he was a writer. She said he was open-minded and believed in freedom and respect. She said he helped young people and later traveled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful because she could see where her family came from.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and traveling, and she is open-minded too. She said what he taught his two daughters, her mother taught her, and she taught it to her sons.

She was happy to get the message, but she hopes people today will not throw message bottles into the sea. She said the sea already has too many bottles and too much rubbish.

The message and bottle will be on display at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until early May 2014. After that, experts will try to read the rest of the text. It is not clear what will happen to the bottle later, but Erdmann hopes it will stay in the museum.

She said they want to show photos with the bottle, so visitors can see the young man who threw it into the water.
