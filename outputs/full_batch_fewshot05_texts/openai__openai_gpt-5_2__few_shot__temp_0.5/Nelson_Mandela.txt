Nelson Mandela, a famous leader in Africa’s fight for freedom, has died at age 95.

South Africa’s first black president died at home in Johannesburg. He was with his family. For years, his health had been getting worse, so he stayed out of public life.

The current president, Jacob Zuma, told the country on TV. He said Mandela died at about 8.50pm local time.

Zuma said the nation was very sad. He said Mandela brought South Africans together. Zuma said Mandela would have a state funeral, and flags would fly at half-mast.

Archbishop Desmond Tutu led a memorial service in Cape Town. He said South Africa should try to be like Mandela.

Leaders around the world also spoke about Mandela. US president Barack Obama called him by his clan name, Madiba. Obama said Mandela gave up his own freedom for others. He said Mandela went from prisoner to president, and showed that people and countries can change for the better. Obama also said Mandela gave up power peacefully and tried to reconcile with the people who jailed him.

UK prime minister David Cameron called Mandela “a hero of our time”.

FW de Klerk, the South African president who freed Mandela and shared the Nobel Peace Prize with him in 1993, said the news was very sad. He said Mandela believed in reconciliation and brought people together.

In South Africa, people gathered in the streets to remember Mandela. In Soweto, people sang and danced near the house where he once lived. Some people wore South African flags and the green, yellow, and black colours of Mandela’s party, the African National Congress (ANC).

Mandela’s death has made South Africa mourn and think deeply, almost 20 years after he led the country from racial apartheid to an inclusive democracy.

People around the world also feel his death. Many saw him as a great statesman and a moral leader, like Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi and Martin Luther King.

After 27 years in prison, including 18 years on Robben Island, Mandela chose forgiveness. When South Africa faced possible civil war, he tried to reconcile with the white minority and build a new democracy.

He led the ANC to victory in South Africa’s first multiracial election in 1994. After one term, he stepped down by choice.

Mandela was born Rolihlahla Dalibhunga on 18 July, 1918, in a small village in the Eastern Cape. A teacher gave him the English name Nelson.

He joined the ANC in 1943 and helped start its youth league. In 1952, he started South Africa’s first black law firm with Oliver Tambo.

He liked boxing and he liked women. He once joked that he could not help it if women noticed him.

In 1960, the ANC was banned, and Mandela went underground. After the Sharpeville massacre, where police shot dead 69 black protesters, Mandela decided to begin an armed struggle. He was arrested and later charged with sabotage and trying to overthrow the government.

At the Rivonia trial in 1964, he defended himself. He said he wanted a democratic and free society where everyone could live together with equal opportunities. He said he was prepared to die for this ideal.

He was not sentenced to death, but he was sentenced to life in prison. The ANC had to reorganize. Unrest grew in townships, and international pressure against apartheid slowly increased.

In 1990, FW de Klerk lifted the ban on the ANC, and Mandela was released from prison. People around the world celebrated.

Mandela became president. Many people around the world supported him, but there were also problems. After leaving main politics in 1999, he said he should have acted sooner against the spread of HIV and Aids in South Africa.

Desmond Tutu said Mandela’s warmth, his listening, and his empathy helped him rise above race and class. He said Mandela helped restore faith in Africa and Africans.

Mandela sometimes appeared at ANC events and attended the inauguration of Jacob Zuma. His 91st birthday became the first yearly “Mandela Day” in his honour.

Mandela was married three times. He had six children, 17 grandchildren, and 14 great-grandchildren.
