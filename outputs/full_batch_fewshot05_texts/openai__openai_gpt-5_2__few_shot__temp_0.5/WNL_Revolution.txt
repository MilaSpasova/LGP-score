Rebecka Singerer got tired of hearing people say the beer she wanted was “too dark and too strong” and that she should drink something sweeter.

She said, “No, I don’t want a fruit beer. Women can drink whatever we want.”

Singerer works as a childminder. She joined FemAle in Gothenburg. FemAle is a group of women who like beer. They helped launch Sweden’s first beer made by women.

The beer is called We Can Do It. It is a bottled pale ale. It is now sold in stores across Sweden. The label shows Rosie the Riveter, from a US propaganda campaign in the Second World War. Rosie later became a symbol of women’s power at work.

FemAle was started by Elin Carlsson, 25. She paints cars at the Volvo factory outside Gothenburg. She says, “We Can Do It is not a female beer. It is a beer brewed by women that anyone can drink. It’s not about feminism. It’s about equality. We wanted to show we can do it.”

FemAle faces old prejudice in the beer world. Some big breweries have tried to sell beer to women by making it “pink” and smaller. Carlsberg’s Eve and Copenhagen beers, Foster’s Radler, and Coors’s Animée were lighter, flavored, and even “bloat-resistant” beers. They did not sell well.

FemAle does something different. They hold women-only tastings. This lets women try new flavors and styles they might not try before. The group says this is a way to get more girls into the beer world. They say, “Bring your mother, sister, girlfriend, aunt and grandmother so we can all learn more about beer.”

FemAle began because the women kept meeting each other at beer festivals.

The idea for We Can Do It came from Felicia Nordström, who works in a bar. She was tired of men telling her, “What do you know about beer, sweetie?” She talked to FemAle, and they worked with Ocean, a local independent micro-brewery. One weekend they made the recipe. The next weekend they brewed 1,600 litres.

Thomas Bingebo, the head brewer at Ocean, says, “This is not a beer aimed at women. It’s our hoppiest brew. When big breweries target women, it usually fails. This is completely different.”

The first batch of We Can Do It sold out very fast. Other breweries have asked FemAle if they can make new beers together.

We Can Do It uses three malts: Maris Otter, Amber, and a Thomas Fawcett wheat malt. It uses two hops: Galaxy and Cascade. Its bitterness level (IBU) is 65, and its alcohol content is 4.6%.

Carlsson says, “Women choose wine because they don’t know much about beer. They don’t know what to order. We open up new worlds to them.”

Singerer, 38, says she did not like stout before. At that time, she only drank Pripps, a light Swedish lager. Now she says Guinness tastes like water to her. She says some imperial stouts taste like biscotti dipped in espresso.

The women are part of a big increase in brewing in Sweden. Sweden is getting more interested in craft ales, in bottles and on draught. The women say the usual stor stark lager is now almost gone in Gothenburg. Many pubs and bars now offer many specialist beers instead of big brands.

Emma Henriksson, 22, a FemAle member who works at a garden equipment company, says, “All the girls are different. There is no typical woman beer-lover. Anyone can do it.”

Singerer adds, “Every pub wants to learn how to reach women. And Elin has found the way. It’s awesome. We feel so proud.”
