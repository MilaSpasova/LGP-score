This is life for a modern chemist: you wait a long time for a new element, and then four appear at the same time. Researchers in Japan, Russia, and the US discovered four new elements. These are the first new elements added to the periodic table since 2011, when elements 114 and 116 were added. The new elements live for a very short time and are very radioactive. They complete the seventh row of the periodic table and make science textbooks around the world out of date.

The International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry (IUPAC) is based in the US. It is the world group that controls chemical names, terms, and measurements. IUPAC confirmed the elements on 30 December 2015, after studying research going back to 2004. The scientists who found the elements must now choose official names. For now, the elements have long Latin-style temporary names: ununtrium, ununpentium, ununseptium, and ununoctium. These names match their atomic numbers: 113, 115, 117, and 118. The atomic number is the number of protons in the nucleus of an atom.

IUPAC said a Russian-American team at the Joint Institute for Nuclear Research in Dubna and Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory in California gave enough evidence for elements 115, 117, and 118. IUPAC gave credit for element 113 to a team from the RIKEN Institute in Japan, even though the Russians and Americans also claimed it. This means Japan becomes the first Asian country to name an element. Under IUPAC rules, new elements can be named after myths, minerals, a place or country, or a scientist.

When elements 114 and 116 got official names in 2012, scientists chose flerovium and livermorium. They were named after the Flerov Lab at Dubna’s Joint Institute for Research and the Lawrence Livermore Lab in the US, where the elements were found. Kosuke Morita, who led the RIKEN research, said his team plans to look for element 119 and beyond. Jan Reedijk, president of the Inorganic Chemistry Division of IUPAC, said the chemistry community is eager to see the table completed down to the seventh row.

The Japanese team is believed to be thinking about three possible names for ununtrium: japonium, rikenium, and nishinarium. Nishinarium would be named after the Nishina Center for Accelerator-Based Science, where the element was found. Polly Arnold, a chemistry professor at Edinburgh University, said they have probably been thinking about names for some time. She said this work is very careful and slow. She said it helps scientists improve their models and understand radioactive decay. If we understand decay better, we may deal better with nuclear waste and other real-world problems. She also said it can lead to new technology for building the equipment used to make these observations.

The scientists also must suggest two-letter symbols for the elements. After IUPAC gets the suggestions, they will be open for public review for five months. This lets scientists and other people object. In 1996, the symbol Cp was suggested for copernicium (element 112), but it was changed to Cn because scientists said Cp already meant another substance.

To find the elements, researchers at the three labs crashed lighter nuclei into each other. Then they looked for special radioactive decays that should come from the new elements. Ununtrium and ununpentium are thought to be metals. Ununseptium could be a metalloid, meaning it has some metal-like properties. Ununoctium may be a noble gas, like other group-18 elements such as helium, neon, and argon. It is hard to be sure because only a few atoms of each element have ever been made.

Paul Karol, the chair of the IUPAC panel that confirmed the elements, said the results will mostly help nuclear theorists. They will use them to understand the structure and stability of very heavy nuclei. Experiments are also looking for the possible “island of stability” at or near element 120, or perhaps 126. Those elements might live long enough for scientists to study their chemistry in detail. Any practical uses for the new elements, if they exist, are far in the future because they are very hard to make.
