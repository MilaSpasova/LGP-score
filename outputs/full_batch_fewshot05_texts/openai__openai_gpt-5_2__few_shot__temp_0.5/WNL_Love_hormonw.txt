US researchers say a nasal spray with the “love hormone” oxytocin may help children with autism handle social situations better. Brain scans of children with autism spectrum disorder showed that one dose changed how their brains reacted to facial expressions. This change could make social contact feel more natural and rewarding.

The researchers said that taking oxytocin for a period of time might help behavioural therapies work better. These therapies already help people with autism learn how to cope in social situations. Kevin Pelphrey, director of the Child Neuroscience Lab at Yale University, said that over time children might respond more appropriately in social situations, want to interact more, make more eye contact, and talk more.

Autism is a developmental disorder found in more than 1 in 100 people. It affects people in different ways, but it often causes problems with social interaction and communication. There is not yet an established treatment for the social problems caused by autism.

Researchers at Yale studied oxytocin because it is important for bonding and trust. But results in studies have been mixed. One recent study found no clear benefit when young people took oxytocin for several days. Pelphrey said oxytocin might help the brain learn from social experiences, and it may work best when used together with therapies that encourage social engagement. He said the study shows oxytocin affects the brain and might act like a “social enhancer” when combined with behavioural treatment.

The scientists used functional MRI to scan the brains of 17 young people with autism, aged 8 to 16. During the scan, they looked at pictures of cars or at the eyes of people showing different emotions. The scans were done 45 minutes after the youths breathed in a placebo or oxytocin through a nasal spray.

The scans showed that after oxytocin, the brain’s reward system acted more like usual. It was more active when looking at faces and less active when looking at cars. The study was published in the journal Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences in the United States.

Pelphrey told the Guardian that if other studies find the same result, oxytocin might help with a key problem in autism: social motivation.

But he warned it is too early to use oxytocin to treat social difficulties in autism. He also warned people not to buy oxytocin online. He said there is no way to know what they would be buying, and this is not a ready treatment. It needs more follow-up.

Simon Baron-Cohen, director of the Autism Research Centre at Cambridge University, said this is an important new study because it shows changes in brain activity in key brain areas involved in social thinking in autism after oxytocin is given.

One surprising result was that the oxytocin spray did not improve performance on the social thinking task. It is also not clear if oxytocin helps only people with autism or if it has unwanted side effects. Also, the effects last only about 45 minutes, so it may be hard to use as a treatment.

Simon Baron-Cohen said the study has strong reasons to be taken seriously, but more research is needed. Doctors should be careful about using this hormone in clinics until we know much more, from much larger studies.

Uta Frith at University College London said the study suggests oxytocin may make faces more interesting, shown by higher activity in brain areas linked to reward. But she said this is only seen in brain activity and not in behaviour. Showing a change in behaviour will be very important if a nasal spray treatment is to be useful.
