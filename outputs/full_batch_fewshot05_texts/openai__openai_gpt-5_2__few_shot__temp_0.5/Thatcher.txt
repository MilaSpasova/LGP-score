Margaret Thatcher has died. She was one of the most powerful British prime ministers since Winston Churchill in 1940. She supported free market economic ideas in the late 20th century. The British government said she would have a ceremonial funeral with military honours at St Paul’s Cathedral.

Prime minister David Cameron said he was very sad. He said Britain had lost a great leader, a great prime minister, and a great Briton. He said she was Britain’s first woman prime minister. He said she did not only lead the country, but saved it, and that she would be remembered as the greatest British prime minister in peacetime.

President Barack Obama also spoke about her. He said many Americans would remember her working closely with President Reagan. He said she showed that people can shape history with strong moral beliefs, courage, and a strong will.

Lady Thatcher was the first woman elected to lead a major western state. She served 11 years without a break at No 10 Downing Street, the longest time for a prime minister since 1827.

Thatcher was 87. Her health had been getting worse for some years, and she had dementia. After several small strokes in 2002, she stopped appearing in public.

Many people across politics praised her after her death.

The Labour Party said it would stop campaigning in local elections as a sign of respect. Labour leader Ed Miliband said she was a unique person who changed politics for a whole generation. He said Labour disagreed with many of her actions and she was always controversial. But he said people can disagree and still respect her achievements and her personal strength.

Deputy prime minister Nick Clegg said she was one of the most important people in modern British politics. He said that whatever your politics, no one can deny she made a special and lasting mark on the country.

Former Conservative prime minister Sir John Major said she would be remembered for courage and determination in politics, and for kindness and generosity in private life.

Thatcher was known as the “Iron Lady”. She was an important ally of US president Ronald Reagan during the cold war. In the final conflict with the Soviet Union, the Soviet Union later broke up under reforms led by Mikhail Gorbachev. Thatcher said she could “do business” with Gorbachev. Many ordinary voters in former Soviet bloc states saw her as a strong supporter of their freedom. Many people in the US also shared this view, but many people in Britain and some EU partners did not.

Before 1975, Thatcher was a mid-level Conservative politician. In 1975 she became party leader unexpectedly. Within 10 years she was known worldwide. Some people admired her and some hated her. People talked about her for her pro-market changes at home and her hard foreign policy. This included her long battle with the IRA. In 1984 the IRA almost killed her with a bomb at the Grand Hotel in Brighton.

In Britain, “Thatcherism” included selling state-owned industries to private companies. The word “privatization” became common in many countries. It also included defeating strong trade unions, especially the National Union of Miners. The miners’ strike in 1984–85 lasted a year and was very bitter.

Britain was also getting new money from North Sea oil. This gave Thatcher more freedom to change the old industrial economy. She also used this power to defeat her enemies, including some moderate people in her own party.

In the European Union, she often used strong words to get a British rebate, which she called “my money”. She was less successful in stopping what she saw as the European Commission becoming too powerful. This was especially true when the Commission was led by the French socialist Jacques Delors.

Some tabloid newspapers supported her strongly. Later, the British economy recovered from a severe recession. Her policies were meant to control the unions and stop inflation. For a short time, she seemed impossible to defeat.

But later her power was less controlled. Some allies were defeated or retired, and she made mistakes. She became more unpopular. Sir Geoffrey Howe, called her deputy, finally broke with her, mainly about Europe. His resignation speech led to a challenge for her leadership. Thatcher stepped down and John Major replaced her, instead of risking losing in a vote.

After she retired, she wrote very successful memoirs in two volumes. She also worked hard to support the Thatcher Foundation, which tried to spread her values around the world.
