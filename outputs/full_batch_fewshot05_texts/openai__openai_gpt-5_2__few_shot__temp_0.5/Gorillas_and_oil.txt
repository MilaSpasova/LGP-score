Virunga National Park in DR Congo is home to rare mountain gorillas. A WWF report says the park could bring DR Congo $400m a year from tourism, hydropower, and carbon credits. But a British company wants to look for oil there.

WWF says that if oil is taken from this UNESCO World Heritage Site, it could cause very serious pollution and long-term conflict in a region that is already unstable.

SOCO International is the only company that wants to explore for oil inside Virunga park. SOCO says it would work only in an area called Block V and would not harm the gorillas.

SOCO chairman Rui de Sousa said SOCO cares about the environment in Virunga. He said oil companies are still important for the world’s energy supply, and a successful oil project could improve a whole country’s economy and social well-being. He also said the park has been getting worse for many years and is below World Heritage standards, and that development might help change this.

Raymond Lumbuenamo, WWF’s country director in DR Congo, said security in and near the park would get worse if SOCO continues. He said the situation is already bad. The UN is involved with fighting units, and the M23 rebel force is inside the park. He said oil would increase conflict and could bring sabotage. He warned the park could become like the Niger Delta.

He also said the area is very crowded, with over 350 people per square kilometre. If oil takes some land, it puts more pressure on the rest. He said oil would not create many jobs, but many people would still come looking for work.

WWF also fears the area has earthquakes. A volcano in the park could erupt again, damage oil equipment, and cause oil spills in the lakes. Lumbuenamo said Virunga’s natural resources should help the Congolese people, not foreign oil prospectors. He said the country needs sustainable economic development. He said protecting the park is the best choice, because once oil drilling starts, it cannot be undone.

Lumbuenamo said the gorillas are safe now, but it is unlikely the park can earn $400m a year soon because security is so bad.

WWF says the park’s ecosystems could support hydropower, fishing, and ecotourism. They could also help give safe water supplies, control climate, and stop soil erosion.

Virunga is Africa’s oldest and most diverse park. It has over 3,000 kinds of animals. But many very poor people now live there, including many who fled after the Rwanda massacre in 1994.

The report says the park could support about 45,000 permanent jobs. It also says people around the world gain value from knowing the park is well managed and safe for future generations.

WWF says plans to explore for oil and use oil reserves could put Virunga’s value at risk. Lasse Gustavsson of WWF International said WWF will not stop until Virunga is safe from a possible environmental disaster. He said Virunga has snow fields and lava fields, but it should not have oil fields.

The UNESCO World Heritage Committee asked for all Virunga oil permits to be cancelled. It also asked Total SA and SOCO International plc not to explore in World Heritage Sites. Total said it will respect Virunga’s current boundary. This leaves UK-based SOCO as the only oil company that still plans to explore inside the park.
