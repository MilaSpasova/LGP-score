Benjamin Carle is 26 years old. He tried to use only products made in France. After the test, an auditor said he was 96.9% “made in France”. He was not 100% because of six Ikea forks, a Chinese guitar, and wall paint with no clear origin.

Carle started this project in 2013 for a television documentary. He wanted to see if people could really do what the Minister for Economic Renewal, Arnaud Montebourg, asked: buy French products to help French industry.

For ten months, Carle tried to live with only French-made things. He had three rules: eat only food produced in France, avoid foreign-made goods, and live on €1,800 a month.

At the start, he learned that only 4.5% of the things in his flat were made in France. Many things had to go, like lightbulbs from China and green beans from Kenya. Removal men took away many items, and his home became almost empty.

He had to give up many products: his smartphone, television, and refrigerator (made in China); his glasses (Italian); his underpants (Moroccan); his morning coffee (Guatemalan); and his David Bowie music (British). His girlfriend Anaïs and his cat Loon stayed with him.

Because France makes no refrigerators, he had no fridge. He cooled food on the window ledge. He also had no nail clippers, so he cut his toenails with a penknife.

He replaced his clothes with French-made ones, but they were more expensive. For example: underpants (€26), socks (€9), a polo shirt (€75), and espadrille sandals (€26). He could not buy jeans because none are made in France.

He spent a lot of time looking for 100% French-made products in supermarkets. He learned to cook seasonal fruit and vegetables grown in France. He used what he said was the last toothbrush made in France, from a company in Picardie with 29 workers. He washed small clothes by hand until he found what he said was the last French-made washing machine, but it did not fit under his kitchen counter.

Going out with friends was hard. He could not watch American films, drink Belgian beer, or eat sushi or pizza. At home, he had no sofa for the first few months and no television. He listened to Michel Sardou and read French novels. French wine was allowed, and French-Canadian singer Céline Dion was allowed. But his advisers said he should not listen to French bands like Daft Punk because they sing in English.

He could not use his British-made bicycle. He also could not use a French car, because the affordable Peugeot, Renault, and Citroën models were mostly made overseas. So he bought an orange Mobylette moped that gave off bad fumes.

Later, he replaced his computer with a Qooq, a recipe tablet that connects slowly to the internet. He replaced his iPhone with an old Sagem phone. In the documentary, he smashes his iPhone with a new French-made Tefal saucepan. His girlfriend shouts, “Are you crazy? Those are new pans!”

Carle said his goal was to “save the French economy. After all, I like Mission Impossible”. He said the experiment was partly serious and partly a joke. He even asked a French language expert if he should use the word “cool”. He was told to use “chouette” instead.

Carle learned that France does not make televisions or refrigerators (except wine coolers), but it does make aeroplane seats and windmills. He said that would not help in his apartment.

At the end, Carle took a bank loan to buy furniture and clothes again. The auditor declared him 96.9% “made in France”, and Montebourg came to give him a medal.

Carle said it is not fully possible, or even good, to live 100% “made in France”, especially for new technology. He said the point was not French nationalism. He wanted people to think about how they buy things, in all countries. He said that if people want to save jobs and industries, they can support them by their choices.

He said a T-shirt is more expensive in France, but he can be sure workers are correctly paid and have good working conditions. He said he cannot be sure about a cheaper T-shirt made in Asia or Morocco. He also said it is hypocritical to blame capitalists for economic decline if consumers could do more.

Carle said he hopes to keep supporting French producers, but not 100%, because finding everything is too much work.

When the experiment ended, he invited friends over. They had a plate of cheese and listened to David Bowie’s album Aladdin Sane. He said it was hard to invite people before because there was nowhere to sit, but he would choose Bowie over a sofa any day.
