“Some people are MOOC junkies,” says Dr Ben Brabon from Edgehill University. “They take MOOCs because they are free and they like being there.” His massive open online course (MOOC) about vampire fiction is one of only two accredited MOOCs in the UK. Brabon is not insulting people. He is saying that some people join for different reasons. MOOCs have no entry rules and no fees, so people may act differently from students in a normal university course.

MOOCs are seen as a new way to make higher education available to more people. A lot of venture capital money is going into online platforms. These platforms can give more advanced and interactive lessons to very large groups, from hundreds to tens of thousands of people. Investors want to find a way to make MOOCs earn money. So far, universities have paid for top teachers to design the courses and spend time teaching online. One possible way to earn money is to use the data that shows how, why, and when millions of people join, use the materials, hand in work, message each other, and leave the course.

A big problem is that nobody is sure who MOOCs are mainly for. Are they for universities that want to attract fee-paying international postgraduate students by showing their best programmes online? Are they for students in developing countries who want access to first-world universities? Are they for workers who want more job knowledge? Are they for people without qualifications who want a bridge to higher education? Or are they for hobby learners who just want to learn something they like?

Brabon says MOOCs often start with many learners, but few finish. In his vampire fiction course, 1,000 people enrolled and 31 finished. He says almost all who finished already had a first degree or education at degree level. So MOOCs may not yet be reaching new groups of people.

David Kernohan, programme manager for eLearning Innovation at Jisc, agrees that online learning is different and needs strong study skills. He says MOOCs have very little support. Students often do not get personal attention. Instead, they get peer support, for example in online discussion forums. This can make MOOCs hard or unattractive for people without high-level qualifications. But he says MOOCs could be a very good tool for continuing education.

At a time when part-time student numbers have fallen sharply and degree prices have risen, could free open courses offer a new path to university-level learning? Could a whole degree be taught through MOOCs? Brabon says he does not think that is how MOOCs work. He suggests a blended approach, with both campus study and a MOOC. He also says it might be possible to create a global degree, with students taking courses from universities across the world.

But that is for the future. For now, Brabon says employers will not care much about a MOOC completion certificate if there is no quality assurance for the course content or the marking standards. So accreditation is now the main challenge. Brabon is on a Quality Assurance Agency working group that is trying to agree on standards and marking.

Some people like the idea that MOOCs can bring first-world teaching to students in less developed countries. But others are cynical. They say universities might use MOOCs to advertise their on-campus courses to more students from outside the EU, who can be lucrative even if they are not always wealthy.

Mike Sharples, chair of Educational Technology, does not accept that cynical view. He says MOOCs are mainly a way to show and share a university’s best teaching, and to get interaction and feedback from students around the world. He says recruiting international students is only a secondary goal, though MOOCs can be smart marketing. He says that if 20,000 people sign up to a MOOC, you only need 20 of them to enrol later to run a master’s.

Matthew Poyiadgi, managing director at Pearson VUE, says worries about whether UK universities are being patronizing to developing countries may soon not matter. He says British universities may have no choice in the future. He warns that they may feel, “if we don’t have a presence in China, then we’ll get left behind.”

Sharples adds that in South America, China, and countries in Africa, many people want to learn, and some of the world’s best courses are offered online. He says that if people truly love learning, then why not? But he says the real challenge is to let those countries not only use and study MOOCs, but also to create them.
