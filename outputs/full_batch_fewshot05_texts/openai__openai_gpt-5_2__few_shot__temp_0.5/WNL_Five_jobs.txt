The start of the year can be a bad time for work. The next holiday feels far away. It is dark in the morning and when you get home. If you do not like your job, you may want to update your CV and look for a new one. But before you search job ads, try to think about the bigger picture. We asked five people with unusual jobs how much they earn, what is worst, and why they like their work.

1. Dog-food taster
Job: Taste dog food to check the quality for a premium brand.
What they do: They open tins from each new batch of dog or cat food, smell it, and eat it. Philip Wells, chief taster for Lily’s Kitchen, says taste helps check that the ingredients are balanced. Tasting also helps them notice small differences in how the food is cooked, especially dry food.
Pay: About £20,000 for an entry-level job in the quality department. Wells says £50,000 or more is possible for an experienced technical director. This person may also create new recipes and advise the business on technical and legal rules.
Worst part: Deadlines. Wells says he likes the food. The meat must come from animals approved for people to eat, under the Animal Feed Regulations 2010. He says the company uses “human-grade freshly prepared raw food”. He also says some other pet foods smell so bad that the smell alone can make him feel sick when he does market research.
Good part: Every day is different. Wells feels happy that his work can help pets be healthier. He also says another key taster is Lily, the border terrier.

2. Hygiene technician
Job: Disinfect places that may have dangerous biological material.
What they do: They clean crime scenes, road accidents, and suicides. They also clear houses of hoarders, with rubbish, rats, and excrement. Richard Lewis, a hygiene technician for Rentokil, says the job is about keeping people safe.
Pay: About £14,500 at entry level, up to £22,000.
Worst part: Cleaning up after suicides. Lewis says you can get used to the disgusting parts, but the emotional side is still hard. He says you learn not to take the work home, and you need a sense of humour.
Good part: He likes that the work is different each day. One day he may clean after a dead body. Another day he may work in a prison cell, or 100 feet in the air, lowered into a silo to clean it. He is proud to make a dangerous place safe again, and he says it helps society.

3. Biogas engineer
Job: Set up biogas plants in developing countries.
What they do: They connect digesters to toilets. The digesters can be filled with human excrement, animal dung, and other waste. This makes biogas that people can use for cooking and lighting. Baburam Paudel, chief technical officer in Nepal for the charity Renewable World, says you must know the right size and shape, the best temperature, and the best place to build the plant. You also must convince poor communities that poo can be useful, because many people dislike the idea of linking toilets to kitchens.
Pay: About £10,000 for an entry-level job. About £30,000 for a chief technical officer.
Worst part: Seeing people living with very little money. Paudel also says you must be ready to get your hands dirty when building and checking the system. Anaerobic digestion, when bacteria break down waste and make methane, smells like rotten eggs. It can be disgusting, and mistakes are not allowed.
Good part: Paudel says it is very satisfying to help people earn more money and to help girls go to school, because they do not need to collect firewood. He says the work improves health and hygiene for whole communities.

4. Eel ecologist
Job: Protect the critically endangered European eel.
What they do: Ecologists go into the Thames and other London rivers and marshes to measure eels. They may stand in water up to their armpits and reach into a net with up to 20 adult eels, using bare hands. Stephen Mowat, an eel conservationist and ecologist for the Zoological Society of London, says adult eels can be 1 metre long or more, and weigh up to 2kg. They are not dangerous, but they are very strong and a bit slimy. The team weighs and measures them, and the eels wriggle a lot.
Worst part: Eels are hard to work with, and it can be embarrassing when an eel outsmarts you. Mowat says you also must switch quickly between projects. Once he dissected a tub of dead eels to look for parasites, then soon after went to a meeting with government officials. He says the worst thing is often learning how much damage British people are doing to the environment.
Good part: He likes working outdoors and seeing British wildlife closely. He says eels are beautiful. Protecting eels also helps whole river systems, estuaries, and coastal habitats.

5. Shopping channel presenter
Job: Sell and show many products on live TV.
What they do: They present for many hours, show how products work, and seem excited and informed. Shaun Ryan, presenter for Ideal World TV, says he prepares and researches technical and practical information for every product. He also says you must connect with many types of products and many viewers.
Pay: A trainee presenter starts at at least £30,000. An experienced presenter can earn over £55,000.
Worst part: Working at unsociable times. Ryan says he often works weekends, bank holidays, and late evenings, and sometimes starts at 5am. He says his worst task was selling female slimming pants alone for an hour.
Good part: He loves the speed and pressure of live TV, and having to think fast all the time. He also likes knowing that sometimes thousands of viewers order the product he is presenting.
