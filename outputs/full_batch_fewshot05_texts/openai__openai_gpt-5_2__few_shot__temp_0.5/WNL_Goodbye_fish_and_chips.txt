In homes and cafes across the UK, tea, baked beans on toast, and fish and chips have been important foods for a long time. But people’s tastes have changed over the years. This change is shown in data from the National Food Survey. The government started this survey in 1940 because of worries about health and having enough food.

Even though Britain is famous for tea, people drink much less tea now than before. Tea use has dropped from 68g per person per week in the 1970s to 25g now. A teabag or a small amount of loose tea is about 3g. This means people drink about eight cups of tea a week today, down from 23 cups in 1974. Tea is still the most popular hot drink in the UK, but households now spend more money on coffee.

The Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs published the data as part of its “open data” scheme. The data comes from 150,000 households in the survey from 1974 to 2000, plus information from 2000 to 2014. The data shows some healthier changes, like more low-calorie soft drinks, more skimmed milk instead of whole milk, and more fresh fruit. But people also eat more chips, pizza, crisps, and ready meals each week.

People have also changed from eating white bread to brown, wholemeal, and other bread. But they eat less bread than before: about 15 slices a week now, compared with 25 slices a week in the past. This is based on a 40g slice from a medium sliced loaf. People also eat fewer baked beans in sauce, down by a fifth. At the same time, other convenience foods have risen, especially Italian foods. Adults now eat 75g of pizza each week, compared with none in 1974. Pasta use has almost tripled.

Fresh potatoes are less common too. Since 1974, adults eat 67% less fresh potatoes. In 1974, adults ate the same as 188g per day. People now eat more vegetables like cucumbers, courgettes, aubergines, and mushrooms. Takeaway food has almost doubled since 1974, from 80g per person per week to 150g. Of this, about 33g is chips and 56g is meat. Popular meats include kebabs (10g), chicken (7g), burgers (5g), and “meat-based meals” (32g).

Some trends show people may be choosing better food. Fruit eating, fresh and processed, has risen by 50% since 1974. In 2014, adults ate 157g of fruit per day. That is almost two portions out of the government’s five-a-day advice. Bananas have been the most popular fruit since 1996. In 2014, adults ate 221g of bananas per week, more than apples (131g) and oranges (48g). In 2014, low-calorie soft drinks were half of all soft drinks for the first time.

The survey also shows social changes. In 1991, it stopped asking about owning chickens and getting your own eggs. In the same year, it also stopped asking the “housewife” to fill in the form. People in Britain now spend a smaller part of their pay on food: 11% today, compared with 24% in 1974.

The UK Environment Secretary, Elizabeth Truss, said the data shows what people ate 40 years ago and how culture changes led to a “food revolution”. She said shoppers know more about where food comes from, the internet can bring good food to homes, pop-up restaurants show new trends, and world foods are now as common as fish and chips.

She also said that by opening the data, people can find the moments that changed eating habits. She said we have only learned a small part of what the National Food Survey can show. She looks forward to ways the data can be used, from local food maps and school projects to predicting new food trends. She also said it can help people learn about the past and help grow the UK food and farming industry in the future.
