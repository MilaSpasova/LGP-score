Oxfam says the huge fortunes of the world’s richest 100 billionaires are increasing inequality and making it harder to fight poverty.

Oxfam says wealth and income are growing for a small group on a scale never seen before. It says this often happens while poor people lose secure jobs and decent wages. Oxfam says people who live on aid or low pay then find it harder to improve their lives and escape poverty.

Oxfam says the world’s poorest people could be lifted out of poverty several times if the richest 100 billionaires gave away the money they made in 2012.

Oxfam does not blame specific people. It says the $240bn net income made in 2012 by the richest 100 billionaires could end extreme poverty four times over.

Charities rarely criticize the rich, because rich people often give money to charities. Bill Gates and Warren Buffett are part of a group of 40 US billionaires who promised to give much of their wealth to aid projects. But there is little detail about how much they give each year. Oxfam says Russian, Middle Eastern, and Chinese billionaires have not done the same.

In its report, The Cost of Inequality: How Wealth and Income Extremes Hurt Us All, published just before the World Economic Forum in Davos, Oxfam asks world leaders to reduce very high incomes and to lower inequality to at least the levels of 1990.

The report says the richest 1% increased their incomes by 60% in the past 20 years. It says the financial crisis made this faster, not slower.

Barbara Stocking, Oxfam’s Chief Executive, says extreme wealth is “economically inefficient, politically corrosive, socially divisive and environmentally destructive”.

She says studies show that countries have low investment and low growth when workers must live on a smaller share of total income.

She says we can no longer believe that making wealth for a few will always help everyone. Often, she says, the opposite happens.

The report says this problem is worldwide. In the UK, inequality is quickly going back to levels not seen since the time of Charles Dickens. In China, the top 10% now get nearly 60% of income. It says inequality in China is now similar to South Africa, which it calls the most unequal country on Earth, and more unequal than at the end of apartheid.

In the US, the report says the share of national income going to the top 1% doubled from 10% to 20% since 1980. For the top 0.01%, the share is higher than levels last seen in the 1920s.

The World Bank and the International Monetary Fund say very high inequality can weaken growth. Both have tried to connect their loans to programs that limit the growth of inequality.

Oxfam says members of the richest 1% may use up to 10,000 times more carbon than the average US citizen.

Oxfam says world leaders should learn from countries like Brazil, which has grown quickly while reducing inequality.

Stocking says the world needs a “global new deal” to reverse many years of rising inequality. She says the first step is for world leaders to promise to reduce inequality to 1990 levels.

She also says closing tax havens could raise more tax money. The Tax Justice Network says tax havens hold as much as $31 trillion, or about one third of all global wealth. She says this could bring in $189bn in extra tax revenue.
