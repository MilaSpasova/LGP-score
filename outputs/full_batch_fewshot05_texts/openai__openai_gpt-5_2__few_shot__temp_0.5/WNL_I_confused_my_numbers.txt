My Spanish degree helped me get my first job as a journalist. I worked for an international news agency in Mexico City. But I still made big mistakes as a new reporter.

I arrived in Mexico City after a long Greyhound bus trip from New York. The job interview tested my Spanish. In the daytime, I went out to cover political rallies. At night, I stayed alone in the office and helped manage news from El Salvador, Nicaragua, Guatemala, and other parts of Central America. I also reported on disasters like fires, floods, and explosions at firework factories.

Soon I learned that I was bad at understanding numbers in Spanish, just like I was bad at math in English. Sometimes I called the wrong number. Calls meant for the police woke up Mexican grandmothers at 2 a.m. because I dialed one wrong digit. Worse, I wrote wrong numbers in my stories. Almost 83 people dead in a fire at 6 p.m. became only 38 by 7 p.m. Twelve people hurt in a bus crash soon became two. Finally, an editor from the main office in Washington called me and shouted, “No one ever told you the death toll can’t go down?!”

Why are numbers so hard in another language? One reason may be different ways of saying numbers. In German, for example, 2:30 p.m. is halb drei (“half of three”), and 21 is einundzwanzig (“one and twenty”). This can confuse people. Some indigenous languages also have very unusual number systems, and some are close to dying out.

Some experts think there is a link between dyscalculia (difficulty understanding arithmetic) and trouble learning foreign languages, especially when students learn by repeating lists in order. Students with dyscalculia can find these step-by-step processes hard. But some students who struggle with grammar books do well when they live in another country and learn more naturally. For me, languages were usually easy, except for numbers.

Another reason may be that people often hear numbers alone, without context. The listener may not be fully focused on the foreign language and cannot quickly “switch on” again. Many of my multilingual friends can speak French or Italian well when ordering food, but they freeze when they must say or understand numbers, especially on the phone. Numbers seem tiring, but no one could clearly explain why.

My problem with numbers followed me from Mexico to other countries, and from Spanish to German and Portuguese. But in my first journalism job, getting numbers wrong did not always mean I failed.

One night, a Mexican coworker learned that the American consul in the port city of Veracruz was being held hostage at gunpoint in his office. There was no senior English-speaking reporter in the office, so I had to try to call the consulate. I dialed the number wrong and reached a different phone in the building. I knew who answered. I talked for 15 minutes to the gunman. I did not convince him to put away his gun, but my reputation as a new reporter grew overnight.
