Insects are crunchy and full of protein. People can find them under rocks. In most places, people have not eaten insects much. But now insects are getting closer to our plates. In Spring 2013, there will be a push to make insects seem less “yucky”. People want insects not only on special restaurant menus, but also in supermarkets.

In April, there will be a festival in London called Pestival 2013. It is backed by the Wellcome Trust. It is an insect appreciation event, and eating insects is an important part of it. There will be a two-day “pop-up” restaurant by the Nordic Food Lab. This Scandinavian team is linked to the Danish restaurant Noma. Noma served ants at Claridge’s hotel in Mayfair in 2012 for a ten-day run, and it sold out.

Restaurant magazine has named Noma the world’s best restaurant for three years in a row. Its chef, René Redzepi, says ants taste like lemon. He says a purée of fermented grasshoppers and moth larvae tastes like a strong fish sauce. Bee larvae can make a sweet mayonnaise used instead of eggs. Scientists keep finding new ways to use small creatures.

In March, a BBC documentary will show food writer Stefan Gates looking for and eating deep-fried locusts and barbecued tarantulas. People may joke about “flies in the soup”, but there is also a serious message. Many experts think there are clear environmental benefits if humans eat insects.

The UN’s Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) has funded projects since 2011 to promote eating and farming insects in south-east Asia and Africa. About two billion people there already eat insects and caterpillar larvae as a regular part of their diet. In 2012, the FAO published a list of 1,909 edible insect species. With support from the Dutch government, it planned a big international conference on “this valuable food source” in 2013.

There are many insects. Globally, for every human there are 40 tonnes of insects. So it is not very likely they will become endangered, and they are unlikely to have been dosed with chemicals. Redzepi said: “I know it’s taboo to eat bugs in the western world, but why not?” He said that in south-east Asia it is common. He also said that if you like mushrooms, you have eaten many worms without knowing it. He added that we eat honey, and that honey is the vomit of a bee.

Redzepi said the basic idea behind Nordic Food Lab was: “Nothing is not edible.”

Insects are important for life on Earth. There are more than a million insect species, and insects are the most diverse group of creatures on the planet. But people often misunderstand them, hate them, and kill them just because they are there.

In the next 30 years, the human population will grow to nine billion. Already, one billion people do not have enough food. More people will put more pressure on farm land, water, forests, fisheries, biodiversity resources, and also nutrients and energy supplies.

Meat is getting more costly. This is not only about money. Rainforest is also destroyed for grazing or for growing feedstuff for cattle. Cows also produce methane. Livestock farming makes a very large amount of greenhouse gas emissions: 35% of the planet’s methane, 65% of its nitrous oxide, and 9% of the carbon dioxide.

Edible insects produce fewer gases. They have high-quality protein, vitamins, and amino acids. They also turn food into protein efficiently. To make the same amount of protein, they need a quarter of the food that sheep need, and half of what pigs and chickens need. They produce fewer greenhouse gases and less ammonia than cows, and they can be grown on organic waste.

China is already setting up huge maggot farms. Zimbabwe has a strong mapone caterpillar industry. Laos got nearly $500,000 from the FAO for an insect-harvesting project. In the UK, it is already big business, but not always official. Gatwick customs recently detained a man coming from Burkina Faso with 94 kilos of mapone in his luggage, worth nearly £40,000.

A study by FoodServiceWarehouse.com said that swapping pork and beef for crickets and locusts could cut greenhouse gas emissions by as much as 95%. The text also says that when we die, worms and insects will get a chance to nibble us back.
