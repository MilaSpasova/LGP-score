Every morning in Delhi, before it gets hot, a small group of old friends meet. On the dry grass near the India Gate monument, they stretch, breathe, and meditate.

At 6.15am, Arvind Singh did breathing exercises on a bench. Singh is a 42-year-old salesman. He said this is the only healthy way to start the day, better than an egg, a sandwich, or a cup of tea.

Singh and his friends are not the only ones. In crowded cities across India, people do the same thing in the green spaces that are left.

On 21 June, the new International Day of Yoga, Prime Minister Narendra Modi hopes the world will join. The grass near India Gate will become a big place for yoga. They hope it will be the biggest single yoga session ever, with up to 45,000 people doing a 35-minute routine.

The group will include Modi, who is 64, and most of his government. They also hope some celebrities will come. Officials have been sent to find volunteers from many countries, to show the practice is international.

Getting people to do yoga has become important for Modi. He led the Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) to a big election win in 2014. In May 2015, schools were told to make sure students went to yoga events linked to the big event in Delhi, even though it is on a Sunday.

Officials have said yoga will become compulsory for India’s police officers, who are known for being out of shape. They also said three million civil servants and their families will get free daily yoga lessons. Air India said it will add yoga for trainee pilots. Some senior politicians also said that more yoga could reduce the very high levels of sexual violence in the country.

Modi is an ascetic. He is a strict vegetarian and he also practises yoga. In 2014, on a visit to New York, he spoke at the United Nations and suggested an international yoga day.

Modi said yoga is a valuable gift from India’s ancient tradition. He said it joins mind and body, thought and action, and brings harmony between people and nature. He said it is not only exercise, but also a way to feel oneness with yourself, the world, and nature. He added that yoga could help with climate change.

Yoga is between 3,000 and 6,000 years old. It began somewhere on the Indian subcontinent, possibly among religious ascetics. For a long time, its meditation and physical exercises have been linked to local religions, including Buddhism and Jainism, and Hinduism, which 80% of Indians practise.

Modi started his career as an organizer for a hardline Hindu nationalist organization. He has been criticized before for supporting an idea of Indian culture that leaves little space for other traditions. One commentator called the 21 June event a mix of cultural nationalism, commercialization, and subtle coercion.

Novelist Ajaz Ashraf wrote on India’s Scroll website that the hope is to bring modern-day gurus and their many followers into the BJP, and that these followers are mostly the urban middle classes.

Other people point to a recent US court ruling that said yoga is not always linked to religion. A court in California said that yoga classes in the San Diego district were “devoid of any religious, mystical or spiritual trappings”. This came after two parents said yoga in schools promoted Hinduism and blocked Christianity.

Amish Tripathi writes best-selling novels set 4,000 years ago in India, based on Hindu mythology. He said characters in his books practise yoga. He said that in ancient India, yoga was part of daily life, both physical and mental. He said every culture gives something to the world, and yoga is India’s gift.

Suneel Singh, a guru in south Delhi, agreed that yoga does not belong to only one religion. He said, “Is t’ai chi just Chinese? Is football just English? Same with yoga.” He said yoga is a full package for everybody’s body, and a cheap way to stay healthy.
