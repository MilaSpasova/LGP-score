Most exercise classes do not have a tea break in the middle. But Margaret Allen’s class does. After a gentle warm-up and some faster moves, the 93-year-old great-grandmother lets the class drink tea and sit down for a short rest.

There are eight people in the class. Some look like they need the break more than others. Allen is wearing a thick turquoise shirt, a navy knitted waistcoat, black trousers, and sensible shoes. She is not sweating. She has a very painful trapped nerve in one leg, and her knee needs to be replaced. But she still looks like she could exercise for hours.

Usually it is not a good idea to eat right before sport, or during it. But when I visit Allen’s class at a church hall in Saltburn-by-the-Sea near Middlesbrough, people eat fruitcake during the break. The cake was made for Allen’s recent birthday by her sister Joan, who is 89. Local people call Joan the “scone queen of Saltburn”.

Soon after they finish the cake, Allen stands up again. She leads a lively Scottish dance with lots of toe pointing and leg kicking. Forty-five minutes later, the class ends.

Allen used to volunteer with the Red Cross. She has led these classes in the north-east seaside town for 45 years. She was not very sporty at school. During the second world war, she played the piano for a keep-fit class, mainly to have something to do during the blackouts. Later, when she was in her 40s, she took over the class after the old instructor retired.

At its busiest time, the class had more than 18 regular members. Each person paid £1 each time. Now the group is getting smaller. During the tea break, the women talk about a funeral they went to that week. It was for one of the younger members of the group, who died at 68 from motor neurone disease.

Allen is the oldest in the class, and her sister is next. The youngest is Jean Cunion, who is 60. She says the group helped her during a hard time after her mother died. She feels a little embarrassed because she thinks she is the least fit. She says that the first time she came, Margaret asked, “Who’s that huffing and panting?” and Jean had to say it was her. Another member, Ruth Steere, who is 76, is amazed that Allen notices everything even with her back to the class. Ruth says Allen shouts if they do it wrong, and she is very good at knowing what they are doing.

Allen loves dancing, but she has never had formal training as a fitness instructor. She makes her own routines using five tapes by the BBC’s first fitness teacher, Eileen Fowler. Fowler died in 2000 when she was 93, which is Allen’s age now. Allen thinks her good health comes from staying busy, especially since her husband Joe died in 1997. When she was 80, she started writing poetry.

“I write poems about everything. I write a lot. I just can’t stop,” she says. A few days after the interview, she phones me to read a short poem about the joy of exercise. Winnie Robertson, an 84-year-old former teacher in the class, thinks the secret to staying fit is not to stop using your body. She says, “Use it or lose it.”

Allen still plays the piano and gives speeches. She is president of the Women’s Fellowship at the local Methodist church. She is also one of three people aged over 90 at the Scrabble club of the University of the Third Age. She took a computer course when she was 88 and tried to use the internet, but it did not work out.

She admits that getting older is not fun. She reads me a few lines from her poem called ‘That Beast Called Age’. She also remembers a doctor who met her for the first time a few years ago and said she could not be older than 78. Allen said, “Thank you, doctor. You can go now.”

She is also strict about weight gain. She says people should not eat too much. When someone says, “I can’t lose weight,” she says, “Sellotape.” She acts like she is taping her mouth closed. She says she said this recently to a very fat man. She says her motto is “Everything in moderation.”

Earlier in 2013, Allen watched the news and saw a woman receive the British Empire Medal. She means Margaret Chartwood from Horley, who received the honour in January at age 77. Allen says the woman said, “I’m 80 and I’m the oldest fitness instructor in the country!” Allen thought, “No, you’re not.” But she says she will not write to Buckingham Palace.
