From glow-in-the-dark trees to bike storage under the ground and bins that run on solar power, people are trying unusual ideas to make cities nicer places to live.

City life has many good points, but it is getting harder to live in a way that is good for the planet. City people deal with pollution, traffic, and fewer green spaces. Inventors are now offering some of the stranger ideas to improve city life.

Pop-up parks
Some cities seem built more for cars than for people. A pop-up park is a simple idea: use an empty parking space, a little money, and a few plants to make a tiny park. The PARK(ing) project started as an art experiment in San Francisco and then spread around the world. People have also tried temporary city farms and ecology demonstrations. The movement has a day of action every September.

Subterranean storage
Not everyone in cities uses a car. Bikes are becoming more popular, but they need to be stored safely. A Tokyo engineering firm called Giken made an idea called an “eco-cycle, anti-seismic underground bicycle park.” It is a round storage space that is seven metres wide and goes deep into the ground. It can hold 204 bikes. Bike owners press a button, and the system brings the bike back up in about 13 seconds.

Glow-in-the-dark trees
Some people think of Christmas decorations when they think of glowing trees, but Daan Roosegaarde thinks differently. This Dutch designer-artist has created an experimental “bioluminescent” plant. The technology combines DNA from glowing marine bacteria with part of a plant to make a glow like a jellyfish. Tests are happening to make a large-scale version. Roosegaarde hopes it could one day replace normal street lights.

Footfall harvesting
In West Ham in east London, many commuters and shoppers cross an elevated walkway near the underground station. Most people do not notice that the floor feels slightly springy. That small movement in the rubber surface helps power the streetlights above. The floor has smart tiles that capture energy from footsteps and turn it into electricity. The UK firm Pavegen made this idea and has also installed similar systems at London’s Heathrow Airport and other places.

Supertrees
Singapore’s Gardens by the Bay has man-made “supertrees.” They are steel-framed and can be up to 50 metres high. Flowers and ferns grow on them, and their canopies help take in and spread out heat. They can also collect rainwater and help provide air ventilation for two “climate-controlled biomes” (large conservatories) below. Eleven of the 18 trees also have solar panels on their “branches.”
