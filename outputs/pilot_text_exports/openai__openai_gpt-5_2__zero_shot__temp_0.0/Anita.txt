Anitta is a new Brazilian funk star. She has won millions of fans by bringing the sound of the favela into popular music. But she is now in the middle of a debate about skin colour.

Some anti-discrimination activists and writers say the music industry’s fastest rising star had to give up part of her black identity to succeed in the mostly white, middle-class market.

The debate grew after people shared “then-and-now” photos of Anitta. They seem to show her skin looking much lighter after she signed a deal with Warner.

In the first photo, from when she was not famous, she looked darker. In the second photo, a marketing picture after she became famous, she looks paler. People asked if this happened because of skin-whitening products, cosmetic surgery, or (more likely) Photoshop. The big difference started the old question again: do you need to be pale to get ahead in Brazil?

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote a post criticizing hidden discrimination in media and marketing. She said many people do not want to admit they are racist, and they think Brazil is a multiracial democracy, but statistics show it is not. She said whitening shows an intolerant society that does not support diversity. She said white is often seen as the image of the rich, nice, successful, and good, while black is seen as the opposite.

Anitta’s real name is Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

She later became very famous with an album and a hit song, “Show das Poderosas.” The song topped the charts and got 52 million views on YouTube.

Many fans love her as a pop idol with a strong message and catchy songs. Her supporters also present her as a bridge between the mostly black and mixed-race shanty towns on Rio’s hillsides and the richer, whiter areas below.

She has made baile funk less explicit. She reduced suggestive dancing, “gangsta” references, and sexual lyrics. But now people ask if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract a richer audience. Arraes said this can pressure other pop stars too: if they have curly hair, they may feel forced to straighten it; if they have a big nose, they may feel forced to get rhinoplasty. She said this can hurt self-esteem.

Race is a sensitive topic in Brazil, a country where many people are mixed-race. Brazil was one of the last large countries in the world to end slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. But in Brazil, colour often matters more than race or ancestry. Even though the country wants to be a multiracial democracy, there is a clear link between skin tone and inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Until 2011, black or mixed-race students also spent, on average, two fewer years in school.

The government says the gap is getting smaller because of university quotas and other affirmative action policies. But the difference is still easy to see. Most business and government leaders are white, while many low-paid jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In Ipanema, Gávea, and other rich areas, you are much more likely to see black nannies pushing strollers with white toddlers than the opposite.

Colour categories in Brazil can be complicated. In the 2010 census, people who called themselves white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million said black, two million said Asian, and 0.5% said indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology lecturer at the Federal University of Pernambuco, said Anitta won middle-class fans by taking a rebellious sound and making it calmer and easier for everyone to accept. He said this was not done by racial whitening. He said it happened because Anitta changed the social place where she made her art, from the periphery to the centre.

Other people say the colour issue is too big. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who worked with Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot but not her colour. He said it was more about makeup, hairstyling, and clothes. He also said he did not think she was pushed into it. He said she always liked to show off, sing, and dance, and that this naturalness helped her succeed.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador, said the prejudice was not from the public but from music producers and media bosses. He said the public showed they cared about music itself when they chose Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, as a recent winner of the talent show The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for comment. But in a recent interview, she said identity should be defined by the person. She said: an “all-powerful” woman does not need to be beautiful, but she has so much attitude that she is marvellous and powerful. She said she tries to show in her work that we can be who we want.
