Dan Sullivan was late to breakfast with the Guardian. He was late because police stopped him while he was riding his motorcycle.

Sullivan says he works too much. He misses dinner dates. He forgets to buy presents.

So, like many people in Silicon Valley, the 27-year-old venture capitalist made a start-up to help. It is called BetterBoyfriend.me. For about $70 a month, it sends a present each month to girlfriends and wives.

Sullivan works in finance. He has been testing the service quietly. About 350 boyfriends have signed up. They bring in about $17,000 each month. Most members are his Harvard and investor friends. They include venture capitalists, founders, and workers at companies like Apple, Google, and Facebook. Their girlfriends have been getting presents from Sullivan for the last year.

Each month, members choose from seven possible gifts, like chocolate, tea sets, or manicures. The package is sent to the boyfriend with no brand name and no label.

Sullivan tests ideas by buying Facebook ads to see what happens. BetterBoyfriend.me became popular fast. Sullivan said it solved a problem many people have.

BetterBoyfriend.me was not his first idea. For a few days, he thought about a dog-sharing idea. He said he saw rich guys looking unhappy while walking dogs. He thought a dog is only valuable 5% of the time. He wondered if someone could watch dogs and other people could rent them.

Another start-up is called InvisibleBoyfriend. It lets users create a fake social partner who is always available to talk. The result is a “boyfriend” who can text with you all day.

The founder, Kyle Tabor, started it to help people stop unwanted attention. But he found that people started to feel close to their computer-made partners. He said many users want companionship through conversation, not just “proof” of a relationship.

For Sullivan and Tabor, a surprise was how real the customer relationships became. Sullivan says he now feels like a kind of relationship adviser for the boyfriends.

Sullivan says he made mistakes. At first, the packages had receipts with his name, Dan Sullivan. One boyfriend wrote to him and said, “Listen, dude, she’s not mad but Cynthia found out.”

About 50% of the women who get BetterBoyfriend.me packages know Sullivan is involved. Sullivan says it relates to age. He thinks that after people have been married for a while, they do not keep many secrets.

Over the year, Sullivan says he got to know the boyfriends well. They even delivered a package to a hospital delivery room. He said the important thing is to remember his relationship is with the boyfriend.

When he first started the company, he put BetterBoyfriend.me tags on flower bouquets. He went to Dolores Park, a popular place in the Mission District.

He looked for couples and gave the flowers to the girl. The boyfriend did not like that. Sullivan said it made sense. So he changed his plan and started giving the flowers to the boyfriends instead.
