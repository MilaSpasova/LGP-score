James Hamblin is a senior editor at The Atlantic magazine. In his series “If Our Bodies Could Talk,” he tried a no-showering challenge. He wanted to see what happens when we clean our bodies too much. He took fewer showers. When he did shower, he did not use shampoo or soap.

He found something many people have found: when we scrub a lot with soap and body wash, our skin tries harder to get back to normal. Showering can wash away the skin’s natural oils and bacteria. Hot water and cleaning products can make this even stronger.

Have you ever felt very tight and dry after a shower? That can happen because the skin’s natural moisture gets washed away. Our skin also has millions of helpful bacteria, like our gut does. Frequent rubbing and scrubbing can destroy these bacteria. When the bacteria grow back, the new microbes may make more body odor. So, showering too often may sometimes make you smell more. If you stop showering and using soap, your skin may go through an early adjustment period. After that, the skin often becomes more balanced, makes less oil, and healthy bacteria can grow again.

After his experiment, Hamblin felt that the human body can work well on its own. It is not only about smell or looks. Dermatologists say that using less soap can help acne and eczema. Taking fewer showers and using fewer cleaning products can also help the environment. The average shower lasts seven minutes and uses 65 litres of water. That is 65 litres of clean, drinkable water mixed with soap and sent down the drain every day, sometimes more than once.

Clean water is becoming harder to ignore as California has another summer of drought. It is becoming clear that clean water is one of the world’s most valuable resources, and it may soon be in short supply. Also, all the body wash bottles can hurt the environment. These are strong reasons to let your body be more natural.

If this idea makes you feel nervous because you remember being close to people who smell, relax. Many people who cut back on cleaning still use deodorant if they need it. People’s natural smell is different, and it can change with diet, hydration, and exercise. Also, washing hands with soap is still recommended to help stop infectious diseases from spreading.

You do not need to stop all at once like Hamblin did. But between cleaning many times a day and never showering, there is a middle choice. Reduce. Skip a few showers, use less soap, and let your skin’s helpful bacteria grow.
