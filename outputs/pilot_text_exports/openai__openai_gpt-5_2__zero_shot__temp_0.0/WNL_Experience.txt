It was a beautiful summer evening, and I decided to go for a swim near Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland, where I moved in 2012. A woman was already in the water with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty often swims with a group of people, and she seems to know them well.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Since then, hundreds of people have swum with her, so many people think she is completely tame. She was even in an Irish tourist-board advertisement, where a girl says she wants to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty’s tummy, and it looked safe and friendly. About 20 tourists and local people stood on the pier watching. Soon after I got into the water, Dusty left the woman and suddenly became wild. I later learned that she is very territorial when she is with someone. Her tail was slapping hard. At first I thought she was just showing off, but then I realized she might be angry.

I knew I needed to get out, so I swam toward the pier. But almost instantly, Dusty crashed into me with her snout. It was very strong and very painful, and she was moving fast. I was thrown forward.

Everyone on the pier stared at me in shock. Dusty stayed in the water next to me, still slapping her tail. That was the scariest part. I thought, if she hits me with her tail, I could go under the water and die.

I reached the pier, but I could not climb out because I was hurt. I felt pure terror. I shouted for help, and a man reached in and pulled me onto the steps. Then another man arrived and said he was an orthopaedic surgeon who specialized in marine trauma. He had been driving into Doolin when he saw what was happening.

I was very cold and very worried because I did not know how bad my injuries were. My biggest fear was internal bleeding. He checked me and told me he could not feel signs of internal bleeding, but he thought I probably had broken bones. Later I learned I had six spinal fractures, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for several weeks. I could not work for five months, and I had limited movement, stiffness, and pain. Then doctors said I had post-traumatic stress. After the near-death experience, I became anxious about everything and overreacted in ways I never had before. I felt like people were looking at me the wrong way. Loud noises were hard for me, and I had memory loss.

Three months before the accident, I had opened a health-food shop, but I had to give it up because I could not work anymore. It was the hardest year ever, but now it is behind me. I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage, and now I am building up my own osteopathy practice. I understand my patients better because I have been a patient myself.

I am thankful that I am healthy, and I want to stop other people from getting hurt. People have a happy idea about dolphins and trust them. Who thinks a dolphin would attack a person? If a dangerous animal comes at you with its teeth showing, it is clearly scary. But dolphins have a wide smile, so they do not look threatening.

I am not angry with Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her territory, and she is a wild and unpredictable animal. People need to understand that. Many people come to swim with her and do not know how dangerous it can be. People said my injuries were among several that summer.

After the man pulled me out of the water, Dusty swam away. Then she came back and floated upright next to me, looking at me. We looked into each other’s eyes, and I felt she was truly sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin, and the anger was gone. The people on the pier were amazed. After that moment, I did not feel the terror anymore. I felt at peace with her.
