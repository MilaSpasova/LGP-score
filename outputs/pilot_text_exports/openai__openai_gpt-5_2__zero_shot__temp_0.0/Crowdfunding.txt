This invention could be as useful as Trevor Baylis’s wind-up radio in the 1990s, before the internet. It is a cheap light that uses gravity to make free power. It could help stop the use of dangerous kerosene lamps in Africa and India.

British designer Patrick Hunt tried the usual way to get money from banks or venture capital firms. But he had trouble. He said getting funding was slow, complicated, unproven, and people did not want to take a risk.

So he tried crowdfunding on a US website called Indiegogo, which had just started in the UK. In five days, he reached his goal and raised £36,200. By the end of 2012, his campaign was so popular that in 40 days he raised £400,000.

The LED light uses a dynamo. The dynamo is powered when a 10kg bag of rocks slowly falls. The weight is attached to the light and lifted to about 2m. As it slowly drops to the ground, it makes enough power for 30 minutes of light.

Hunt is getting ready to make the light in China. Before making many more, he will send 1,000 lights to Africa to test the market. He hopes to later produce millions.

He is part of a new group of entrepreneurs who use crowdfunding to get money. Another new site is InvestingZone, which connects rich people with start-up entrepreneurs.

Indiegogo does not give shares. Instead, people who donate can get “perks” at different donation levels. People who helped fund Hunt’s light could feel good about helping poor people, and they also got their own light.

Danae Ringelmann, a co-founder of Indiegogo, says the “gravity light” shows how crowdfunding can work in a fair way and can test an idea. She says money can be fast and efficient because it is not about knowing people in a bank or a venture capital firm. It is about proving your value to customers and fans, getting them to support your idea, and paying for it.

She also says that even if an idea does not get funding, it is still useful to test it. Then you can learn faster and not waste too much time.

Ringelmann lives in the US and first worked as a Wall Street analyst. In 2008, she quit and used her skills to help friends in the arts raise money.

At first, the website focused on films. It launched at the Sundance Film Festival that year.

Five years later, it was raising about $2m each week for new businesses that were starting or growing. In December 2012, it added services in euros and pounds to expand in Europe. It says Britain is its third biggest market. International activity has risen 41% since December.

There are many competitors, such as Kickstarter, Seedrs, and Funding Circle. But Ringelmann says Indiegogo is different because anyone can start a campaign. No project is seen as too strange.

Indiegogo takes a 4% fee from successful campaigns. If a campaign does not reach its goal, users can choose to return all the money to donors for free, or keep the money but pay a 9% fee.

A British woman, Lauren Pears, raised £100,000 on the site to open a “cat café” in London. It is called Lady Dinah’s Cat Emporium. It has not opened yet, but it is described as a place where people can sit in a comfortable wingback chair with hot tea, a book, and a cat.

Ringelmann says some venture capitalists reject ideas because they do not know if people really want them. They may think it could be a gadget that nobody cares about.

She says entrepreneurs can run an Indiegogo campaign, even without launching the product, and the campaign can show proof that people want it. Then venture capitalists may believe there is a real market.

She says crowdfunding lets you test who your customers are, what price works, and which features people want. It can also help you find new ways to make money and get important feedback.

Because of her Wall Street experience and helping 100,000 businesses and services raise money, Ringelmann gives advice to new entrepreneurs.

She says ideas are common. What matters is doing the work well. She says if you are afraid someone will steal your idea and do it better and faster, then you may not be the right person to do it. She says you need confidence, speed, and a willingness to learn.

Ringelmann says Indiegogo’s move into Europe, and a deal with a web payment provider, are part of a dream to make finance open to more people. Payments will work through local card services like Maestro in the UK and Carte Bleue in France, and also through PayPal. More than 7,000 fundraising campaigns are live on the site.

Crowdfunding has grown as an alternative to banks, but it does not interest all big investors. Many big investors want a stake in a business. That may start to change in the UK with the launch of InvestingZone.
