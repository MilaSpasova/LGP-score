When the lottery balls fall and all six numbers match, it can feel like it is time to buy an Audi, book a holiday in the US, and call an estate agent. An analysis of lottery jackpot winners says many millionaires do things like this.

Since it started in 1994, the lottery has made 3,000 millionaires. Together they have won more than £8.5bn in total, with an average of £2.8m each. The study authors, Oxford Economics, say this money also helped create 3,780 more millionaires among the winners’ children, family, and friends.

Most winners (59%) stop working right away. But 19% keep doing their normal job, and 31% do unpaid voluntary work. The study says 98% of winners’ spending stayed in the UK. By spending on homes, vehicles, and holidays, it is estimated that each winner supports six full-time jobs for one year.

Winners added almost £750m to GDP and created more than £500m in tax for the Exchequer. Most of the money went on property. Winners spent £2.72bn on their main homes, and £170m paying off debts and mortgages.

Many winners wanted to keep an income, so £2.125bn was spent on investments. Gifts to family and friends were £1.17bn. Cars and holidays cost £680m.

Camelot, the company that runs the UK National Lottery, paid for the study to mark 3,000 winners. It used research from 100 winners who had won £1m or more. The study found that the 3,000 winners bought 7,958 houses or flats in the UK, about 2.7 each, spending £3.3bn. Most winners (82%) moved to a new main home, spending about £900,000 on average.

Many new homes had extra features. Almost a third (29%) bought a hot tub. A walk-in wardrobe was important for 28%. Almost a quarter (24%) chose a home behind electric gates. About 22% had a games room, and 7% put in a snooker table.

Bigger homes also needed help. About 30% hired a cleaner and 24% hired a gardener. A small number (5%) hired a beautician.

Audis were the most popular car for 16% of winners. Range Rovers and BMWs were also popular (11% each), as well as Mercedes (10%) and Land Rovers (5%). Winners spent £463m on 17,190 cars. The average price of the favourite car was £46,116.

Holidays were important too. Most winners (68%) chose five-star hotels overseas. The US was the top destination for 27%, followed by the Caribbean (9%). In the UK, caravan sales also gained. Over the past 18 years, 10% of millionaires bought a caravan, creating about £7.4m in sales.

Some winners (15%) started their own business. About 9% helped other people start businesses, and 6% invested in or bought other people’s businesses. The study says these businesses employ 3,195 people.

Andy Logan, who wrote the report, said a win spreads further than they expected. He said it changes the lives of friends and family, and each win also affects the UK economy because so much money is spent in the UK. He said each win can have a ripple effect for this generation and often the next.
