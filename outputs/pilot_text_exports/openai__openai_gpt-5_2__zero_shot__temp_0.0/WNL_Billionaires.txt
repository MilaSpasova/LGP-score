A thick layer of bird droppings covers a gold-painted railing at one of Britain’s most expensive homes. Pigeon skeletons lie among broken mirrors, and water runs through broken parts of the building. This house is called The Towers. It is a £30m palace on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London. Its damage has been kept secret until now.

The Towers is one of ten mansions in the middle of The Bishops Avenue. This street is part of London’s fast-rising housing market. These houses have been almost empty since they were bought about 25 years ago. It is believed they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. Their Greek-style columns are breaking. Their swimming pools with mosaic tiles are full of broken pieces. Nature has taken over so much that owls have moved in.

This empty and broken look is seen along the street. Lloyds Bank says this is the second most expensive street in Britain. At the same time, many people in London struggle to buy a home because house prices rise 11.2% a year. But 16 mansions on the most expensive part of The Bishops Avenue are empty. Many are behind locked gates. Their windows are covered with steel bars, and their gardens are overgrown. Guard dogs patrol the grounds.

Across the street, there is another empty mansion worth £18m, with broken windows and special paint to stop people climbing. Another house has metal bars on the windows and has been sold for £20m.

Still, prices keep rising. Dryades, a mansion that was owned until recently by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007. It is believed to be worth about £30m today. Heath Lodge, where fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou was murdered in 1984 by a silver bullet, is worth £13m today. It was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

For people who struggle to pay for housing in one of the world’s most expensive cities, these empty homes can feel painful to see. A security guard said it was very upsetting to watch so much space—enough to house dozens of people—falling apart.

Rich royals from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia were among the first to come to this curved road near Hampstead Heath. Iranians came here after the fall of the shah. Now, Chinese buyers are following Russians and Kazakhs. They have paid millions to live on a street that estate agents say is as famous as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions were for sale for £65m and £38m. They promised Italian marble, lifts with leather walls, and luxury panic rooms.

But around these empty houses, stone fountains are breaking and lawns have turned into muddy wetlands. Inside one house, water drips through a huge crystal chandelier onto a thick carpet that is rotting under parts of a collapsed ceiling. Moss grows through broken bricks, and mirrored tiles are scattered in a bathroom. The swimming pool has about a foot of dirty, salty water, and flowers grow through the tiles. Wooden slats in the sauna bend outward.

The most dramatic ruin is The Towers. It is a large mansion among many acres of hornbeams, oaks, and limes. Pigeons fill its big halls. Water pours through three floors, turning the walls bright green with algae, and drops into a huge empty basement swimming pool. Unopened wooden crates marked “bullet-proof glass” show that the old owners were worried about safety.

Today, very few people live on The Bishops Avenue all the time. A security guard outside one mansion said the owners were not there. Another guard outside Royal Mansion would not say if anyone was home. A staff member at another mansion warned the Guardian about the guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare mogul worth £45m, said he is one of the few people who live there permanently.

He said, “It’s not a friendly place where you can talk over the fence. I have never seen what my neighbours look like. Next door, a Saudi princess spent £35m on a new house and I’ve never seen her. About three houses are lived in all the time, and half the properties are used three to six months a year. The other half, who knows if they come or not?”

These broken, very expensive homes show a housing culture where the world’s richest people treat British property as investments. One Hyde Park, an apartment building in Knightsbridge, is another example. More than half the flats there are registered with the council as empty or second homes.

Even so, people on the avenue are talking about building £5m apartments instead of £50m mansions, to bring people back.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, wants to rebuild one of the most valuable sites on the avenue. He plans 20 apartments with a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

He said, “If you build a big house and try and sell for £30m to £40m, it won’t sell. Locals won’t buy, so you have to bring in overseas buyers.”

But using the empty homes to help with the housing crisis still seems far away. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some of the empty homes could become affordable housing. He said the land price would be too high.

He said, “Very wealthy people own property there. Sometimes they live in them and sometimes they don’t.”
