Opposition to Western Australia’s shark cull has grown. Thousands of people went to beaches across Australia to ask the state’s premier to stop the policy. RSPCA Australia and Richard Branson, who owns Virgin Atlantic, also spoke against it.

The policy catches and kills sharks longer than three metres. It started after what the state government called an “unprecedented” number of shark attacks on Western Australia’s coast. A 35-year-old surfer was killed in November 2013. He was the sixth person to die from a shark attack in two years.

But the Shark Attack File says Australia has averaged one shark-related death a year for the last 50 years.

Kate Faehrmann from the Sea Shepherd Conservation Society spoke at a protest in Perth. She said the policy will not work. She said drumlines, used to catch sharks, can kill many animals. They can catch sharks of any size, and also dolphins, turtles, and other animals. She said this is why the community does not want the policy.

Thousands protested at Perth’s Cottesloe Beach and Sydney’s Manly Beach. Hundreds protested at Glenelg in south-west Adelaide, and at beaches in Victoria and Queensland. Faehrmann said the protests showed many Australians want sharks protected. She said that even with fear from the movie Jaws, people are coming out to say the ocean is the sharks’ home and they do not want them killed.

Surfer Anthony Joyce, who once had his foot caught in a shark’s mouth, said the number of sharks killed would not make a difference.

The state government has refused to give an updated total of sharks killed. There have been reports of sharks smaller than three metres being released after being caught on drumlines. Drumlines are floating drums tied to the sea bed, with baited hooks under them.

Conservationists say there is no evidence the cull will reduce shark attacks on humans. They say no earlier cull used only drumlines. Researchers at the University of Western Australia say the recent increase in shark attacks may be linked to the state having the fastest-growing population in Australia, not to more sharks.

Richard Peirce, chairman of the UK charity the Shark Trust, said the cull would not work and might bring more predators near the coast. He said it is sad the government did not try other options instead of killing animals in a way that can harm many species. He also said that leaving drumlines in overnight could attract other predators to the area because sharks and other animals may be hooked and injured.

Worldwide in 2012, there were 80 unprovoked shark attacks, and seven were fatal. Humans kill nearly 100m sharks each year. RSPCA Australia said it believes the cull is not justified. It said there is no evidence that more attacks are caused by more sharks. It said the change fits with population and human behaviour, meaning more people are in the water.

Richard Branson told Fairfax Radio the policy was backfiring. He said Premier Colin Barnett may have thought it would help tourism, but he thinks it will do the opposite. He said it advertises a problem that is not big, and could stop people from coming to Perth and the countryside around it. He said it may only make people worry for no reason.
