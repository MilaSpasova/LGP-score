Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is theft. He says it is like reaching into someone’s pocket and stealing a wallet. In an article for Index on Censorship, Pullman defends copyright laws. He criticizes internet users who download music or books without paying.

Pullman writes that new technology can seem so impressive that people do not notice the wrong thing they are doing. He says it is wrong for anyone to steal an artist’s work and not be punished.

His article was written after music industry leaders met British Prime Minister David Cameron at Downing Street. They talked about web piracy.

Pullman, who wrote the His Dark Materials trilogy, says authors and musicians often work for years with little money and little attention. He says that when their work finally becomes popular, their chance to earn a living can be taken away by illegal downloading. He says the rule is simple: if we enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

Pullman is writing in the next issue of the campaign group’s magazine in a dialogue with Cathy Casserly. She is the chief executive of Creative Commons. Creative Commons offers open content licences that let creators control copyright in a different way. Casserly says copyright has many problems because it was made “in an analogue age”. She says copyright can stop people from sharing, building on, and remixing each other’s work.

Casserly says artists need to think of new ways to distribute and monetize their work. She quotes science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees with her. The magazine’s editor, Rachael Jolley, says current copyright laws do not work well in the digital age and might make consumers into criminals. She says new ways are needed so artists, writers, and musicians can earn a living.

The debate is very active, and illegal downloading is very large. Data from Ofcom, the UK communications regulator, suggests that in the UK, between November 2012 and January 2013, people illegally copied 280 million music tracks. They also copied 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files.

Ofcom says 18% of internet users over age 12 say they recently pirated content. Also, 9% say they fear being caught. Pullman writes that it is still surprising how easily and quickly people can get music as MP3 downloads, even to people who have been building up their iTunes list for some time.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron appointed Conservative MP Mike Weatherley as his adviser on this issue. A spokesman for the BPI, the record industry trade body, said Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creative producers it protects. The spokesman said they hope Weatherley and the prime minister will support copyright in the work of the UK’s intellectual property office.
