Kenton Cool usually likes to talk, but now he can hardly speak. Climbing high mountains has made his voice rough. He is in Kathmandu, the capital of Nepal. He flew down from Everest base camp that morning.

Cool is thinking about a surprising set of climbs he finished the weekend before. Early on Saturday, he reached the top of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of three main summits in the Everest “horseshoe.” This horseshoe is around an icy valley called the Western Cwm.

That same day, he climbed to the top of Everest. He reached the summit in complete darkness early on Sunday. Then he and his climbing partner kept going. They climbed to the top of Lhotse on Monday morning. Lhotse is the third peak in this three-mountain challenge.

“It was a snatched opportunity,” he says. “For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. That doesn’t take away the physical achievement of what I did. I’ve set the bar at a certain level. But whoever comes along next will move the bar further and do it without ropes or bottled oxygen.”

Sixty years after people first climbed Everest, many stories look back to Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. They talk about an earlier time, before today’s commercialism and environmental damage. I asked Cool to look ahead and imagine what top climbers might do 60 years from now.

“I hate to think,” he says. He mentions the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. Steck left the mountain in April after what Cool calls “an altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck planned to climb Everest’s west ridge, first climbed in 1963. Then he would go down to the South Col and immediately climb Lhotse by a new route. He planned to do this without fixed ropes. “Ueli had been training like a machine,” Cool says. “He’s a climber in a class all his own. He’s technically brilliant but he had also taken his physical condition to an astronomic level. It would have been amazing to see what he could have done.”

What tourism will be like is a different question. One clue is a helicopter rescue done by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro’s angry words helped start the confrontation at Camp 2. On Tuesday, Moro flew back to Everest in a high-powered helicopter. He rescued a sick climber at 7,800 metres.

This was the highest rescue ever done on Everest. It shows how quickly helicopter flights have increased in recent years. By 2073, the mountain could even have a helipad on the South Col. Tourists could be brought there while breathing bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing what rescues are possible for both climbers and the many trekkers who go as far as base camp.

Mountain geographer and environmentalist Alton Byers says it is not clear if the Everest region can keep handling fast-growing tourism. He says climate change and tourism together are putting new pressure on the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers are shrinking, and some are disappearing. This is already having a big effect. “Everywhere you go, people are talking about how there’s less water. There’s less water for agriculture and less water for all the new lodges that are getting built.”

In the Sherpa town of Namche Bazaar, Byers says, a new five-mile pipeline is being built to bring water for tourists who want showers and flush toilets. The local stream has been polluted by human waste. It also does not have enough water when it is high season and the town is very crowded. “Every village is digging a pit just beyond the houses for garbage. Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world,” he says. Human waste at base camp is now handled well and taken away in plastic barrels. But Byers says the barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley. That pit could leak into the region’s water.

“These problems can be solved, but we need to get serious about it,” he says. “One climber can spend $85,000 climbing Everest. And that’s fine. But at some point we’re going to have to address these other priorities. For half a million dollars a year, you could solve most of them.”

Climate change is harder to deal with. Byers works with local conservation committees to plan for climate change. They often look for new water sources or use rainwater harvesting. Another danger is that glacial lakes are growing fast. These lakes could burst and flood the Sherpa homeland. “There’s going to come a time when people are going to have to get out of their way.”

Changing weather is also affecting tourism. More clouds during times that are usually clear are closing Lukla Airport more often. Lukla Airport is the main entry to the Everest region. A new road for 4x4s is being built to Lukla to keep tourists and money coming. But Byers worries the road building in Nepal is being done cheaply. He says this can cause serious soil erosion and landslides.

“Everest is the icon everyone knows,” he says. “It’s the canary in the coalmine that everyone understands. It’s the perfect laboratory for figuring out how to address some of these problems, like the impacts of climate change and tourism.”
