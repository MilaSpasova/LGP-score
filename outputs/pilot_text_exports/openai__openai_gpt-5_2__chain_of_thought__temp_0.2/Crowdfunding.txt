This invention could be as useful as Trevor Baylis’s wind-up radio was in the pre-internet 1990s. It is a cheap light that uses free power from gravity. It could stop people in Africa and India from using dangerous kerosene lamps.

British designer Patrick Hunt wanted to sell his invention. He first tried normal funding from banks or venture capital firms. But he had a problem. He said getting funding was “slow and complex,” the idea was “unproven,” and nobody wanted to take a risk.

So he tried crowdfunding on a US website called Indiegogo, which had recently opened in the UK. In five days, he reached his goal and raised £36,200. By the end of 2012, his campaign was so popular that in 40 days he raised £400,000.

The LED light uses a dynamo. The dynamo is powered when a 10kg bag of rocks slowly drops down. The bag is attached to the light and lifted to about 2m high. As it slowly falls to the ground, it makes enough power for 30 minutes of light.

Hunt is getting ready to make the light in China. He will test the market again by sending 1,000 lights to Africa before full mass production. He hopes to make millions of units.

Hunt is part of a new group of entrepreneurs who use the fast-growing crowdfunding industry for money. Another new website is InvestingZone. It matches wealthy individuals with start-up entrepreneurs.

Indiegogo does not offer shares. Instead, it lets users offer “perks” for different donation levels. People who helped fund Hunt’s light felt good about helping poorer people, and they also got their own light.

Danae Ringelmann, a co-founder of Indiegogo, says the “gravity light” shows how crowdfunding can test an idea. She says finance can be fast and fair because it is not about knowing people in a bank or a venture capital firm. It is about proving your worth to customers and fans, getting them to support your idea, and getting them to fund it.

She also says even ideas that do not get funding are still worth testing. That way, you find out sooner if an idea is not good, and you learn faster.

Ringelmann lives in the US. She started her career as a Wall Street analyst. In 2008, she quit and used her skills to help friends in the arts raise money.

At first, the site focused on films. It launched at the Sundance Film Festival that year.

Five years later, Indiegogo was raising about $2m each week for new businesses that were starting or growing. In December 2012, it launched services in euros and pounds sterling to build a place in Europe. It says Britain is its third biggest market. It also says international activity is up 41% since December.

There are many competitors, including Kickstarter, Seedrs, and Funding Circle. But Ringelmann says Indiegogo is different because anyone can start a campaign. No project is called too strange.

Indiegogo charges a 4% fee for successful campaigns. If a campaign does not reach its goal, users can choose to refund all money to donors at no charge. Or they can keep the money raised, but then the fee is 9%.

A British woman, Lauren Pears, raised £100,000 on the site to open a “cat café” in London. It is called Lady Dinah’s Cat Emporium. It has not opened yet, but it is described as a place where people can “come in from the cold to a comfortable wingback chair, a hot cup of tea, a book and a cat”.

Ringelmann says some projects are rejected by venture capitalists. They may say, “Great idea but no idea if the market actually wants it; it could be a gadget that no one cares about.”

She says entrepreneurs can run an Indiegogo campaign even before they launch the project. The campaign can be proof that people want it. Then venture capitalists may decide there is a market.

She says it helps entrepreneurs test their market, pricing, and features. It can also help them find new revenue streams and get important feedback.

Because of her Wall Street experience, and because she has helped 100,000 businesses and services raise money, Ringelmann gives advice to new entrepreneurs. She says, “Ideas are a dime a dozen.” She says success depends on doing the work well. She also says that if you fear someone will steal your idea and do it better and faster, then you may not be the right person to carry it out. She says it is about being confident, moving fast, and learning.

Ringelmann says expanding into Europe, and making a deal with a web payment provider, are part of a dream to make finance open to more people. The deal will allow payments through local card services like Maestro in the UK and Carte Bleue in France, as well as PayPal. More than 7,000 campaigns are live on the site.

Crowdfunding as an option instead of banks has grown. But it is not as attractive to big investors, who usually want a stake in a promising business. This could start to change in the UK with the launch of InvestingZone.
