Kenton Cool usually likes to talk, but now he can hardly speak. Climbing high mountains has made his voice rough. He is now in Kathmandu, the capital of Nepal. He flew down from Everest base camp that morning. He is thinking about an amazing set of climbs he finished the weekend before.

Early Saturday morning, he reached the top of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of three main summits in the Everest “horseshoe.” This horseshoe surrounds the icy valley called the Western Cwm.

Later that same day, he climbed to the top of Everest. He reached the summit in complete darkness early on Sunday. Then he and his climbing partner kept going. On Monday morning, they reached the top of Lhotse, the third mountain in this three-peak challenge.

“It was a snatched opportunity,” he says. “For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. That does not take away the physical achievement of what I did. I have set the bar at a certain level. But whoever comes next will move the bar further and do it without ropes or bottled oxygen.”

Sixty years after Everest was first climbed, many stories look back to Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. They compare their time to today’s time of commercialism and environmental degradation. I ask Cool to look ahead and imagine what top climbers might be doing 60 years from now.

“I hate to think,” he says. But he talks about the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. Steck left the mountain in April after what Cool calls “an altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck planned to climb Everest’s west ridge, first climbed in 1963. Then he would go down to the South Col and immediately climb Lhotse by a new route, all without fixed ropes. “Ueli had been training like a machine,” Cool says. “He’s a climber in a class all his own. He’s technically brilliant, and he had also taken his physical condition to an astronomic level. It would have been amazing to see what he could have done.”

Tourism is a different question. One clue is a helicopter rescue done by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro’s angry language helped start the confrontation at Camp 2. On Tuesday, Moro flew back to Everest in a high-powered helicopter. He rescued a sick climber at 7,800 metres.

It was the highest rescue ever done on Everest. It also shows the fast growth of helicopter flights in recent years. By 2073, the mountain could even have a helipad on the South Col. Tourists could arrive there using bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing how rescues can happen for climbers and for the many trekkers who go as far as base camp.

Mountain geographer and environmentalist Alton Byers says it is not clear if the Everest region can keep handling fast-growing tourism. He says climate change and tourism together are putting new pressure on the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers are shrinking, and some are disappearing. This is already having a big effect. “Everywhere you go, people are talking about how there’s less water,” he says. “There’s less water for agriculture and less water for all the new lodges that are getting built.”

In the Sherpa town of Namche Bazaar, Byers says a new five-mile pipeline is being built to bring more water. It is needed for the growing tourist demand for showers and flush toilets. The local stream is polluted with human waste. It also does not have enough water for a place that, in high season, is very crowded. “Every village is digging a pit just beyond the houses for garbage. Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world,” he says. Human waste at base camp is now handled well and taken away in plastic barrels. But Byers says these barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley, and it could leak into the region’s watercourses.

“These problems can be solved, but we need to get serious about it,” he says. “One climber can spend $85,000 climbing Everest. And that’s fine. But at some point we’re going to have to address these other priorities. For half a million dollars a year, you could solve most of them.”

Climate change is harder to deal with. Byers works with local conservation committees to find and plan for climate change effects. They often look for new water sources or set up rainwater harvesting. Another danger is that glacial lakes are growing quickly. These lakes could burst and flood the Sherpa homeland. “There’s going to come a time when people are going to have to get out of their way,” he says.

Changing weather is also affecting tourism. More cloud cover during times that are usually clear is closing Lukla Airport more often. Lukla Airport is the main entry to the Everest region. A new road for 4x4s is being built to Lukla to keep tourists and money coming. But Byers worries that Nepal is building roads cheaply and quickly. He says this can cause serious soil erosion and landslides.

“Everest is the icon everyone knows,” he says. “It’s the canary in the coalmine that everyone understands. It’s the perfect laboratory for figuring out how to address some of these problems, like the impacts of climate change and tourism.”
