Opposition to Western Australia’s shark cull has grown. Thousands of people went to beaches around Australia to ask the state’s premier to end the policy. RSPCA Australia and Virgin Atlantic owner Richard Branson also spoke against it.

The policy involves catching and killing sharks longer than three metres. It started after what the state government called an “unprecedented” number of shark attacks on Western Australia’s coast. A 35-year-old surfer was killed in November 2013. He was the sixth person to die from a shark attack in two years.

However, the Shark Attack File says that Australia has averaged one shark-related death a year for the last 50 years.

Kate Faehrmann, a board member at the Sea Shepherd Conservation Society, spoke at a protest in Perth. She said the policy will not work. She said drumlines are used to catch sharks, but they can kill many animals. She said they can kill sharks of any size, and also dolphins, turtles, and other animals. She said this is why the community does not want the policy.

Thousands of people protested at Perth’s Cottesloe Beach and Sydney’s Manly Beach. Hundreds protested at Glenelg, in south-west Adelaide, and at beaches in Victoria and Queensland. Faehrmann said the protests showed that many Australians want sharks protected. She said that even though people fear sharks because of movies like Jaws, many people are still coming out to say, “That’s their ocean. We respect them, we love them and we don’t want them killed.”

Anthony Joyce, a surfer who once had his foot caught in a shark’s mouth, said killing sharks will make no real difference.

The state government has refused to give an ongoing total of how many sharks have been killed. There have been reports that sharks smaller than three metres were released after being caught. Drumlines are floating drums fixed to the sea bed, with bait on hooks under them.

Conservationists say there is no evidence the cull will reduce shark attacks on humans. They say no earlier cull has used only drumlines. Researchers at the University of Western Australia say the recent group of shark attacks may be linked to Western Australia having the fastest-growing population in Australia, not to more sharks.

Richard Peirce, chairman of the UK-based conservation charity the Shark Trust, said the cull would not work and could bring more predators near the coast. He said the policy adds to the tragedy of shark attacks. He said the government ignored good advice and chose a plan that is ineffective and causes harm. He also said that even if drumlines are checked during the day, leaving them in overnight could attract other predators. Those predators may come because sharks and other animals are hooked and injured.

Worldwide in 2012, there were 80 unprovoked shark attacks, and seven were fatal. In the same year, humans killed nearly 100m sharks. RSPCA Australia said it believes the cull is unjustified. It said there is no evidence that more attacks are happening because there are more sharks. Instead, it said it fits with changes in population and human behaviour, meaning more people are in the water.

Richard Branson told Fairfax Radio the policy was backfiring. He said Premier Colin Barnett may have thought it would help tourism, but he thinks it will do the opposite. He said it makes the problem look bigger than it is and may stop people from coming to Perth and the countryside around it. He said it will only make people worry for no reason.
