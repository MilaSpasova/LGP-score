From glow-in-the-dark trees to underground bike sheds and solar-powered bins, there are some unusual ideas to make cities nicer places to live.

Living in a city has many good things. But living in a way that is good for the planet is getting harder. People deal with pollution, traffic, and fewer green spaces every day.

Now inventors are trying to help. Here are some of the wackier ideas to make cities more livable.

Pop-up parks
Some cities look like they are built more for cars than for people. A pop-up park is a simple idea. Use an empty car park, a little money, and a plant or two, and turn the space into a small park.

The PARK(ing) project started as an art experiment in San Francisco. It has spread around the world. People have also tried temporary city farms and ecology demonstrations. The movement has a day of action every September.

Subterranean storage
Not everyone in a city uses a car. Bikes are becoming more popular. But where can you keep a bike safe?

A Tokyo-based engineering firm called Giken made an idea called an “eco-cycle, anti-seismic underground bicycle park.” It is a round storage space that is only seven metres wide. It goes deep underground and can hold 204 bikes. Owners can get their bike back by pressing a button. The system brings the bike up in about 13 seconds.

Glow-in-the-dark trees
Most people think of glowing trees as Christmas decorations. But Daan Roosegaarde does not.

This Dutch designer-artist created an experimental “bioluminescent” plant. The technology mixes DNA from glowing marine bacteria with part of a plant’s cells, so the plant can glow like a jellyfish. Tests are happening to make a large-scale version. Roosegaarde hopes it could one day replace normal street lights.

Footfall harvesting
Every day, hundreds of commuters and shoppers in West Ham in east London cross a raised walkway near the underground station. Few people notice that the ground feels a little springy. Even fewer people link the five-millimetre bend in the rubber surface to powering the streetlights above.

The floor has smart tiles that capture energy from people’s footsteps and turn it into electricity. Pavegen, the UK firm behind the idea, has put a similar system in London’s Heathrow Airport and in other places around the world.

Supertrees
One day, people were sure to make man-made trees. Singapore’s Gardens by the Bay has a small group of them.

These steel-framed “supertrees” are up to 50 metres high. Flowers and ferns grow on them. Their metal tops also help take in and spread out heat. They can collect rainwater too. They also help move air for two “climate-controlled biomes” (large conservatories) below. Eleven of the 18 trees also have solar panels on their “branches.”
