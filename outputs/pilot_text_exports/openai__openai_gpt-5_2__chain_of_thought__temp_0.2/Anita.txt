Brazil’s newest funk star, Anitta, has won millions of fans. She helped bring the sound of the favela into popular music. But people are now arguing about her skin colour.

Campaigners against discrimination and some writers say Anitta had to give up looking black to succeed in a market that is mostly white and middle class.

The argument grew after “before and after” photos were shared. They show Anitta’s skin looking much lighter after she signed a deal with Warner.

In the first photo, when she was not well known, she looked darker. In the second photo, a marketing picture after she became famous, she looks paler. Some people think this could be from skin-whitening products, cosmetic surgery, or—more likely—Photoshop. The big difference made people talk again about whether you need to be pale to succeed in Brazil.

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote a post about hidden discrimination in media and marketing. She said many people do not accept that they are racist. She said they think Brazil is a multiracial democracy, but statistics show it is not. She said whitening shows a society that does not support diversity. She said people often link white skin with being rich, nice, successful, and good, and link black skin with the opposite.

Anitta was born Larissa de Macedo Machado. When she was a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

She later became very famous with an album and a big hit song, Show das Poderosas. The song reached number one and got 52 million views on YouTube.

Many fans love her as a pop star with a strong message and catchy songs. People who support her present her as a bridge between the mostly black and mixed-race shanty towns on Rio’s hills and the richer and whiter areas below.

She has made baile funk less shocking for a wider audience. She has reduced sexual dancing, gang-style references, and very direct lyrics. Now, some people ask if she—or her marketing team—changed her image too much to attract a richer audience. Arraes said that if pop stars have curly hair, they may feel forced to straighten it. If they have a big nose, they may feel forced to get a nose surgery (rhinoplasty). She said this can create a harmful cycle for self-esteem.

This topic is sensitive in Brazil, where many people are mixed race. Brazil was one of the last large countries to end slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. In Brazil, people often focus more on skin colour than on race or family background. Even though Brazil aims to be a multiracial democracy, there is a clear link between skin colour and inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Until 2011, black or mixed-race students also spent about two fewer years in school on average.

The government says the gap is getting smaller because of quota systems for university places and other affirmative action programs. But the difference is still easy to see. Most business and government leaders are white. Many low-paying jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In places like Ipanema and Gávea and other rich areas, you are more likely to see black nannies pushing strollers with white toddlers than the other way around.

Skin colour categories can be complicated. In the 2010 census, people who called themselves white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million said they were black, two million said they were Asian, and 0.5% said they were indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology teacher at the Federal University of Pernambuco, thinks Anitta won middle-class fans by taking a rebellious sound and making it calmer and easier for more people to like.

He said this was not done by racial whitening. He said what changed was the social place where Anitta made her art: from the periphery to the centre.

Other people say the colour issue is too big. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who knew Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot, but not her colour.

He said he does not believe it was whitening. He said it was more about makeup, hairstyling, and clothing. He said he did not think she was being pressured. He said she always liked to show off, sing, and dance, and that it was natural for her. He said he believes this natural way is why she is successful today.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador in the north-east, said the prejudice was not mainly from the public. He said it was from music producers and media bosses. He said the public showed they care about music itself by choosing Ellen Oléria—a black lesbian—as the recent winner of the talent show The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for a comment. But in a recent interview, she said identity should be chosen by the person.

She said an “all-powerful” woman does not need to be beautiful. She said a woman can be marvellous and powerful because of her attitude. She said she tries to show that we can be who we want.
