Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8 April 2014, she told about a very special moment. She received a message in a bottle. Her grandfather had thrown the bottle into the Baltic Sea 101 years earlier. People think it may be the oldest message in a bottle in the world. A museum in Germany, which is showing it, gave it to her.

Erdmann, 62, said it was very surprising. She said a man came to her door and told her he had mail from her grandfather. He said a bottle with a message had been found, and the name on the card was her grandfather’s. The man was a family history researcher. He found her in Berlin after the letter was given to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg.

The bottle was a brown beer bottle. It had been in the water for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it in his catch. He was fishing in the Baltic Sea near the northern city of Kiel. Holger von Neuhoff, who works at the museum, said this bottle message was the oldest he had seen. He said the museum has older documents, but they were found without a bottle. With both the bottle and the document, he said, this one is the oldest for now. He said it is in very good condition.

Researchers believe Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913. He was hiking with a group that enjoyed nature. He was 20 years old then.

Much of the postcard could not be read. But people could read the Berlin address on the front. They could also read a polite request from the writer. He asked the person who found it to send the note to his home address.

Erdmann said her grandfather also put two stamps from that time in the bottle. This was so the finder would not have to pay to send the card. But, she said, he did not think it would take 101 years.

She said the message made her feel emotional. She had not known her grandfather because he died at age 54, six years before she was born.

She said she knew very little about him. But she learned he was a writer who was open-minded. She said he believed in freedom and thought people should respect each other. She said he did a lot for young people and later traveled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful because she could see where her roots came from.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and traveling around the world. She said she is open-minded too. She said what her grandfather taught his two daughters, her mother taught her, and she then taught her sons.

Even though she was happy to get the message, she said she hopes other people will not throw bottles with messages into the sea. She said the sea today is already full of bottles and trash.

The message and the bottle will be on display at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts will try to read the rest of the text. It is not clear what will happen to the bottle later, but Erdmann hopes it will stay at the museum.

She said they want to share a few photos to go with the bottle. This way, visitors can see the young man who threw the bottle into the water.
