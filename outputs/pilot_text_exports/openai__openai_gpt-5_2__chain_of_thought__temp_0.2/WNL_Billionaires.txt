A thick layer of bird droppings covers a gold-painted railing at one of Britain’s most expensive homes. Dead pigeons are inside. Mirrors are broken, and water runs through broken ceiling edges. This home is called The Towers. It is a £30m palace on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London. People have kept its damage secret until now.

The Towers is one of ten big houses in the middle of The Bishops Avenue. This street is a key place in London’s rising home market. These houses have been almost empty for about 25 years. It is believed they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. Their Greek-style columns are breaking. Their pools with mosaic tiles are filled with broken pieces. Nature has taken over so much that owls now live there.

This sad view is seen along this street. Lloyds Bank says The Bishops Avenue is the second most expensive street in Britain. At the same time, more people in London struggle to buy a home. House prices rise by 11.2% a year. Yet 16 mansions on the most expensive part of The Bishops Avenue sit empty. Many are behind locked gates. Windows are covered with steel bars, and the gardens are overgrown. Guard dogs patrol some grounds.

Across the street is another empty mansion worth £18m. It has broken windows and anti-climb paint on the walls. Another house has metal bars on its windows. It was sold for £20m.

Still, prices keep going up. Dryades, a mansion once owned by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007. It is believed to be worth about £30m today. Heath Lodge is where fashion businessman Aristos Constantinou was murdered in 1984 by a silver bullet. Heath Lodge is worth £13m today. It was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

For people trying to afford housing in one of the world’s most expensive cities, these empty mansions can feel very painful. One security guard said it was upsetting to see so much space falling apart. He said it was enough space to house dozens of people.

Rich royals from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia were some of the first to come to this curved road near Hampstead Heath. Iranians came here after the fall of the shah. Now, Chinese buyers are following Russians and Kazakhs. They have paid millions to live on a street that agents say is as famous as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions were for sale for £65m and £38m. They promised lots of Italian marble, lifts with leather padding, and luxury panic rooms.

But on the empty properties, stone fountains are breaking and lawns have turned into wet mud. Inside one house, water drips through a huge crystal chandelier. It falls onto a thick carpet that is rotting under parts of a collapsed ceiling. Moss grows through broken bricks. Broken mirror tiles cover a bathroom floor. The swimming pool has about a foot of dirty salty water, and flowers grow through the tiles. Wood pieces on the sauna bend outward.

The most dramatic ruin is The Towers. It is a large home surrounded by acres of hornbeams, oaks, and limes. Pigeons fill the large halls. Water pours down through three storeys. The walls have turned bright green from algae. The water drops into a huge, empty basement swimming pool. Unopened wooden crates marked “bullet-proof glass” show the old owners were worried about security.

Today, very few people live on The Bishops Avenue all year. One security guard said the owners of one mansion were not there. Another guard outside Royal Mansion would not say if anyone was home. A worker at another mansion warned the Guardian about the guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare mogul, said he is one of the few full-time residents. His personal wealth is £45m.

He said, “It’s not a neighbourly place, where you can chat over the fence. To be honest, I have never seen what my neighbours look like. Next door, a Saudi princess spent £35m on a new house and I’ve never seen her. There are about three houses that are lived in 24/7 and half of the properties are occupied three to six months a year. The other half, who knows if they come or not?”

These broken, very expensive homes show a property culture where the richest people in the world treat British homes as investments. One Hyde Park, a group of flats in Knightsbridge, is another example. More than half of the flats there are registered with the council as empty or second homes.

Even so, people on the avenue talk about building £5m apartments instead of £50m mansions, to bring people back.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, plans to rebuild one very valuable site on the avenue. He wants to build 20 apartments with a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

He said, “If you build a big house and try and sell for £30m to £40m, it won’t sell. Locals won’t buy and so you have to bring in overseas buyers.”

But using the empty homes to help fix the housing crisis still seems far away. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some of the empty homes could become affordable homes. He said the land price would be too high.

He said, “Very wealthy people own property there. Sometimes they live in them and sometimes they don’t.”
