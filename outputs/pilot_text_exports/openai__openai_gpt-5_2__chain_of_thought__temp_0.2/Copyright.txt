Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is theft. He says it is like putting your hand in someone’s pocket and stealing their wallet. He wrote this in an article for Index on Censorship. Pullman is president of the Society of Authors. He strongly supports copyright laws. He is very critical of people who download music or books without paying.

Pullman writes that the technology looks very impressive, so some people do not see that what they are doing is wrong. He says it is shocking that someone can steal an artist’s work and not be punished. He repeats that it is theft, just like stealing a wallet.

His article came after music industry leaders met British Prime Minister David Cameron at Downing Street. They talked about web piracy.

Pullman, who wrote the His Dark Materials trilogy, says authors and musicians often work for years in poverty and without being known. They work hard to make something that audiences enjoy. But when they finally succeed, he says they can lose the chance to earn a living from it because people take it for free. He ends by saying the rule is simple: if we want to enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

In the next issue of the group’s magazine, Pullman is in a discussion with Cathy Casserly. She is the chief executive of Creative Commons. Creative Commons offers open content licences that let creators control how they use copyright. Casserly says many things are wrong with copyright, which was made in an “analogue age.” She says copyright often stops people from sharing, building on, and remixing each other’s work in many ways.

She says artists need to think of new ways to share their work and make money from it. She quotes science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees. The magazine’s editor, Rachael Jolley, said today’s copyright laws do not work well in the digital age and could make ordinary consumers into criminals. She said we need new ways for artists, writers, and musicians to earn money from their work.

Many people download illegally. Data from Ofcom (the independent regulator and competition authority for the UK communications industries) suggests that in the UK, between November 2012 and January 2013, 280 million music tracks were pirated. Also, 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files were pirated.

Ofcom has said 18% of internet users aged over 12 admit they have recently pirated content. Another 9% say they are afraid of getting caught. Pullman writes that it is still amazing how quickly people can get music as MP3 downloads, especially for people who have been building up their iTunes list for some time.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron appointed Conservative MP Mike Weatherley as his adviser on this issue. A spokesman for the BPI, the record industry trade body, said Mike Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creative producers that it protects. The spokesman said they hope Weatherley’s influence, and the prime minister’s support, will affect the approach of the UK’s intellectual property office.
