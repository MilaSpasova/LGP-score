It was a beautiful summer evening. I decided to go for a swim near Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland, where I moved in 2012. A woman was in the water with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty has a close relationship with a group of people she often swims with.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Since then, hundreds of people have swum with her. This made it seem like she was completely tame. She even appeared in an Irish tourist-board ad, where a girl says she would like to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty’s tummy. It looked very tempting. About 20 tourists and local people stood on the pier watching. Soon after I got into the water, Dusty left the woman and suddenly went wild. I learned later that she can be very protective of her space when she is with someone. Her tail was flapping hard. At first, I thought she was just showing off. Then I realized she might be angry.

I knew I had to get out, so I swam toward the pier. But almost at once, Dusty slammed into me with her nose. It was very strong and very painful. She was moving incredibly fast, and I was thrown forward.

Everyone on the pier stared down at me with their mouths open. Dusty was still beside me in the water, and her tail kept flapping. That scared me most. I thought that if she hit me with her tail, I could go under the water and die.

I reached the pier, but I could not climb out because I was hurt. I felt pure terror. I shouted for help. A man reached in and pulled me out onto the steps.

Then another man came. He said he was an orthopaedic surgeon who specialized in injuries from the sea. He had been driving into Doolin when he saw what was happening on the pier. I was very cold and very worried. I did not know how bad my injuries were, and I was most afraid of bleeding inside my body. He examined me and tried to calm me. He said he could not feel signs of internal bleeding, but he thought I probably had broken bones. Later I learned I had six spinal fractures, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in the hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for several weeks. I could not work for five months. I had limited movement, stiffness, and pain. Then I was diagnosed with post-traumatic stress. After my near-death experience, I felt anxious about everything. I overreacted in a way I never had before. I felt like people were looking at me the wrong way. Loud noises became hard to handle. I also had memory loss.

Three months before the accident, I had opened a health-food shop. But I had to give it up because I could not work anymore. It was the toughest year ever, but now it is behind me. I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building up my own osteopathy practice. I understand patients better because I have been one.

I am thankful that I am healthy, and I really want to stop other people from getting hurt. People often think dolphins are always friendly and safe. Who would think a dolphin could attack a person? If you see a fierce animal coming at you with its teeth showing, you feel scared. But dolphins have a wide smile that looks friendly.

I am not angry at Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her space, and she is a wild and unpredictable animal. People need to know that. Many people come to swim with her, and they do not understand how dangerous it can be. My injuries were said to be among several injuries that summer.

After the man pulled me out, Dusty swam away. Then she came back. She floated upright next to me and looked at me. We looked into each other’s eyes, and I felt she was sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin. The anger was gone. The people on the pier watched in amazement. When she had that quiet moment with me, the terror ended. I made peace with her.
