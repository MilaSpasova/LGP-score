James Hamblin is a senior editor at the American magazine The Atlantic. In his series, “If Our Bodies Could Talk,” he tried a challenge: he stopped showering so much. He took fewer showers and stopped using shampoo and soap when he did shower.

He learned something many people have noticed. When we try very hard to clean our bodies with soap and body wash, our skin works harder to get back to normal. Showering can wash away the skin’s natural oil and helpful bacteria. This can be even stronger when you use hot water and cleaning products.

Have you ever felt tight and dry after a shower? That happens because your skin’s natural moisture gets washed away. Also, your skin has millions of helpful bacteria, like your gut does. Frequent showering and scrubbing can kill many of these bacteria. When the bacteria grow back, they may include more kinds that make bad smells. So showering too often may sometimes make you smell more. If you stop showering and using soap, your skin may go through a first period that feels unpleasant. After that, the skin often gets back in balance, makes less oil, and healthy bacteria grow again.

After his challenge, Hamblin agreed with other people who use less soap and shower less: the human body can work well on its own. This is not only about smell or looks. Dermatologists say using less soap can help acne and eczema. Showering less can also help the environment. The average shower lasts seven minutes and uses 65 litres of water. That is 65 litres of clean, drinkable water mixed with soap and sent down the drain every day, sometimes more than once.

Clean water is becoming more important, as California goes into another summer of drought. It is clear that clean water is one of the world’s most valuable resources, and it may soon be in short supply. When you also think about all the body wash bottles, there are strong reasons to use fewer products and be more natural.

If this idea makes you feel uncomfortable because you remember being close to people who do not shower much and still smell, don’t worry. Many people who shower less still use deodorant if they need it. Everyone’s natural smell is different, and it can change because of things like diet, how much water you drink, and exercise. Also, washing your hands with soap is still recommended to help stop the spread of infectious diseases.

You do not have to stop showering all at once like Hamblin did. But there is a middle choice between cleaning many times a day and never showering. Shower a little less, use less soap, and let your skin’s helpful bacteria grow.
