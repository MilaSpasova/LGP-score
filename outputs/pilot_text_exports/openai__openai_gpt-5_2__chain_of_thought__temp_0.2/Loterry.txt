The lottery balls have fallen down the chute and all six numbers match. So some people think it is time to buy an Audi, book a holiday in the US, and call an estate agent. An analysis says this is what many lottery millionaires do.

Since it started in 1994, the lottery has made 3,000 millionaires. Together they have won more than £8.5bn, with an average of £2.8m each. The study says their money also helped make 3,780 more millionaires among their children, family, and friends. The authors of the study were Oxford Economics.

Most winners (59%) stop working right away. But 19% keep doing their job, and 31% do unpaid volunteer work. The study says 98% of winners’ spending stayed in the UK. By spending money on homes, vehicles, and holidays, each winner is estimated to support six full-time jobs for one year.

The winners added almost £750m to gross domestic product (GDP). They also created more than £500m in tax money for the Exchequer. Most of the money was spent on property. Winners spent £2.72bn on their main homes, and £170m on paying off debts and mortgages.

Keeping a steady income was important, so £2.125bn was spent on investments. Gifts to family and friends were £1.17bn. Also, £680m was spent on cars and holidays.

Camelot, the company that runs the UK National Lottery, asked for the study to celebrate having 3,000 winners. The study used research from 100 winners who won £1m or more. It found that all 3,000 winners bought 7,958 houses or flats in the UK, or 2.7 each, and spent £3.3bn. Most winners (82%) changed their main home, spending an average of £900,000.

Many winners added special things to their new homes. Almost a third (29%) bought a hot tub. A walk-in wardrobe was important for 28%. Almost a quarter (24%) chose a home behind electric gates. Also, 22% had a games room, and 7% put in a snooker table.

Bigger homes need care. So 30% of winners hired a cleaner and 24% hired a gardener. A small number (5%) hired a beautician.

Audis were the top choice for 16% of winners. Range Rovers and BMWs were also popular (11% each), as well as Mercedes (10%) and Land Rovers (5%). Winners spent £463m on 17,190 cars. The average price of their favourite car was £46,116.

Holidays were important too. Most winners (68%) chose five-star hotels in other countries. The US was the top place to visit for 27%, followed by the Caribbean (9%). In the UK, caravan sales also went up. In the past 18 years, 10% of millionaires bought a caravan, creating about £7.4m in sales.

Some winners (15%) started their own businesses. Another 9% helped other people start businesses, and 6% invested in or bought other people’s businesses. The study says these businesses employ 3,195 people.

Andy Logan, who wrote the report, said a win spreads further than expected. He said it changes the lives of friends and family, and each win also affects the UK economy because so much money is spent in the UK. He said each win creates a ripple effect for this generation and often the next.
