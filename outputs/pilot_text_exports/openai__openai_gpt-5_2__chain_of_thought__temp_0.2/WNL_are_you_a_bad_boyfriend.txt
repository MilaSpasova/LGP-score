Dan Sullivan arrived late to breakfast with the Guardian. He was late because police pulled him over while he was riding his motorcycle.

Sullivan says he works too much. He misses dinner dates. He forgets to buy presents.

So, like many people in Silicon Valley, the 27-year-old venture capitalist created a start-up to help: BetterBoyfriend.me. This service sends a present to girlfriends and wives every month for about $70.

Sullivan works in finance. He has been testing the program quietly. He has signed up about 350 boyfriends. The service makes about $17,000 in revenue each month. He says most members are his Harvard and investor friends, including venture capitalists, founders, and employees at companies like Apple, Google, and Facebook. Their girlfriends have been getting presents from Sullivan for the last year.

Each month, members choose from a list of seven gifts, such as chocolate, tea sets, and manicures. The package is sent to the boyfriend with no brand name and no label.

Sullivan tests ideas by buying Facebook ads to see how much interest they get. BetterBoyfriend.me became popular quickly. Sullivan said, “BetterBoyfriend hit something. It’s a pain point everyone has.”

BetterBoyfriend.me was not Sullivan’s first idea. For a couple of days, he thought about a dog-sharing idea. He said he saw rich guys looking miserable while walking dogs, and he realized “a dog is only valuable 5% of the time.” He asked: what if someone could babysit the dogs and other people could rent them?

Another start-up, called InvisibleBoyfriend, lets users “design and build a believable social partner who is everything you want and is always available to talk, whenever you want.” The result is a “boyfriend” who can text with you all day.

Founder Kyle Tabor started it to give users a fake boyfriend to stop unwanted advances. But he found that people became attached to their computer-based partners. He said, “Many more users are seeking companionship through conversation rather than external ‘proof’ of a relationship to get people off their back.”

For Sullivan and Tabor, the surprise was the real relationships they formed with customers. Sullivan says he has started to see himself as a kind of relationship helper for the boyfriends.

Sullivan admits he made mistakes. At first, the packages included receipts with his name, Dan Sullivan. “One of the boyfriends wrote to me and said, ‘Listen, dude, she’s not mad but Cynthia found out’.”

Sullivan says about 50% of the women who receive BetterBoyfriend.me packages know he is involved in their relationships. He says this connects with age. He thinks that after people have been married for a while, they do not keep many secrets.

Over the year, Sullivan says he has gotten to know the boyfriends very well. They even did a delivery to a hospital delivery room. He said the key is to remember that his relationship is with the boyfriend.

When he first started the company, he put BetterBoyfriend.me tags on flower bouquets. Then he went to Dolores Park, a popular place in the Mission District with many tech workers and young people.

He said, “I looked for couples and gave the bouquet to the girl but that didn’t go down well with the boyfriend. Like, not at all. It made sense, though. It was cool.” So he changed his plan and started giving the bouquets to the boyfriends.
