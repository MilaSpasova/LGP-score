From glow-in-the-dark trees to underground bike storage and solar-powered bins, there are some unusual ideas to make cities nicer places to live.

Living in a city has many good points, but living in a clean and earth-friendly way is getting harder. People have to deal with pollution, traffic, and fewer green spaces. So inventors are trying new ideas. Here are some of the wackier ones.

Pop-up parks
Some cities seem built more for cars than for people. A pop-up park is a simple idea: use an empty parking space, a little money, and a plant or two to make a small park. The PARK(ing) project started as an art experiment in San Francisco and then spread around the world. People have also made temporary city farms and ecology demonstrations. The movement has a day of action every September.

Subterranean storage
Many city people use bikes instead of cars, but they need a safe place to keep them. A Tokyo engineering company called Giken made an idea called an “eco-cycle, anti-seismic underground bicycle park.” It is a round storage space that is only seven metres wide, and it goes deep underground. It can hold 204 bikes. Owners press a button, and an automatic system brings the bike back above ground in about 13 seconds.

Glow-in-the-dark trees
When people think of glowing trees, they may think of Christmas decorations. But Dutch designer and artist Daan Roosegaarde has created a “bioluminescent” plant. The experimental technology mixes DNA from glowing marine bacteria with a plant’s chloroplast genome to make a glow like a jellyfish. Tests are happening to make a large-scale version. Roosegaarde hopes it could one day replace normal street lights.

Footfall harvesting
In West Ham in east London, many commuters and shoppers walk across a raised walkway near the underground station. Most do not notice that the ground feels a little springy. The rubber surface bends about five millimetres, and this helps power the streetlights above. The walkway has smart tiles that capture energy from footsteps and turn it into electricity. The UK company Pavegen made this system and has also installed similar tiles at London’s Heathrow Airport and in other places.

Supertrees
There are also man-made trees. Singapore’s Gardens by the Bay has a small group of them. These steel “supertrees” can be up to 50 metres tall. Flowers and ferns grow on them, and their metal tops help absorb and spread heat. They can collect rainwater and help provide air ventilation for two “climate-controlled biomes” (large conservatories) below. Eleven of the 18 trees also have solar panels on their “branches.”
