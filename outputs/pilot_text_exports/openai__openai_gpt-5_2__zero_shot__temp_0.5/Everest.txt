Kenton Cool usually likes to talk, but now he can hardly speak. Climbing high mountains made his voice rough. He is in Kathmandu, the capital of Nepal. That morning, he flew down from Everest base camp.

Cool is thinking about three big climbs he did over the weekend. Early Saturday morning, he reached the top of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of three main peaks in the Everest “horseshoe” around a icy valley called the Western Cwm.

That same day, he climbed to the top of Everest. He reached the summit in complete darkness early Sunday. Then he and his climbing partner went on to the top of Lhotse on Monday morning.

“It was a snatched opportunity,” he says. “For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. That doesn’t take away the physical achievement of what I did. I’ve set the bar at a certain level. But whoever comes along next will move the bar further and do it without ropes or bottled oxygen.”

Sixty years after Everest was first climbed, many stories look back to Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. People compare their time to today, with more business tourism and more damage to the environment. I ask Cool to look ahead and imagine what top climbers might do 60 years from now.

“I hate to think,” he says. He talks about the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. In April, Steck left the mountain after what Cool calls “an altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck planned to climb Everest’s west ridge, first climbed in 1963. Then Steck planned to go down to the South Col and climb Lhotse right away by a new route, without fixed ropes. “Ueli had been training like a machine,” Cool says. “He’s a climber in a class all his own. He’s technically brilliant but he had also taken his physical condition to an astronomic level. It would have been amazing to see what he could have done.”

What tourism will look like is a different question. One clue is a helicopter rescue by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro’s angry language helped start the confrontation at Camp 2. On Tuesday, Moro flew a powerful helicopter back to Everest to rescue a very sick or injured climber at 7,800 metres.

It was the highest rescue ever done on Everest. It also shows how quickly helicopter flights have increased in recent years. By 2073, the mountain could even have a helipad on the South Col. Tourists could be brought there while breathing bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing rescue possibilities for climbers and for many more trekkers who go as far as base camp.

Mountain geographer and environmentalist Alton Byers says it is not clear if the Everest region can handle fast-growing tourism. He says climate change and tourism together are putting new pressure on the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers are shrinking, and some are disappearing. This is already causing major effects. “Everywhere you go, people are talking about how there’s less water. There’s less water for agriculture and less water for all the new lodges that are getting built.”

Byers says that in the Sherpa town of Namche Bazaar, a new five-mile pipeline is being built to bring water for the growing number of tourists who want showers and flush toilets. The local stream is polluted with human waste. It also does not provide enough water for a place that, in high season, is extremely crowded. “Every village is digging a pit just beyond the houses for garbage. Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world,” he says. Human waste at base camp is now handled well and removed in plastic barrels. But Byers says these barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley, and it could leak into the region’s waterways.

“These problems can be solved, but we need to get serious about it,” he says. “One climber can spend $85,000 climbing Everest. And that’s fine. But at some point we’re going to have to address these other priorities. For half a million dollars a year, you could solve most of them.”

Climate change is harder. Byers works with local conservation committees to find and plan for climate change effects. They often look for new water sources or collect rainwater. Glacial lakes are growing fast, and they could burst and flood the Sherpa homeland. This danger is always there. “There’s going to come a time when people are going to have to get out of their way.”

Changing weather is also affecting tourism. More clouds during times that are usually clear are closing Lukla Airport more often. Lukla Airport is the main entry point to the Everest region. A new road for 4x4 vehicles is being built to Lukla to keep tourists and money coming. But Byers worries the road system in Nepal is spreading too fast and too cheaply. He says this can cause serious soil erosion and landslides.

“Everest is the icon everyone knows,” he says. “It’s the canary in the coalmine that everyone understands. It’s the perfect laboratory for figuring out how to address some of these problems, like the impacts of climate change and tourism.”
