James Hamblin is a senior editor at The Atlantic magazine. In his series “If Our Bodies Could Talk,” he tried a challenge: he did not shower as much, and when he did shower, he did not use shampoo or soap. He wanted to see what happens when we clean our bodies too much.

He found something many people have noticed. When we scrub our skin a lot with soap, body wash, and scrubbers, the skin tries hard to get back to normal. Showering can wash away the skin’s natural oils and bacteria. Hot water and cleaning products can make this happen even more.

Have you ever felt tight and dry after a shower? That can happen because the shower washes away natural moisture. Also, your skin has millions of helpful bacteria, like your gut does. Frequent rubbing and scrubbing can kill these bacteria. When the bacteria grow back, the ones that cause body odour may grow more, so showering too often may make you smell worse. If you stop using soap and shower less, your skin may have an early adjustment time that can seem unpleasant. After that, the skin often becomes balanced again, makes less oil, and healthy bacteria grow.

After his test, Hamblin felt the human body can be fine on its own. It is not only about smell or looks. Dermatologists say using less soap can help acne and eczema. Showering less and using fewer products can also help the environment. The average shower is seven minutes and uses 65 litres of water. That is 65 litres of clean drinking water that gets mixed with soap and goes down the drain each day, sometimes more than once.

Clean water is becoming more important, especially as California faces another summer of drought. It is becoming clear that clean water is one of the world’s most valuable resources, and it may soon be in short supply. Also, body wash bottles create environmental waste. These are reasons to let your body be more natural.

If this idea makes you feel uncomfortable, relax. Many people who shower less still use deodorant if they need it. Everyone’s natural smell is different, and it can change with diet, hydration, and exercise. Also, washing your hands with soap is still recommended to help stop infectious diseases from spreading.

You do not need to stop showering completely like Hamblin did. But between showering many times a day and never showering at all, there is a middle choice. Shower a little less, use less soap, and let your skin’s bacteria grow.
