This invention could be as important as Trevor Baylis’s wind-up radio in the 1990s, before the internet was common. It is a cheap light that uses gravity for free power. It could help stop people in Africa and India from using dangerous kerosene lamps.

British designer Patrick Hunt tried to get money from banks or venture capital to start selling his invention. But it was hard. He said funding was slow and complicated, and people did not want to take a risk on something unproven.

So he tried crowdfunding on a US website called Indiegogo, which had just started in the UK. In five days, he reached his goal and raised £36,200. By the end of 2012, after 40 days, he had raised £400,000.

The LED light uses a dynamo. The dynamo is turned by a 10kg bag of rocks that slowly falls. The bag is tied to the light and lifted about 2m high. As it falls to the ground, it makes enough power for about 30 minutes of light.

Hunt is getting ready to make the product in China. Before making many more, he plans to send 1,000 lights to Africa to test the market. He hopes later to make millions of units.

He is part of a new group of business founders using crowdfunding to raise money. Another new website is InvestingZone, which connects wealthy people with start-up entrepreneurs.

Indiegogo does not sell shares. Instead, it lets campaign owners offer “perks” for different donation amounts. People who supported Hunt’s light could feel good about helping poorer people, and they also got their own light.

Danae Ringelmann, a co-founder of Indiegogo, says the “gravity light” shows how crowdfunding can work fairly and can test an idea. She says it is fast and efficient because it is not about knowing the right people at a bank or a venture capital company. It is about proving your idea to customers and fans, and getting them to support it.

She also says even ideas that do not get enough money are still worth testing. Then you learn sooner that the idea was not good, and you become smarter faster.

Ringelmann lives in the US. She first worked as a Wall Street analyst. In 2008 she quit and used her skills to help friends in the arts raise money.

At first, the website focused on films. It launched at the Sundance Film Festival that year.

Five years later, it was raising about $2m each week for new businesses that are starting or growing. In December 2012, it added euro and sterling services to grow in Europe. It says Britain is its third biggest market. International activity has risen 41% since December.

Indiegogo has many competitors, like Kickstarter, Seedrs, and Funding Circle. But Ringelmann says Indiegogo is different because anyone can start a campaign. No project is seen as too strange.

Indiegogo charges a 4% fee for campaigns that reach their goal. If a campaign does not reach its goal, users can either return all the money to donors for no charge, or keep the money raised and pay a 9% fee.

A British woman, Lauren Pears, raised £100,000 on the site to open a “cat café” in London. It is called Lady Dinah’s Cat Emporium. It has not opened yet, but it is described as a place where people can sit in a comfortable wingback chair, have hot tea, read a book, and spend time with a cat.

Ringelmann says some ideas are rejected by venture capitalists because they are not sure people will buy them. She says entrepreneurs can run an Indiegogo campaign, and even if they do not launch the project yet, the campaign can show that there is a real market.

She says crowdfunding lets you test your market, price, and features, find new ways to make money, and get important feedback.

Because of her Wall Street experience and helping 100,000 businesses and services raise money, Ringelmann has advice for new entrepreneurs. She says ideas are very common, and what matters is doing the work well. She says if you fear someone will steal your idea and do it better and faster, then you are not the right person to carry it out. She says you need confidence to move fast and learn.

Ringelmann says expanding into Europe is part of a dream to make finance more open to everyone. Indiegogo also has a deal with a web transaction provider, so people can pay using local card services like Maestro in the UK and Carte Bleue in France, and also PayPal. More than 7,000 funding campaigns are live on the site.

Crowdfunding has grown as another choice besides banks. But it is not as attractive to very rich investors, who usually want a stake in a business. This may start to change in the UK with the launch of InvestingZone.
