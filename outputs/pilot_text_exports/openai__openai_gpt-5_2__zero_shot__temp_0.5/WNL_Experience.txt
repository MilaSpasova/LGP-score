One beautiful summer evening, I decided to go swimming at Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland. I moved there in 2012. In the water, there was a woman with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty often swims with a group of people, and she seems close to them.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Since then, hundreds of people have swum with her. This makes people think she is completely tame. She was even in an Irish tourist-board advertisement, where a girl says she wants to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty’s tummy, and it looked friendly. About 20 tourists and local people were on the pier watching. Soon after I got into the water, Dusty left the woman and suddenly became very wild. I learned later that she is very territorial when she is with someone. Her tail was flapping hard. At first I thought she was only showing off, but then I realized she might be angry.

I knew I had to get out, so I swam toward the pier. But very quickly, Dusty hit me with her nose. It was extremely strong and painful, and she was very fast. I was thrown forward.

People on the pier stared at me with their mouths open. Dusty stayed beside me, still flapping her tail hard. That scared me most. I thought if she hit me with her tail, I could go under the water and die.

I reached the pier, but I could not get out because I was hurt. I felt terrified. I shouted for help. A man reached in and pulled me onto the steps. Then another man arrived. He said he was an orthopaedic surgeon who specialized in marine trauma. He was driving into Doolin and saw what was happening.

I was very cold and very worried. I did not know how bad my injuries were. I was most afraid of internal bleeding. The surgeon checked me and tried to calm me. He said he could not feel signs of internal bleeding, but he thought I probably had broken bones. Later I learned I had six fractures in my spine, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in the hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for several weeks. I could not work for five months, and I had limited movement, stiffness, and pain. Then doctors said I had post-traumatic stress. After the near-death experience, I became anxious and reacted strongly in ways I never had before. I felt people were looking at me the wrong way. Loud noises bothered me, and I had memory loss.

Three months before the accident, I had opened a health-food shop. But I had to give it up because I could not work anymore. It was the hardest year of my life, but now it is in the past. I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building my own osteopathy practice. I understand my patients better because I have been a patient.

I am thankful that I am healthy, and I want to stop other people from getting hurt. People often have a sweet idea about dolphins and trust them. Who thinks a dolphin would attack a person? If a dangerous animal comes at you with its teeth showing, it looks scary. But dolphins have a wide smile.

I am not angry with Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her space, and she is a wild animal that can be unpredictable. People need to understand that. Many people come to swim with her, and they do not know how dangerous it can be. People said my injuries were among several injuries that summer.

After the man pulled me out, Dusty swam away. Then she came back. She floated upright next to me and looked at me. We looked into each other’s eyes, and I felt she was sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin, and the anger was gone. The people on the pier were amazed. After that moment, my terror ended. I felt at peace with her.
