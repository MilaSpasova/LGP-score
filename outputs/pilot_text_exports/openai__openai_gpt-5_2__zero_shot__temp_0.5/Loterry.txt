When the lottery balls fall and all six numbers match, some people think it is time to buy an Audi, book a holiday in the US, and call an estate agent. An analysis says many lottery millionaires spend money like this.

Since it started in 1994, the UK National Lottery has made 3,000 millionaires. Together they have won more than £8.5bn in total. The average win was £2.8m each. A study by Oxford Economics says these winners also helped create 3,780 more millionaires among their children, family, and friends.

Most winners (59%) stop working right away. But 19% keep their day job, and 31% do unpaid voluntary work. The study says 98% of winners’ spending stayed in the UK. By spending on homes, cars, and holidays, it is estimated that each winner supports six full-time jobs for a year.

The study says winners added almost £750m to GDP and created more than £500m in tax for the Exchequer. Most money went on property. Winners spent £2.72bn on their main homes, and £170m on paying off debts and mortgages.

Keeping a steady income was important, so £2.125bn was spent on investments. Winners gave £1.17bn as gifts to family and friends. They spent £680m on cars and holidays.

Camelot, the operator of the UK National Lottery, paid for the study to mark the milestone of 3,000 winners. The study used research from 100 winners who won £1m or more. It found that the 3,000 winners bought 7,958 houses or flats in the UK, which is 2.7 each on average, and spent £3.3bn. Most winners (82%) changed their main home and spent about £900,000 on average.

Many winners bought luxury home items. Almost a third (29%) bought a hot tub. A walk-in wardrobe was important for 28%. Almost a quarter (24%) chose a home behind electric gates. 22% had a games room, and 7% installed a snooker table.

Bigger homes need more help. 30% of winners hired a cleaner, and 24% hired a gardener. A small number (5%) hired a beautician.

Audis were the favourite car for 16% of winners. Range Rovers and BMWs were also popular (11% each), as well as Mercedes (10%) and Land Rovers (5%). Winners spent £463m on 17,190 cars. The average price of the favourite car was £46,116.

Holidays were also important. Most winners (68%) chose five-star hotels overseas. The US was the top place to go for 27%, followed by the Caribbean (9%). In the UK, caravan sales also went up. Over the past 18 years, 10% of millionaires bought a caravan, creating sales of about £7.4m.

Some winners (15%) started their own businesses. 9% helped other people start businesses, and 6% invested in or bought other people’s businesses. The study says businesses started or supported by winners employ 3,195 people.

Andy Logan, who wrote the report, said a win affects more people than they expected. He said it can change the lives of friends and family, and each win has a clear effect on the UK economy, especially because so much money is spent in the UK. He said each win creates a ripple effect for this generation and often the next.
