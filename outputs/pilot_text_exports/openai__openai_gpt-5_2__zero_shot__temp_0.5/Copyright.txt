Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is theft. He says it is like taking a wallet from someone’s pocket. Pullman wrote this in an article for Index on Censorship. He is president of the Society of Authors. He strongly supports copyright laws. He criticizes people who download music or books without paying.

Pullman writes that new technology is so impressive that some people forget it is wrong. He says it is shocking when people steal an artist’s work and are not punished.

His article came after leaders from the music industry met British Prime Minister David Cameron at Downing Street. They talked about web piracy.

Pullman, who wrote the His Dark Materials trilogy, says authors and musicians can be poor and unknown for years while they work. When they finally make work that people enjoy, he says they may lose the chance to earn money from it. He says the rule is simple: if we enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

In the next issue of the magazine, Pullman is in a dialogue with Cathy Casserly, the chief executive of Creative Commons. Creative Commons offers open content licences that let creators control how they use copyright. Casserly says many things are wrong with copyright, because it was made in an “analogue age.” She says copyright can stop people from sharing, building on, and remixing other people’s work.

Casserly says artists should think of new ways to share their work and make money from it. She quotes science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees with her. The editor, Rachael Jolley, says current copyright laws do not work well in the digital age and may make consumers into criminals. She says new ways are needed so artists, writers, and musicians can earn a living.

The debate is strong, and illegal downloading is very common. Ofcom, the UK communications regulator, collected data. It suggests that in the UK, between November 2012 and January 2013, 280 million music tracks were pirated. Also pirated were 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files.

Ofcom says 18% of internet users over age 12 admit they recently pirated content. It says 9% fear getting caught. Pullman also writes that getting music as MP3 downloads is still surprising to people who have been building an iTunes list for a long time.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron appointed Conservative MP Mike Weatherley as his adviser on this topic. A spokesman for the BPI, the record industry trade body, said Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creative producers it protects. The spokesman said they hope Weatherley and the prime minister will influence the UK’s intellectual property office.
