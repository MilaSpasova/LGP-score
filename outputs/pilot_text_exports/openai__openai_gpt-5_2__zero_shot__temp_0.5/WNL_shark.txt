Opposition to Western Australia’s shark cull has grown. Thousands of people went to beaches across Australia to ask the state’s premier to stop the policy. RSPCA Australia and Richard Branson, who owns Virgin Atlantic, also spoke against it.

The policy involves catching and killing sharks longer than three metres. It started after what the state government called an “unprecedented” number of shark attacks on the Western Australia coast. A 35-year-old surfer was killed in November 2013. He was the sixth person to die from a shark attack in two years.

But the Shark Attack File says that Australia has averaged one shark-related death a year for the last 50 years.

Kate Faehrmann from the Sea Shepherd Conservation Society spoke at a protest in Perth. She said the policy will not work. She said drumlines, used to catch sharks, are not selective. They can catch and kill sharks of any size, and also dolphins, turtles, and other animals. She said this is why the community does not want the policy.

Protests happened at Perth’s Cottesloe Beach and Sydney’s Manly Beach. There were also protests at Glenelg in south-west Adelaide, and at beaches in Victoria and Queensland. Faehrmann said the protests showed many Australians want sharks protected. She said that even with fear from movies like Jaws, many people are saying the ocean belongs to sharks too and they do not want sharks killed.

Anthony Joyce, a surfer who once had his foot caught in a shark’s mouth, said killing these sharks will not make a difference.

The state government has not given a running count of how many sharks have been killed. There have been reports that sharks smaller than three metres were released after being caught. Drumlines are floating drums fixed to the sea floor, with bait on hooks below them.

Conservationists say there is no evidence the cull will reduce shark attacks on people. They say no earlier cull used only drumlines. Researchers at the University of Western Australia say the recent increase in attacks may be linked to Western Australia having the fastest-growing population in Australia, not to more sharks.

Richard Peirce from the UK charity the Shark Trust said the cull will not work and could bring more predators closer to the coast. He said leaving drumlines in the water overnight could attract other predators to the area because sharks and other animals may be hooked and hurt.

In 2012 worldwide, there were 80 unprovoked shark attacks, and seven were fatal. Humans kill nearly 100 million sharks each year. RSPCA Australia said it believes the cull is not justified. It said there is no evidence the rise in attacks is because there are more sharks. It said it fits better with changes in population and human behaviour, meaning more people are in the water.

Richard Branson told Fairfax Radio the policy could have the opposite effect on tourism. He said it may make people think there is a big problem when there is not, and it could stop people from visiting Perth and the surrounding countryside. He said it may only make people worry for no need.
