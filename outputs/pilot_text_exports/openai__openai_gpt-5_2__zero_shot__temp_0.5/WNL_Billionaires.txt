A thick layer of bird droppings covers a gold-painted railing at one of Britain’s most expensive homes. Pigeon bones lie among broken mirrors, and water runs through broken roof edges. This house is The Towers. It is a £30m palace on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London. People have kept its ruin secret until now.

The Towers is one of ten mansions in the middle of The Bishops Avenue. This street is part of London’s fast-rising property market. These houses have been almost empty for about 25 years. People believe they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. The Greek-style columns are cracking. Swimming pools with mosaic tiles are full of rubble. Nature has taken over so much that owls now live there.

This empty and broken look can be seen along the avenue, even though it is supposed to be a famous street. Lloyds Bank says it is the second most expensive street in Britain. At the same time, many people in London struggle to buy a home because prices rise by 11.2% each year. But 16 mansions on the most expensive part of The Bishops Avenue are sitting empty. Many are behind locked gates. Their windows are covered with steel bars, and the gardens are overgrown. Guard dogs patrol the grounds.

Across the street is another empty mansion worth £18m, with smashed windows and walls covered in anti-climb paint. Another mansion has metal bars on the windows and sold for £20m.

Even so, prices keep going up. Dryades, a mansion once owned by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007 and is believed to be worth about £30m today. Heath Lodge is worth £13m today. It was the place where fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou was murdered in 1984, by a silver bullet. Heath Lodge was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

Seeing these empty mansions can be painful for people who struggle to pay for a home in one of the world’s most expensive cities. One security guard said it was upsetting to watch so many huge houses—big enough for dozens of people—fall apart.

Rich royals with oil money from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia were among the first to move to this curved road near Hampstead Heath. Iranians came after the fall of the shah. Now Chinese buyers are coming too, after Russians and Kazakhs. They have paid millions to live on a street that estate agents say is as famous as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions were for sale for £65m and £38m. They offered Italian marble, lifts with leather padding, and luxury panic rooms.

But on the grounds of the empty mansions, stone fountains are breaking and lawns have turned into muddy wet areas. Inside one house, water drips through a huge crystal chandelier onto a thick carpet that is rotting under parts of a fallen ceiling. Moss grows through broken bricks. Mirror tiles are scattered across a bathroom. The swimming pool has about a foot of dirty, salty water, and flowers grow through the tiles. Wooden boards stick out from the sauna.

The most dramatic ruin is The Towers. It is a grand mansion among acres of hornbeams, oaks, and limes. Pigeons fill the large rooms with high ceilings. Water pours through three floors. The walls have turned bright green from algae. The water drops into a huge, empty basement swimming pool. Unopened wooden crates marked “bullet-proof glass” show the old owners worried about safety.

Today, very few people live on The Bishops Avenue all the time. A security guard outside one mansion said the owners were not there. Another guard outside Royal Mansion would not say if anyone was home. At another mansion, a staff member warned the Guardian about the guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare mogul with personal wealth of £45m, said he is one of the few people who live there permanently.

He said, “It’s not a neighbourly place, where you can chat over the fence. To be honest, I have never seen what my neighbours look like. Next door, a Saudi princess spent £35m on a new house and I’ve never seen her. There are about three houses that are lived in 24/7 and half of the properties are occupied three to six months a year. The other half, who knows if they come or not?”

These broken, very expensive homes show a property culture where some of the world’s richest people treat British homes as investments. One Hyde Park in Knightsbridge is another example. More than half of its flats are registered with the council as empty or second homes.

Still, people on the avenue are talking about building £5m apartments instead of £50m mansions, to bring people back.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, plans to rebuild one valuable site on the avenue into 20 apartments. They would have a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

He said, “If you build a big house and try and sell for £30m to £40m, it won’t sell. Locals won’t buy and so you have to bring in overseas buyers.”

But using these empty homes to help solve the housing crisis still seems far away. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some of the ruined homes could become affordable housing. He said the land price would be too high.

He said, “Very wealthy people own property there. Sometimes they live in them and sometimes they don’t.”
