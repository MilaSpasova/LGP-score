Dan Sullivan was late to breakfast with the Guardian. He was late because police stopped him while he was riding his motorcycle.

Sullivan says he works too much. He misses dinner dates. He forgets to buy presents.

So the 27-year-old venture capitalist made a start-up to help: BetterBoyfriend.me. This service sends a girlfriend or wife a present every month for about $70.

Sullivan works in finance. He has been testing the program quietly. About 350 boyfriends have signed up. The service makes about $17,000 each month. Most members are his Harvard and investor friends. They include venture capitalists, founders, and employees at companies like Apple, Google, and Facebook. Their girlfriends have been getting presents from Sullivan for the last year.

Each month, members choose one gift from a list of seven choices, like chocolate, tea sets, or manicures. The package is sent to the boyfriend with no brand name and no label.

Sullivan tests ideas by buying Facebook ads to see how much interest there is. BetterBoyfriend.me grew fast. Sullivan said it solves a problem many people have.

BetterBoyfriend.me was not his first idea. For a few days, he thought about a dog-sharing idea. He said he saw rich guys looking unhappy while walking dogs. He said a dog is only valuable 5% of the time. He wondered if someone could babysit dogs and other people could rent them.

Another start-up is called InvisibleBoyfriend. It lets users design a fake social partner who is always available to talk. The result is a “boyfriend” who can text with you all day.

Founder Kyle Tabor started it to help users avoid unwanted advances. But he found that people can become attached to their computer-made partners. He said many users want companionship through conversation, not just “proof” of a relationship.

For Sullivan and Tabor, a surprise was how real the relationships with customers became. Sullivan says he now feels like a relationship consultant for the boyfriends.

Sullivan says he made mistakes. Early packages had receipts with his name, Dan Sullivan. One boyfriend wrote to him and said, “Listen, dude, she’s not mad but Cynthia found out.”

Sullivan says about 50% of the women who get packages know he is involved. He says this is linked to age. He thinks that after people have been married for a while, they keep fewer secrets.

Over the year, Sullivan says he has gotten to know the boyfriends well. The service has even delivered to a hospital delivery room. He says the key is to remember that his relationship is with the boyfriend.

When he first started the company, he put BetterBoyfriend.me tags on flower bouquets. He went to Dolores Park in the Mission District, a popular place for young people and tech workers.

He looked for couples and gave the flowers to the girl. The boyfriend did not like that. Sullivan said it made sense, so he changed his plan and started giving the bouquets to the boyfriends.
