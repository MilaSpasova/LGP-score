Anitta is a new Brazilian funk star. She has millions of fans. She helped bring music from the favelas into popular music. But now people are debating her skin colour.

Some anti-discrimination activists and writers say that, to become famous with the mostly white middle class, Anitta had to give up part of her “blackness.”

The debate started after people shared “before and after” photos. In the older photo, before she was famous, she looked darker. In a later marketing photo, after she signed with Warner, she looks lighter. People are not sure why. It could be makeup, photo editing (Photoshop), or other changes. The difference made people talk again about whether you need lighter skin to succeed in Brazil.

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote that this shows hidden discrimination in media and marketing. She said many people think Brazil is not racist, but statistics say otherwise. She said society often links being white with being rich, successful, and good, and links being black with the opposite. She also said stars may feel pressure to change their looks, like straightening curly hair or getting nose surgery, and this can hurt self-esteem.

Anitta’s real name is Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

Later she became very famous with an album and a hit song, “Show das Poderosas.” The song reached number one and got 52 million YouTube views.

Many fans love her as a pop idol with catchy songs and a strong message. Her supporters also present her as a bridge between the mostly black and mixed-race poor areas on Rio’s hills and the richer and whiter areas below.

She changed her style to reach a wider audience. She reduced sexual dancing, gang references, and explicit lyrics. Now some people ask if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract a richer audience.

Race is a sensitive issue in Brazil. Brazil was one of the last big countries to ban slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. In Brazil, people often focus more on skin colour than on family background. Even though Brazil wants to be a multiracial democracy, there is still a strong link between skin tone and inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Until 2011, black or mixed-race students also spent about two fewer years in school on average.

The government says the gap is getting smaller because of university quota systems and other affirmative action. But inequality is still easy to see. Most business and government leaders are white, while many low-paid jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In wealthy areas like Ipanema and Gávea, it is common to see black nannies pushing strollers with white children.

Colour categories in Brazil can be complicated. In the 2010 census, people who said they were white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million said black, two million said Asian, and 0.5% said indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology lecturer at the Federal University of Pernambuco, said Anitta won middle-class fans by taking a rebellious sound and making it calmer and easier for many people to accept. He said it was not racial whitening. He said the change was that her music moved from the “periphery” to the “centre” of society.

Others also say the colour issue is exaggerated. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who worked with Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot, but not her skin colour. He said it was more about makeup, hair styling, and clothes, and not because she was forced. He said she always liked to sing, dance, and show off, and that this natural style helped her succeed.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador, said the prejudice is more in music producers and media leaders than in the public. He pointed to Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, who won the talent show The Voice Brazil, as proof that people can choose music for its quality.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for a comment. But in a recent interview, she said identity should be defined by the person. She said a powerful woman does not need to be beautiful. She said her message is that people can be who they want.
