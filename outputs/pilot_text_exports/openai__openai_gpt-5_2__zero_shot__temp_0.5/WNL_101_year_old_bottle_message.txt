Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8th April, 2014, she told people about an amazing moment. She received a message in a bottle. Her grandfather had thrown it into the Baltic Sea in 1913, and it was found 101 years later. People think it may be the world’s oldest message in a bottle. A museum in Germany showed it and gave it to Erdmann.

Erdmann, 62, said it was very surprising. A man came to her door and said he had post from her grandfather. He said a message in a bottle had been found, and the name on the card was her grandfather’s. The visitor was a genealogical researcher. He found her in Berlin after the letter was taken to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg.

The bottle was a brown beer bottle. It had been in the water for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it while fishing in the Baltic Sea near the city of Kiel. Holger von Neuhoff, who works at the museum, said it was the oldest bottled message he had seen. He said the museum has older documents, but not with the bottle. He said the bottle and paper were in very good condition.

Researchers think Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913 during a hike with a nature group. He was 20 years old.

People could not read much of the postcard. But they could read the Berlin address on the front. They could also read his polite request: the finder should send the note to his home.

Erdmann said he also put two stamps in the bottle. That way, the finder would not have to pay anything to send it. But he did not think it would take 101 years.

Erdmann said the message made her emotional. She did not know her grandfather because he died at age 54, before she was born.

She said she learned a little about him. She found out he was a writer. She said he was open-minded, believed in freedom, and thought people should respect each other. She said he did a lot for young people. Later, he traveled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful because she could see where her family came from.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and traveling around the world. She also called herself open-minded. She said what her grandfather taught his two daughters, her mother taught her, and she then taught her sons.

Even though she was happy, she said she hopes people today do not throw bottles with messages into the sea. She said the sea is already full of bottles and rubbish.

The bottle and message will be on display at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts will try to read the rest of the text. It is not clear what will happen to the bottle later, but Erdmann hopes it will stay in the museum.

She said they want to add some photos with the bottle, so visitors can see the young man who threw it into the water.
