One warm summer evening, I went to swim near Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland. I moved there in 2012. A woman was in the water with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty often swims with a group of people.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Since then, hundreds of people have swum with her, so she can seem very tame. She was even in an Irish tourist-board ad where a girl says she wants to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty’s belly. It looked safe and friendly. About 20 tourists and local people stood on the pier watching.

Soon after I got in the water, Dusty suddenly left the woman and got very wild. Later I learned she is very territorial when she is with someone. Her tail was hitting the water hard. At first I thought she was just showing off, but then I realized she might be angry. I swam toward the pier to get out. But very quickly, Dusty crashed into me with her nose. It was very strong and very painful, and she was moving fast. I was thrown forward.

People on the pier stared down at me in shock. Dusty stayed beside me, still flapping her tail. That scared me the most. I thought if she hit me with her tail, I could go under the water and die.

I reached the pier, but I could not get out because I was hurt. I felt huge fear. I shouted for help. A man reached in and pulled me onto the steps. Then another man came and said he was an orthopaedic surgeon who specialized in marine trauma. He had been driving into Doolin and saw what was happening.

I was very cold and very worried. I did not know how bad my injuries were, and I was most afraid of bleeding inside my body. He checked me and spoke calmly. He said he could not feel signs of internal bleeding, but I probably had broken bones. Later I learned I had six fractures in my spine, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in the hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for several weeks. I could not work for five months. I had limited movement, stiffness, and pain.

Later I was diagnosed with post-traumatic stress. The near-death experience made me anxious and made me overreact in ways I never did before. I felt like people were looking at me the wrong way. Loud noises became hard for me, and I had memory loss.

Three months before the accident, I had opened a health-food shop, but I had to give it up because I could not work. It was the hardest year, but now it is behind me. I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building my own osteopathy practice. I understand my patients better because I have been a patient.

I am thankful that I am healthy, and I want to stop other people from getting hurt. People often think dolphins are always kind and safe. Who would think a dolphin could attack a person? If a scary animal comes at you with its teeth showing, you know it is dangerous. But dolphins have a wide “smile,” so people trust them.

I am not angry at Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her territory, and she is a wild animal that can be unpredictable. People need to know this. Many people come to swim with her and do not understand how dangerous it can be. Mine were reportedly among several injuries that summer.

After the man pulled me out, Dusty swam away. Then she came back and floated upright next to me, looking at me. We looked into each other’s eyes, and I felt she was sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin, and the anger was gone. The people on the pier were amazed. After that moment, I stopped feeling terror. I made peace with her.
