Kenton Cool is usually talkative, but now he can hardly speak. Climbing high mountains has made his voice rough. He is in Kathmandu, the capital of Nepal. He flew down from Everest base camp that morning.

Cool is thinking about what he climbed the weekend before. Early on Saturday, he reached the top of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of three main peaks in the Everest “horseshoe” around the icy valley called the Western Cwm.

Later that same day, he climbed to the top of Everest. He reached the top early on Sunday, in complete darkness. Then he and his climbing partner kept going. On Monday morning, they reached the top of Lhotse, the third peak.

“It was a sudden chance,” he says. “For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. That does not take away the physical achievement of what I did. I have set the bar at a certain level. But the next person will raise the bar and do it without ropes or bottled oxygen.”

Sixty years after Everest was first climbed, many stories look back to Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. People also talk about today’s commercial tourism and damage to the environment. I asked Cool to think about what top climbers might do 60 years from now.

“I hate to think,” he says. He talks about the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. Steck left the mountain in April after what Cool calls “an altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck planned a big climb: go up Everest’s west ridge (first done in 1963), go down to the South Col, and then immediately climb Lhotse by a new route. He planned to do this without fixed ropes. “Ueli had been training like a machine,” Cool says. “He’s a climber in a class all his own. He’s technically brilliant, and his fitness was at an astronomic level. It would have been amazing to see what he could have done.”

Tourism is another question. One sign is a helicopter rescue by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro’s angry words helped start the confrontation at Camp 2. On Tuesday, Moro flew a powerful helicopter back to Everest. He rescued a very sick climber at 7,800 metres.

This was the highest rescue ever done on Everest. It also shows how fast helicopter flights have increased in recent years. By 2073, the mountain could even have a helipad on the South Col, bringing tourists who breathe bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing how rescues can happen for climbers and for the many trekkers who go as far as base camp.

Mountain geographer and environmentalist Alton Byers says it is not clear if the Everest region can keep handling so much tourism. He says climate change and tourism together are putting new pressure on the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers in the Everest region are shrinking, and some are disappearing. This is already causing big problems. “Everywhere you go, people are talking about how there’s less water,” he says. “There’s less water for agriculture and less water for all the new lodges that are getting built.”

In the Sherpa town of Namche Bazaar, he says, workers are laying a new five-mile pipeline to bring water. Tourists want showers and flush toilets. The local stream has human waste in it, and it does not have enough water. In the busy season, the town is very crowded. “Every village is digging a pit just beyond the houses for garbage. Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world,” he says. Human waste at base camp is now handled well and taken away in plastic barrels. But Byers says these barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley, and it could leak into the area’s water sources.

“These problems can be solved, but we need to get serious about it,” he says. “One climber can spend $85,000 climbing Everest. And that’s fine. But at some point we will have to deal with these other needs. For half a million dollars a year, you could solve most of them.”

Climate change is harder. Byers works with local conservation groups to plan for climate change. They often look for new water sources or collect rainwater. Growing glacial lakes that might burst and flood the Sherpa homeland are a constant danger. “There’s going to come a time when people are going to have to get out of their way,” he says.

Weather changes are also affecting tourism. More clouds during times that are usually clear are closing Lukla Airport more often. Lukla Airport is the main entry point to the Everest region. A new road for 4x4 vehicles is being built to Lukla to keep tourists and money coming. But Byers worries the road building in Nepal is being done cheaply. He says this could cause serious soil erosion and landslides.

“Everest is the icon everyone knows,” he says. “It’s the warning sign everyone understands. It’s a perfect place for learning how to deal with problems like climate change and tourism.”
