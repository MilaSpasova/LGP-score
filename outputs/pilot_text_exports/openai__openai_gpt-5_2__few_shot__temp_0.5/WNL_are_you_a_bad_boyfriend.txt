Dan Sullivan met the Guardian for breakfast, but he was late. He said it was because police stopped him while he was riding his motorcycle.

Sullivan says he works too much. He misses dinner dates. He forgets to give gifts.

Like many people in Silicon Valley, the 27-year-old venture capitalist made a start-up to help with this. It is called BetterBoyfriend.me. It sends a gift to a girlfriend or wife every month for about $70.

Sullivan works in finance. He has been testing the service quietly. About 350 boyfriends have signed up. It makes about $17,000 each month. He says most members are his Harvard and investor friends. They include venture capitalists, founders, and workers at companies like Apple, Google, and Facebook. Their girlfriends have been getting gifts from Sullivan for the last year.

Each month, members pick from seven gifts, like chocolate, tea sets, and manicures. The gift is shipped to the boyfriend in a plain package with no name on it.

Sullivan tests ideas by buying Facebook ads to see how many people click. BetterBoyfriend.me became popular fast. Sullivan said it solved a problem many people have.

BetterBoyfriend.me was not his first idea. For a few days, he thought about a dog-sharing plan. He said he saw rich guys looking unhappy while walking dogs. He said a dog is only valuable 5% of the time. His idea was to have someone watch the dogs, and let other people rent them.

Another start-up is called InvisibleBoyfriend. It lets users create a believable social partner who is always available to talk. The result is a “boyfriend” who can text with you all day.

The founder, Kyle Tabor, started it to help users stop unwanted attention. But he found many people became close to their computer-made partners. He said more users want companionship through talking, not just “proof” of a relationship.

For Sullivan and Tabor, a surprise was the real relationships they formed with customers. Sullivan says he has started to feel like a relationship adviser for the boyfriends.

Sullivan says he made mistakes. At first, the packages had receipts with his name, Dan Sullivan. One boyfriend wrote to him and said, “Listen, dude, she’s not mad but Cynthia found out.”

Sullivan says about 50% of the women who get the packages know he is involved. He says this is connected to age. He thinks that after people have been married a while, they do not keep many secrets.

Over the year, Sullivan says he got to know the boyfriends well. One time, they even delivered a gift to a hospital delivery room. He says the main thing is that his business relationship is with the boyfriend.

When Sullivan first started the company, he put BetterBoyfriend.me tags on flower bouquets and went to Dolores Park in the Mission District. He looked for couples and gave flowers to the girl, but the boyfriends did not like that. So he changed and started giving the flowers to the boyfriends instead.
