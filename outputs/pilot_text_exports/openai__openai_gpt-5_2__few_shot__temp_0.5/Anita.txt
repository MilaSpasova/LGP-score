Brazil’s new funk star, Anitta, has won millions of fans. She brought favela music into popular music. But people are now arguing about her skin colour.

Some anti-discrimination groups and writers say she had to hide being black to sell music to a mostly white, middle-class market.

The argument grew after old and new photos were published. They seem to show that Anitta’s skin looks much lighter after she signed a deal with Warner.

In the first photo, from when she was not famous, she looks darker. In the second photo, used for marketing after she became famous, she looks paler. People ask if this happened because of skin-whitening products, cosmetic surgery, or more likely Photoshop. This has started the old question again: do you need to be pale to succeed in Brazil?

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote that this shows hidden discrimination in media and marketing. She said many people do not accept that they are racist and believe Brazil is a “multiracial democracy,” but statistics show something different. She said the “whitening” suggests society does not support diversity, and that people often connect being white with being rich and successful, and being black with the opposite.

Anitta was born Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

She became very famous with an album and a big hit song, Show das Poderosas. It reached number one and got 52 million views on YouTube.

Many people love her as a pop star with a strong message and catchy songs. Her supporters also present her as a bridge between the mostly black and mixed-race shanty towns on Rio’s hills and the richer, whiter areas below.

She changed her style by reducing sexy dancing, “gangsta” talk, and very explicit lyrics from baile funk. Now some people ask if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract a richer group of buyers. Arraes said pop stars may feel forced to change things like curly hair or a big nose, and that this can hurt self-esteem.

Race and colour are sensitive issues in Brazil, a country with many mixed-race people. Brazil was one of the last big countries to ban slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. But in Brazil, colour often matters more than race or family background. Even though Brazil aims to be a multiracial democracy, skin tone is clearly linked to inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Until 2011, black or mixed-race students also spent about two fewer years in school on average.

The government says the gap is getting smaller because of university quotas and other affirmative action. But big differences remain. Most business and government leaders are white, while many low-paid jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In Ipanema, Gávea, and other rich areas, you are much more likely to see black nannies pushing strollers with white toddlers than the other way around.

Colour categories are also hard to define. In the 2010 census, people who called themselves white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million said black, two million said Asian, and 0.5% said indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology teacher at the Federal University of Pernambuco, said Anitta won middle-class fans by taking a rebellious sound and making it calmer and easier for many people to like. He said this was not done by racial whitening. He said it was a change in where she made her art: from the edge of society to the centre.

Others say people talk too much about colour here. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who worked with Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot, but not her colour. He said it was more about makeup, hair styling, and clothing, not pressure. He said she always liked to show off, sing, and dance, and that this helped her succeed.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador, said the problem is not the public but music producers and media bosses. He said people showed they care about music itself when they chose Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, as the recent winner of The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for comment. But in a recent interview, she said identity should be decided by the person. She said a powerful woman does not need to be beautiful; she can be powerful because of her attitude. She said her work tries to show that we can be who we want.
