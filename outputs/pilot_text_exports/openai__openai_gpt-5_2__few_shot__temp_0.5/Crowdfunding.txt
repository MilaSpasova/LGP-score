This new light could be as useful as Trevor Baylis’s wind-up radio in the pre-internet 1990s. It is a cheap light that uses free power from gravity. It could help stop the use of dangerous kerosene lamps in Africa and India.

British designer Patrick Hunt tried to get money from banks and venture capital to start selling his invention, but it was hard. He said getting funding was slow and complex, the idea was unproven, and nobody wanted to take a risk.

So he tried crowdfunding on a US website called Indiegogo, which had recently opened in the UK. In five days he reached his target and raised £36,200. By the end of 2012, his campaign was so popular that in 40 days he raised £400,000.

The LED light works with a dynamo that is turned by a 10kg bag of rocks going down. The bag is attached to the light and lifted to about 2m high. When the bag slowly falls to the ground, it makes enough power for half an hour of light.

Hunt is getting ready to make the light in China. Before full mass production, he will test the market again by sending 1,000 lights to Africa. He hopes mass production will later reach millions of units.

Hunt is part of a new wave of entrepreneurs using the fast-growing crowdfunding industry for finance. Another new site is InvestingZone, which connects wealthy people with start-up entrepreneurs.

Indiegogo does not give shares. Instead, it lets users offer “perks” for different donation amounts. People who helped fund Hunt’s light felt good about helping poorer people, and they also got their own light.

Danae Ringelmann, a co-founder of Indiegogo, said the “gravity light” shows how crowdfunding can quickly test an idea. She said it is not about knowing the right people at a bank or a venture capital firm. It is about proving value to customers and fans, getting them to support the idea, and paying for it. She also said even ideas that do not get funding are worth testing, because it saves time and helps you learn faster.

Ringelmann is based in the US. She began her career as a Wall Street analyst. In 2008, she quit and used her skills to help friends in the arts raise money. The site first focused on films and launched at the Sundance Film Festival that year.

Five years later, Indiegogo was raising about $2m a week for new businesses that were starting or growing. In December 2012, it launched euro and sterling services to build a presence in Europe and said Britain was its third biggest market. It also said international activity was up 41% since December.

Indiegogo has many competitors, including Kickstarter, Seedrs, and Funding Circle. Ringelmann said Indiegogo is different because anyone can start a campaign, and no project is seen as too wacky.

Indiegogo charges a 4% fee for successful campaigns. If a campaign does not reach its target, users can either return all the money to contributors for free, or keep all the money raised and pay a 9% fee.

A British woman, Lauren Pears, raised £100,000 on the site to open a “cat café” in London. It is called Lady Dinah’s Cat Emporium. It has not opened yet, but it is advertised as a place where people can sit in a comfortable wingback chair with a hot cup of tea, a book, and a cat.

Ringelmann said she has seen campaigns rejected by venture capitalists who said they did not know if the market really wanted the product. She said entrepreneurs can run an Indiegogo campaign, and even if they do not launch the project, the campaign can be enough proof for venture capitalists that a market exists. She said it also helps people test the market, pricing, and features, find new revenue streams, and get important feedback.

Because she worked on Wall Street and has helped 100,000 businesses and services raise money, Ringelmann has advice for new entrepreneurs. She said ideas are common, and what matters is doing the work well. She said if you fear someone else will steal your idea and do it better and faster, then you are not the right person to carry it out. She said you need confidence to move fast and learn.

Ringelmann said Indiegogo’s growth in Europe is part of a dream to make finance more open to everyone. The company made a deal with a web transaction provider so people can pay using local card services like Maestro in the UK and Carte Bleue in France, as well as PayPal. More than 7,000 finance campaigns are live on the site.

Crowdfunding has grown as another choice besides banks, but it does not attract many big-money investors, who usually want a stake in a strong business. This may start to change in the UK with the launch of InvestingZone.
