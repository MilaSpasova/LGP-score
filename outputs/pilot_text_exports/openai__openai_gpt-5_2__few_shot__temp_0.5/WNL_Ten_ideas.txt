From glow-in-the-dark trees to underground bike storage and solar-powered bins, people are trying unusual ideas to make cities nicer to live in.

Living in a city has many good points, but it is getting harder to live in a way that is good for the environment. People in cities often deal with pollution, heavy traffic, and fewer green spaces. Inventors are trying new ideas to help. Here are some of the wackier ideas.

Pop-up parks
Some cities look like they are built more for cars than for people. A pop-up park is a simple idea. You take an empty parking space, spend a little money, add a plant or two, and make a small park. The PARK(ing) project started as an art experiment in San Francisco. It has now spread around the world. People have also tried temporary urban farms and ecology demonstrations. The movement has a day of action every September.

Subterranean storage
Not everyone in cities uses a car. Bikes are getting more popular. But where can you keep a bike safe? A Tokyo engineering firm called Giken made a plan for an “eco-cycle, anti-seismic underground bicycle park.” It is a cylinder that is seven metres wide. It goes deep into the ground and can store 204 bikes. Owners can get their bike back by pressing a button. The system brings the bike above ground in about 13 seconds.

Glow-in-the-dark trees
Many people think of glowing trees as Christmas lights. But Daan Roosegaarde does not. This Dutch designer-artist created an idea for a “bioluminescent” plant. The experimental technology mixes DNA from glowing marine bacteria with part of a plant to make a glow like a jellyfish. Trials are happening to make a large industrial version. Roosegaarde hopes it could one day replace normal street lights.

Footfall harvesting
In the east London area of West Ham, many commuters and shoppers cross a raised walkway near the underground station every day. Few people notice that the ground feels a bit springy. Even fewer know that the rubber surface bends about five millimetres and helps power the streetlights above. The walkway has smart tiles that capture energy from people’s footsteps and turn it into electricity. Pavegen, a UK company, has also put a similar system in London’s Heathrow Airport and other places.

Supertrees
People have made man-made trees. Singapore’s Gardens by the Bay has a small group of them. These steel-framed “supertrees” can be up to 50 metres tall. Flowers and ferns grow on them. Their metal tops help take in and spread out heat. They can collect rainwater and help provide air ventilation for two “climate-controlled biomes” (large conservatories) below. Eleven of the 18 trees also have solar panels on their “branches.”
