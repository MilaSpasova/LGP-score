Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is wrong and is theft. He says it is like reaching into someone’s pocket and stealing a wallet. In an article for Index on Censorship, Pullman, who is president of the Society of Authors, strongly supports copyright laws. He is very critical of people who think it is OK to download music or books without paying.

Pullman writes that new technology looks impressive, but people do not see that what they are doing is morally wrong. He says it is shocking that people can steal an artist’s work and not be punished.

His article came after music industry leaders met British Prime Minister David Cameron at Downing Street to talk about web piracy.

Pullman, the writer of the His Dark Materials trilogy, says authors and musicians often work for years in poverty and without attention. When their work finally becomes popular and brings joy to audiences, he says they can lose the chance to earn a living because of illegal downloading. He says the rule is simple: if we want to enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

Pullman’s article is in the next issue of the campaign group’s magazine. It is part of a dialogue with Cathy Casserly, the chief executive of Creative Commons. Creative Commons offers open content licences that let creators choose how to use copyright. Casserly says copyright has many problems because it was created in an “analogue age.” She says copyright often stops people from sharing, building on, and remixing each other’s work in ways that were not imagined when the laws were made.

Casserly says artists need to think of new ways to share and make money from their work. She quotes science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees that current laws are not working well. The editor, Rachael Jolley, says copyright laws do not work in the digital age and may turn normal consumers into criminals. She says new ways are needed for artists, writers, and musicians to earn money from their work.

Illegal downloading is very common. Ofcom, the UK communications regulator, collected data that suggests that in the UK between November 2012 and January 2013, people illegally copied 280 million music tracks. They also copied 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files.

Ofcom says 18% of internet users over age 12 say they recently copied content illegally. Also, 9% say they worry about getting caught. Pullman writes that it is still amazing how fast and easy it is to get music as MP3 downloads, especially for people who have been building up their iTunes list for some time.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron chose Conservative MP Mike Weatherley to advise him about this issue. A spokesman for the BPI, the record industry trade body, said Mike Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creative producers it is meant to protect. The BPI hopes his influence, and the prime minister’s support for copyright, will affect the UK’s intellectual property office.
