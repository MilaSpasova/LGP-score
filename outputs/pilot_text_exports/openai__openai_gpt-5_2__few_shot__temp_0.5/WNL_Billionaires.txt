A thick layer of bird droppings covers a gold-painted railing at one of Britain’s most expensive homes. Pigeon bones lie among broken mirrors, and water runs through broken parts of the ceiling. This house is called The Towers. It is a £30m home on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London. Its terrible condition was kept secret until now.

The Towers is one of ten big houses on The Bishops Avenue. This street is in the middle of London’s rising housing market. These houses have been almost empty for about 25 years. People believe they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. The Greek-style columns are breaking. Swimming pools with mosaic tiles are full of rubble. Nature has taken over so much that owls live there.

This sad sight is seen along the avenue. Lloyds Bank says The Bishops Avenue is the second most expensive street in Britain. At the same time, many people in London struggle to buy a home because prices rise by 11.2% a year. But 16 mansions on the most expensive part of the street sit empty. Many are behind locked gates. Their windows are covered with metal, the gardens are overgrown, and guard dogs patrol.

Across the street is another empty mansion worth £18m, with broken windows and walls covered in anti-climb paint. Another house has metal bars on the windows, even though it sold for £20m.

Still, prices keep rising. Dryades, a mansion that was owned until recently by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007 and is believed to be worth about £30m today. Heath Lodge, where fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou was murdered in 1984 by a silver bullet, is worth £13m today. It was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

For people who struggle to pay for a home in one of the world’s most expensive cities, these empty, falling-apart mansions can be painful to see. A security guard said it was frustrating to watch so much space—enough to house dozens of people—go to waste.

Rich royals with oil money from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia were among the first to come to this curving road near Hampstead Heath. Iranians came here after the fall of the shah. Now Chinese buyers are following Russians and Kazakhs, who have paid millions for an address that estate agents say is as famous as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions were for sale for £65m and £38m. They promised lots of Italian marble, lifts with leather padding, and luxury panic rooms.

But on the empty properties, stone fountains break apart and lawns turn into muddy bogs. Inside one house, water drips through a huge crystal chandelier onto a thick carpet that is rotting under collapsed parts of the ceiling. Moss grows through broken bricks. Broken mirror tiles cover a bathroom floor. The swimming pool has about a foot of dirty water and flowers growing through its tiles. Wooden pieces in the sauna bend outward.

The most shocking ruin is The Towers. It is a grand house set among acres of hornbeam, oak, and lime trees. Pigeons live in its large, high rooms. Water pours through three floors, and the walls have turned bright green from algae. The water drops into a huge, empty basement swimming pool. Wooden crates marked “bullet-proof glass” have never been opened, showing the old owners were worried about security.

Today, very few people live on The Bishops Avenue all the time. A security guard outside one mansion said the owners were not there. Another guard outside Royal Mansion would not say if anyone was home. A staff member at another mansion warned the Guardian about the guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare businessman worth £45m, said he is one of the few people who lives there permanently.

He said, “It’s not a friendly place where you can chat over the fence. I have never seen what my neighbours look like. Next door, a Saudi princess spent £35m on a new house and I’ve never seen her. About three houses are lived in 24/7. Half the properties are used three to six months a year. The other half, who knows if they come or not?”

These broken, very expensive homes show a culture where the world’s richest people buy British property as investments. One Hyde Park in Knightsbridge is another example. More than half the flats there are registered with the council as empty homes or second homes.

Even so, people on the avenue talk about building £5m apartments instead of £50m mansions to bring people back.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, plans to rebuild one valuable site as 20 apartments. They would have a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

He said, “If you build a big house and try and sell for £30m to £40m, it won’t sell. Locals won’t buy and so you have to bring in overseas buyers.”

But using these empty homes to help solve the housing crisis still seems far away. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some of the empty homes could become affordable housing. He said the land price would be too high.

He said, “Very wealthy people own property there. Sometimes they live in them and sometimes they don’t.”
