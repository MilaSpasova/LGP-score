When the lottery balls fall and all six numbers match, it is time to spend money. Many lottery millionaires buy an Audi, book a holiday in the US, and call an estate agent. This is what many winners do, says a study of how jackpot winners spend and invest.

The lottery started in 1994. Since then, it has made 3,000 millionaires. Together they have won more than £8.5bn, about £2.8m each on average. The study by Oxford Economics says their money also helped make 3,780 more millionaires among their children, family, and friends.

Most winners (59%) stop working right away. But 19% keep their normal jobs, and 31% do unpaid volunteer work. The study says 98% of the winners’ spending stayed in the UK. By spending on houses, cars, and holidays, each winner is estimated to keep six people in full-time work for one year.

The winners added almost £750m to GDP and created more than £500m in taxes for the Exchequer. Most money went on property. Winners spent £2.72bn on their main homes, and £170m paying off debts and mortgages.

Keeping an income was important, so £2.125bn was spent on investments. Gifts to family and friends were £1.17bn. Cars and holidays cost £680m.

Camelot, which runs the UK National Lottery, paid for the study to mark 3,000 winners. The study used research from 100 winners who got £1m or more. It says the 3,000 winners bought 7,958 houses or flats in the UK, or 2.7 each, and spent £3.3bn. Most winners (82%) moved to a new main home and spent about £900,000 on average.

Many winners added special things to their homes. Almost a third (29%) bought a hot tub. A walk-in wardrobe was important for 28%. Almost a quarter (24%) chose a home behind electric gates. Some (22%) had a games room, and 7% put in a snooker table.

Big homes need help to keep them clean and nice. About 30% hired a cleaner and 24% hired a gardener. A few (5%) hired a beautician.

Audis were the top car choice for 16% of winners. Range Rovers and BMWs were also popular (11% each), plus Mercedes (10%) and Land Rovers (5%). Winners spent £463m on 17,190 cars. The average price of their favourite car was £46,116.

Holidays were also important. Most winners (68%) chose five-star hotels abroad. The US was the top place to go (27%), followed by the Caribbean (9%). In the UK, caravan sales also gained. In the past 18 years, 10% of millionaires bought a caravan, adding about £7.4m in sales.

Some winners (15%) started their own businesses. Another 9% helped other people start businesses, and 6% invested in or bought other people’s businesses. Businesses started or helped by winners employ 3,195 people, the study says.

Andy Logan, who wrote the report, said the effects of a win go further than expected. He said it changes the lives of friends and family, and each win also affects the UK economy because so much money is spent in the UK. He said each win creates a ripple effect for this generation and often the next one too.
