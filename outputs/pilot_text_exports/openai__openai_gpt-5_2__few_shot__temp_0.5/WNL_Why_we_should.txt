James Hamblin is a senior editor at The Atlantic magazine. In his series “If Our Bodies Could Talk,” he tried a challenge: he stopped showering often to see what happens when we clean our bodies too much. He took fewer showers, and when he did shower, he did not use shampoo or soap.

He found something many other people have found. When we use a lot of soap and body wash, our skin tries harder to get back to normal. Showering can wash away the skin’s natural oils and helpful bacteria. This can happen even more when we use hot water and cleaning products.

Have you ever felt very dry and tight after a shower? That is because your skin’s natural moisture was washed away. Our skin also has millions of helpful bacteria, like our gut does. Frequent scrubbing can kill these bacteria. When the bacteria grow back, they may include more germs that cause bad smell. So showering too often can sometimes make you smell more. If you stop showering and stop using soap, your skin may go through an early adjustment time that can seem gross. After that, the skin often becomes balanced again, makes less oil, and healthy bacteria grow.

In the end, Hamblin learned what other people who avoid soap and showers have said for years: the human body can be nice when it is left alone. It is not only about smell or looks. Doctors who study skin say using less soap can help acne and eczema.

Showering less can also help the environment. The average shower is seven minutes and uses 65 litres of water. That is 65 litres of clean drinking water that we mix with soap and send down the drain every day, sometimes more than once.

Clean water is becoming more important, as California has another summer of drought. It is becoming clear that clean water is one of the world’s most valuable resources, and it may soon be hard to get. Also, body wash bottles can harm the environment. These are strong reasons to let your body be more natural.

If this idea makes you feel uneasy because you have been near people who smell, try to relax. Many people who use less soap still use deodorant if they need it. Everyone’s natural smell is different and can change because of diet, how much water you drink, and exercise. Washing hands with soap is still recommended to help stop infectious diseases from spreading.

You do not need to stop all at once like Hamblin did. But between cleaning many times every day and never showering at all, there is a middle choice. Shower less often, use less soap, and let the helpful bacteria grow a little.
