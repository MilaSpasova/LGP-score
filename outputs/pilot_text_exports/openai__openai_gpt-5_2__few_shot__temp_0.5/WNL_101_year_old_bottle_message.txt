Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8th April, 2014, she told about an unusual moment. She got a message in a bottle, 101 years after her grandfather had thrown it into the Baltic Sea. People think it may be the world’s oldest message in a bottle. A museum in Germany gave it to her and is showing it to visitors.

Erdmann, 62, said it was a big surprise. A man came to her door and said he had mail from her grandfather. He explained that someone had found a message in a bottle, and the name on the card was her grandfather’s. The man was a family-history researcher. He found her in Berlin after the letter was given to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg.

The brown beer bottle had been in the water for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it in his catch in the Baltic Sea near the city of Kiel. Holger von Neuhoff, a museum curator, said it was the oldest bottled message he had seen. He said the museum has older documents without bottles, but with both the bottle and the document, this is the oldest right now. He also said it is in very good condition.

Researchers think Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913, while hiking with a nature group. He was 20 years old then.

People could not read much of the postcard. But they could read the Berlin address on the front. They could also read his polite request: he asked the finder to send the note to his home address.

Erdmann said he also put two stamps from that time in the bottle, so the finder would not have to pay. She said he did not think it would take 101 years.

Erdmann said the message made her feel emotional. She did not know her grandfather because he died at age 54, six years before she was born.

She said she knew little about him, but she learned he was a writer. She said he was open-minded, believed in freedom, and thought everyone should respect each other. She said he did a lot for young people and later traveled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful because she could see where her roots came from.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and traveling around the world, and she is open-minded too. She said what her grandfather taught his two daughters, her mother taught her, and she has taught it to her sons.

Even though she was happy to get the message, she said she hoped people today would not throw bottles with messages into the sea. She said the sea already has many bottles and much rubbish.

The message and bottle will be shown at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts will try to read the rest of the text. It is not clear what will happen to the bottle later, but Erdmann hopes it will stay at the museum.

She said they want to add a few photos with the bottle, so visitors can see the young man who threw it into the water.
