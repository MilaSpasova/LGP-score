Opposition to Western Australia’s shark cull has grown. Thousands of people went to beaches across Australia to ask the state’s premier to stop the policy. RSPCA Australia and Richard Branson, the owner of Virgin Atlantic, also spoke against it.

The government started catching and killing sharks longer than three metres after what it called an “unprecedented” number of shark attacks on the coast. In November 2013, a 35-year-old surfer was killed. He was the sixth person to die from a shark attack in two years.

But the Shark Attack File says Australia has averaged one shark-related death each year for the last 50 years.

Kate Faehrmann from the Sea Shepherd Conservation Society spoke at a protest in Perth. She said the policy will not work. She said drumlines are not selective. She said they can kill sharks of any size, and also dolphins, turtles, and other animals. She said this is why the community does not want the cull.

Thousands protested at Perth’s Cottesloe Beach and Sydney’s Manly Beach. Hundreds also protested at Glenelg in south-west Adelaide, and at beaches in Victoria and Queensland. Faehrmann said the protests showed many Australians want sharks protected. She said people are saying, even with the fear from movies like Jaws, “That’s their ocean. We respect them, we love them and we don’t want them killed.”

Anthony Joyce, a surfer who once had his foot caught in a shark’s mouth, said the number of sharks that will be killed will not make a difference.

The state government has not given a running total of sharks killed. There have been reports that sharks smaller than three metres were released after being caught on drumlines. Drumlines are floating drums fixed to the sea bed, with baited hooks hanging under them.

Conservationists say there is no evidence the cull will reduce shark attacks on humans. They say no earlier cull used only drumlines. Researchers at the University of Western Australia say the recent rise in shark attacks may be linked to the state having Australia’s fastest-growing population, not to more sharks.

Richard Peirce, chairman of the UK charity the Shark Trust, said the cull would not work and could even bring more predators near the coast. He said it is sad the government ignored advice to try other ways instead of killing animals without choosing which ones. He said drumlines can attract other predators, especially if the lines are left in overnight, because sharks and other hooked animals may be injured.

Around the world in 2012, there were 80 unprovoked shark attacks, and seven were fatal. In the same period, humans kill almost 100m sharks each year.

RSPCA Australia said it believes the cull is not justified. It said there is no evidence the increase in attacks is because there are more sharks. It said it matches changes in population and human behaviour, meaning more people are in the water.

Richard Branson told Fairfax Radio the policy is backfiring. He said Premier Colin Barnett may have thought it would help tourism, but he thinks it will do the opposite. He said it makes people think there is a big problem, and it could stop people from visiting Perth and the countryside nearby. He said it will only make people worry for no good reason.
