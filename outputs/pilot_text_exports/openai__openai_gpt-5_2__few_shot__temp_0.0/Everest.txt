Kenton Cool usually likes to talk, but now he can hardly speak. Climbing high mountains has made his voice rough. He is in Kathmandu, the capital of Nepal. He flew there that morning from Everest base camp.

Cool is thinking about three big climbs he finished last weekend. Early on Saturday morning, he reached the top of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of three main peaks that form an Everest “horseshoe” around an icy valley called the Western Cwm.

Later that same day, he climbed to the top of Everest. He reached the summit in complete darkness early on Sunday. Then he and his climbing partner went on to the top of Lhotse on Monday morning. Lhotse was the third peak in this three-mountain challenge.

“It was a snatched opportunity,” he says. For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. He says this does not remove the physical achievement. But he thinks the next person will set a new higher standard, and do it without ropes or bottled oxygen.

Sixty years after Everest was first climbed, many stories look back to Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. They talk about an earlier time, before modern commercial climbing and environmental damage. I ask Cool to look ahead and imagine what top climbers might do 60 years from now.

“I hate to think,” he says. But he talks about the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. Steck left the mountain in April after what Cool calls “an altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck planned to climb Everest’s west ridge, first climbed in 1963, then go down to the South Col, and then immediately climb Lhotse by a new route. He planned to do it all without fixed ropes. Cool says Steck trained extremely hard. He calls him a very special climber who was both highly skilled and in amazing physical shape.

Tourism is another question. One sign of change is a helicopter rescue by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro flew back to Everest on Tuesday in a powerful helicopter to rescue a sick or injured climber at 7,800 metres.

This was the highest rescue ever done on Everest. It shows that helicopter flights have increased very fast in recent years. By 2073, the mountain might even have a helipad on the South Col, bringing tourists who use bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing what is possible for rescuing climbers and the many trekkers who go as far as base camp.

Mountain geographer and environmentalist Alton Byers says it is not clear if the Everest region can handle the fast growth of tourism. He says climate change and tourism together are creating new pressure on the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers are shrinking, and some are disappearing. This is already causing major problems. He says people everywhere are talking about having less water. There is less water for farming and less water for the new lodges being built.

In the Sherpa town of Namche Bazaar, a new five-mile pipeline is being built to bring in water. This is to meet tourist demand for showers and flush toilets. The local stream has been polluted by human waste, and it does not provide enough water when the town is very crowded in the busy season. Byers says every village is digging a pit near the houses for garbage. He says Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world. At base camp, human waste is now handled well and taken away in plastic barrels. But Byers says the barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley, and it could leak into the region’s water.

Byers says these problems can be solved, but people must take them seriously. He says one climber can spend $85,000 to climb Everest. He says that is fine, but people also need to deal with other needs. He says that for half a million dollars a year, most of these problems could be fixed.

Climate change is harder. Byers works with local conservation groups to find and plan for the effects of climate change. This often means finding new water sources or collecting rainwater. Another danger is the fast growth of glacial lakes. These lakes could burst and flood the Sherpa homeland. Byers says a time may come when people will have to move away.

Changing weather is also affecting tourism. More clouds during times that are usually clear are closing Lukla Airport more often. Lukla Airport is the main entry point to the Everest region. A new road for 4x4 vehicles is being built to Lukla to make sure tourists and their money can still come. But Byers worries that Nepal is building roads cheaply and quickly, and this can cause serious soil erosion and landslides.

Byers says everyone knows Everest. He says it is like an early warning sign that people understand. He says it is a perfect place to learn how to deal with problems like climate change and tourism.
