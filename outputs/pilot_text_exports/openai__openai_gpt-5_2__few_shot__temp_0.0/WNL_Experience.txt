One warm summer evening, I decided to swim near Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland. I moved there in 2012. In the water, a woman was swimming with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty often swims with a group of people.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Since then, hundreds of people have swum with her, so she can seem very tame. She was even in an Irish tourist-board ad, where a girl says she wants to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty’s belly. It looked friendly and fun. About 20 tourists and local people stood on the pier and watched.

Right after I got into the water, Dusty left the woman and suddenly went wild. Later I learned that Dusty can be very protective of her space when she is with someone. Her tail hit the water hard. At first I thought she was just showing off, but then I realized she might be angry. I knew I had to get out, so I swam toward the pier. But very fast, Dusty slammed into me with her nose. It was very strong and very painful, and I was pushed forward.

People on the pier stared at me in shock. Dusty stayed next to me, still hitting the water with her tail. That scared me the most. I thought if her tail hit me, I could go under the water and die.

I reached the pier, but I could not climb out because I was hurt. I felt terrified. I yelled for help, and a man reached down and pulled me onto the steps. Then another man came and said he was an orthopaedic surgeon who specialized in marine trauma. He had been driving into Doolin and saw what was happening. I was very cold and very worried. My biggest fear was bleeding inside my body. He checked me and said he could not find signs of internal bleeding, but he thought I probably had broken bones. Later I learned I had six spinal fractures, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in the hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for several weeks. I could not work for five months, and I had trouble moving. I was stiff and in pain. Later I was diagnosed with post-traumatic stress. After the near-death experience, I felt anxious about everything and reacted too strongly. I thought people were looking at me the wrong way. Loud noises became hard for me, and I had memory loss. Three months before the accident, I had opened a health-food shop, but I had to give it up because I could not work. It was the hardest year, but now it is in the past. I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building up my own osteopathy practice. I understand patients better because I have been one.

I am thankful that I am healthy, and I want to stop other people from getting hurt. People often think dolphins are always safe and kind. Who would expect a dolphin to attack a person? If a scary animal comes at you with its teeth showing, you feel afraid. But dolphins have a wide smile.

I am not angry with Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her territory, and she is a wild animal that can be unpredictable. People need to understand that. Many people come to swim with her and do not know how dangerous it can be. People said my injuries were one of several that summer.

After I was pulled out of the water, Dusty swam away. Then she came back and floated upright next to me, looking at me. We looked into each other’s eyes, and I felt she was sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin, and the anger was gone. The people on the pier were amazed. After that moment, my terror ended. I felt at peace with her.
