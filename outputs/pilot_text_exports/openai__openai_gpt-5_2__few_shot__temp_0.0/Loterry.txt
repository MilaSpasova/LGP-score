The balls fall down the machine and all six numbers match. Then many winners buy an Audi, book a holiday in the US, and call an estate agent. A study says this is what many lottery millionaires do.

The UK National Lottery started in 1994. Since then, it has made 3,000 millionaires. Together they have won more than £8.5bn in total. On average, each winner got £2.8m. The study authors, Oxford Economics, say the winners also helped create 3,780 more millionaires among their children, family, and friends.

Most winners (59%) stop working right away. But 19% keep doing their normal job. Also, 31% do unpaid voluntary work. The study says 98% of winners’ spending stayed in the UK. It estimates that each winner’s spending on homes, vehicles, and holidays keeps six people in a full-time job for one year.

The winners added almost £750m to GDP. They also created more than £500m in tax receipts for the Exchequer. Most money went on property. Winners spent £2.72bn on their main homes. They spent £170m paying off existing debt and mortgages.

Keeping a steady income was important. Winners spent £2.125bn on investments. They gave £1.17bn as gifts to family and friends. They spent £680m on cars and holidays.

Camelot, the operator of the UK National Lottery, asked for the study to mark the 3,000-winners milestone. It used research from 100 winners who won £1m or more. The study found that the 3,000 winners bought 7,958 houses or flats in the UK, about 2.7 each. They spent £3.3bn. Most winners (82%) changed their main home and spent an average of £900,000.

Many new homes had extra features. Almost a third (29%) bought a hot tub. A walk-in wardrobe was important for 28%. Almost a quarter (24%) chose a home behind electric gates. Also, 22% had a games room, and 7% put in a snooker table.

Bigger homes need help to look after them. So 30% hired a cleaner and 24% hired a gardener. A small number (5%) hired a beautician.

Audis were the favorite car for 16% of winners. Range Rovers and BMWs were also popular (11% each). Mercedes were 10%, and Land Rovers were 5%. Winners spent £463m on 17,190 cars. The average price of their favorite car was £46,116.

Holidays were important too. Most winners (68%) chose five-star hotels overseas. The US was the top place to visit for 27%, followed by the Caribbean (9%). Closer to home, UK caravan sales also gained. In the past 18 years, 10% of millionaires bought a caravan, creating about £7.4m in sales.

Some winners (15%) started their own business. Another 9% helped others start a business. Also, 6% invested in or bought other people’s businesses. Businesses started or supported by lottery winners employ 3,195 people.

Andy Logan, who wrote the report, said a win spreads further than expected. He said it changes the lives of friends and family, and it also has a clear effect on the UK economy because so much money is spent in the UK. He said each win creates a ripple effect in this generation and often the next.
