From glow-in-the-dark trees to underground bike storage and solar-powered bins, people are trying unusual ideas to make cities better to live in.

Living in a city has many good points, but it is getting harder to live in a sustainable way. City people often face pollution, traffic, and fewer green spaces.

Inventors are stepping in. Here are some of the wackier ideas, from a list of ten, to make cities more livable.

Pop-up parks
Some cities look like they are built more for cars than for people. A pop-up park is a simple idea. You take an empty parking space, a little money, and one or two plants, and you make a small park. The PARK(ing) project started as an art experiment in San Francisco and then spread around the world. People have tried ideas like temporary city farms and ecology demonstrations. The movement has a day of action every September.

Subterranean storage
Not everyone in cities uses a car. Bikes are becoming more popular. But where can you keep a bike safe? A Tokyo engineering company called Giken created an idea called an “eco-cycle, anti-seismic underground bicycle park.” The round storage space is only seven metres wide, but it goes deep underground and can hold 204 bikes. Owners can get their bike back by pressing a button. The machine brings the bike up in about 13 seconds.

Glow-in-the-dark trees
When many people think of glowing trees, they think of Christmas decorations. Daan Roosegaarde does not. This Dutch designer-artist made an idea for a “bioluminescent” plant. The technology mixes DNA from glowing marine bacteria with part of a plant’s cells to make a glow like a jellyfish. Trials are happening to make a large-scale version. Roosegaarde hopes it could one day replace normal street lights.

Footfall harvesting
Every day, hundreds of commuters and shoppers in West Ham in east London cross a raised walkway near the underground station. Many do not notice that the ground feels a little springy. The rubber surface bends about five millimetres. This helps power the streetlights above. The walkway has smart tiles that catch energy from people’s footsteps and turn it into electricity. Pavegen, the UK company behind it, has also put a similar system in London’s Heathrow Airport and other places.

Supertrees
People have even made man-made trees. Singapore’s Gardens by the Bay has a small group of them. These steel “supertrees” can be up to 50 metres tall. They have flowers and ferns growing on them, and their metal tops help absorb and spread heat. They can also collect rainwater. They also help provide air ventilation for two “climate-controlled biomes” (large conservatories) below. Eleven of the 18 trees also have solar panels on their “branches.”
