Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is theft. He says it is like reaching into someone’s pocket and stealing their wallet. In an article for Index on Censorship, Pullman, who is president of the Society of Authors, strongly supports copyright laws. He is very critical of internet users who think it is OK to download music or books without paying.

Pullman writes that the technology is so impressive that people do not see the wrong thing they are doing. He says it is shocking that people can steal an artist’s work and not be punished.

His article came after music industry leaders met British Prime Minister David Cameron at Downing Street to talk about web piracy.

Pullman, who wrote the His Dark Materials trilogy, says authors and musicians often work for years in poverty and without attention. He says that when their work finally becomes popular, their chance to make a living can be taken away by illegal downloading. He says the rule is simple: if we want to enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

Pullman’s article is in the next issue of the group’s magazine, in a dialogue with Cathy Casserly, the chief executive of Creative Commons. Creative Commons offers open content licences that let creators control how they use copyright. Casserly says there are many problems with copyright, which was made in an “analogue age.” She says copyright often stops people from sharing, building on, and remixing each other’s work in many ways.

Casserly says artists should think of new ways to share and make money from their work. She quotes science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees. The magazine’s editor, Rachael Jolley, said today’s copyright laws do not work well in the digital age and could make consumers into criminals. She said new ways are needed so artists, writers, and musicians can earn a living from their work.

The argument is active, and illegal downloading is very large. Ofcom collected data that suggests that in the UK, between November 2012 and January 2013, 280 million music tracks were pirated. Also pirated were 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files.

Ofcom said 18% of internet users over age 12 say they recently pirated content, and 9% say they worry about getting caught. Pullman writes that getting music quickly as MP3 downloads is still surprising to people who have been building up their iTunes list for some time.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron chose Conservative MP Mike Weatherley to be his adviser on this topic. A spokesman for the BPI, the record industry trade body, said Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creative producers it protects. The spokesman said they hope Weatherley and the prime minister’s support for copyright will influence the UK’s intellectual property office.
