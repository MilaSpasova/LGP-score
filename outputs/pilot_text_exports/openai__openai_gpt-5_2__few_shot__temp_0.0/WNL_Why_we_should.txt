James Hamblin is a senior editor at The Atlantic magazine. In his series “If Our Bodies Could Talk,” he tried a challenge: he stopped showering for a while. He took fewer showers, and when he did shower, he did not use shampoo or soap. He wanted to see what happens when we clean our bodies too much.

He found something many people have also noticed. When we use a lot of soap, body wash, and scrubbing tools, our skin works harder to get back to normal. Showering can wash away the skin’s natural oil and helpful bacteria. This can happen even more when we use hot water and cleaning products.

After a shower, your skin may feel dry and tight. That is because natural moisture was washed away. Our skin also has millions of helpful bacteria, like our gut does. Frequent rubbing and scrubbing can destroy these bacteria. Then, when bacteria grow back, the new ones may cause more body smell. So showering too often can sometimes make you smell worse.

If you stop showering and using soap, your skin may go through an early adjustment time that can seem unpleasant. After that, the skin often returns to balance. Oil production can slow down, and healthy bacteria can grow again.

In the end, Hamblin decided the human body can do well on its own. It is not only about smell or looks. Dermatologists also say using less soap can help acne and eczema.

Taking fewer showers and using fewer products can also help the environment. The average shower lasts seven minutes and uses 65 litres of water. That is 65 litres of clean, drinkable water mixed with soap and sent down the drain each day, sometimes more than once.

Clean water is becoming more important, especially as California has another summer of drought. Clean water is one of the world’s most valuable resources, and it may soon be harder to get. Also, body wash bottles can harm the environment. These are good reasons to use fewer cleaning products.

If this idea makes you feel worried because you have been near people who smell, try not to panic. Many people who wash less still use deodorant if they need it. People’s natural smell is different, and it can change because of diet, how much water they drink, and exercise. Also, washing your hands with soap is still recommended to help stop infectious diseases from spreading.

You do not have to stop showering all at once like Hamblin did. But you can try a simple change: reduce. Skip a few showers, use less soap, and let the helpful bacteria on your skin grow a little.
