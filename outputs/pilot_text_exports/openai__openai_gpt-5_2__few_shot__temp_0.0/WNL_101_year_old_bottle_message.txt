Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8th April, 2014, she told people about an amazing moment. She received a message in a bottle, 101 years after her grandfather threw it into the Baltic Sea. People think it may be the oldest message in a bottle in the world. A museum in Germany gave it to her and is showing it.

Erdmann, 62, said it was a big surprise. A man came to her door and said he had mail from her grandfather. He said a message in a bottle had been found, and the name on the card was her grandfather’s. The visitor was a family-history researcher. He found her in Berlin after the letter was given to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg.

The bottle was a brown beer bottle. It had been in the sea for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it while fishing in the Baltic Sea near the city of Kiel. Holger von Neuhoff, a museum worker, said it was the oldest bottled message he had seen. He said the museum has older documents, but not with the bottle. With both the bottle and the paper, this is the oldest right now. He said it is in very good condition.

Researchers think Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913. He was hiking with a nature group. He was 20 years old.

People could not read most of the postcard. But they could read the Berlin address on the front. They could also read his polite request asking the finder to send the note to his home address.

Erdmann said he also put two stamps in the bottle, so the finder would not have to pay. She said he did not think it would take 101 years.

Erdmann said the message made her feel very emotional. She did not know her grandfather because he died at age 54, six years before she was born.

She said she knew very little about him. But she learned he was a writer who was open-minded. He believed in freedom and that everyone should respect each other. She said he helped young people and later traveled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful because she could see where her family came from.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and traveling around the world, and she is open-minded too. She said what her grandfather taught his two daughters, her mother taught her, and she then taught it to her sons.

Even though she was happy to get the message, she said she hopes people today will not throw bottles with messages into the sea. She said the sea now has so many bottles and so much rubbish that people should not add more.

The bottle and message will be shown at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts will try to read the rest of the text. It is not clear what will happen to the bottle later, but Erdmann hopes it will stay at the museum.

She said they want to show a few photos with the bottle, so visitors can see the young man who threw it into the water.
