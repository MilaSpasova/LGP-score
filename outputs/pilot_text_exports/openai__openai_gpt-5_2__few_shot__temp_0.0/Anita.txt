Anitta is a new star in Brazil’s funk music. She became famous by bringing the sound of the favela into popular music. But now people are arguing about her skin colour.

Some people who fight discrimination say Anitta had to give up looking black to sell music to the mostly white middle-class market. The argument started after “before and after” photos were published. They show her skin looking much lighter after she signed a deal with Warner.

In the first photo, when she was not well known, she looked darker. In the second photo, a marketing picture after she became famous, she looks paler. People are not sure why. It could be whitening products, cosmetic surgery, or more likely changes with Photoshop. The big change made people talk again about a question in Brazil: do you need to be pale to succeed?

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote online about this. She said Anitta’s image change showed hidden discrimination in media and marketing. Arraes wrote that many people do not accept that they are racist, and they think Brazil is a “multiracial democracy,” but statistics show it is not. She said whitening shows a society that does not support diversity. She wrote that white is linked with being rich and successful, and black is seen as the opposite.

Anitta’s real name is Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

Now she is very famous. She released an album and had a huge hit song, “Show das Poderosas.” The song reached number one and got 52 million views on YouTube.

Many people love her as a pop idol with catchy songs and a strong message. The people who support her also present her as a bridge between the mostly black and mixed-race shanty towns on Rio’s hills and the richer and whiter areas below.

Anitta has changed her style to reach more people. She reduced the sexy dancing, “gangsta” references, and explicit lyrics that are common in baile funk. But now some people ask if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract a more profitable audience. Arraes said this kind of pressure can affect many pop stars. She said if a pop star has curly hair, they may feel forced to straighten it, and if they have a big nose, they may feel forced to get rhinoplasty. She said it can hurt self-esteem.

This topic is sensitive in Brazil, where many people are mixed race. Brazil was one of the last large countries to ban slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. But in Brazil, colour often matters more than race or family background. Even though the country aims to be a multiracial democracy, skin tone is clearly linked to inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Up to 2011, black or mixed-race students also spent about two fewer years in school on average.

The government says the gap is getting smaller because of quota systems for university places and other affirmative action. But the difference is still easy to see. Most business and government leaders are white, while most low-paid jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In rich areas like Ipanema and Gávea, you are much more likely to see black nannies pushing strollers with white toddlers than to see a white nanny pushing a black child.

It is not easy to define colour in Brazil. In the 2010 census, people who called themselves white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million said they were black, two million said they were Asian, and 0.5% said they were indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology lecturer at the Federal University of Pernambuco, said Anitta won the middle class by taking a rebellious sound and making it calmer and easier for everyone to accept. He said this was not because of racial whitening. He said what changed was the social space where she made her art: from the periphery to the centre.

Other people also say the focus on colour is too big. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who worked with Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot, but not her colour. He said it was more about makeup, hairstylists, and the way she dresses. He also said he did not think she was pressured. He said she always liked to show off, sing, and dance, and that this natural side helped her succeed.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador in north-eastern Brazil, said the prejudice was not mainly in the public, but in music producers and media executives. He said the public showed they cared about music itself by choosing Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, as the recent winner of the talent show The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for a comment. But in a recent interview, she said identity should be chosen by the person. She said an “all-powerful” woman does not need to be beautiful. She said a woman can be marvellous and powerful because of her attitude. She said she tries to show people that we can be who we want.
