Opposition to Western Australia’s shark cull has grown. Thousands of people went to beaches around Australia to ask the state’s premier to stop the policy. RSPCA Australia and Virgin Atlantic owner Richard Branson also spoke against it.

The government began catching and killing sharks longer than three metres after what it called an “unprecedented” number of shark attacks on Western Australia’s coast. A 35-year-old surfer was killed in November 2013. He was the sixth person to die from a shark attack in two years.

But the Shark Attack File says Australia has averaged one shark-related death a year for the last 50 years.

Kate Faehrmann, a board member of the Sea Shepherd Conservation Society, spoke at a protest in Perth. She said the policy will not work. She said drumlines are not selective. They can kill sharks of many sizes, and also dolphins, turtles, and other animals.

Thousands of people protested at Perth’s Cottesloe Beach and Sydney’s Manly Beach. Hundreds protested at Glenelg in south-west Adelaide, and at beaches in Victoria and Queensland. Faehrmann said the protests showed many Australians want sharks protected. Surfer Anthony Joyce, who once had his foot caught in a shark’s mouth, said killing sharks will make no real difference.

The state government has refused to give a running total of sharks killed. There have been reports that sharks smaller than three metres were released after being caught. Drumlines are floating drums anchored to the sea bed, with bait on hooks under them.

Conservationists say there is no evidence the cull will reduce shark attacks on humans. They say no earlier cull used only drumlines. Researchers at the University of Western Australia say the recent rise in attacks may be linked to the state having the fastest-growing population in Australia, not to more sharks.

Richard Peirce, chairman of the UK conservation charity the Shark Trust, said the cull would not work and could even bring more predators near the coast. He said leaving drumlines in overnight could attract other predators to the area because sharks and other animals may be hooked and injured.

Around the world in 2012, there were 80 unprovoked shark attacks, and seven were fatal. Humans kill nearly 100m sharks each year. RSPCA Australia said it believes the cull is not justified. It said there is no evidence the rise in attacks is due to more sharks. Instead, it said, more people are in the water.

Richard Branson told Fairfax Radio the policy was backfiring. He said Premier Colin Barnett may have thought it would help tourism, but Branson said it would do the opposite. He said it makes people think there is a big problem and could stop people from visiting Perth and the countryside around it. He said it could worry people for no good reason.
