A thick layer of bird droppings covers a shiny railing at one of Britain’s most expensive homes. Pigeon bones are inside, with broken mirrors, and water runs through damaged parts of the ceiling. This house is called The Towers. It is a £30m palace on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London. People have kept its terrible condition secret until now.

The Towers is one of ten very large houses in the middle of The Bishops Avenue. This street is in the center of London’s fast-rising property market. The houses have been almost empty since they were bought about 25 years ago. It is believed they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. Their Greek-style columns are breaking, and their mosaic swimming pools are full of rubble. Nature has taken over so much that owls have moved in.

This empty and ruined scene appears all along this famous street. Lloyds Bank says The Bishops Avenue is the second most expensive street in Britain. At the same time, more and more people in London struggle to buy a home because house prices rise by 11.2% each year. Yet 16 mansions on the most expensive part of the street are empty. Many are behind locked gates, with steel bars over windows, overgrown gardens, and guard dogs.

Across the street is another empty mansion, worth £18m, with broken windows and walls covered with anti-climb paint. Another house has metal bars on its windows and was sold for £20m.

Even so, prices keep rising. Dryades, a mansion that was owned until recently by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007 and is believed to be worth about £30m today. Heath Lodge, where fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou was murdered in 1984 by a silver bullet, is worth £13m today. It was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

Seeing these empty, falling-apart homes can be painful for people who struggle to afford housing in one of the world’s most expensive cities. A security guard on the street said it was upsetting to watch so much space—enough to house dozens of people—being left to decay.

In the past, rich royals with oil money from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia were among the first to move to this curving road near Hampstead Heath. Iranians came after the fall of the shah. Now Chinese buyers are coming, after Russians and Kazakhs who have paid millions for an address that estate agents say is as famous as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions were for sale for £65m and £38m. They promised lots of Italian marble, lifts with leather padding, and luxury panic rooms.

But on the grounds of the empty houses, stone fountains break apart and lawns turn into muddy swamps. Inside one house, water drips through a huge crystal chandelier onto a thick carpet that is rotting under parts of a collapsed ceiling. Moss grows through broken bricks, and mirrored tiles are scattered in a bathroom. The swimming pool has about a foot of dirty, salty water, with flowers growing through the tiles. Wooden boards in the sauna stick out.

The worst ruin is The Towers, a big mansion on land with many trees, including hornbeams, oaks, and limes. Pigeons fill its large, high rooms. Water pours through three floors, and the walls have turned bright green with algae. Water drops into a huge, empty basement swimming pool. Unopened wooden crates marked “bullet-proof glass” show the past owners were worried about security.

Today, very few people live on The Bishops Avenue all the time. A security guard outside one mansion said the owners were not there. Another guard outside Royal Mansion would not say if anyone was home. A worker at another house warned the Guardian about guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare businessman with personal wealth of £45m, said he is one of the few people who live there permanently.

He said, “It’s not a friendly place where you can talk over the fence. To be honest, I have never seen what my neighbours look like. Next door, a Saudi princess spent £35m on a new house and I’ve never seen her. There are about three houses lived in 24/7 and half the properties are used three to six months a year. The other half, who knows if they come or not?”

These broken, multi-million-pound houses show a property culture where the world’s richest people treat British homes as investments. One Hyde Park, a group of apartments in Knightsbridge, is another example. More than half of its flats are registered with the council as empty or second homes.

Still, people on the street talk about building £5m apartments instead of £50m mansions, to bring people back.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, plans to rebuild one valuable site on the street into 20 apartments. They will have a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

He said, “If you build a big house and try and sell for £30m to £40m, it won’t sell. Locals won’t buy and so you have to bring in overseas buyers.”

But using the empty homes to help fix the housing crisis still seems far away. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some of the ruined homes could become affordable homes. He said the land would cost too much.

He said, “Very wealthy people own property there. Sometimes they live in them and sometimes they don’t.”
