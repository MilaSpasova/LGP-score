This new light could be as helpful as Trevor Baylis’s wind-up radio was in the pre-internet 1990s. It is a cheap light that gets free power from gravity. It could help stop people in Africa and India from using dangerous kerosene lamps.

But when British designer Patrick Hunt tried to get money in the usual way, from banks or venture capital, he had a problem. “We tried to get funding to make it happen, but it’s slow and complex and it’s unproven and nobody wants to take a risk,” he said.

So he tried crowdfunding on a US website called Indiegogo, which had just opened in the UK. In five days he reached his goal and raised £36,200. His campaign was so popular at the end of 2012 that in 40 days he raised £400,000.

The LED light uses a dynamo. The dynamo is turned by a 10kg bag of rocks that slowly drops down. The bag is attached to the light and lifted to about 2m high. As it falls slowly to the ground, it makes enough power for 30 minutes of light.

Hunt is getting ready to make the lights in China. He will test the market again by sending 1,000 lights to Africa before full production. He hopes to make millions of units.

He is part of a new group of business founders using the fast-growing crowdfunding world to get money. Another new site is InvestingZone, which connects rich people with start-up entrepreneurs.

Indiegogo does not give people shares. Instead, it lets campaign owners offer “perks” for different donation amounts. People who helped fund Hunt’s light could feel good about helping poorer people, and they also got their own light.

Danae Ringelmann, a co-founder of Indiegogo, says the “gravity light” shows how crowdfunding can be fair and can test an idea. She said, “It is the first time that finance has been fast, efficient and meritocratic, because it is not about ‘How do I get access to the decision makers in that bank?’ or ‘Who do I know in that venture capital outfit?’ This is all about proving your worth to your customers and fans, getting them to validate your idea and fund it.”

She also said that even ideas that do not get funded are still worth testing, because you save time and learn faster.

Ringelmann lives in the US. She started her career as a Wall Street analyst. In 2008, she quit and used her skills to help friends in the arts raise money.

At first, the site focused on films. It launched at the Sundance Film Festival that year.

Five years later, it is raising about $2m a week for new businesses that are starting or growing. In December 2012, it started euro and sterling services to grow in Europe. It says Britain is its third biggest market. International activity has risen 41% since December.

There are many competitors, like Kickstarter, Seedrs, and Funding Circle. But Ringelmann says Indiegogo is different because anyone can start a campaign. No project is too strange.

Indiegogo charges a 4% fee if a campaign succeeds. If a campaign does not reach its goal, users can choose to return all the money to donors for free, or keep the money but pay a 9% fee.

A British woman, Lauren Pears, raised £100,000 on the site to open a “cat café” in London. It is called Lady Dinah’s Cat Emporium. It has not opened yet, but it is described as a place where people can “come in from the cold to a comfortable wingback chair, a hot cup of tea, a book and a cat”.

Ringelmann said, “We’ve seen campaigns that go to venture capitalists get rejected because the venture guys say, ‘Great idea but no idea if the market actually wants it; it could be a gadget that no one cares about,’”.

She said that entrepreneurs can run an Indiegogo campaign, and even the campaign can show investors that people want the product. She said, “It allows you to test your market, test your pricing, test your features, discover new revenue streams, get vital feedback.”

With her Wall Street experience and work helping 100,000 businesses and services raise money, Ringelmann has advice for new entrepreneurs.

She said, “Ideas are a dime a dozen. It’s all about the execution and, if you are afraid that your idea will be stolen by someone who could execute it better and faster than you, then you are not the right person to execute that idea. It’s all about confidence to move fast and to learn.”

Ringelmann says Indiegogo’s growth in Europe, and a deal with a web payments company, are part of her dream to make finance more open to everyone. This deal will let people pay with local cards like Maestro in the UK and Carte Bleue in France, as well as PayPal. More than 7,000 finance campaigns are live on the site.

Crowdfunding has grown as another choice instead of banks. But it is not as attractive to very rich investors, who usually want a share of a promising business. That may start to change in the UK with the launch of InvestingZone.
