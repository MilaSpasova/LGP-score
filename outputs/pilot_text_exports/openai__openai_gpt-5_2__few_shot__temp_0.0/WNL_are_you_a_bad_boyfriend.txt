Dan Sullivan was late to breakfast with the Guardian. He said it was because police stopped him while he was riding his motorcycle.

Sullivan says he works too much. He misses dinner dates. He forgets to give presents.

So the 27-year-old venture capitalist made a start-up to help: BetterBoyfriend.me. It is a service that sends a present to girlfriends and wives every month for about $70.

Sullivan works in finance. He has been quietly testing the service. About 350 boyfriends have signed up. They bring in about $17,000 each month. He says most members are his Harvard and investor friends, like venture capitalists, founders, and workers at companies like Apple, Google, and Facebook. Their girlfriends have been getting presents from Sullivan for the last year.

Each month, members choose from a list of seven gifts, like chocolate, tea sets, and manicures. The package is sent to the boyfriend with no brand name and no label.

Sullivan tests ideas by buying Facebook ads to see what happens. BetterBoyfriend.me became popular fast. Sullivan said, “BetterBoyfriend hit something. It’s a pain point everyone has.”

BetterBoyfriend.me was not his first idea. For a few days, he thought about a dog-sharing idea. He said he saw rich guys looking miserable while walking dogs. He said a dog is only valuable 5% of the time. He suggested having someone babysit dogs so other people could rent them.

Another start-up, called InvisibleBoyfriend, lets users make a fake social partner who is always available to talk. The result is a “boyfriend” who can text with you all day.

Founder Kyle Tabor started it to help users stop unwanted attention. But he found that people became close to their computer-made partners. He said many users want companionship through conversation, not just “proof” of a relationship.

For Sullivan and Tabor, a surprise was how real the customer relationships became. Sullivan says he now feels like a relationship helper for the boyfriends.

Sullivan says he made mistakes. At first, the packages had receipts with his name, Dan Sullivan. He said one boyfriend wrote, “Listen, dude, she’s not mad but Cynthia found out.”

Sullivan says about 50% of the women who get packages know he is involved. He says it depends on age. He thinks that after people have been married a while, they do not keep many secrets.

Over the year, Sullivan says he has gotten to know the boyfriends well. They even delivered a gift to a hospital delivery room. He says the key is to remember that his business relationship is with the boyfriend.

When he first started the company, he put BetterBoyfriend.me tags on flower bouquets. Then he went to Dolores Park in the Mission District. It is a popular place where many tech and young people hang out.

He looked for couples and gave the flowers to the girl. But the boyfriend did not like that at all. Sullivan said it made sense. So he changed his plan and started giving the bouquets to the boyfriends instead.
