This new invention could be as important as Trevor Baylis’s wind-up radio in the 1990s, before the internet. It is a cheap light that uses gravity for power. It could help stop the use of dangerous kerosene lamps in Africa and India.

But British designer Patrick Hunt had trouble getting money in the usual way, from banks or venture capital. He said funding was slow and hard because the idea was unproven and people did not want to take a risk.

So he tried crowdfunding on a US website called Indiegogo, which had just started in the UK. In five days, he reached his goal and raised £36,200. By the end of 2012, after 40 days, he had raised £400,000.

The LED light uses a dynamo. It is powered by a 10kg bag of rocks that slowly falls. The bag is attached to the light and lifted about 2m. As it falls, it makes enough power for half an hour of light.

Hunt is getting ready to make the light in China. He will test the market again by sending 1,000 lights to Africa before making many more. He hopes to make millions of units.

Hunt is part of a new group of entrepreneurs who use crowdfunding to raise money. Another new site is InvestingZone, which matches rich people with start-up entrepreneurs.

Indiegogo does not give shares. Instead, people who donate can get “perks” at different donation levels. People who helped fund Hunt’s light could feel good about helping poorer people, and they could also get their own light.

Danae Ringelmann, a co-founder of Indiegogo, says the “gravity light” shows how crowdfunding can be fair and can test an idea. She says it can be fast and efficient, because it is not about knowing the right people at a bank or a venture capital firm. It is about showing customers and fans that the idea is good, and getting them to support it.

She also says it is useful to test ideas even if they may fail. If it is not a good idea, you find out sooner and learn faster.

Ringelmann lives in the US and began her career as a Wall Street analyst. In 2008, she quit and used her skills to help friends in the arts raise money.

At first, the site focused on films and launched at the Sundance Film Festival that year.

Five years later, Indiegogo raises about $2m a week for new businesses that are starting or growing. In December 2012, it added euro and sterling services to grow in Europe. It says Britain is its third biggest market. International activity has risen 41% since December.

There are many competitors, like Kickstarter, Seedrs, and Funding Circle. But Ringelmann says Indiegogo is different because anyone can start a campaign, and no project is seen as too strange.

Indiegogo charges a 4% fee for successful campaigns. If a campaign does not reach its goal, the user can either refund all the money to donors for free, or keep the money raised and pay a 9% fee.

A British woman, Lauren Pears, raised £100,000 on the site to open a “cat café” in London. It is called Lady Dinah’s Cat Emporium. It has not opened yet, but it is described as a place where people can sit in a comfortable wingback chair, have a hot cup of tea, read a book, and be with a cat.

Ringelmann says some campaigns are rejected by venture capitalists. They may say the idea is good, but they do not know if people really want it.

She says entrepreneurs can run an Indiegogo campaign, even before launching the project. The campaign can be proof that there is a market, and this can help convince venture capitalists.

She says crowdfunding lets you test your market, your prices, and your features. It can also help you find new ways to earn money and get important feedback.

With her Wall Street experience and her work helping 100,000 businesses and services raise money, Ringelmann gives advice to new entrepreneurs. She says ideas are very common, and what matters is how well you do the work. She says if you are scared someone will steal your idea and do it better and faster, then you may not be the right person to do it. She says you need confidence, speed, and a willingness to learn.

Ringelmann says expanding into Europe and making a deal with a web payment provider are part of a goal to make finance more open to everyone. This will let people pay using local card services like Maestro in the UK and Carte Bleue in France, as well as PayPal. More than 7,000 finance campaigns are live on the site.

Crowdfunding has grown as an alternative to banks, but it is not as attractive to big investors. Big investors often want a stake in a business. This might start to change in the UK with the launch of InvestingZone.
