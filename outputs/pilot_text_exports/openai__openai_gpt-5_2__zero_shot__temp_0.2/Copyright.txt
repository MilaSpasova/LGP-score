Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is theft. He says it is like putting your hand in someone’s pocket and stealing their wallet. He wrote this in an article for Index on Censorship. Pullman is also president of the Society of Authors. He strongly supports copyright laws. He criticizes people who think it is OK to download music or books without paying.

Pullman says new technology is very impressive, but people forget that stealing is wrong. He says it is unfair when someone steals an artist’s work and is not punished.

His article came after leaders from the music industry met British Prime Minister David Cameron at Downing Street. They talked about web piracy.

Pullman wrote the His Dark Materials trilogy. He says writers and musicians often work for years with little money and little attention. They work hard to make something people enjoy. But when they finally might earn a living from it, illegal downloading takes that money away. He says the idea is simple: if we enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

In the same magazine issue, Pullman is in a dialogue with Cathy Casserly, the chief executive of Creative Commons. Creative Commons offers open content licences that let creators control their copyright in different ways. Casserly says there are problems with copyright because it was made in an “analogue age.” She says copyright can stop people from sharing, building on, and remixing other people’s work in ways that were not possible when the laws were first made.

Casserly says artists should think of new ways to share and make money from their work. She quotes the science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees with her. The editor, Rachael Jolley, says current copyright laws do not work well in the digital age and could make ordinary consumers into criminals. She says new ways are needed so artists, writers, and musicians can earn money.

Illegal downloading is very common. Data from Ofcom, the UK communications regulator, suggests that in the UK between November 2012 and January 2013, people illegally copied 280 million music tracks. They also pirated 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files.

Ofcom says 18% of internet users over age 12 admit they recently pirated content. Also, 9% say they fear getting caught. Pullman says getting music quickly as MP3 downloads still surprises people who have been building their iTunes list for a long time.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron appointed Conservative MP Mike Weatherley as his adviser on this topic. A spokesman for the BPI, the record industry trade body, said Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creators it is meant to protect. The BPI hopes Weatherley’s influence, and the prime minister’s support, will affect the UK’s intellectual property office.
