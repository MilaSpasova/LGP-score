Some people have new and unusual ideas to make cities nicer to live in. City life can be fun, but it is often hard to live in a clean, green way. People in cities deal with pollution, traffic, and fewer green spaces. Here are some of the wilder ideas.

Pop-up parks
Some cities seem built more for cars than for people. A pop-up park is a simple idea. You take an empty parking space, a little money, and a few plants, and you make a small park. The PARK(ing) project started as an art experiment in San Francisco and then spread around the world. People have also tried temporary city farms and ecology demonstrations. The movement has a day of action every September.

Subterranean storage
Not everyone in a city uses a car. Bicycles are becoming more popular. But people need a safe place to keep them. A Tokyo engineering company called Giken made an idea called an “eco-cycle, anti-seismic underground bicycle park.” It is a round storage space only seven metres wide, and it goes deep into the ground. It can hold 204 bikes. Owners press a button, and the system brings the bike back up in about 13 seconds.

Glow-in-the-dark trees
Many people think of Christmas lights when they hear about glowing trees, but Daan Roosegaarde thinks differently. This Dutch designer-artist made an experimental “bioluminescent” plant. The technology mixes DNA from glowing marine bacteria with part of a plant to make it glow like a jellyfish. Tests are happening to make a large-scale version. Roosegaarde hopes it could one day replace normal street lights.

Footfall harvesting
In West Ham in east London, many commuters and shoppers walk over a raised walkway near the underground station. Most people do not notice the slight bounce under their feet. The rubber surface moves about five millimetres, and this helps power the streetlights above. The floor has smart tiles that catch energy from footsteps and turn it into electricity. The UK company Pavegen has also put a similar system in London’s Heathrow Airport and in other places.

Supertrees
Singapore’s Gardens by the Bay has man-made trees called “supertrees.” They are steel-framed and can be up to 50 metres high. Flowers and ferns grow on them. Their metal tops help absorb and spread heat. They can collect rainwater and help move air for two “climate-controlled biomes” (large conservatories) below. Eleven of the 18 trees also have solar panels on their “branches.”
