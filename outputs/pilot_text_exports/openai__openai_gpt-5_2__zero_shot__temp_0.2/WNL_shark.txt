Opposition to Western Australia’s shark cull has grown. Thousands of people went to beaches across Australia to ask the state’s premier to stop the policy. RSPCA Australia and Virgin Atlantic owner Richard Branson also spoke against it.

The policy catches and kills sharks longer than three metres. It started after what the state government called an “unprecedented” number of shark attacks on Western Australia’s coast. A 35-year-old surfer was killed in November 2013. He was the sixth person to die from a shark attack in two years.

However, the Shark Attack File says Australia has averaged one shark-related death a year for the last 50 years.

Kate Faehrmann, a board member at the Sea Shepherd Conservation Society, spoke at a protest in Perth. She said the policy will not work. She said drumlines, used to catch sharks, kill many animals by mistake. She said they can kill sharks of any size, and also dolphins, turtles, and other animals. She said this is why the community does not want the policy.

Thousands protested on Perth’s Cottesloe Beach and Sydney’s Manly Beach. Hundreds also protested at Glenelg in south-west Adelaide, and at beaches in Victoria and Queensland. Faehrmann said the protests showed Australians want sharks protected. She said many people love sharks and do not want them killed, even though people are scared because of movies like Jaws.

Anthony Joyce, a surfer who once had his foot caught in a shark’s mouth, said killing the sharks will make no real difference.

The state government has refused to give an ongoing count of how many sharks were killed. There were reports of sharks smaller than three metres being released after getting caught. Drumlines are floating drums fixed to the sea bed, with bait hanging on hooks under them.

Conservationists say there is no evidence the cull will reduce shark attacks on humans. They say no earlier cull used only drumlines. Researchers at the University of Western Australia say the recent rise in attacks may be more connected to the state having the fastest-growing population in Australia, not to more sharks.

Richard Peirce, chairman of the UK-based Shark Trust, said the cull would not work and could even bring more predators close to the coast. He said it is sad the government did not try other ways instead of killing animals without choosing which ones. He said leaving drumlines in overnight could attract other predators, because sharks and other animals may be hooked and hurt.

Around the world in 2012, there were 80 unprovoked shark attacks, and seven were fatal. In comparison, humans kill nearly 100m sharks each year.

RSPCA Australia said the cull is not justified. It said there is no evidence that more attacks are happening because there are more sharks. It said the increase fits with changes in population and human behaviour, meaning more people are in the water.

Richard Branson told Fairfax Radio the policy was backfiring. He said the premier, Colin Barnett, may have thought it would help tourism, but it could do the opposite. He said it makes the problem seem bigger than it is, and could make people avoid Perth and the countryside around it. He said it may only make people worry for no reason.
