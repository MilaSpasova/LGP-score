A thick layer of bird droppings covers a gold-painted railing at one of Britain’s most expensive homes. Pigeon bones lie among broken mirrors, and water runs through broken parts of the ceiling. This house is called The Towers. It is a £30m mansion on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London. Its ruin has been kept secret until now.

The Towers is one of ten mansions in the middle of The Bishops Avenue. This street is at the centre of London’s rising house prices. These mansions have been almost empty since they were bought about 25 years ago. It is believed they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. Their Greek-style columns are breaking, and their mosaic swimming pools are full of rubble. Nature has moved in so much that owls now live there.

This empty and broken scene is found along the avenue. Lloyds Bank says The Bishops Avenue is the second most expensive street in Britain. At the same time, many people in London struggle to buy a home because prices rise by 11.2% a year. Yet 16 mansions on the most expensive part of the street are empty. Many are behind locked gates. Their windows have steel bars, and their gardens are overgrown and watched by guard dogs.

Across the street is another empty mansion worth £18m, with broken windows and anti-climb paint on the walls. Another house has metal bars on the windows and was sold for £20m.

Even so, prices keep rising. Dryades, a mansion once owned by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007 and is believed to be worth about £30m today. Heath Lodge, where fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou was murdered in 1984 with a silver bullet, is worth £13m today. It was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

Seeing these empty homes can be painful for people who struggle to pay for housing in this very expensive city. A security guard on the street said it is upsetting to see so much space—enough to house dozens of people—falling apart.

Rich royals from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia were among the first to move to this road near Hampstead Heath. Iranians came after the fall of the shah. Now, Chinese buyers are following Russians and Kazakhs. They have spent millions to get an address that estate agents say is as famous as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions were for sale for £65m and £38m. They promised Italian marble, lifts with leather walls, and luxury panic rooms.

But the empty mansions are still falling apart. In the gardens, stone fountains break and lawns turn into wet bogs. Inside one house, water drips through a large crystal chandelier. The carpet is rotting under parts of a collapsed ceiling. Moss grows through broken bricks. Mirror tiles are scattered across a bathroom. The swimming pool has about a foot of dirty water, and flowers grow through its tiles. Wood panels bend away from the sauna.

The most dramatic ruin is The Towers. It is a grand mansion set among acres of hornbeams, oaks, and lime trees. Pigeons fill its large halls with high ceilings. Water pours through three floors, and the walls are bright green from algae. The water drops into a large, empty basement swimming pool. Wooden crates marked “bullet-proof glass” show the owners were worried about security.

Today, very few people live full time on The Bishops Avenue. One security guard outside a mansion said the owners were not there. Another, outside Royal Mansion, would not say if anyone was home. A staff member at another mansion only warned the Guardian about the guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare businessman with personal wealth of £45m, said he is one of the few who lives there all the time.

He said, “It’s not a neighbourly place, where you can chat over the fence. To be honest, I have never seen what my neighbours look like. Next door, a Saudi princess spent £35m on a new house and I’ve never seen her. There are about three houses that are lived in 24/7 and half of the properties are occupied three to six months a year. The other half, who knows if they come or not?”

These damaged, very expensive homes show a culture where the richest people treat British property as investments. One Hyde Park, a group of apartments in Knightsbridge, is another example. More than half of its flats are registered with the council as empty or second homes.

Even so, people on the avenue talk about building £5m apartments instead of £50m mansions, to bring more people back.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, plans to rebuild one valuable site as 20 apartments. They would have a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

He said, “If you build a big house and try and sell for £30m to £40m, it won’t sell. Locals won’t buy and so you have to bring in overseas buyers.”

But it still seems unlikely that these empty homes will help solve the housing crisis. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some of the empty houses could become affordable homes. He said the land price would be too high.

He said, “Very wealthy people own property there. Sometimes they live in them and sometimes they don’t.”
