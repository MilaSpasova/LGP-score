Anitta is a new famous funk singer from Brazil. She has millions of fans because she brought music from the favelas (poor neighborhoods) into popular music. But now people are arguing about her skin color.

Some anti-discrimination groups and writers say Anitta had to seem “less black” to succeed in a mostly white middle-class market.

The argument grew after people shared old and new photos of her. In the older photo, when she was not famous, she looked darker. In a newer photo used for marketing after she became famous, she looks lighter. Some people think this could be from skin-lightening products, cosmetic surgery, or, more likely, Photoshop. These photos made people talk again about whether you must have lighter skin to succeed in Brazil.

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote that this shows hidden discrimination in media and marketing. She said many people do not admit they are racist and say Brazil is a multiracial democracy, but statistics show it is not true. She wrote that society often connects white skin with being rich and successful, and connects black skin with the opposite. She also said that if pop stars have curly hair, they may feel forced to straighten it, and if they have a big nose, they may feel forced to get a nose job. She said this harms self-esteem.

Anitta was born Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

Now she is very famous. She released an album and a hit song, Show das Poderosas. It reached the top of the charts and got 52 million views on YouTube.

People like her pop songs and her message. Her supporters also show her as a “bridge” between mostly black and mixed-race shanty towns on Rio’s hills and the wealthier, whiter areas below.

She changed baile funk to fit a bigger audience. She reduced suggestive dancing, gangsta references, and explicit lyrics. But some people now ask if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract richer customers.

Race and skin color are sensitive issues in Brazil. Brazil was one of the last big countries to end slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. In Brazil, people often pay more attention to skin color than to race or family background. Even though Brazil says it wants to be a multiracial democracy, skin color is strongly linked to inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Until 2011, black or mixed-race students also spent about two fewer years in school on average.

The government says the gap is getting smaller because of university quotas and other affirmative action. But the difference is still easy to see. Most business and government leaders are white, while many low-paid jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In rich neighborhoods like Ipanema and Gávea, you are more likely to see black nannies pushing strollers with white toddlers than the other way around.

Skin color categories in Brazil can be confusing. In the 2010 census, people who said they were white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million said black, two million said Asian, and 0.5% said indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology teacher at the Federal University of Pernambuco, thinks Anitta became popular with the middle class by taking a rebellious style of music and making it calmer and easier for everyone to accept. He said this was not done by making her look whiter. He said the main change was that her music moved from the outskirts of society to the center.

Other people also think the focus on color is too big. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who knew Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot, but not her color. He said it is more about makeup, hairstylists, and clothing. He also said she was not forced, because she always liked to sing, dance, and show off, and that this natural style helped her succeed.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador, said the problem is not the public, but music producers and media leaders. He said the public showed they care about music by choosing Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, as the recent winner of the show The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta to comment. But in a recent interview, she said a person’s identity should be defined by the person. She said a powerful woman does not need to be beautiful, but has strong attitude. She said her work tries to show that people can be who they want.
