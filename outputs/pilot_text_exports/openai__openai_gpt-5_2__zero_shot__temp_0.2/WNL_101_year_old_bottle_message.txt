Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8th April, 2014, she told the story of a very unusual event. She got a message in a bottle 101 years after her grandfather threw it into the Baltic Sea. People think it may be the world’s oldest message in a bottle. A museum in Germany gave it to her and is showing it there.

Erdmann, 62, said it was a big surprise. A man came to her door and said he had mail from her grandfather. He said a bottle had been found, and the name on the card was her grandfather’s name. The man was a genealogy researcher. He found her in Berlin after the letter was given to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg.

The brown beer bottle was found by a fisherman named Konrad Fischer. He was fishing in the Baltic Sea near the northern city of Kiel. Holger von Neuhoff, a curator at the museum, said it was the oldest bottle message he had seen. He said the museum has older documents without a bottle, but the bottle and the document together make this the oldest right now. He said it is in very good condition.

Researchers believe Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913. He was hiking with a nature appreciation group. He was 20 years old then.

People could not read much of the postcard. But they could read the Berlin address on the front. They could also read his polite request: the person who found it should send the note to his home address.

Erdmann said he also put two stamps from that time in the bottle. This was so the finder would not have to pay. But he did not think it would take 101 years.

Erdmann said she felt very moved. She did not know her grandfather because he died at age 54, six years before she was born.

She said she knew very little about him, but learned he was a writer. She said he was open-minded, believed in freedom, and believed people should respect each other. She said he did a lot for young people, and later traveled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful because she could see where her roots came from.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and traveling around the world. She said she is open-minded too. She said what he taught his two daughters, her mother taught her, and she has passed it on to her sons.

Even though she was happy to get the message, she said she hopes others will not do the same thing today. She said the sea is already full of bottles and rubbish, so people should not throw more in.

The message and the bottle will be on display at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts will try to read the rest of the text. It is not clear what will happen to the bottle later, but Erdmann hopes it will stay at the museum.

She said they want to add some photos with the bottle, so visitors can see the young man who threw it into the water.
