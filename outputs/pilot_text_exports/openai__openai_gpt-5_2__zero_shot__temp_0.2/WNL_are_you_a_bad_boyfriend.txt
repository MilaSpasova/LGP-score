Dan Sullivan was late to breakfast with the Guardian. He was stopped by police while riding his motorcycle.

Sullivan says he works too much. He misses dinner dates. He forgets to buy presents.

So the 27-year-old venture capitalist made a start-up to help. It is called BetterBoyfriend.me. For about $70 a month, it sends a present each month to a girlfriend or wife.

Sullivan works in finance. He has been quietly testing the service. About 350 boyfriends have signed up. The service makes about $17,000 each month. He says most members are his Harvard and investor friends. They include venture capitalists, founders, and workers at companies like Apple, Google, and Facebook. Their girlfriends have been getting presents from Sullivan for the last year.

Each month, members pick from seven gifts, like chocolate, tea sets, and manicures. The package is sent to the boyfriend with no brand name or label.

Sullivan tests ideas by buying Facebook ads to see how much interest they get. BetterBoyfriend.me became popular. He said it solved a problem many people have.

BetterBoyfriend.me was not his first idea. For a few days, he thought about sharing dogs. He said he saw rich men looking unhappy while walking dogs. He said a dog is only valuable 5% of the time. He wondered if someone could babysit dogs and other people could rent them.

Another start-up is called InvisibleBoyfriend. It lets users create a believable social partner who is always available to talk. The result is a “boyfriend” who can text with you all day.

Founder Kyle Tabor started it to help users avoid unwanted advances. But he found that people became attached to their computer-made partners. He said many users want companionship through conversation, not just “proof” of a relationship.

For Sullivan and Tabor, the surprise was the real relationships they formed with customers. Sullivan says he now feels like a relationship consultant for the boyfriends.

Sullivan says he made mistakes. Early packages had receipts with his name, Dan Sullivan. One boyfriend wrote to him and said, “Listen, dude, she’s not mad but Cynthia found out.”

Sullivan says about 50% of the women who receive the packages know he is involved. He says it is linked to age. He thinks that after people have been married a while, they keep fewer secrets.

Over the year, Sullivan says he has gotten to know the boyfriends well. They even delivered a present to a hospital delivery room. He said the important thing is that his relationship is with the boyfriend.

When he first started the company, he put BetterBoyfriend.me tags on flower bouquets. He went to Dolores Park in the Mission District, a popular place with many tech workers and young people.

He looked for couples and gave the bouquet to the girl, but boyfriends did not like it. He said it made sense. So he changed his plan and started giving the bouquets to the boyfriends.
