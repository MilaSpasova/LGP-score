One warm summer evening, I decided to go swimming at Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland. I moved there in 2012. A woman was in the water with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty often swims with a group of people.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Hundreds of people have swum with her, so she seems very tame. She was even in an Irish tourist-board ad where a girl says she wants to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty’s tummy. It looked safe and friendly. About 20 tourists and local people were watching from the pier. Soon after I got into the water, Dusty left the woman and suddenly went wild. Later I learned that Dusty can be very protective of her space when she is with someone. Her tail was flapping hard. At first I thought she was just showing off, but then I realized she might be angry.

I tried to swim back to the pier to get out. But very quickly, Dusty crashed into me with her nose. It was very strong and painful, and she was very fast. I was thrown forward.

People on the pier stared at me in shock. Dusty stayed close to me in the water, still flapping her tail. That scared me most. I thought if she hit me with her tail, I could sink.

I reached the pier, but I could not climb out because I was hurt. I felt terrified. I shouted for help. A man reached down and pulled me onto the steps. Then another man came and said he was an orthopaedic surgeon who specialized in marine trauma. He had been driving into Doolin and saw what was happening.

I was very cold and very worried. I did not know how bad my injuries were, and I was most afraid of internal bleeding. The surgeon checked me and told me he could not feel signs of internal bleeding, but he thought I probably had broken bones. Later I learned I had six spinal fractures, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for several weeks. I could not work for five months, and I had trouble moving, with stiffness and pain. Then I was diagnosed with post-traumatic stress. After the near-death experience, I felt anxious about everything and reacted strongly in ways I never had before. I felt like people looked at me the wrong way. Loud noises upset me, and I had memory loss.

Three months before the accident, I opened a health-food shop. But I had to give it up because I could not work anymore. It was the hardest year of my life, but now it is in the past. I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building up my own osteopathy practice. I understand patients better because I have been one.

I am thankful that I am healthy, and I want to stop other people from getting hurt. Many people have a lovely idea about dolphins and trust them. Who would think a dolphin could attack a person? If a dangerous animal comes at you with its teeth showing, it looks scary. But dolphins seem friendly because they have a wide “smile.”

I am not angry with Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her area, and she is a wild, unpredictable animal. People need to know that. Many people come to swim with her, and they do not understand how dangerous it can be. My injuries were reported to be among several injuries that summer.

After the man pulled me out of the water, Dusty swam away. Then she came back and floated upright next to me, looking at me. We looked into each other’s eyes, and I felt she was sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin, and the anger was gone. The people on the pier were amazed. After that moment, my terror ended. I felt at peace with her.
