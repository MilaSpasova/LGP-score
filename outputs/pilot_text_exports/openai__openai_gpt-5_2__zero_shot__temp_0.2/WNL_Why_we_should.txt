James Hamblin is a senior editor at The Atlantic magazine. In his series, “If Our Bodies Could Talk,” he tried a challenge: shower much less. He also stopped using shampoo and soap when he did shower. He wanted to see what happens when we clean our bodies too much.

He found what many other people have found. When we scrub a lot with soap, body wash, and body tools, our skin tries harder to fix itself and get back to normal. Showering can wash away the skin’s natural oil and bacteria. Hot water and cleaning products can make this even stronger.

After a shower, your skin may feel tight, like it is too small. This can happen because much of your skin’s natural moisture gets washed away. Also, our skin, like our gut, has millions of helpful bacteria living on it. Frequent rubbing and scrubbing can destroy these bacteria.

When the bacteria come back after being washed off, they may be the kind that make more smell. So showering too often may make you smell more. If you stop showering and stop using soap, there can be an early adjustment time that may feel gross. After that, the skin often returns to balance, makes less oil, and healthy bacteria can grow again.

After his experiment, Hamblin thought the human body can be quite lovely when it works on its own. It is not only about smell or looks. Dermatologists suggest that using soap less may help acne and eczema.

Showering less can also help the environment. The average shower lasts seven minutes and uses 65 litres of water. That is 65 litres of clean, drinkable water mixed with soap and sent down the drain every day, sometimes more than once.

Clean water is becoming more important, especially as California faces another summer of drought. Clean water is one of the world’s most valuable resources, and it may soon be in short supply. There is also waste from all the body wash bottles. These are reasons to let your body be more natural.

If this idea makes you feel uncomfortable, relax. Many people who use less soap still use deodorant if they need it. People’s natural smell is different, and it can change with diet, hydration, and exercise. Also, washing your hands with soap is still recommended to help stop infectious diseases from spreading.

You do not have to stop showering all at once like Hamblin did. But between showering many times a day and never showering, there is a middle choice. Shower less often, use less soap, and let the helpful bacteria grow.
