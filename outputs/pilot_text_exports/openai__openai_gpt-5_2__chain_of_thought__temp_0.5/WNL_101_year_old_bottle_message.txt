Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8th April, 2014, she talked about an amazing moment. She received a message in a bottle. Her grandfather had thrown it into the Baltic Sea 101 years earlier. People think it may be the world’s oldest message in a bottle. A museum in Germany, which is showing it, gave it to her.

“It was very surprising,” said Erdmann, 62. She explained how she learned about the bottle. “A man came to my door and said he had mail from my grandfather. He said a message in a bottle had been found, and the name on the card was my grandfather’s.” The visitor was a family-history researcher. He found her in Berlin after the letter was given to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg, a port city in the north.

The bottle was a brown beer bottle. It had been in the sea for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it in his catch while fishing in the Baltic Sea near the northern city of Kiel. Holger von Neuhoff, a museum worker for ocean and science, said this bottled message was the oldest he had seen. He said the museum has older documents, but they were found without a bottle. He said that with both the bottle and the document, this is the oldest right now. He also said it is in very good condition.

Researchers believe Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913. He was hiking with a nature group. He was 20 years old then.

Much of the postcard cannot be read. But people can read the address in Berlin on the front. They can also read his polite request that the person who found it should send the note to his home address.

Erdmann said, “He also put two stamps from that time in the bottle, so the finder would not have to pay. But he did not think it would take 101 years.”

She said she felt very emotional when the message arrived. She did not know her grandfather, because he died at age 54, six years before she was born.

“I knew very little about my grandfather,” she said. “But I found out he was a writer. He was open-minded. He believed in freedom and that everyone should respect each other.” She said he did a lot for young people. Later, he travelled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful, because she could learn where her family came from.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and travelling around the world. She said she is open-minded too. “What he taught his two daughters, my mother taught me, and I passed it on to my sons,” she said.

Even though she was happy to get the message, she said she hopes others will not do the same thing and throw bottles with messages into the sea. She said, “Today, the sea is so full of bottles and rubbish that more should not be thrown in.”

The message and bottle will be on display at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts will try to read the rest of the text. It is not clear what will happen to the bottle after that, but Erdmann hopes it will stay at the museum.

“We want to make some photos available to put with the bottle and give it a face,” she said. “Then visitors can see the young man who threw the bottle into the water.”
