This invention could be as useful as Trevor Baylis’s wind-up radio in the 1990s, before the internet was common. It is a cheap light that uses gravity to make free power. It could help people stop using dangerous kerosene lamps in Africa and India.

The British designer Patrick Hunt first tried to get money from banks or venture capital investors to start selling his invention. But he had trouble. He said getting funding was slow and complicated. The idea was new and not proven, so people did not want to take the risk.

So he tried crowdfunding on a US website called Indiegogo, which had just opened in the UK. In five days, he reached his goal and raised £36,200. His campaign was very popular at the end of 2012. In 40 days, he raised £400,000.

The LED light uses a dynamo to make power. The dynamo is turned by a 10kg bag of rocks as it goes down. The weight is tied to the light and lifted to about 2m high. When it slowly falls to the ground, it makes enough power for 30 minutes of light.

Hunt is getting ready to make the light in China. Before making many more, he will deliver 1,000 lights to Africa to test the market. He hopes later to make millions of units.

Hunt is part of a new group of entrepreneurs who use crowdfunding to get money. Another new site is InvestingZone. It connects rich people with start-up entrepreneurs.

Indiegogo does not give shares. Instead, projects can offer “perks” for different donation amounts. People who gave money to Hunt’s new light could feel good about helping poorer people, and they also got their own light.

Danae Ringelmann, a co-founder of Indiegogo, says the “gravity light” shows how crowdfunding can work fairly. She says it can also test an entrepreneur’s idea. She says money can be raised fast and well because it is not about knowing the right people at a bank or a venture capital firm. It is about showing customers and fans that your idea is good, and getting them to support it.

Ringelmann also says it is worth testing ideas even if they do not get funding. Then you can learn faster and not waste time on a bad idea.

Ringelmann lives in the US. She first worked on Wall Street as an analyst. In 2008, she quit. She wanted to use her skills to help friends in the arts raise money.

At first, the site focused on films. It launched at the Sundance Film Festival that year.

Five years later, Indiegogo was raising about $2m a week for new businesses that were starting or growing. In December 2012, it added euro and sterling services to build its business in Europe. It says Britain is its third biggest market. International activity has risen 41% since December.

There are many competitors, such as Kickstarter, Seedrs, and Funding Circle. But Ringelmann says Indiegogo is different because anyone can start a campaign. No project is seen as too strange.

Indiegogo charges a 4% fee for successful campaigns. If a campaign does not reach its goal, the user can choose one of two options. They can refund all the money to contributors for no charge. Or they can keep the money raised, but then pay a 9% fee.

A British woman, Lauren Pears, raised £100,000 on the site to open a cat café in London. It is called Lady Dinah’s Cat Emporium. It has not opened yet. It is described as a place where people can come in from the cold and sit in a comfortable wingback chair with a hot cup of tea, a book, and a cat.

Ringelmann says some campaigns are rejected by venture capitalists. The investors may say the idea is good, but they do not know if people really want it.

She says entrepreneurs can run an Indiegogo campaign even before they fully launch the project. The campaign can show proof that people want it. That proof can help convince venture capitalists that there is a market.

Ringelmann says crowdfunding lets you test your market, price, and features. It can also help you find new ways to earn money and get important feedback.

With her Wall Street experience and work helping 100,000 businesses and services raise money, Ringelmann gives advice to new entrepreneurs. She says ideas are common. What matters is doing the work well. She says if you are afraid someone will steal your idea and do it better and faster, then you may not be the right person to do it. She says you need confidence to move fast and learn.

Ringelmann says expanding into Europe and making a deal with a web payment provider are part of her dream to make finance more open to everyone. The deal will let people pay with local card services like Maestro in the UK and Carte Bleue in France, and also with PayPal. More than 7,000 finance campaigns are live on the site.

Crowdfunding has grown as another choice besides banks. But it is not as attractive to big investors with lots of money. They usually want a stake in a promising business. That may start to change in the UK with the launch of InvestingZone.
