A thick layer of bird droppings covers a gold-painted railing at one of Britain’s most expensive homes. Dead pigeons lie near broken mirrors, and water runs through broken ceiling edges. This house is called The Towers. It is a £30m palace on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London. Its serious damage has been kept secret until now.

The Towers is one of ten large houses in the middle of The Bishops Avenue. This street is part of London’s fast-rising housing market. These houses have been almost empty since they were bought about 25 years ago. It is believed they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. Their Greek-style columns are breaking. Swimming pools with mosaic tiles are full of broken pieces. Nature has taken over so much that owls have moved in.

This empty and broken scene happens all along the street. Lloyds Bank says The Bishops Avenue is the second most expensive street in Britain. At the same time, more people struggle to buy a home as London house prices rise 11.2% a year. On the most expensive part of the street, 16 mansions are empty. Many are behind locked gates. Their windows are covered with steel bars, and the gardens are overgrown. Guard dogs patrol the grounds.

Across the street is another ruined mansion worth £18m. It has broken windows and walls covered in paint that makes it hard to climb. Another house has metal bars on its windows and has been sold for £20m.

Even so, prices keep rising. Dryades, a mansion that was owned until recently by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007 and is believed to be worth about £30m today. Heath Lodge is where fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou was murdered in 1984 with a silver bullet. It is worth £13m today. It was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

For people who struggle to pay for a home in one of the world’s most expensive cities, these empty mansions can feel painful. A security guard on the street said it was very frustrating to see so many huge homes, big enough to house dozens of people, falling apart.

Some of the first people to move to this curved road near Hampstead Heath were royals with oil wealth from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia. Iranians came after the fall of the shah. Now Chinese buyers are following Russians and Kazakhs, who have spent millions to get an address that estate agents say is as famous as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions have been for sale for £65m and £38m. They promise Italian marble, lifts with leather padding, and luxury panic rooms.

But on the empty properties, stone fountains are breaking and lawns have turned into muddy, wet ground. Inside one house, water drips through a huge crystal chandelier onto a thick carpet that is rotting under parts of a collapsed ceiling. Moss grows through broken bricks, and mirror tiles are scattered in a bathroom. The swimming pool has about a foot of dirty, salty water, and flowers grow through its tiles. Wooden boards in the sauna bend outward.

The most dramatic ruin is The Towers. It is a grand house set among acres of hornbeams, oaks, and limes. Pigeons fill its large halls. Water pours through three floors, turning walls bright green with algae, and drops into a huge, empty basement swimming pool. Unopened wooden crates marked “bullet-proof glass” show the security fears of the earlier owners.

Today, very few people live on The Bishops Avenue all the time. A security guard outside one mansion said the owners were not there. Another, outside Royal Mansion, would not say if anyone was home. A staff member at another mansion only warned the Guardian about the guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare mogul worth £45m, said he is one of the few people who lives there permanently.

He said, “It’s not a neighbourly place, where you can chat over the fence. To be honest, I have never seen what my neighbours look like. Next door, a Saudi princess spent £35m on a new house and I’ve never seen her. There are about three houses that are lived in 24/7 and half of the properties are occupied three to six months a year. The other half, who knows if they come or not?”

These broken, very expensive homes show a property culture where the world’s richest people see British homes as investments. One Hyde Park, a block of flats in Knightsbridge, is another example. More than half of its flats are registered with the council as empty or second homes.

Still, people on the avenue talk about building £5m flats instead of £50m mansions, to try to bring people back.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, plans to rebuild one of the most valuable sites on the avenue. He wants to create 20 flats with a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

He said, “If you build a big house and try and sell for £30m to £40m, it won’t sell. Locals won’t buy and so you have to bring in overseas buyers.”

But using these empty homes to help solve the housing crisis still seems far away. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some of the ruined homes could become affordable homes. He said the land price would be too high.

He said, “Very wealthy people own property there. Sometimes they live in them and sometimes they don’t.”
