Kenton Cool usually likes to talk. But now he can hardly speak. Climbing high mountains has made his voice rough.

He is now in Kathmandu, the capital of Nepal. He flew down from Everest base camp that morning. He is thinking about an amazing set of climbs he finished last weekend.

Early on Saturday morning, he reached the top of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of the three main summits in the Everest “horseshoe.” This horseshoe surrounds a glacier valley called the Western Cwm.

That same day, he climbed to the top of Everest. He reached the summit in complete darkness early on Sunday. Then he and his climbing partner kept going. On Monday morning they reached the summit of Lhotse, the third mountain in this three-peaks challenge.

“It was a snatched opportunity,” he says. “For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. That does not take away from what I did. I set the bar at a certain level. But whoever comes next will raise the bar and do it without ropes or bottled oxygen.”

Sixty years after Everest was first climbed, many stories look back to Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. They talk about a more innocent time, before today’s commercial climbing and damage to the environment. I ask Cool to look ahead and imagine what top climbers might do 60 years from now.

“I hate to think,” he says. But he brings up the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. Steck left the mountain in April after what Cool calls “an altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck planned to climb Everest’s west ridge, first climbed in 1963. Then he planned to go down to the South Col and immediately climb Lhotse by a new route, all without fixed ropes. “Ueli had been training like a machine,” Cool says. “He’s a climber in a class all his own. He’s technically brilliant, and he had pushed his fitness to an astronomic level. It would have been amazing to see what he could have done.”

What tourism will look like is a different question. One sign is a dramatic helicopter rescue by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro’s angry words helped cause the conflict at Camp 2. Moro flew back to Everest on Tuesday in a high-powered helicopter. He rescued a very sick climber at 7,800 metres.

This was the highest rescue ever done on Everest. It also shows how fast helicopter flights have increased in recent years. By 2073, there could even be a helipad on the South Col. Tourists might be brought there while breathing bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing rescue options for both climbers and the many more trekkers who go as far as base camp.

Mountain geographer and environmentalist Alton Byers says it is not clear if the Everest region can keep handling booming tourism. He says climate change and tourism together are putting new pressure on the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers in the Everest region are shrinking, and some are disappearing. This is already causing big problems. “Everywhere you go, people are talking about how there’s less water. There’s less water for farming and less water for all the new lodges that are getting built.”

Byers says that in the Sherpa town of Namche Bazaar, workers are building a new five-mile pipeline. It will bring water to meet the growing tourist demand for showers and flush toilets. The local stream is polluted with human waste. It also does not have enough water for a town that, in the busy season, is extremely crowded. “Every village is digging a pit just beyond the houses for garbage. Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world,” he says.

Human waste at base camp is now handled well and taken away in plastic barrels. But Byers says the barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley. That pit could leak into the area’s streams and rivers.

“These problems can be solved, but we need to get serious about it,” he says. “One climber can spend $85,000 climbing Everest, and that’s fine. But at some point we will have to deal with these other needs too. For half a million dollars a year, you could solve most of them.”

Climate change is another issue. Byers works with local conservation groups to find and plan for climate change effects. They often look for new water sources or set up rainwater harvesting. Fast growth of glacial lakes is a constant danger. Some lakes could burst and flood the Sherpa homeland. “There’s going to come a time when people are going to have to get out of their way.”

Changing weather is also affecting tourism. More cloud cover during times that are usually clear is closing Lukla Airport more often. Lukla Airport is the main entry point to the Everest region. A new road for 4x4s is being built to Lukla to keep tourists and their money coming. But Byers worries the fast spread of roads in Nepal is being done cheaply. He says this can lead to serious soil erosion and landslides.

“Everest is the icon everyone knows,” he says. “It’s the canary in the coalmine that everyone understands. It’s the perfect place to learn how to deal with problems like climate change and tourism.”
