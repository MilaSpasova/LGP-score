James Hamblin is a senior editor at the American magazine The Atlantic. In his series “If Our Bodies Could Talk,” he tried a challenge: he stopped showering for a while to see what happens when we clean our bodies too much. He took fewer showers, and when he did shower, he did not use shampoo or soap.

He learned something many others have learned. When we scrub and wash a lot with soap and body wash, our skin works harder to get back to its normal balance. Showering can remove the skin’s natural oil and helpful bacteria. Hot water and cleaning products can remove too much.

After a shower, your skin may feel tight and dry. This happens because natural moisture has been washed away. Our skin also has millions of helpful bacteria, like our gut does. Frequent showering and scrubbing can destroy many of these bacteria. When the bacteria grow back, they may include more kinds that cause bad smells. So, showering too often may make you smell more. When people stop using soap and shower less, their skin may smell worse at first. After some time, the skin often returns to balance, makes less oil, and healthy bacteria grow again.

After the experiment, Hamblin felt the body can do well on its own. It is not only about smell or looks. Dermatologists say using less soap can help acne and eczema. Showering less can also help the environment. The average shower lasts seven minutes and uses 65 litres of water. That is 65 litres of clean, drinkable water that gets mixed with soap and goes down the drain every day, sometimes more than once.

Clean water is becoming more important, especially as California faces another summer of drought. It is becoming clear that clean water is very valuable and may soon be hard to get. Also, body wash bottles create waste. These are strong reasons to let your body be more natural.

If this idea makes you feel nervous because you remember being close to people who did not shower much, do not worry. Many people who shower less still use deodorant if they need it. Everyone’s natural smell is different, and it can change with diet, hydration, and exercise. Also, washing hands with soap is still recommended to help stop the spread of infectious diseases.

You do not have to stop showering suddenly like Hamblin did. But you can make a simple change: reduce. Skip some showers, use less soap, and let your skin’s natural bacteria grow.
