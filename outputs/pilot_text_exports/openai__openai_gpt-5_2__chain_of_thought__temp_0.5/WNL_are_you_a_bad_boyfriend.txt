Dan Sullivan showed up late to breakfast with the Guardian. He was late because the police stopped him while he was riding his motorcycle.

Sullivan says he works too much. He misses dinner dates. He forgets to buy gifts.

So, like many people in Silicon Valley, the 27-year-old venture capitalist started a company to help. It is called BetterBoyfriend.me. For about $70 a month, it sends a gift to a girlfriend or wife every month.

Sullivan works in finance. He has been quietly testing the service. About 350 boyfriends have signed up. Together they bring in about $17,000 each month. He says most members are his Harvard friends and investor friends: venture capitalists, founders, and workers at companies like Apple, Google, and Facebook. Their girlfriends have been getting gifts from Sullivan for the last year.

Each month, members pick from a list of seven gifts, like chocolate, tea sets, and manicures. The gift is sent to the boyfriend with no brand name and no label.

Sullivan tests ideas by buying Facebook ads to see how much interest they get. BetterBoyfriend.me became popular fast. Sullivan said, “BetterBoyfriend hit something. It’s a pain point everyone has.”

BetterBoyfriend.me was not Sullivan’s first idea. For a couple of days, he thought about sharing dogs. He said he saw rich guys looking miserable while walking dogs. He said a dog is only valuable 5% of the time. He wondered if someone could babysit the dogs and other people could rent them.

Another start-up, called InvisibleBoyfriend, lets users make a fake social partner who is always ready to talk. In the end, you get a “boyfriend” who can text with you all day.

Founder Kyle Tabor started it so users could have a fake boyfriend to stop unwanted attention. But he found that people became attached to their computer-made partners. He said more users want friendship through talking, not just outside “proof” of a relationship to get people to leave them alone.

For Sullivan and Tabor, a surprise was the real connections that formed with customers. Sullivan says he has started to see himself as a kind of relationship helper for the boyfriends.

Sullivan admits he made mistakes. At first, the packages included receipts with his name on them, Dan Sullivan. He said one boyfriend wrote, “Listen, dude, she’s not mad but Cynthia found out.”

About 50% of the women who get BetterBoyfriend.me packages know Sullivan is involved. He says it connects to age. He thinks that after people have been married for a while, they do not keep many secrets.

Over the year, Sullivan says he has gotten to know the boyfriends well. They even made a delivery to a hospital delivery room. He said the key is to remember that his business relationship is with the boyfriend.

When he first started the company, he put BetterBoyfriend.me tags on flower bouquets. Then he went to Dolores Park, a popular relaxing spot in the Mission District, where many tech workers and young people go.

He said he looked for couples and gave the flowers to the girl. But the boyfriend did not like that. He said it made sense, and it was cool. So he changed his plan and started giving the flowers to the boyfriends.
