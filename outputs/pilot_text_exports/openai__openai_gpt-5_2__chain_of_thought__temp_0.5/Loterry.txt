The lottery balls drop down the chute, and all six numbers match. Then it is time to buy an Audi, book a holiday in the US, and phone an estate agent. An analysis says many lottery millionaires do these things.

Since it started in 1994, the lottery has made 3,000 millionaires. Together they have won more than £8.5bn. That is an average of £2.8m each. The study authors, Oxford Economics, say their money also helped create 3,780 more millionaires among their children, family, and friends.

Most winners (59%) stop working straight away. But 19% keep their regular jobs. Also, 31% do unpaid volunteer work. The study says 98% of winners’ spending stayed in the UK. By buying property, vehicles, and holidays, it is estimated that each winner supports six people in full-time jobs for a year.

Winners have added almost £750m to gross domestic product (GDP). They also produced more than £500m in tax receipts for the Exchequer. Most of the money was spent on property. Winners spent £2.72bn on their main homes. They also spent £170m paying off debt and mortgages.

Keeping income was important, so £2.125bn was spent on investments. Gifts to family and friends were £1.17bn. Winners spent £680m on cars and holidays.

Camelot, which runs the UK National Lottery, asked for the study to mark the 3,000-winners milestone. The study used research from 100 winners who won £1m or more. It found that the 3,000 winners bought 7,958 houses or flats in the UK. That is 2.7 each, spending £3.3bn. Most winners (82%) moved to a new main home and spent an average of £900,000.

Many new homes had extra features. Almost a third (29%) bought a hot tub. A walk-in wardrobe was important for 28%. Almost a quarter (24%) chose a home behind electric gates. Also, 22% had a games room, and 7% put in a snooker table.

Bigger homes need care. So 30% of winners hired a cleaner, and 24% hired a gardener. A small number (5%) hired a beautician.

Audis were the top choice for 16% of winners. Range Rovers and BMWs were also popular (11% each). Mercedes were 10%, and Land Rovers were 5%. Winners spent £463m on 17,190 cars. The average price of their favourite car was £46,116.

Holidays were important too. Most winners (68%) chose five-star hotels overseas. The US was the top destination for 27%, followed by the Caribbean (9%). In the UK, caravan sales also gained. Over the past 18 years, 10% of millionaires bought a caravan, creating sales of about £7.4m.

Some winners (15%) started their own businesses. Another 9% helped other people start businesses. Also, 6% invested in or bought other people’s businesses. The study says these businesses employ 3,195 people.

Andy Logan, the report author, said a win spreads further than expected. He said it changes the lives of friends and family. He also said each win has a clear effect on the UK economy because so much money is spent in the UK. He said each win creates a ripple effect across this generation and often the next.
