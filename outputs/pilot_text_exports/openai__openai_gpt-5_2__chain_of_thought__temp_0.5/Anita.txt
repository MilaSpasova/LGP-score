Brazil’s newest funk star, Anitta, has gained millions of fans. She brought music from the favelas into popular music. But now people are debating her skin colour.

Anti-discrimination activists and writers say Anitta may have had to hide her black identity to succeed in a mostly white, middle-class market.

The debate grew after “before and after” photos were shared. They show Anitta’s skin looking much lighter after she signed a deal with Warner. In an older photo, when she was not famous, she looks darker. In a newer marketing photo, she looks paler. People wonder if this came from skin-lightening products, cosmetic surgery, or more likely Photoshop. The difference has restarted an old question in Brazil: do you need to be pale to get ahead?

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote that the change showed hidden discrimination in media and marketing. She said many people deny racism, but statistics show it exists. She said society often connects “white” with being rich and successful, and “black” with the opposite. She also said this can hurt people’s self-esteem. She gave examples like pressure to straighten curly hair or get a nose job.

Anitta was born Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

She became very famous after releasing an album and a big hit song, Show das Poderosas. The song reached number one and got 52 million views on YouTube.

Many people love her as a pop star with catchy songs and a strong message. Her supporters present her as a bridge between the mostly black and mixed-race shanty towns on Rio’s hillsides and the richer, whiter neighbourhoods below.

She has made baile funk less shocking for a wider audience. She reduced sexual dancing, gang references, and explicit words. But some people ask if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract a richer audience.

Race and colour are sensitive topics in Brazil. Brazil was one of the last large countries to end slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. In Brazil, people often care more about skin colour than about ancestry. Even though the country wants to be a multiracial democracy, skin tone is clearly linked to inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Until 2011, black or mixed-race students also spent about two fewer years in school on average.

The government says this gap is shrinking because of university quotas and other affirmative action policies. But the gap is still easy to see. Most business and government leaders are white, while most low-paid jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In Ipanema, Gávea, and other rich areas, it is more common to see black nannies pushing strollers with white toddlers than the other way around.

Colour categories in Brazil can be complicated. In the 2010 census, people who called themselves white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million said black, two million said Asian, and 0.5% said indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology teacher at the Federal University of Pernambuco, said Anitta won over the middle class by taking a rebellious sound and making it softer and easier for everyone to accept. He said it was not racial whitening. He said the bigger change was that Anitta moved her art from the “periphery” to the “centre.”

Other people say the colour issue is exaggerated. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who knew Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot, but not her colour. He said makeup, hairstylists, and clothing made the difference. He also said he did not think she was forced to change, and that she has always liked singing, dancing, and showing confidence.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador, said the prejudice was more in music producers and media executives than in the public. He said the public showed they cared about the music itself by choosing Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, as the recent winner of The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for comment. But in a recent interview, she said identity should be chosen by each person. She said a powerful woman does not need to be beautiful; she can be strong because of her attitude. She said her message is that we can be who we want.
