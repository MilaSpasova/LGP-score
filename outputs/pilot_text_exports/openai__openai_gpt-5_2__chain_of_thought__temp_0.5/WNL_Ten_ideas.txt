From glow-in-the-dark trees to underground bike sheds and solar-powered bins, there are some unusual ideas to make cities nicer to live in.

Living in a city has many good points, but it is often hard to live in a way that is good for the environment. People deal with pollution, traffic, and less green space every day.

Now inventors are trying to help. Here are some of the wackier ideas for making cities more livable.

Pop-up parks
Some cities look like they are built more for cars than for people. A pop-up park is a simple idea. You take an empty car park, a little money, and one or two potted plants, and you make a small park for a short time. The PARK(ing) project started as an art experiment in San Francisco, and it has spread around the world. People have also tried ideas like temporary city farms and ecology demonstrations. The movement has a day of action every September.

Subterranean storage
Not everyone in a city uses a car. Bikes are becoming more popular. But where can you keep a bike safe? A Tokyo-based engineering firm called Giken has an idea: an “eco-cycle, anti-seismic underground bicycle park”. It is a cylinder that is seven metres wide and goes deep underground. It can hold 204 bikes. Owners can get their bike back by pressing a button. The automated system brings it above ground in about 13 seconds.

Glow-in-the-dark trees
When many people think about trees that glow in the dark, they think of Christmas decorations. But Daan Roosegaarde does not. This Dutch designer-artist has created a “bioluminescent” plant. The experimental technology mixes DNA from glowing marine bacteria with the chloroplast genome of a plant, so it can glow like a jellyfish. Trials are happening to make an industrial-scale version. Roosegaarde hopes it could one day replace normal street lighting.

Footfall harvesting
Every day, hundreds of commuters and shoppers in West Ham in east London cross an elevated walkway near the underground station. Most people do not notice that it feels a little springy. Even fewer people link the five-millimetre bend in the rubber surface to the streetlights above. The walkway has smart tiles that capture energy from people’s footsteps and turn it into electricity. Pavegen, the UK firm that made it, has also installed a similar system at London’s Heathrow Airport and other places around the world.

Supertrees
It was bound to happen: man-made trees. Singapore’s Gardens by the Bay has a small group of them. These steel-framed “supertrees” can be up to 50 metres high. Flowers and ferns grow on them, and their metal tops help take in and spread out heat. They can also collect rainwater and help move air for two “climate-controlled biomes” (large conservatories) below. Eleven of the 18 trees also have solar panels on their “branches”.
