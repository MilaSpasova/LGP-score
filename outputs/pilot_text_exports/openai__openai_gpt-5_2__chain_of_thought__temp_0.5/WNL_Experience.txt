It was a beautiful summer evening. I decided to go swimming near Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland. I moved there in 2012. A woman was in the water with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty often swims with a group of people, and she seems close to them.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Since then, hundreds of people have swum with her. This can make people think she is completely tame. She was even in an Irish tourist-board ad, where a girl says she wants to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty’s belly. It looked very friendly. About 20 tourists and local people were on the pier, watching. Soon after I got into the water, Dusty left the woman and suddenly went wild. Later I learned she can be very protective of her space when she is with someone. Her tail was beating the water hard. At first, I thought she was just showing off. Then I realized she might be angry.

I knew I had to get out. I swam toward the pier. But almost right away, Dusty rammed into me with her nose. It was very strong and very painful. She hit me so fast that I flew forward.

People on the pier stared at me with their mouths open. Dusty stayed beside me, still beating her tail. That scared me the most. I thought, if she hits me with her tail, I could sink. I could die.

I reached the pier, but I could not climb out because I was hurt. I felt terrible fear. I shouted for help. A man reached in, grabbed my arm, and pulled me onto the steps. Then another man came. He said he was an orthopaedic surgeon, a doctor for bones, and that he specialized in marine trauma, injuries from the sea. He had been driving into Doolin when he saw what was happening.

I was very cold and very worried. I did not know how bad my injuries were. My biggest fear was bleeding inside my body. The doctor checked me and tried to calm me. He said he could not feel signs of that, but he thought I probably had broken bones. Later I learned I had six spinal fractures, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in the hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for several weeks. I could not work for five months. I could not move well, and I had stiffness and pain. Then doctors said I had post-traumatic stress. The experience made me anxious and made me overreact in ways I never had before. I felt like people were looking at me the wrong way. Loud noises bothered me. I also had memory loss.

Three months before the accident, I opened a health-food shop. But I had to give it up because I could no longer work. It was the toughest year ever. Now it is behind me. I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building my own osteopathy practice. I understand patients more because I have been a patient.

I am thankful that I am healthy. I really want to stop other people from getting hurt. Many of us think dolphins are always kind and safe. Who would expect a dolphin to attack a person? If a fierce animal comes at you showing its teeth, it looks scary. But dolphins have a wide smile, so they can seem harmless.

I am not angry with Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her area, and she is a wild animal that can be unpredictable. People need to know this. Many people come to swim with her, and they do not understand how dangerous it can be. My injuries were reported as one of several injuries that summer.

After the man pulled me out, Dusty swam away. Then she came back. She floated upright next to me and looked at me. We looked straight at each other. I felt she was truly sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin. The anger was gone. The people on the pier watched in amazement. After that moment, the terror ended for me. I made peace with her.
