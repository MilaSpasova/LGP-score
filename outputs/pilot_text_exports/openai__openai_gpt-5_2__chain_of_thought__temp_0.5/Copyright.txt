Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is theft. He says it is like putting your hand in someone’s pocket and stealing their wallet. In an article for Index on Censorship, Pullman (who is president of the Society of Authors) strongly defends copyright laws. He criticizes internet users who think it is OK to download music or books without paying.

Pullman writes that the technology looks so impressive that people do not notice how wrong their actions are. He says it is shocking that someone can steal an artist’s work and not be punished.

His article was written after music industry leaders met British Prime Minister David Cameron at Downing Street to talk about web piracy.

Pullman, who wrote the His Dark Materials trilogy, says authors and musicians often work for years in poverty and without being well known. They work to make something that their audiences enjoy. But when they finally might earn a living, illegal downloading takes that chance away. He says the rule is simple: if we want to enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

Pullman’s article will appear in the next issue of the campaign group’s magazine. It is part of a dialogue with Cathy Casserly, the chief executive of Creative Commons. Creative Commons offers open content licences that “lets creators take copyright into their own hands”. Casserly says many things are wrong with copyright, which was made “in an analogue age”. She writes that copyright often stops many ways people could share, build on, and remix each other’s work—things that were hard to imagine when the laws were created.

Casserly says artists need to find creative ways to share and make money from their work. She quotes science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees. The magazine’s editor, Rachael Jolley, said that today’s copyright laws do not work well in the digital age and could turn normal consumers into criminals. She said new ways are needed so artists, writers, and musicians can earn a living from their work.

The argument is strong, and illegal downloading is very widespread. Data from Ofcom (the independent regulator and competition authority for the UK communications industries) suggests that in the UK, between November 2012 and January 2013, 280 million music tracks were pirated online. Also pirated were 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files.

Ofcom said 18% of internet users aged over 12 admit they have recently pirated content, and 9% say they worry about getting caught. Pullman writes that it is still surprising how fast and easy it is to get music as MP3 downloads, even for people who have been building their iTunes list for some time.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron appointed Conservative MP Mike Weatherley to advise him on this issue. A spokesman for the BPI, the record industry trade body, said Mike Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creative producers that copyright is meant to protect. The spokesman said they hope Weatherley’s influence, and the prime minister’s support for copyright, will affect the approach of the UK’s intellectual property office.
