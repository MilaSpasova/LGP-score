Opposition to Western Australia’s shark cull has grown. Thousands of people went to beaches across Australia to ask the state’s premier to stop the policy. RSPCA Australia and Richard Branson, the owner of Virgin Atlantic, also spoke against it.

The policy catches and kills sharks longer than three metres. It started after the state government said there was an “unprecedented” number of shark attacks on Western Australia’s coast. A 35-year-old surfer was killed in November 2013. He was the sixth person to die from a shark attack in two years.

However, the Shark Attack File says Australia as a whole has averaged one shark-related death a year for the last 50 years.

Kate Faehrmann, from the Sea Shepherd Conservation Society, spoke at a protest in Perth. She said the policy will not work. She said drumlines are not selective. They can catch and kill sharks of any size, and also dolphins, turtles, and other animals. She said that is why the community does not want it.

Thousands of people protested at Cottesloe Beach in Perth and Manly Beach in Sydney. Hundreds also protested at Glenelg in south-west Adelaide, and at beaches in Victoria and Queensland. Faehrmann said the protests showed many Australians want sharks protected. She said people came out in large numbers to say the ocean belongs to sharks too, and they do not want them killed.

Anthony Joyce, a surfer who once had his foot caught in a shark’s mouth, said killing sharks will not make a real difference.

The state government has refused to give a continuing total of how many sharks have been killed. There have been reports that sharks smaller than three metres were released after being caught. Drumlines are floating drums fixed to the sea bed, with baited hooks hanging below.

Conservationists say there is no evidence the cull will reduce shark attacks on humans. They say no earlier cull used only drumlines. Researchers at the University of Western Australia say the recent group of attacks may be linked more to the state’s fast-growing population, not to more sharks.

Richard Peirce, from the UK charity the Shark Trust, said the cull would not work and could bring more predators near the coast. He said leaving drumlines in the water overnight could attract other predators, because sharks and other animals may be hooked and injured.

Worldwide in 2012, there were 80 unprovoked shark attacks, and seven were fatal. This was compared to nearly 100m sharks killed by humans each year.

RSPCA Australia said it believes the cull is not justified. It said there is no evidence that more attacks are because there are more sharks. It said the rise fits with changes in population and human behaviour, meaning more people are in the water.

Richard Branson told Fairfax Radio the policy could have the opposite effect of what was intended. He said Premier Colin Barnett may have thought it would help tourism, but Branson thinks it will do the reverse. He said it makes the problem seem bigger than it is, and could stop people from wanting to visit Perth and the countryside around it. He said it may only make people worry for no good reason.
