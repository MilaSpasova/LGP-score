One warm summer evening, I decided to swim near Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland. I moved there in 2012. A woman was in the water with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty often swims with a group of people.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Since then, hundreds of people have swum with her, so she can seem very tame. She was even in an Irish tourist ad where a girl says she wants to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty’s belly. It looked safe and friendly. About 20 tourists and local people watched from the pier. Soon after I got into the water, Dusty left the woman and suddenly went wild. Later I learned she can be very protective of her space when she is with someone. Her tail slapped the water hard. At first I thought she was just showing off, but then I realized she might be angry.

I tried to swim back to the pier to get out. But almost right away, Dusty slammed into me with her nose. It was very strong and very painful, and she was very fast. I was thrown forward.

People on the pier stared at me in shock. Dusty stayed next to me, still hitting the water with her tail. That scared me most. I thought if she hit me with her tail, I could go under the water.

I reached the pier, but I could not get out because I was hurt. I was terrified. I shouted for help. A man reached down and pulled me onto the steps. Then another man came and said he was an orthopaedic surgeon who specialized in marine trauma. He was driving into Doolin and saw what was happening. I was very cold and very worried. I feared I might be bleeding inside. He checked me and said he could not feel signs of internal bleeding, but he thought I probably had broken bones. Later I learned I had six fractures in my spine, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in the hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for weeks. I could not work for five months, and I had trouble moving, with stiffness and pain. Then doctors said I had post-traumatic stress. After the near-death experience, I felt anxious and overreacted in ways I never had before. I felt like people looked at me the wrong way. Loud noises bothered me, and I had memory loss.

Three months before the accident, I opened a health-food shop, but I had to give it up because I could not work. It was the hardest year of my life, but now it is behind me. I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building my own osteopathy practice. I understand patients better because I have been a patient.

I am thankful I am healthy, and I want to stop other people from getting hurt. People often think dolphins are always safe and gentle. Who expects a dolphin to attack a person? If a fierce animal comes at you with its teeth showing, it looks dangerous. But dolphins have a wide smile.

I am not angry at Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her area, and she is a wild animal that can act in sudden ways. People need to understand that. Many people come to swim with her and do not know how dangerous it can be. People said my injuries were one of several injuries that summer.

After I was pulled from the water, Dusty swam away. Then she came back and floated upright next to me, looking at me. We looked into each other’s eyes. I felt she was sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin, and the anger was gone. The people on the pier were amazed. After that moment, the terror ended for me, and I felt at peace with her.
