The balls fall down the chute and all six numbers match. Then it is time to buy an Audi, book a holiday in the US, and call an estate agent. An analysis says many lottery millionaires do this.

The UK National Lottery started in 1994. Since then, it has made 3,000 millionaires. Together they have won more than £8.5bn, with an average of £2.8m each. Oxford Economics, the authors of the study, say the money also helped make 3,780 more millionaires among winners’ children, family, and friends.

Most winners (59%) stop working right away. But 19% keep doing their normal job, and 31% do unpaid volunteer work. For the economy, 98% of winners’ spending stayed in the UK. By spending on homes, cars, and holidays, it is estimated that each winner keeps six people in full-time work for one year.

Winners added almost £750m to GDP and created more than £500m in tax money for the Exchequer. Most money went on property. Winners spent £2.72bn on their main homes, and £170m on paying off old debt and mortgages.

Keeping income was important too, so £2.125bn was spent on investments. Gifts to family and friends were £1.17bn. Cars and holidays were £680m.

Camelot, the company that runs the UK National Lottery, paid for the study to mark the 3,000-winners milestone. The study used research from 100 winners who won £1m or more. It found that the 3,000 winners bought 7,958 houses or flats in the UK in total. That is 2.7 each. They spent £3.3bn. Most winners (82%) changed their main home and spent an average of £900,000.

Many new homes had extra features. Almost a third (29%) bought a hot tub. A walk-in wardrobe was a must for 28%. Almost a quarter (24%) chose a home behind electric gates. Also, 22% had a games room, and 7% put in a snooker table.

Big homes need care. So 30% hired a cleaner and 24% hired a gardener. A small group (5%) hired a beautician.

Audis were the top car choice for 16% of winners. Range Rovers and BMWs were also popular (11% each). Mercedes were 10%, and Land Rovers were 5%. Winners spent £463m on 17,190 cars. The average price of their favourite car was £46,116.

Holidays were important too. Most winners (68%) chose five-star hotels overseas. The US was the top place to go for 27%, followed by the Caribbean (9%). But UK caravan sales also gained. Over the past 18 years, 10% of millionaires bought a caravan, creating about £7.4m in sales.

Some winners (15%) started their own business. Also, 9% helped other people start a business, and 6% invested in or bought other people’s businesses. Businesses started or supported by lottery winners employ 3,195 people, the study says.

Andy Logan, who wrote the report, said the effects of a win spread further than they expected. He said it changes the lives of friends and family, and each win has a clear effect on the UK economy, because so much money is spent in the UK. He said each win creates a ripple effect for this generation and often the next.
