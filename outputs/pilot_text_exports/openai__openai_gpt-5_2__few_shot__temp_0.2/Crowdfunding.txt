This invention could be as helpful as Trevor Baylis’s wind-up radio was in the pre-internet 1990s. It is a cheap light that uses gravity for free power. It could stop people in Africa and India from using dangerous kerosene-fuelled lamps.

The British designer Patrick Hunt tried to get money from banks or venture capital firms to start selling his invention. But he had a problem. He said getting funding was slow and complex. The idea was unproven, and nobody wanted to take a risk.

So he tried crowdfunding on a US website called Indiegogo, which had recently opened in the UK. In five days he reached his goal and raised £36,200. His campaign was so popular at the end of 2012 that within 40 days he raised £400,000.

The LED light uses a dynamo. The dynamo is powered when a 10kg bag of rocks slowly drops down. The weight is attached to the light and lifted to about 2m high. As it slowly falls to the ground, it makes enough power for half an hour of light.

Hunt is getting ready to make the product in China. He will test the market again by sending 1,000 lights to Africa before full mass production. He hopes to produce millions of units.

Hunt is part of a new wave of entrepreneurs who use the fast-growing crowdfunding industry to get money. Another new site is InvestingZone, which matches wealthy individuals with start-up entrepreneurs.

Indiegogo does not offer shares. Instead, people who donate can get “perks” at different donation levels. People who funded Hunt’s new light could feel good about helping poorer people, and they also got their own light.

Danae Ringelmann, co-founder of Indiegogo, said the “gravity light” shows how crowdfunding can be fair and can test an idea. She said it is not about knowing the right people at a bank or a venture capital firm. It is about showing customers and fans that your idea is good, and getting them to support it with money.

She also said that even ideas that do not get funded are still worth testing. Then you find out faster if the idea is not good, and you learn sooner.

Ringelmann lives in the US. She started her career as a Wall Street analyst. In 2008 she quit and used her skills to help friends in the arts raise money.

At first, the site focused on films. It launched at the Sundance Film Festival that year.

Five years later, it was raising about $2m a week for new businesses that were starting or growing. In December 2012, it launched services in euros and sterling to expand in Europe. It says Britain is its third biggest market. International activity is up 41% since December.

There are many competitors, such as Kickstarter, Seedrs, and Funding Circle. But Ringelmann says Indiegogo is the only crowdfunder where anyone can start a campaign. No project is seen as too wacky.

Indiegogo charges a 4% fee for successful campaigns. If a campaign does not reach its target, users can choose to refund all the money to contributors at no charge, or keep all the money raised but pay a 9% fee.

A British woman, Lauren Pears, raised £100,000 on the site to open a “cat café” in London. It is called Lady Dinah’s Cat Emporium. It has not opened yet, but it is described as a place where people can sit in a comfortable wingback chair, have a hot cup of tea, read a book, and be with a cat.

Ringelmann said venture capitalists sometimes reject ideas because they do not know if people really want the product. She said entrepreneurs can run an Indiegogo campaign, even without launching the project, and the campaign can show that there is a market.

She said crowdfunding lets you test your market, your pricing, and your features. It can help you find new revenue streams and get important feedback.

Ringelmann said ideas are very common, and what matters is doing the work well. She said if you are afraid someone will steal your idea and do it better and faster, then you are not the right person to carry it out. She said you need confidence to move fast and learn.

For Ringelmann, expanding in Europe is part of a dream to make finance more open to everyone. Indiegogo also made a deal with a web transaction provider so people can pay through local card services like Maestro in the UK and Carte Bleue in France, as well as PayPal. More than 7,000 finance campaigns are live on the site.

Crowdfunding has grown as an alternative to banks, but it does not interest many big-bucks investors. These investors usually want a stake in a promising business. This could start to change in the UK with the launch of InvestingZone.
