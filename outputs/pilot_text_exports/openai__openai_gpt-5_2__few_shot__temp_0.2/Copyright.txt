Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is theft. He says it is like reaching into someone’s pocket and stealing a wallet. Pullman wrote this in an article for Index on Censorship. Pullman is president of the Society of Authors. He strongly supports copyright laws. He is very critical of people who download music or books without paying.

Pullman writes that the technology is so impressive that people do not see how wrong it is. He says it is shocking that someone can steal an artist’s work and not be punished.

His article came after leaders in the music industry met British Prime Minister David Cameron at Downing Street. They talked about web piracy.

Pullman, who wrote the His Dark Materials trilogy, says authors and musicians often work in poverty and are unknown for years. They work hard to make something that audiences enjoy. But when they finally might be able to earn a living, that chance is taken away. He says the idea is simple: if we want to enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

In the next issue of the magazine, Pullman is in a dialogue with Cathy Casserly. She is the chief executive of Creative Commons. Creative Commons offers open content licences that let creators control how their copyright is used. Casserly says many things are wrong with copyright because it was made in an “analogue age.” She says copyright rules can block many ways people can share, add to, and remix each other’s work.

Casserly says artists need to think of new ways to share and make money from their work. She quotes science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees that current laws do not fit the digital age. The editor, Rachael Jolley, said existing copyright laws can turn consumers into criminals. She said we need new ways for artists, writers, and musicians to earn money from their work.

Illegal downloading is very large. Ofcom collected data in the UK from November 2012 to January 2013. It suggests 280 million music tracks were pirated. It also suggests 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files were pirated.

Ofcom said 18% of internet users aged over 12 admit they recently pirated content. It also said 9% fear getting caught. Pullman writes that getting music as MP3 downloads is very easy and fast. He says this still amazes people who have been building their iTunes list for some time.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron appointed Conservative MP Mike Weatherley as his adviser on this issue. A spokesman for the BPI, the record industry trade body, said Mike Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creative producers it protects. The spokesman said they hope Weatherley’s influence and the prime minister’s support for copyright will affect the UK’s intellectual property office.
