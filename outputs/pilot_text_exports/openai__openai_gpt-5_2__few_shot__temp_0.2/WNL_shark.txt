Opposition to Western Australia’s shark cull has grown. Thousands of people went to beaches across Australia to ask the state’s premier to stop the policy. RSPCA Australia and Virgin Atlantic owner Richard Branson also spoke against it.

The policy is to catch and kill sharks longer than three metres. It started after what the state government called an “unprecedented” number of shark attacks on Western Australia’s coast. In November 2013, a 35-year-old surfer was killed. He was the sixth person to die from a shark attack in two years.

But the Shark Attack File says Australia as a whole has averaged one shark-related death a year for the last 50 years.

Kate Faehrmann, a board member at the Sea Shepherd Conservation Society, spoke at a protest in Perth. She said the policy will not work. She said drumlines, which are used to catch sharks, can kill many animals. She said they can catch sharks of different sizes, and also dolphins, turtles, and other animals. She said this is why the community does not want it.

Thousands protested at Perth’s Cottesloe Beach and Sydney’s Manly Beach. Hundreds also protested at Glenelg in south-west Adelaide, and at beaches in Victoria and Queensland. Faehrmann said the protests showed many Australians want sharks protected. She said that even with fear from movies like Jaws, people are coming out in large numbers to say the ocean belongs to sharks too and they do not want them killed.

Anthony Joyce, a surfer who once had his foot caught in a shark’s mouth, said the number of sharks killed will not make a difference.

The state government has refused to give an ongoing count of how many sharks have been killed. There have been reports that sharks smaller than three metres were released after being caught. Drumlines are floating drums anchored to the sea bed, with bait hanging on hooks under them.

Conservationists say there is no evidence the cull will reduce shark attacks on humans. They also say no earlier cull has used only drumlines. Researchers at the University of Western Australia say the recent rise in shark attacks may be linked to Western Australia having the fastest-growing population in Australia, not to more sharks.

Richard Peirce, chairman of the UK-based conservation charity the Shark Trust, said the cull would not work and could bring more predators near the coast. He said that even if drumlines are checked during the day, leaving them in overnight could attract other predators to the area, drawn by sharks and other animals that are hooked and injured.

Worldwide in 2012, there were 80 unprovoked shark attacks, and seven were fatal. This compares with nearly 100 million sharks killed by humans each year.

RSPCA Australia said it believes the cull is not justified. It said there is no evidence the increase in attacks is because there are more sharks. Instead, it said the change fits with population and human behaviour, meaning more people are in the water.

Richard Branson told Fairfax Radio the policy is backfiring. He said he thinks Premier Colin Barnett may have hoped it would help tourism, but it will do the opposite. He said it makes the problem look bigger than it is, and it may stop people from coming to Perth and the countryside around it. He said it will only make people worry for no good reason.
