This is unusual for Kenton Cool, who likes to talk, but he can hardly speak. Working hard high on the mountain has made his voice rough. He is now in Kathmandu, the capital of Nepal. That morning he flew down from Everest base camp.

Cool is thinking about three big climbs he finished last weekend. Early on Saturday, he reached the top of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of the three main peaks in the Everest “horseshoe” around a icy valley called the Western Cwm.

That same day, he climbed to the top of Everest. He reached the summit in complete darkness early on Sunday. Then he and his climbing partner kept going. On Monday morning, they reached the summit of Lhotse, the third peak in this three-mountain challenge.

“It was a chance I grabbed,” he says. “For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. That does not remove the physical achievement of what I did. I set a high standard. But the next person may set it even higher, and do it without ropes or bottled oxygen.”

Sixty years after Everest was first climbed, many stories look back to Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. They remember a time before today’s commercial climbing and environmental damage. I asked Cool to think ahead and imagine what top climbers might do 60 years from now.

“I hate to think,” he says. He talks about the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. Steck left the mountain in April after what Cool calls “an altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck planned to climb Everest’s west ridge, first climbed in 1963. Then Steck planned to go down to the South Col and immediately climb Lhotse by a new route, without fixed ropes. “Ueli trained like a machine,” Cool says. “He is in a class of his own. He was very skilled, and his fitness was extremely high. It would have been amazing to see what he could have done.”

Tourism is another question. One sign is a helicopter rescue by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro flew back to Everest on Tuesday in a powerful helicopter. He rescued a very sick climber at 7,800 metres.

It was the highest rescue ever done on Everest. It also shows how fast helicopter flights have increased in recent years. By 2073, there could even be a helipad on the South Col, bringing tourists who breathe bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing how people can rescue both climbers and the many more trekkers who go as far as base camp.

Mountain geographer and environmentalist Alton Byers says it is not clear if the Everest region can keep handling fast-growing tourism. He says climate change and tourism together are creating new problems for the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers are shrinking, and some are disappearing. This is already having a big effect. “Everywhere you go, people talk about having less water,” he says. “There is less water for farming and less water for all the new lodges being built.”

Byers says that in the Sherpa town of Namche Bazaar, workers are building a new five-mile pipeline to bring water for the growing tourist need for showers and flush toilets. The local stream is polluted with human waste and does not give enough water. In high season, the town is extremely crowded. “Every village is digging a pit just beyond the houses for garbage. Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world,” he says. Human waste at base camp is now handled well and removed in plastic barrels. But Byers says the barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley, and it could leak into the region’s water.

“These problems can be solved, but we need to take them seriously,” he says. “One climber can spend $85,000 climbing Everest. That is fine. But at some point we must deal with these other needs. For half a million dollars a year, you could solve most of them.”

Climate change is harder. Byers works with local conservation committees to find and plan for the effects of climate change. They often look for new water sources or collect rainwater. Another danger is that glacial lakes are growing quickly. They can burst and flood the Sherpa homeland. “A time will come when people will have to get out of their way,” he says.

Changing weather is also affecting tourism. More clouds during times that are usually clear are closing Lukla Airport more often. Lukla Airport is the main entrance to the Everest region. A new road for 4x4s is being built to Lukla to keep tourists and money coming. But Byers worries the fast spread of roads in Nepal is being done cheaply, which can cause serious soil erosion and landslides.

“Everest is the famous mountain everyone knows,” he says. “It is the warning sign everyone understands. It is a perfect place to learn how to deal with problems like climate change and tourism.”
