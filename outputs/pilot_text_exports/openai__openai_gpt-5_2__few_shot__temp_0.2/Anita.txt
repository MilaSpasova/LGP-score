Anitta is a new and very popular funk singer from Brazil. She has won millions of fans by bringing favela music to the mainstream. But people are arguing about her skin colour.

People who fight discrimination and people who write about society say Anitta had to give up looking black to sell music to a mostly white, middle-class market.

The argument grew after people shared old and new photos of her. The photos show her skin looks much lighter since she signed a deal with Warner.

In the first photo, from when she was not famous, she looks darker. In the second photo, a marketing picture after she became famous, she looks paler. Some people think this could be from skin-whitening products or cosmetic surgery. Others think it is more likely from Photoshop changes. The big difference made people talk again about whether you need to be pale to succeed in Brazil.

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote a post criticizing hidden discrimination in media and marketing. She said Anitta’s image change showed this problem. Arraes said many people do not accept that they are racist, and they believe Brazil is a multiracial democracy, but statistics show this is not true. She said whitening shows a society that does not support diversity. She said white is linked with rich, nice, successful, and good, while people see black as the opposite.

Anitta’s real name is Larissa de Macedo Machado. When she was a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

She became very famous with an album and a hit song called Show das Poderosas. The song reached number one and got 52 million YouTube views.

Many people love her as a pop star with a strong message and catchy songs. The people who support her also present her as a cultural bridge. They say she connects the mostly black and mixed-race shanty towns on Rio’s hillsides with the richer and whiter areas below.

She has made baile funk softer for a wider audience. She reduced the sexual dancing, gangsta talk, and explicit lyrics. Now people ask if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract a more profitable group of buyers. Arraes said that if pop stars have curly hair, they may feel forced to straighten it. If they have a big nose, they may feel forced to get a nose job (rhinoplasty). She said this can hurt self-esteem and create a bad cycle.

This topic is sensitive in Brazil, where many people are mixed-race. Brazil was one of the last large countries to ban slavery. It has the largest population of people of African descent outside Africa. But in Brazil, colour often matters more than race or family background. Even though the country aims to be a multiracial democracy, skin tone is clearly linked to inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Up to 2011, black or mixed-race students also spent about two fewer years in school on average.

The government says this gap is getting smaller because of quota systems for university places and other kinds of affirmative action. But the difference is still easy to see. Most business and government leaders are white. Most low-paid jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In Ipanema, Gávea, and other rich areas, you are much more likely to see black nannies pushing strollers with white toddlers than a white nanny pushing a black child.

It is hard to define colour. In the 2010 census, for the first time, people who called themselves white were not the majority. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million said black, two million said Asian, and 0.5% said indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology lecturer at the Federal University of Pernambuco, says Anitta won middle-class fans by taking a rebellious sound and making it calmer and easier for everyone to enjoy. He said it was not done by racial whitening. He said what changed was the social space where she made her art: from the periphery to the centre.

Other people say the talk about colour is too big. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who worked with Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot, but not her colour. He said it is more about makeup, hair styling, and clothes. He said he does not think she was pushed into it. He said she always liked to show off, sing, and dance, and that this came naturally to her. He said he believes this natural side helped her get where she is today.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador in the north-east, said the prejudice is not mainly in society, but in music producers and media bosses. He said the public showed they can choose music for its own sake when they chose Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, as the recent winner of the talent show The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for comment. But in a recent interview, she said identity should be defined by the person. She said an “all-powerful” woman does not need to be beautiful, but has so much attitude that she is marvellous and powerful. She said her work tries to show that we can be who we want.
