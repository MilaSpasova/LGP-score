Dan Sullivan was late to breakfast with the Guardian because police stopped him while he was riding his motorcycle.

Sullivan says he works too much. He misses dinner dates. He forgets to give gifts.

So the 27-year-old venture capitalist started a service to help: BetterBoyfriend.me. It sends a gift to girlfriends and wives every month for about $70.

Sullivan works in finance. He has been testing the service quietly. About 350 boyfriends have signed up. The service makes about $17,000 each month. Most members, he says, are his Harvard and investor friends. They include venture capitalists, founders, and workers at companies like Apple, Google, and Facebook. Their girlfriends have been getting gifts from Sullivan for the last year.

Each month, members pick from a list of seven gifts, like chocolate, tea sets, or manicures. The package is sent to the boyfriend with no brand name and no label.

Sullivan tests ideas by buying Facebook ads to see what results he gets. BetterBoyfriend.me became popular fast. Sullivan said it solved a problem many people have.

BetterBoyfriend.me was not his first idea. For a few days, he thought about a dog-sharing business. He said he saw rich men looking unhappy while walking dogs. He thought a dog is only useful 5% of the time. He asked: what if someone babysat the dogs and other people could rent them?

Another start-up, called InvisibleBoyfriend, lets users create a believable social partner who is always available to talk. The result is a “boyfriend” who can text with you all day.

Founder Kyle Tabor started it to help users stop unwanted advances. But he found that people became attached to their computer-made partners. He said many users want companionship through conversation, not just “proof” of a relationship.

For Sullivan and Tabor, a surprise was the real relationships they have formed with customers. Sullivan says he now feels like a kind of relationship adviser for the boyfriends.

Sullivan says he has made mistakes. At first, the packages included receipts with his name, Dan Sullivan. He said one boyfriend wrote to him and said, “Listen, dude, she’s not mad but Cynthia found out.”

Sullivan says about 50% of the women who get BetterBoyfriend.me packages know he is involved. He says this is linked to age. He thinks that after people have been married a while, they do not keep many secrets.

Over the year, Sullivan says he has gotten to know the boyfriends well. One time, they even delivered a package to a hospital delivery room. He said the key is to remember that his business relationship is with the boyfriend.

When he first started the company, he put BetterBoyfriend.me tags on flower bouquets and went to Dolores Park in the Mission District. He looked for couples and gave the flowers to the girl. The boyfriend did not like that. So he changed his plan and started giving the flowers to the boyfriends instead.
