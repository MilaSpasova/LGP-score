Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8 April 2014, she told people about a very special moment. She received a message in a bottle, 101 years after her grandfather threw it into the Baltic Sea. People think it may be the world’s oldest message in a bottle. A museum in Germany gave it to her, and the museum is showing it now.

Erdmann, 62, said it was a big surprise. She said a man came to her door and told her he had mail from her grandfather. He said a message in a bottle had been found, and the name on the card was her grandfather’s. The man was a family history researcher. He found her in Berlin after the bottle and letter were given to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg.

The bottle was a brown beer bottle. It had been in the water for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it in his catch in the Baltic Sea near the northern city of Kiel. Holger von Neuhoff, a curator at the museum, said it was the oldest bottled message he had seen. He said the museum has older documents, but not with the bottle. He also said the bottle and note were in very good condition.

Researchers think Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913. He was hiking with a nature group. He was 20 years old then.

Much of the postcard could not be read. But people could read the Berlin address on the front. They could also read that he politely asked the finder to send the note to his home address.

Erdmann said he also put two stamps from that time in the bottle, so the finder would not have to pay. She said he did not think it would take 101 years.

Erdmann said the message made her feel very moved. She did not know her grandfather because he died at age 54, six years before she was born.

She said she knew very little about him. But she found out he was a writer. She said he was open-minded, believed in freedom, and thought everyone should respect each other. She said he did a lot for young people, and later travelled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful to learn where her family roots came from.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and travelling around the world. She said she is open-minded too. She said what her grandfather taught his two daughters, her mother taught her, and she has passed it on to her sons.

Even though she was happy, she said she hopes other people will not throw bottles with messages into the sea today. She said the sea already has too many bottles and too much rubbish.

The message and bottle will be on show at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts will try to read the rest of the text. It is not clear what will happen to the bottle later, but Erdmann hopes it will stay at the museum.

She said they want to add some photos with the bottle, so visitors can see the young man who threw it into the water.
