James Hamblin is a senior editor at The Atlantic magazine. In his series “If Our Bodies Could Talk,” he tried a challenge: he stopped showering as much as usual. When he did shower, he did not use shampoo or soap. He wanted to see what happens when people clean their bodies too much.

He found something many others have found. When we scrub our skin a lot with soap and body wash, the skin tries hard to get back to balance. Showering can wash away the skin’s natural oil and helpful bacteria. This can happen even more when we use hot water and cleaning products.

After a shower, you might feel dry and tight. This is because your skin’s natural moisture has been washed away. Your skin also has millions of helpful bacteria, like your gut does. Frequent washing and scrubbing can kill many of these bacteria. When the bacteria grow back, the ones that return may make more smell. So showering too often can sometimes make you smell more. If you stop using soap and shower less, your skin may go through an early adjustment time that might feel gross. After that, the skin often returns to balance, makes less oil, and healthy bacteria can grow again.

In the end, Hamblin realized what other people who avoid soap and showers have said for years: the human body can work well on its own. It is not only about smell or looks. Dermatologists say using less soap can help acne and eczema.

Showering less can also help the environment. The average shower lasts seven minutes and uses 65 litres of water. That is 65 litres of clean, drinkable water that we mix with soap and send down the drain every day, sometimes more than once.

Clean water is becoming more important, as California has another summer of drought. It is becoming clear that clean water is one of the world’s most valuable resources, and it may soon be in short supply. Also, all the body wash bottles affect the environment. These are strong reasons to use fewer products and be more natural.

If this idea makes you feel nervous because you remember being near someone who smelled bad, relax. Many people who wash less still use deodorant if they need it. Everyone’s natural smell is different, and it can change with diet, how much water you drink, and exercise. Also, washing your hands with soap is still recommended to help stop infectious diseases from spreading.

You do not have to stop all at once like Hamblin did. But there is a middle choice between washing many times a day and never showering. Shower less often, use less soap, and let your skin’s helpful bacteria grow.
