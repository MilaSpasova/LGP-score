A thick layer of bird droppings covers a gold-colored railing at one of Britain’s most expensive homes. Pigeon bones lie among broken mirrors, and water runs through damaged parts of the ceiling. This home is called The Towers. It is a £30m mansion on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London, and its serious decay has been kept secret until now.

The Towers is one of ten big houses in the middle of The Bishops Avenue. This street is at the center of London’s rising property market. These houses have been almost empty since they were bought about 25 years ago. It is believed they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. The Greek-style columns are breaking, and the swimming pools with mosaic tiles are filled with rubble. Nature has moved in so much that owls live there.

This sad scene is seen along the whole street. Lloyds Bank says The Bishops Avenue is the second most expensive street in Britain. At the same time, many people in London cannot afford a home as prices rise by 11.2% a year. But 16 mansions on the most expensive part of the street are empty. Many are behind locked gates, with metal covers on the windows, overgrown gardens, and guard dogs.

Across the street is another empty mansion worth £18m, with broken windows and paint meant to stop people climbing. Another house has metal bars on its windows and was sold for £20m.

Even so, prices keep rising. Dryades, a mansion that was owned until recently by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007 and is believed to be worth about £30m today. Heath Lodge, where fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou was murdered in 1984 by a silver bullet, is worth £13m today. It was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

For people who are struggling to afford housing in one of the world’s most expensive cities, these empty homes can feel very upsetting. A security guard on the street said it was annoying to watch so much space—enough to house dozens of people—falling apart.

Very rich royals with oil money from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia were among the first to come to this curved road near Hampstead Heath. Iranians came here after the fall of the shah. Now, Chinese buyers are following Russians and Kazakhs who have paid millions for an address that estate agents say is as famous as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions were for sale for £65m and £38m, offering Italian marble, lifts with leather padding, and luxury panic rooms.

But on the grounds of the empty homes, stone fountains are breaking and lawns have turned into muddy bogs. Inside one house, water drips through a huge crystal chandelier onto a thick carpet that is rotting under parts of a fallen ceiling. Moss grows through broken bricks, and pieces of mirror are scattered in a bathroom. A swimming pool has about a foot of dirty, salty water, and flowers grow through its tiles. Wooden boards bend away from the sauna.

The most dramatic ruin is The Towers. It is a grand home set among many trees, including hornbeams, oaks, and limes. Pigeons fill its tall halls, and algae has turned the walls bright green. Water pours down through three floors and drops into a huge, empty basement swimming pool. Unopened wooden boxes marked “bullet-proof glass” show that the earlier owners worried about security.

Today, very few people live on The Bishops Avenue all the time. A security guard outside one mansion said the owners were not there. Another guard, outside Royal Mansion, would not say if anyone was home. A worker at another house only warned the Guardian about the guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare mogul with personal wealth of £45m, said he is one of the few people who live there permanently.

He said, “It’s not a friendly place where you can talk over the fence. I have never seen what my neighbours look like. Next door, a Saudi princess spent £35m on a new house and I’ve never seen her. About three houses are lived in 24/7. Half the properties are used three to six months a year. The other half, who knows if they come or not?”

These broken, very expensive homes show a culture where the world’s richest people see British property as an investment. One Hyde Park, an apartment building in Knightsbridge, is another example. More than half its flats are registered with the council as empty or second homes.

Even so, people on the street talk about building £5m apartments instead of £50m mansions, to try to bring people back.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, plans to rebuild one valuable site into 20 apartments. They would have a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

He said, “If you build a big house and try and sell for £30m to £40m, it won’t sell. Locals won’t buy, so you have to bring in overseas buyers.”

But the idea of using the empty homes to help solve the housing crisis still seems far away. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some of the empty houses could become affordable homes. He said the land price would be too high.

He said, “Very wealthy people own property there. Sometimes they live in them and sometimes they don’t.”
