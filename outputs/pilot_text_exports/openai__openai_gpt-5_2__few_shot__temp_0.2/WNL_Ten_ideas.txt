From glow-in-the-dark trees to bike storage under the ground and bins that use solar power, here are some unusual ideas to make city life better.

Living in a city has good points, but it is often hard to live in a sustainable way. People face pollution, heavy traffic, and fewer green spaces. Inventors have created many strange ideas to help.

Pop-up parks
Some cities seem built more for cars than for people. A pop-up park is a simple idea: use an empty parking space, a little money, and a few plants to make a small park for a short time. The PARK(ing) project started as an art experiment in San Francisco and then spread around the world. People have also tried temporary city farms and ecology demonstrations. The movement has a day of action every September.

Storage under the ground
Not everyone uses a car. More people ride bikes, but they need a safe place to keep them. A Tokyo engineering company called Giken created an “eco-cycle, anti-seismic underground bicycle park.” It is a round storage space only seven metres wide. It goes deep into the ground and can hold 204 bikes. Owners press a button, and the system brings their bike up in about 13 seconds.

Glow-in-the-dark trees
Some people think of Christmas decorations when they hear about glowing trees, but designer-artist Daan Roosegaarde has a different idea. He created an experimental “bioluminescent” plant. The technology mixes DNA from glowing marine bacteria with parts of a plant to make a jellyfish-like glow. Tests are happening to make a large-scale version. Roosegaarde hopes it could one day replace normal street lights.

Energy from footsteps
In West Ham in east London, many commuters and shoppers cross a raised walkway near the underground station. They may notice the floor feels a bit springy. That small movement helps power streetlights. The walkway has smart tiles that catch energy from footsteps and turn it into electricity. The UK company Pavegen made this system. It has also installed a similar system at Heathrow Airport and other places.

Supertrees
Singapore’s Gardens by the Bay has man-made “supertrees.” They are steel frames up to 50 metres high, with flowers and ferns growing on them. Their metal tops help take in and spread out heat. They can collect rainwater and help provide air ventilation for two “climate-controlled biomes” below (large conservatories). Eleven of the 18 trees also have solar panels on their “branches.”
