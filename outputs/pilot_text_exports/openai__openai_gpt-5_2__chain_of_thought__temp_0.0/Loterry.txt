The lottery balls have fallen, and all six numbers match. So it may be time to buy an Audi, book a holiday in the US, and call an estate agent. A study says many lottery millionaires do things like this.

Since it started in 1994, the lottery has made 3,000 millionaires. Together they have won more than £8.5bn, with an average of £2.8m each. The study writers, Oxford Economics, say this money also helped create 3,780 more millionaires among the winners’ children, family, and friends.

Most winners (59%) stop working right away. But 19% keep doing their normal job, and 31% do unpaid volunteer work. The study says 98% of winners’ spending stayed in the UK. By spending on homes, cars, and holidays, each winner is thought to help keep six people in full-time work for a year.

Winners added almost £750m to GDP and created more than £500m in tax money for the Exchequer. Most spending went on homes. Winners spent £2.72bn on their main homes, and £170m on paying off debt and mortgages.

Keeping an income was important, so £2.125bn was put into investments. Gifts to family and friends came to £1.17bn. Also, £680m was spent on cars and holidays.

Camelot, which runs the UK National Lottery, paid for the study to mark the 3,000-winner milestone. It used research from 100 winners who won £1m or more. It found that all 3,000 winners bought 7,958 houses or flats in the UK, or 2.7 each, spending £3.3bn. Most winners (82%) moved to a new main home, spending an average of £900,000.

Many new homes had extra features. Almost a third (29%) wanted a hot tub. A walk-in wardrobe was important for 28%. Almost a quarter (24%) chose a home behind electric gates. Also, 22% had a games room, and 7% put in a snooker table.

Big homes need care. So 30% hired a cleaner, and 24% hired a gardener. A small number (5%) hired a beautician.

Audis were the top choice for 16% of winners. Range Rovers and BMWs were also popular (11% each), as well as Mercedes (10%) and Land Rovers (5%). Winners spent £463m on 17,190 cars. The average price of their favourite car was £46,116.

Holidays were important too. Most (68%) chose five-star hotels in other countries. The US was the top place to go for 27%, followed by the Caribbean (9%). In the UK, caravan sales also gained. In the past 18 years, 10% of millionaires bought a caravan, creating sales of about £7.4m.

Some winners (15%) started their own business. Another 9% helped other people start businesses, and 6% invested in or bought other people’s businesses. The study says businesses started or helped by winners employ 3,195 people.

Andy Logan, who wrote the report, said a win affects more people than expected. He said it changes the lives of friends and family, and each win also affects the UK economy because so much money is spent in the UK. He said each win creates a “ripple effect” that can reach this generation and often the next.
