It could be the best new invention since Trevor Baylis’s wind-up radio in the pre-internet 1990s. It is a cheap light that makes free power from gravity. It could stop people using dangerous kerosene lamps in Africa and India.

But the British designer Patrick Hunt had trouble when he tried the usual way to get money from banks or venture capital investors. He said they tried to get funding, but the process was slow and complicated. The idea was new and unproven, so nobody wanted to take the risk.

So he tried crowdfunding on a US website called Indiegogo, which had just opened in the UK. In five days he reached his goal and raised £36,200. By the end of 2012, the campaign was so popular that in 40 days he had raised £400,000.

The LED light uses a dynamo. The dynamo is powered when a 10kg bag of rocks slowly drops down. The bag is attached to the light and lifted to about 2m high. As it falls to the ground, it makes enough power for half an hour of light.

Hunt is getting ready to make the product in China. He will test the market again by sending 1,000 lights to Africa before full mass production. He hopes mass production will be millions of units.

He is part of a new wave of entrepreneurs who use the fast-growing crowdfunding industry for money. Another new site is InvestingZone, which connects wealthy people with start-up entrepreneurs.

Indiegogo does not give shares. Instead, it lets users offer “perks” for different donation levels. People who helped fund Hunt’s light could feel good about helping poorer people, and they also got their own light.

Danae Ringelmann, a co-founder of Indiegogo, says the “gravity light” shows how crowdfunding can be fair and can test an idea. She says it is fast and efficient because it is not about knowing the right people at a bank or a venture capital firm. It is about proving the idea to customers and fans, and getting them to support it with money.

She also says even ideas that do not get funding are worth testing. If it is not a good idea, you find out sooner and learn faster.

Ringelmann lives in the US. She first worked as a Wall Street analyst. In 2008 she quit and used her skills to help friends in the arts raise money.

At first, the site focused on films. It launched at the Sundance Film Festival that year.

Five years later, the site was raising about $2m a week for new businesses that were starting or growing. In December 2012 it launched euro and sterling services to grow on this side of the Atlantic. It says Britain is its third biggest market. International activity is up 41% since December.

There are many competitors, like Kickstarter, Seedrs, and Funding Circle. But Ringelmann says Indiegogo is different because anyone can start a campaign. No project is seen as too strange.

The site charges a 4% fee for successful campaigns. If a campaign does not reach its target, users can choose to refund all the money to contributors at no cost, or keep all the money raised and pay a 9% fee.

A British woman, Lauren Pears, raised £100,000 on the site to open a “cat café” in London. It is called Lady Dinah’s Cat Emporium. It has not opened yet, but it is described as a place where people can sit in a comfortable wingback chair, have a hot cup of tea, read a book, and be with a cat.

Ringelmann says some campaigns are rejected by venture capitalists because they say the idea might not be something people want, and it could be a gadget nobody cares about. She says entrepreneurs can run an Indiegogo campaign, even without launching the project, and the campaign can show proof that a market exists. She says it lets people test the market, pricing, and features, find new revenue streams, and get important feedback.

With her Wall Street experience, and after helping 100,000 businesses and services raise money, Ringelmann has advice for new entrepreneurs. She says ideas are common, and success depends on doing the work well. She says if you are scared someone will steal your idea and do it better and faster, then you are not the right person to carry it out. She says you need confidence to move fast and learn.

Ringelmann says expansion into Europe, and a deal with a web transaction provider, are part of a dream to make finance more open to everyone. This will allow payments through local card services like Maestro in the UK and Carte Bleue in France, as well as PayPal. More than 7,000 finance campaigns are live on the site.

Crowdfunding as an option besides banks has grown, but it is less attractive to big investors. They usually want a stake in a promising business. This could start to change in the UK with the launch of InvestingZone.
