James Hamblin is a senior editor at the American magazine The Atlantic. In his series “If Our Bodies Could Talk,” he tried a challenge: he stopped showering for a while to see what happens when we clean our bodies too much. He took fewer showers. When he did shower, he did not use shampoo or soap.

He learned what many other people have found. When we scrub a lot with soap and body wash, our skin tries harder to get back to balance. Then we feel like we need to clean again. Showering washes away the skin’s natural oil and bacteria. Many people think that is the point. But it can work too well, especially with hot water and cleaning products.

Have you ever felt tight and dry after a shower? That is because much of your skin’s natural moisture got washed away. Also, your skin has millions of helpful bacteria, like your gut does. Frequent rubbing and scrubbing can kill these helpful bacteria. When bacteria grow back after being washed off with soap, they may include more kinds that cause smell. So showering too often can sometimes make you smell more.

If you stop showering and stop using soap, your skin may go through an early adjustment time that can seem gross. After that, the skin often returns to balance. Oil production slows, and healthy bacteria grow again.

After his experiment, Hamblin felt that the human body can be quite lovely when it works on its own. It is not only about smell or looks. Dermatologists say that using less soap can help acne and eczema.

Showering less can also help the environment. The average shower lasts seven minutes and uses 65 litres of water. That is 65 litres of clean, drinkable water that we mix with soap and send down the drain every day, sometimes more than once.

Clean water is becoming harder to ignore, as California enters another summer of drought. It is becoming clear that clean water is one of the world’s most valuable resources, and it may soon be in short supply. Also think about the environmental impact of all those body wash bottles. These are strong reasons to let your body be more natural.

If this idea makes you feel uncomfortable because you have been close to people who do not shower much and do not smell good yet, relax. Many people who cut back on washing still use deodorant if they need it. Everyone’s natural smell is different, and it can change with things like diet, hydration, and exercise. Also, washing your hands with soap is still recommended to help stop infectious diseases from spreading.

You do not need to stop all at once like Hamblin did. But there is a middle ground between cleaning many times a day and never showering. Try to reduce. Skip a few showers, use less soap, and let your skin’s helpful bacteria grow.
