A thick layer of bird droppings covers a gold-colored railing at one of Britain’s most expensive homes. Pigeon skeletons lie near broken mirrors, and water runs through broken ceiling edges. This home is called The Towers. It is a £30m palace on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London. Its ruin has been kept secret until now.

The Towers is one of ten large houses in the middle of The Bishops Avenue. This road is at the center of London’s fast-rising property market. These houses have been almost empty since they were bought about 25 years ago. It is believed they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. Their Greek-style columns are breaking. Their mosaic swimming pools are full of rubble. Nature has taken over so much that owls have moved in.

This sad scene is repeated along the avenue. Lloyds Bank says it is the second most expensive street in Britain. At the same time, more and more people in London struggle to buy a home because prices rise 11.2% a year. But 16 mansions on the most expensive part of The Bishops Avenue are sitting empty. Many are behind locked gates. Their windows are covered with steel bars, and their gardens are overgrown. Guard dogs patrol the grounds.

Across the street is another abandoned mansion, worth £18m. It has smashed windows and walls covered in anti-climb paint. Another house has metal bars on the windows, and it was sold for £20m.

Even so, prices keep going up. Dryades, a mansion that was owned until recently by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007 and is believed to be worth about £30m today. Heath Lodge, where fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou was murdered in 1984 by a silver bullet, is worth £13m today. It was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

Seeing these empty, broken homes can be painful for people who struggle to pay for a place to live in one of the world’s most expensive cities. One security guard said it was upsetting to watch so much space—enough to house dozens of people—falling apart.

Royals with oil money from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia were among the first to come to this curved road near Hampstead Heath. Iranians came here after the fall of the shah. Now Chinese buyers are following Russians and Kazakhs, who have spent millions to get an address that estate agents say is as famous as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions have been for sale for £65m and £38m. They promise lots of Italian marble, lifts with leather padding, and luxury panic rooms.

But on the grounds of the empty mansions, stone fountains crumble and lawns have turned to muddy bogs. Inside one house, water drips through a huge crystal chandelier onto a thick carpet. The carpet is rotting under parts of a fallen ceiling. Moss grows through broken bricks, and mirror tiles are scattered in a bathroom. The swimming pool has about a foot of dirty salty water, and flowers grow through its tiles. Wooden slats in the sauna bulge outward.

The most dramatic ruin is The Towers. It is a large mansion set among acres of hornbeams, oaks, and limes. Pigeons fill its huge halls with high ceilings. Water pours through three floors, and the walls have turned bright green with algae. The water drips into a large, empty basement swimming pool. Unopened wooden crates marked “bullet-proof glass” show the security fears of the previous owners.

Today, very few people live on The Bishops Avenue full time. A security guard outside one mansion said the owners were not there. Another guard, outside Royal Mansion, would not say if anyone was home. A staff member at another mansion warned the Guardian about the guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare mogul with personal wealth of £45m, said he is one of the few people who live there permanently.

“It’s not a friendly place, where you can chat over the fence,” he said. “To be honest, I have never seen what my neighbours look like. Next door, a Saudi princess spent £35m on a new house and I’ve never seen her. There are about three houses that are lived in 24/7 and half of the properties are occupied three to six months a year. The other half, who knows if they come or not?”

These broken, very expensive homes show a property culture where the world’s richest people see British property as investments. One Hyde Park, a group of apartments in Knightsbridge, is another example. There, more than half the flats are registered with the council as empty or second homes.

Still, people on the avenue talk about building £5m apartments instead of £50m mansions, to try to bring people back.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, plans to rebuild one of the most valuable sites on the avenue. He wants to make 20 apartments with a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

“If you build a big house and try and sell for £30m to £40m, it won’t sell,” he said. “Locals won’t buy and so you have to bring in overseas buyers.”

But using the empty homes to help solve the housing crisis still seems far away. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some of the empty homes could become affordable homes. He said the land price would be too high.

“Very wealthy people own property there,” he said. “Sometimes they live in them and sometimes they don’t.”
