It was a beautiful summer evening, and I decided to go for a swim near Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland. I moved there in 2012. A woman was in the water with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty has a close relationship with a group of people she often swims with.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Since then, hundreds of people have swum with her, so she can seem completely tame. She was even in an Irish tourist-board ad, where a girl says she wants to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty’s tummy. It looked safe and tempting. About 20 tourists and local people were on the pier watching.

Soon after I got into the water, Dusty left the woman and suddenly went wild. Later I learned that she is very protective of her space when she is with someone. Her tail was flapping fast. At first I thought she was just showing off, but then I realized she might be angry. I knew I had to get out, so I swam toward the pier. But in a tiny moment, Dusty slammed into me with her snout. It was very strong and very painful, and she was amazingly fast. I was thrown forward.

The people on the pier stared down at me with their mouths open. Dusty stayed beside me, still flapping her tail. That scared me the most. I thought, if she hits me with her tail, I could sink.

I reached the pier, but I could not climb out because I was hurt. I felt pure terror. I shouted for help. A man reached in and pulled me onto the steps. Then another man came and said he was an orthopaedic surgeon, a bone doctor, who specialized in marine trauma, injuries from the sea. He was driving into Doolin when he saw what was happening.

I was very cold and very worried. I did not know how bad my injuries were, and my biggest fear was bleeding inside my body. He checked me and spoke calmly. He said he could not feel any sign of internal bleeding, but he thought I probably had broken bones. Later I learned I had six spinal fractures, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I was in the hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for several weeks. I was off work for five months. I had limited movement, stiffness, and pain. Then doctors said I had post-traumatic stress, a stress problem after a frightening event. My near-death experience made me anxious about everything, and I overreacted in ways I never had before. I felt like people were looking at me the wrong way. Loud noises were hard for me. I also had memory loss.

Three months before the accident, I had opened a health-food shop. But I had to give it up because I could not work anymore. It was the hardest year ever, but now it is behind me. I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building my own osteopathy practice. I understand my patients better because I have been a patient too.

I am thankful that I am healthy, and I really want to stop other people from getting hurt. People often think dolphins are always gentle and safe. Who would think a dolphin could attack a person? If you see a dangerous animal coming at you with teeth showing, it is clearly scary. But dolphins have a wide smile.

I am not angry with Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her space, and she is a wild animal who can be unpredictable. People need to know this. Many people come to swim with her, and they do not understand how dangerous it can be. My injuries were reported as one of several injuries that summer.

After the man pulled me out, Dusty swam away. Then she came back and floated upright next to me, looking at me. We looked into each other’s eyes, and I felt she was sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin. The anger was gone. The people on the pier were amazed. After that moment with her, my terror ended. I made peace with her.
