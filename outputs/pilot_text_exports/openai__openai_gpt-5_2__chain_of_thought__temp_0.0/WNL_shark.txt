Opposition to Western Australia’s shark cull has grown. Thousands of people went to beaches around Australia to ask the state’s premier to stop the policy. RSPCA Australia and Richard Branson, the owner of Virgin Atlantic, also spoke against it.

The policy involves catching and killing sharks longer than three metres. It began after what the state government called an “unprecedented” number of shark attacks on Western Australia’s coast. A 35-year-old surfer was killed in November 2013. He was the sixth person to die from a shark attack in two years.

However, the Shark Attack File says Australia as a whole has averaged one shark-related death each year for the last 50 years.

Kate Faehrmann, a board member of the Sea Shepherd Conservation Society, spoke at a protest in Perth. She said, “We’ve been saying all along that this policy won’t work.” She said drumlines are used to catch sharks, but they kill many animals. She said they can kill sharks of any size, and also dolphins, turtles, and other animals. She said this is why many people do not want the policy.

Thousands of people protested at Perth’s Cottesloe Beach and Sydney’s Manly Beach. Hundreds protested at Glenelg in south-west Adelaide. People also protested at beaches in Victoria and Queensland. Faehrmann said the protests showed many Australians want sharks protected. She said that even with fear from movies like Jaws, people are coming out in large numbers to say the ocean belongs to sharks too.

Anthony Joyce, a surfer whose foot was once caught in a shark’s mouth, said the number of sharks that will be killed will make no real difference.

The state government has refused to give a running total of how many sharks have been killed. There have been reports of sharks smaller than three metres being released after being caught on drumlines. Drumlines are floating drums fixed to the sea bed, with bait on hooks underneath. Conservationists say there is no proof the cull will reduce shark attacks on humans. They say no earlier cull used only drumlines. Researchers at the University of Western Australia say the recent group of shark attacks may be linked to Western Australia having the fastest-growing population in Australia, not to more sharks.

Richard Peirce, chairman of the UK conservation charity the Shark Trust, said the cull will not work and could attract more predators to the coast. He said it is sad that the government chose killing instead of testing other options. He also said that even if drumlines are checked during the day, leaving them overnight could attract other predators. This could happen because sharks and other animals may be hooked and injured.

Around the world in 2012, there were 80 unprovoked shark attacks, and seven were fatal. In comparison, humans kill nearly 100m sharks each year. RSPCA Australia said it believes the cull is not justified. It said there is no evidence that more attacks are caused by more sharks. It said the rise fits with changes in population and human behaviour, meaning more people are in the water.

Richard Branson told Fairfax Radio the policy was backfiring. He said Premier Colin Barnett may have thought it would help tourism, but Branson believes it will do the opposite. He said it makes the problem seem bigger than it is and could stop people from coming to Perth and the countryside nearby. He said it will only make people worry for no good reason.
