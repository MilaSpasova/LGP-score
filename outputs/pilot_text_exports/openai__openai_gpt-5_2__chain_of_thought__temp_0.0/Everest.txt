Kenton Cool usually likes to talk. But now he can hardly speak. Climbing hard at very high altitude has made his voice rough. He is in Kathmandu, the capital of Nepal. He flew down from Everest base camp that morning.

Cool is thinking about a surprising set of climbs he finished over the weekend. Early Saturday morning, he reached the top of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of three main peaks in the Everest “horseshoe.” This horseshoe surrounds the icy valley called the Western Cwm.

Later that same day, he climbed to the top of Everest. He reached the summit in complete darkness early Sunday. Then he and his climbing partner kept going. They climbed to the top of Lhotse on Monday morning.

“It was a chance I grabbed,” he says. “For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. That does not remove the physical success of what I did. I have set the bar at a certain level. But the next person will raise the bar and do it without ropes or bottled oxygen.”

Sixty years after people first climbed Everest, many stories look back to Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. They describe a time before today’s commercial tourism and environmental damage. I ask Cool to look ahead and imagine what top climbers might do 60 years from now.

“I hate to think,” he says. But he talks about the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. In April, Steck left the mountain after what Cool calls “an altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck planned to climb Everest’s west ridge, first climbed in 1963. Then he would go down to the South Col. After that, he would immediately climb Lhotse by a new route. He planned to do all of this without fixed ropes. “Ueli had been training like a machine,” Cool says. “He is a climber in a class all his own. He is very skilled, and his fitness was at an extremely high level. It would have been amazing to see what he could have done.”

What tourism will look like is a different question. One clue is a dramatic helicopter rescue by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro’s angry words helped start the confrontation at Camp 2. Moro flew back to Everest on Tuesday. He was flying a powerful helicopter. He rescued a sick climber at 7,800 metres.

This was the highest rescue ever done on Everest. It also shows how quickly helicopter flights have increased in recent years. By 2073, the mountain could even have a helipad on the South Col. Tourists could be brought there while using bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing how people can rescue both climbers and the many trekkers who go only as far as base camp.

Alton Byers, a mountain geographer and environmentalist, says it is not clear if the Everest region can keep handling fast-growing tourism. He says climate change and tourism together are putting new pressure on the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers in the Everest region are shrinking, and some are disappearing. This is already causing big problems. “Everywhere you go, people talk about having less water,” he says. “There is less water for farming and less water for the new lodges being built.”

Byers says that in the Sherpa town of Namche Bazaar, workers are building a new five-mile pipeline. It will bring water for the growing number of tourists who want showers and flush toilets. The local stream is polluted with human waste. It also does not have enough water for a town that, in the busy season, is extremely crowded. “Every village is digging a pit just beyond the houses for garbage. Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world,” he says. At base camp, human waste is now handled well and taken away in plastic barrels. But Byers says these barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley, and it could leak into the area’s waterways.

“These problems can be solved, but we need to take it seriously,” he says. “One climber can spend $85,000 climbing Everest. That is fine. But at some point we must deal with these other needs too. For half a million dollars a year, you could solve most of them.”

Climate change is harder to deal with. Byers works with local conservation groups to find and plan for climate-change effects. This often means finding new water sources or collecting rainwater. A growing danger is the fast growth of glacial lakes. They could burst and flood the Sherpa homeland. “A time will come when people will have to get out of their way,” he says.

Weather changes are also affecting tourism. More clouds during times that are usually clear are closing Lukla Airport more often. Lukla Airport is the main entry point to the Everest region. A new road for 4x4s is being built to Lukla to make sure tourists and their money can still arrive. But Byers worries the fast spread of roads in Nepal is being done cheaply. He says this can cause serious soil erosion and landslides.

“Everest is the famous mountain everyone knows,” he says. “It is a warning sign everyone understands. It is the perfect place to learn how to deal with problems like climate change and tourism.”
