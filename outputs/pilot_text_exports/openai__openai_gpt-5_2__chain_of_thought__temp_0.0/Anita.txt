Brazil has a new funk star named Anitta. She has won millions of fans by bringing favela music into the mainstream. But there is now a debate about her skin colour.

People who fight discrimination and other writers say the music industry’s fastest rising star had to give up looking black to reach a mostly white, middle-class market.

The debate grew after “before and after” photos were shared. They show Anitta’s skin looking much lighter since she signed a deal with Warner.

In the first photo, before she was famous, she looks darker. In the second photo, used for marketing after she became famous, she looks paler. Some people wonder if this came from whitening products, cosmetic surgery, or more likely, Photoshop. The big difference made people talk again about whether you need to be pale to succeed in Brazil.

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote a post. She said the change in Anitta’s image shows hidden discrimination in media and marketing. She said many people do not accept that they are racist, and they think Brazil is a multiracial democracy, but the numbers show it is not. She said whitening shows a society that does not support diversity. She said people often connect “white” with rich, nice, successful, and good, and see “black” as the opposite.

Anitta was born Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

She later became very famous with an album and a big hit song, Show das Poderosas. The song reached number one and got 52 million views on YouTube.

Many fans love her as a pop idol with catchy songs and a strong message. The people who support her present her as a bridge between the mostly black and mixed-race poor communities on Rio’s hills and the richer and whiter areas below.

She has made baile funk less extreme for a wider audience. She reduced sexual dancing, “gangsta” talk, and explicit lyrics. Now people ask if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract a richer group of buyers. Arraes said this can pressure pop stars to change their looks, like straightening curly hair or getting a nose job, and that it can hurt self-esteem.

This topic is sensitive in Brazil, which has many mixed-race people. Brazil was one of the last big countries to ban slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. But in Brazil, colour often matters more than race and family background. Even though Brazil aims to be a multiracial democracy, skin tone is clearly linked to inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Until 2011, black or mixed-race students also spent about two years less in school on average.

The government says the gap is getting smaller because of quota systems for university places and other affirmative action. But the difference is still easy to see. Most business and government leaders are white, while most low-paid jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In rich areas like Ipanema and Gávea, you are much more likely to see black nannies pushing strollers with white toddlers than the other way around.

Colour is hard to define. In the 2010 census, people who called themselves white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million said black, two million said Asian, and 0.5% said indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology teacher at the Federal University of Pernambuco, said Anitta won middle-class fans by taking a rebellious style and making it calmer and easier for everyone to accept. He said this was not done by making her “whiter.” He said it was a change in where she made her art: from the poorer edges of society to the centre.

Other people say the colour issue is too big. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who worked with Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot, but not her colour. He said it was more about makeup, hair styling, and clothing. He said he did not think she was pressured. He said she always liked to show off, sing, and dance, and that this natural style helped her succeed.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador, said the prejudice was not mainly from the public, but from music producers and media leaders. He said the public showed they cared about music itself when they chose Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, as the recent winner of the talent show The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for comment. But in a recent interview, she said identity should be chosen by the person. She said a powerful woman does not need to be beautiful, but can be marvellous because of her attitude. She said her work tries to show that we can be who we want.
