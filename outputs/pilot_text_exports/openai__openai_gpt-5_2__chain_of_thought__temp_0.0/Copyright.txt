Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is theft. He says it is like putting your hand in someone’s pocket and stealing their wallet. In an article for Index on Censorship, Pullman (the president of the Society of Authors) strongly supports copyright laws. He criticizes people who think it is OK to download music or books without paying.

Pullman writes that new technology looks amazing, but it can make people forget that what they are doing is wrong. He says it is shocking that someone can steal an artist’s work and not be punished.

His article came after leaders from the music industry met British Prime Minister David Cameron at Downing Street. They talked about web piracy.

Pullman, who wrote the His Dark Materials trilogy, says writers and musicians can be poor and unknown for years while they work. Then, when people finally enjoy their work, their chance to earn money from it can be taken away by illegal downloading. He says the rule is simple: if we want to enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

Pullman is writing in the next issue of the campaign group’s magazine in a dialogue with Cathy Casserly. She is the chief executive of Creative Commons, which offers open content licences that let creators control how they use copyright. Casserly says many things are wrong with copyright because it was made in an “analogue age.” She says copyright often stops people from sharing, building on, and remixing each other’s work in ways that were not expected when the laws were made.

Casserly says artists should think in new ways about how they share and make money from their work. She quotes science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees. The magazine’s editor, Rachael Jolley, says today’s copyright laws do not work well in the digital age and could make consumers into criminals. She says we need new ways for artists, writers, and musicians to earn money from their work.

The debate is active, and illegal downloading is very large. Data collected by Ofcom (the independent regulator and competition authority for the UK communications industries) suggests that, between November 2012 and January 2013 in the UK, 280 million music tracks were pirated. Also pirated were 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files.

Ofcom has said 18% of internet users aged over 12 admit they have recently pirated content, and 9% say they fear getting caught. Pullman writes that it is still surprising how quickly people can get music as MP3 downloads. He says this is especially surprising to people who have been building up their iTunes list for some time.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron appointed Conservative MP Mike Weatherley to be his adviser on this issue. A spokesman for the BPI, the record industry trade body, said Mike Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creative producers it protects. The spokesman said they hope Weatherley’s influence, and the prime minister’s support for copyright, will affect how the UK’s intellectual property office acts.
