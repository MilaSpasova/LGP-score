Dan Sullivan was late to breakfast with the Guardian. He was late because the police stopped him while he was riding his motorcycle.

Sullivan says he works too much. He misses dinner dates. He forgets to buy presents.

So, like many people in Silicon Valley, the 27-year-old venture capitalist made a start-up to help. It is called BetterBoyfriend.me. For about $70 a month, it sends a present each month to a girlfriend or wife.

Sullivan works in finance. He has been testing the service quietly. About 350 boyfriends have signed up. Together they bring in about $17,000 in revenue each month. He says most members are his Harvard and investor friends. They include venture capitalists, founders, and workers at companies like Apple, Google, and Facebook. Their girlfriends have been getting presents from Sullivan for the last year.

Each month, members choose from a list of seven gifts, like chocolate, tea sets, and manicures. The package is sent to the boyfriend with no brand name and no label.

Sullivan tests ideas by buying Facebook ads to see what kind of response they get. BetterBoyfriend.me grew fast. Sullivan said, “BetterBoyfriend hit something. It’s a pain point everyone has.”

BetterBoyfriend.me was not Sullivan’s first idea. For a few days, he thought about a dog-sharing business. He said he saw rich guys looking miserable walking dogs. He said a dog is only valuable 5% of the time. He thought about having someone babysit dogs so other people could rent them.

Another start-up, called InvisibleBoyfriend, lets users create a believable social partner. This “boyfriend” can always talk and can text with you all day.

Founder Kyle Tabor started it to give users a fake boyfriend to stop unwanted attention. But he found that people got attached to their computer-made partners. He said many more users want companionship through conversation, not just “proof” of a relationship to make other people stop bothering them.

For Sullivan and Tabor, a surprise was the real connections they formed with customers. Sullivan says he has started to see himself as a kind of relationship helper for the boyfriends.

Sullivan says he made mistakes. At first, the packages included receipts with his name, Dan Sullivan. “One of the boyfriends wrote to me and said, ‘Listen, dude, she’s not mad but Cynthia found out’.”

Sullivan says about 50% of the women who get BetterBoyfriend.me packages know he is involved. He says this is connected to age. He thinks that after people have been married for a while, they do not keep many secrets.

Over the year, Sullivan says he has gotten to know the boyfriends very well. They even delivered a package to a hospital delivery room. He said the key is to remember that his relationship is with the boyfriend.

When he first started the company, he put BetterBoyfriend.me tags on flower bouquets. Then he went to Dolores Park, a popular place in the Mission District with many tech workers and young people.

He said, “I looked for couples and gave the bouquet to the girl but that didn’t go down well with the boyfriend. Like, not at all. It made sense, though. It was cool.” He said he then changed his plan and started giving the flowers to the boyfriends.
