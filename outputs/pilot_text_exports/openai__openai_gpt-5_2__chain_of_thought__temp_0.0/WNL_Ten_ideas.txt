From glow-in-the-dark trees to underground bike sheds and solar-powered bins, there are many unusual ideas to make cities nicer places to live.

Living in a city has many good points, but it can be hard to live in a way that is good for the planet. People deal with pollution, traffic, and fewer green spaces every day.

Inventors are trying new ideas. Here are some of the stranger ways to make cities more livable.

Pop-up parks
Some cities seem built more for cars than for people. A pop-up park is a simple idea. You take an empty parking space, some small items, and a few plants, and you make a tiny park. The PARK(ing) project started as an art experiment in San Francisco and then spread around the world. People have also made temporary city farms and ecology demonstrations. The movement has a day of action every September.

Subterranean storage
Not everyone in a city uses a car. Bicycles are becoming more popular. But where can you keep a bike safe? A Tokyo-based engineering firm called Giken made an idea for this: an “eco-cycle, anti-seismic underground bicycle park.” The storage is a cylinder that is only seven metres wide. It goes deep into the ground and can hold 204 bikes. Owners can get their bike back by pressing a button. The automated system brings it up in about 13 seconds.

Glow-in-the-dark trees
Some people think of Christmas decorations when they think of glowing trees, but not Daan Roosegaarde. This Dutch designer-artist created a “bioluminescent” plant. The experimental technology joins DNA from glowing marine bacteria with the chloroplast genome of a plant, so it can glow like a jellyfish. Trials are happening to make an industrial-scale version. Roosegaarde hopes it could one day replace normal street lighting.

Footfall harvesting
Every day, many commuters and shoppers in West Ham in east London cross an elevated walkway near the underground station. Most people do not notice that the surface feels springy. The rubber surface bends by about five millimetres. The floor has smart tiles that capture energy from people’s footsteps and turn it into electricity. This helps power the streetlights above. Pavegen, the UK firm behind this idea, has also installed a similar system at London’s Heathrow Airport and in other places around the world.

Supertrees
Singapore’s Gardens by the Bay has man-made trees called “supertrees.” They are steel-framed and can be up to 50 metres high. Flowers and ferns grow on them. Their metal tops can absorb and spread out heat. They can also collect rainwater and help provide air ventilation for two “climate-controlled biomes” (large conservatories) below. Eleven of the 18 trees also have solar panels on their “branches.”
