Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8 April 2014, she talked about a special moment. She got a message in a bottle. Her grandfather had thrown it into the Baltic Sea 101 years earlier. People think it may be the world’s oldest message in a bottle. A museum in Germany, which is showing it now, gave it to Erdmann.

Erdmann, 62, said it was a big surprise. She said a man came to her door and said he had mail from her grandfather. He said a bottle with a message had been found, and the name on the card was her grandfather’s name.

The visitor was a family-history researcher. He found Erdmann in Berlin after the letter was given to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg, a port city in northern Germany.

The bottle was a brown beer bottle. It had been in the sea for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it in his catch. He was fishing in the Baltic Sea near the northern city of Kiel.

Holger von Neuhoff, a curator at the museum, said this was the oldest bottled message he had seen. He said the museum has older documents, but those were found without the bottle. With both the bottle and the document, he said this is the oldest right now. He said it is in very good condition.

Researchers believe Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913. They think he did it while hiking with a nature group. He was 20 years old then.

Much of the postcard could not be read. But the Berlin address on the front could be read. The note also asked the finder to send it to his home address.

Erdmann said her grandfather also put two stamps from that time into the bottle, so the finder would not have to pay. But, she said, he did not think it would take 101 years.

Erdmann said the message made her feel strong emotion. She did not know her grandfather because he died at age 54, six years before she was born.

She said she knew very little about him. But she learned that he was a writer. She said he was open-minded, believed in freedom, and thought people should respect each other. She said he did a lot for young people. Later, he traveled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful because she could see where her family roots came from.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and traveling around the world. She said she is open-minded too. She said what her grandfather taught his two daughters was taught to her by her mother, and she passed it on to her sons.

Even though she was happy to get the message, she said she hopes people will not throw bottles with messages into the sea today. She said the sea is already full of bottles and trash.

The message and the bottle will be shown at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts will try to read the rest of the text. It is not clear what will happen to the bottle then, but Erdmann hopes it will stay at the museum.

She said they want to share some photos to show with the bottle. She wants visitors to see the young man who threw the bottle into the water.
