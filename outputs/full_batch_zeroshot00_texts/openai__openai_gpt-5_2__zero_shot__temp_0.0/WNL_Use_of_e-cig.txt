Seeing co-workers and other people use an e-cigarette is now common. But have we reached “peak vape”? Some statistics suggest vaping may already be going down among smokers and recent ex-smokers. These people are most vapers. Big e-cigarette companies will watch these numbers closely, because they have spent millions promoting a product that was expected to keep growing.

In 2014, the health charity Action on Smoking and Health (ASH) released figures about adults in Britain. It said e-cigarette use tripled from about 700,000 users in 2012 to 2.1 million in 2014. E-cigarettes do not contain tobacco. They make vapour, not smoke.

But other figures from the Smoking Toolkit Study suggest interest may be getting weaker. This research group is backed by the Department of Health and reports on smoking trends every three months. It found that vaping among smokers and ex-smokers rose steadily until the end of 2013. At that time, about 22% of smokers and ex-smokers were vaping. During 2014, this stayed about the same, and then fell to 19% in the last three months of 2014. Early signs suggest the fall continued into 2015. Professor Robert West, from UCL’s Department of Epidemiology and Public Health, said this drop is “statistically significant.” He collects the Toolkit figures.

Smokers are the main target for e-cigarette companies because 7 out of 10 vapers are smokers. Only about 1% of people who have never smoked have tried an e-cigarette. West said the number of people who use e-cigarettes while still smoking is going down. He also said the team has only tracked vaping for just over a year, so this is a short time. They are not seeing growth in long-term ex-smokers or “never” smokers using e-cigarettes. He said rates could change, but right now they look steady.

This flat growth in the UK seems different from the US, where e-cigarettes have been promoted strongly and reports said they were growing. But West questioned the US data, because it did not clearly separate people who tried an e-cigarette once from people who vape regularly.

Experts say worries that vaping will become trendy among young non-smokers seem wrong. ASH found only 1.8% of children are regular users. Instead, e-cigarettes seem most popular with adults who want to quit smoking. James Dunworth, director and co-founder of ecigarettedirect.co.uk, said that even though Smoking In England shows use by smokers has levelled off, it also shows a big rise since May 2011. He said customers are happy, and that better technology and hardware are improving the experience and helping people switch from traditional cigarettes.

West agreed and said e-cigarettes act like a “souped-up nicotine patch.” He said they are more popular than nicotine patches, and may or may not be more effective. He said one-third of quit attempts use e-cigarettes, making them the most popular way to try to stop.

Hazel Cheeseman, policy director at ASH, said it is too early to say if vaping has peaked. She said the market in the UK may not have grown for about a year, but there does not seem to be a fall in the number of people using e-cigarettes to help them quit. She said using an e-cigarette is safer than smoking. Some, but not all, people find them helpful to quit. She also said there is little evidence they are causing more young people to smoke.

Recently, it was reported that the European Commission (EC) is considering higher taxes on e-cigarettes. This could affect their popularity. A new EC tobacco directive starts in 2016. It will limit nicotine in e-cigarettes to below current levels. This may mean vapers will need to use them more to get the same effect, which could also make e-cigarettes more expensive.

West said policymakers should treat e-cigarettes as a way to help people stop smoking, and not regulate them the same way as smoking. He said some local authorities and organizations treat e-cigarettes like cigarettes and ban them in public places and outdoors. He said this harms public health messages, because it can sound like an attack on vapers and uses an “everyone with a sledgehammer” approach. He said people must be careful not to stigmatize e-cigarettes.
