During an important day at Liverpool’s Anglican Cathedral, the families of the 96 people who died at Hillsborough talked most about one thing: the truth. Years ago, The Sun newspaper used the words “The Truth” as a headline, but the stories under it were lies. We now know these lies were given to the paper by South Yorkshire Police to move blame away from the police and on to the victims.

Margaret Aspinall’s son James was 18 when he died at the FA Cup semi-final between Liverpool and Nottingham Forest. She said the families had to fight for 23 years to get the truth. Aspinall, Chair of the Hillsborough Family Support Group, said the families’ grief would never go away, but she was “delighted” by the clear and “profound” apology from David Cameron.

The Hillsborough Independent Panel looked at 450,000 documents from the police, Sheffield Wednesday, and other groups. It then wrote a 395-page report. The report showed many official failures and cleared the victims and the football supporters.

Some facts had been shown before. But the report still shocked people because it showed how deep the cover-up was. The families said the police ran a plan to avoid responsibility and to falsely blame the supporters.

The panel said this plan was led by Chief Constable Peter Wright. South Yorkshire Police spread a story that drunk supporters, or supporters without tickets, caused the disaster. The police tested the victims’ blood for alcohol. The panel said this was “an exceptional decision” and found “no rationale” for it. If alcohol was found, the police then checked whether the victims had criminal records.

The report, mainly written by Professor Phil Scraton of Queen’s University, Belfast, and agreed by all eight panel members, found “no evidence” to support the serious claims of unusual drunkenness, having no tickets, or violence by Liverpool fans.

The report said that even as families began suffering, Wright met with the police federation in a Sheffield restaurant to prepare “a defence” and “a rock-solid story”. The Secretary of the South Yorkshire Police Federation branch, Constable Paul Middup, said at the meeting that the Chief Constable had said the truth could not come from him, but that Middup had a free hand and support from many senior officers.

This meeting happened just four days after the disaster. It was the same day The Sun printed “The Truth” on its front page, using lies from four senior South Yorkshire police officers. Middup was encouraged to keep blaming Liverpool supporters for supposed drunkenness and bad behaviour, and to spread “togetherness” across the force.

The report supported the claim made in parliament that senior officers changed junior officers’ statements like a “black propaganda unit”. These statements were used as official police accounts for the later inquiry. They were changed to remove criticism of the police and to mostly add claims about bad behaviour by supporters. The panel found that 116 of 164 statements were changed “to remove or alter comments unfavourable to South Yorkshire police”.

The police said the changes were only to remove “conjecture” and “opinion”. But the panel said it was more than that. Scraton said: “It was done to remove criticism of the police.”

This propaganda did not fool the first inquiry. By August 1989, the inquiry said the claims about drunkenness and misbehaviour were false, and it criticized the police for making them. The report also showed that Sheffield Wednesday’s ground was unsafe in important ways. It said the Football Association chose it for the match without even checking for a valid safety certificate. Hillsborough did not have one.

In this unsafe situation, the report said the main cause of the disaster was poor crowd control by the police, led by an inexperienced Chief Superintendent David Duckenfield. The police lost control outside the ground. About 24,000 Liverpool fans had to go through only 23 turnstiles. Duckenfield then ordered a large exit gate to be opened, letting many people in. The inquiry called his biggest mistake the failure to close the tunnel that led to the already overcrowded central section of the Leppings Lane terrace.

The inquiry showed this, but the police repeated their claims at the later inquest. The inquest was affected by the coroner’s decision not to hear evidence about what happened after 3.15pm. This left out what the panel said was a chaotic emergency response. The panel found that 41 of the 96 people who died might have been saved if the police and ambulance service had done their jobs properly. Aspinall said this was very hard for the families to think about.

After the panel’s report, the Attorney General will consider asking the High Court to cancel the inquest verdict of accidental death and hold a new inquest. There may also be prosecutions, even after all these years, of Sheffield Wednesday, South Yorkshire Police, and Sheffield City Council, which did not properly oversee safety at the ground. Trevor Hicks, President of the HFSG, lost both his teenage daughters, Sarah and Victoria, in the crush. He said: “The truth is out today. Tomorrow is for justice.”
