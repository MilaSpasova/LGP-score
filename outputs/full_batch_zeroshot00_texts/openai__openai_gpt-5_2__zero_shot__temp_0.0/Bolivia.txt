A big international argument has started about Bolivia’s indigenous people and their right to chew coca leaves. Coca leaves are the main plant used to make cocaine. Bolivia got a special exception to the 1961 Single Convention on Narcotic Drugs, an international agreement about drug rules. This exception lets indigenous people in Bolivia chew coca leaves.

Bolivia said the agreement went against its new constitution from 2009. The constitution says Bolivia must “protect native and ancestral coca as cultural patrimony” and says coca “in its natural state … is not a narcotic”.

Indigenous people in South America have chewed coca leaves for hundreds of years. People say the leaves give energy and may help as medicine. People who support Bolivia said it was protecting indigenous rights. Danny Kushlick from the Transform Drug Policy Foundation said Bolivia’s action was “inspirational and groundbreaking”. He said countries can change how they deal with UN drug agreements.

But the UN’s International Narcotics Control Board (INCB) said Bolivia is harming the world drug control system. Several countries, including the UK, the US, Italy, Sweden, the Netherlands, and Russia, opposed Bolivia’s request.

The UK told the UN that it respects the coca leaf’s cultural importance in Bolivia. But it said it is worried the exception could increase coca growing and increase the amount used for cocaine. The UK said this could weaken international law and the world effort to stop the drug trade.

The right to chew coca leaves in the Andean region was removed in 1964. At that time Bolivia was under a dictatorship and signed the agreement. Bolivia was given 25 years to start the ban. That time ended in 1989, and the issue has been argued about since then.

In 2011, Bolivia told the UN it was leaving the agreement. Bolivia’s President, Evo Morales, is a former coca producer. On Friday Bolivia joined the agreement again, but with an exception that allows coca chewing.

This is the first time something like this has happened in UN drug agreements. Some people worry other countries will ask for changes too. Russia said the move will increase illegal cocaine trade and said it sets a “dangerous precedent” for more liberal drug rules.

The UK parliament’s Home Affairs Select Committee said the UK government should support Bolivia’s request. It said it is important for countries to stay inside the agreement. Bolivia’s return could be blocked only if one third or more of the 184 countries in the agreement oppose the request. Some people suspect the US and UK are strongly trying to persuade other countries to block Bolivia.

Nancie Prud’homme from the International Centre on Human Rights and Drug Policy criticized the organized opposition. She said the objections are legally doubtful. She said they support a rule that is too broad and ignore cultural and indigenous rights that support Bolivia’s efforts.

The ban on coca chewing was based on a 1950 UN report about the coca leaf. Supporters of drug law change say the report did not have strong evidence. In 1949, before the inquiry started, the head of the group, Howard B Fonda, said he was against coca chewing. He said it was harmful and spoke about “racial degeneration” and wanted the habit abolished.

In Bolivia, growing coca leaves is legal and licensed. Some people say this policy helped reduce cocaine production in the country. Some experts think Bolivia’s model could help other countries.
