Nelson Mandela, a famous leader in Africa’s fight for freedom, has died at age 95.

South Africa’s first black president died at home in Johannesburg. His family was with him. His health had been getting worse for years, so he stayed out of public life.

The current president, Jacob Zuma, told the country on television. He said Mandela died around 8.50pm local time and was at peace.

Zuma said the nation was very sad. He said Mandela was a great person because he was also human, and people saw in him what they wanted to be.

Zuma said Mandela helped bring South Africans together. Zuma announced a state funeral. He also ordered flags to fly at half-mast.

Archbishop Desmond Tutu led a memorial service in Cape Town. He said South Africa should try to be like Mandela.

Many world leaders spoke about Mandela. Barack Obama called him by his clan name, Madiba. Obama said Mandela gave up his own freedom to help others be free. He said Mandela changed South Africa and inspired people everywhere. Obama also said Mandela showed that people and countries can change for the better. He said Mandela set an example by handing over power and making peace with people who had jailed him.

UK prime minister David Cameron said, “A great light has gone out in the world,” and called Mandela “a hero of our time.”

FW de Klerk, the South African president who freed Mandela and shared the Nobel Peace Prize with him in 1993, said the news was very sad. He said Mandela believed in reconciliation and was a great unifier.

In South Africa, people went into the streets to remember Mandela’s life. In Soweto, people sang and danced near a house where he once lived. They stood in a circle on Vilakazi Street and sang songs from the anti-apartheid struggle. Some people wore South African flags and the green, yellow, and black colours of Mandela’s party, the African National Congress (ANC).

Mandela’s death made South Africa mourn and think deeply, almost 20 years after he helped lead the country from racial apartheid to inclusive democracy.

People around the world also felt his death strongly. Many saw him as a great statesman and a moral leader, like Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi and Martin Luther King.

After spending 27 years in prison, including 18 years on Robben Island, Mandela chose forgiveness. South Africa faced possible civil war, so he tried to make peace with the white minority and build a new democracy.

He led the ANC to win the country’s first multiracial election in 1994. After one term, he chose to step down.

Mandela was born Rolihlahla Dalibhunga in a small village in the Eastern Cape on 18 July, 1918. A teacher at his school gave him the English name Nelson.

He joined the ANC in 1943 and helped start its youth league. In 1952, he started South Africa’s first black law firm with Oliver Tambo.

Mandela was charming and liked boxing. He also joked about women noticing him.

When the ANC was banned in 1960, Mandela went underground. After the Sharpeville massacre, when police shot dead 69 black protesters, he decided to start an armed struggle. He was arrested and later charged with sabotage and trying to overthrow the government.

At the Rivonia trial in 1964, he defended himself. He said he believed in a democratic and free society where everyone could live together in harmony with equal opportunities. He said it was an ideal he hoped to achieve, and if needed, he was prepared to die for it.

He was not given the death penalty, but he was sentenced to life in prison. This was a huge blow to the ANC, but the struggle continued. Unrest grew in townships, and pressure from other countries against apartheid slowly increased.

In 1990, FW de Klerk lifted the ban on the ANC, and Mandela was released from prison. People around the world celebrated.

As president, Mandela had strong support from around the world, but there were also difficulties. After leaving top politics in 1999, he said he should have acted sooner against the spread of HIV and Aids in South Africa.

Desmond Tutu, who led the truth and reconciliation committee after apartheid ended, said Mandela rose above race and class. He said Mandela was warm, listened to others, and understood them. He said Mandela helped people believe in Africa and Africans.

Mandela sometimes appeared at ANC events and went to Jacob Zuma’s inauguration. His 91st birthday began the first yearly “Mandela Day” in his honour.

Mandela was married three times. He had six children, 17 grandchildren, and 14 great-grandchildren.
