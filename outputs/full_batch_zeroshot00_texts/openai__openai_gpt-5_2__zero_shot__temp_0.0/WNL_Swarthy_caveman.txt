Scientists studied DNA from a wisdom tooth of a hunter-gatherer from Spain who lived about 7,000 years ago. This gave them a new look at what modern humans were like before farming began.

The man had an unusual mix of traits: blue eyes, black or brown hair, and dark skin. Scientists think he probably could not digest milk well, and he may have had more trouble digesting starchy foods than later farmers.

Farming brought humans and animals closer together, so people likely developed stronger immune systems to fight infections from animals. But the man’s DNA showed he already had some gene changes that help the immune system fight bacteria and viruses. Some of these changes still exist in Europeans today.

Carles Lalueza-Fox led the study at the Institute of Evolutionary Biology in Barcelona. He said many of his early ideas about the results were wrong.

The work began after cave explorers found two skeletons in 2006 in a deep cave system in the Cantabrian Mountains in northwest Spain. The bones were well preserved by the cool cave. Both skeletons were men in their early 30s.

Carbon dating showed the remains were about 7,000 years old, from before farming spread into Europe from the Middle East. Ancient objects at the site also matched this time, including reindeer teeth with holes that were worn on clothing.

Scientists studied the better preserved skeleton. After several failed tries, they rebuilt the man’s entire genome using DNA from the root of a third molar (a wisdom tooth). This was the first complete genome from a modern European who lived before the Neolithic revolution.

The DNA results were surprising. Lalueza-Fox expected light skin, but the man had gene types linked to much darker skin. The man also had blue eyes. This was unexpected because scientists had thought the blue-eye change happened more recently than the changes for lighter skin. The results suggest blue eyes may have appeared first in Europe, while skin was becoming lighter over time during the Mesolithic period.

Because of this, artists may need to change how they draw Mesolithic people. Lalueza-Fox said many drawings show hunter-gatherers with light skin, and almost never show dark skin with blue eyes.

The team compared the ancient genome with the DNA of modern Europeans from different places. They found the closest match was with people in northern Europe, especially Sweden and Finland.

The immune-system gene changes suggest that farming did not cause all the immune gene changes found in Europeans today. Some changes seem to be older than farming. Lalueza-Fox said scientists still do not know what kinds of germs affected these people.

Martin Jones, a professor at Cambridge University, said the immune genes were the most striking result. He said there is a common story that early farming was bad for health because people lived closer to humans and animals, had fewer kinds of food, and had crowded water supplies. He said the study highlights that diseases also mattered before farming, and that is interesting.
