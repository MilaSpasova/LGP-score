Tua Pittman is a master traditional navigator from Raratonga in the Cook Islands. He says a canoe is more than transport. “The canoe is our island, the crew members are the community and the navigator is the leader,” he says. He also says it can be the other way around. “An island is our canoe, the community are the crew members and the politicians and leaders are the navigators.” He explains that navigation is not only using the stars, the moon, the sun, and the birds to travel. It is also about leadership and showing people the right way to live.

A group of four sailing canoes arrived in Sydney for the start of the World Parks Congress. Tua’s trip began in the Cook Islands on 25 September. The first part of the trip went to Samoa, then Fiji, Vanuatu, and the Gold Coast, and then south to Sydney. About 100 crew members joined at different times. They wanted to travel using only traditional navigation, but sometimes they had to use modern equipment to reach Australia in time for the Congress.

The trip is called the Mua Voyage. It is a partnership between the International Union for Conservation of Nature (IUCN) Oceania Regional Office and five Pacific Island countries: Samoa, Tonga, New Zealand, the Cook Islands, and Fiji. The main goal of the 6,000-nautical-mile (11,000km) journey was to bring a special message to the World Parks Congress.

Part of the message said the ocean is being used too much. The islanders said they do not see fish and other sea animals in the same size, variety, or number as before. They said foreign fishing fleets are taking resources quickly. They said coral reefs, mangroves, and wetlands where sea life begins are disappearing. They said the ocean is huge, but it is not endless. They also said more people in the world and unsustainable development are making the ocean less able to support life.

During the Congress, people spent a lot of time discussing how much of the ocean should be protected in marine sanctuaries. The IUCN said that in 2013, less than three per cent of the world’s oceans were in marine protected areas, and less than one per cent was “no take.” This was even though the last World Parks Congress in South Africa in 2003 had set a target of 20–30% no-take areas.

Professor Callum Roberts from the University of York helped set the 20–30% target in 2003. He said it was not enough. He said the IUCN should lift the target from 30 to 33%. He said new research supports raising the target, and that reducing efforts now would be disastrous for the oceans.

After difficult talks, the Congress delegates passed a motion that changed the goals for managing the ocean. The IUCN’s new official position is to urgently increase the ocean area that is managed well and fairly in connected systems of MPAs (marine protected areas) or other conservation measures by 2030. These should include strictly protected areas of at least 30% of each marine habitat and should protect both biodiversity and ecosystem services.

Tua Pittman was very happy that a strong decision about the oceans was passed. He said it felt like a huge reward for the hard work to come and be heard, and that it was a step in the right direction. He said the sun, moon, and stars do not change, but damage to the environment was becoming clearer during the Pacific voyage.

Tua said he is 55, and in his lifetime it has become much harder to catch fish in the open ocean. He also said pollution was getting worse, especially near big cities like Sydney. He said climate change was already seriously affecting Pacific Islanders. He said decisions made by big countries can have two, three, or four times the impact on small countries. He said many people do not even know where the islands are, but decisions made far away affect their homes.

Tua said the Mua Voyage was a very large planning job. It took years of preparation and navigation planning. The voyagers needed the world to listen and act. He said leaders of wealthy countries should think more like traditional navigators, who know their vessels are tiny in a huge sea. He said politicians must find a different route to avoid the ecological crises that are beginning to affect small island nations. “The world needs to find a different path.”
