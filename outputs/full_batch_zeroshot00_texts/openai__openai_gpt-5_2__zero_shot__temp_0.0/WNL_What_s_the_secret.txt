How long can you hold your breath? I am trying it right now. The first 30 seconds are easy. I want to stop at 45 seconds, but I keep going, and it feels easier for a short time. But when it passes one minute, I know I cannot last much longer. My heart is beating fast. I breathe out a tiny bit, and that helps. In the end, I stop. I breathe out the old air in my lungs and take a big breath in. (Then I keep taking big breaths, and my husband asks what I am doing.) I reach one minute and 12 seconds. I feel proud of myself.

Being able to hold your breath is very important in some sports, especially freediving. In 2006, I was filming a BBC series called Don’t Die Young. It was about how the lungs work. I met Sam Amps, the captain of the UK freedive team. At a pool in Bristol, she taught me simple exercises to help me hold my breath longer while swimming underwater. By the end of the session, I think I held my breath for 90 seconds. That was enough to swim one width of the pool. Sam swam three widths easily. She could hold her breath for five minutes while swimming. Five!

I asked how she did it. She said she breathed very slowly for several minutes before each dive. Then she took one big, deep breath before going under the water. She also said training helped her fight the strong need to breathe for longer than most people.

Some people have said that being able to hold your breath means humans once had a watery part in our evolution. People have even said humans can lower the heart rate and body activity to hold the breath longer. Other things—like having little body hair, where we store body fat, and walking on two legs—have also been linked to this idea. But this “aquatic ape hypothesis” does not match the scientific evidence. It may sound nice, but it is not supported.

When scientists look at breath-holding, they find that humans are not the only land mammals that can do it. (It is hard to test this in other mammals, because they do not follow instructions.) Tests also show that the heart rate does not drop when you hold your breath on land. But in cold water, it is different. Cooling the face can slow the heart rate in most people. Still, this does not prove humans had an aquatic past, because many air-breathing animals show this effect. This slower heart rate is part of what is sometimes called the “mammalian diving reflex.” These body changes can help with diving, but they can also help an animal not drown.

Even if our breath-holding is not very special compared with other animals, it is now helping in one part of medicine. Radiotherapy for breast cancer uses radiation aimed very carefully at the tumour. This can take several minutes, so it is usually done in short bursts between breaths. But if a patient can keep her chest completely still for several minutes, doctors can give the full dose in the right place all at once. The problem is that most people, like me, cannot hold their breath for much more than a minute. Doctors at University Hospital Birmingham did careful experiments. They found that if patients breathe oxygen-rich air before the breath-hold, they can hold their breath for five-and-a-half minutes.

Surprisingly, this seems to work not by tricking the body’s normal sensors for low oxygen or high carbon dioxide in the blood, but by tricking the diaphragm. The diaphragm is a main breathing muscle. When you breathe in, you tighten this muscle and pull it flat, so your chest gets bigger and air goes into your lungs. When you hold your breath, you keep the diaphragm tight like this. If oxygen is raised and carbon dioxide is lowered before the breath-hold, as in the Birmingham experiments, the diaphragm may get tired more slowly. And—though it is not helpful if you need your chest to stay perfectly still—breathing out a little lets the diaphragm relax a little and can help you hold your breath longer, just as I found. In the end, the diaphragm’s signals become too strong, and you have to stop and breathe.
