In the market square in Rjukan, there is a statue of the town’s founder, Sam Eyde. He was a famous Norwegian engineer and businessman. The statue looks north across the square at a very steep mountain.

Behind the statue, to the south, is another steep mountain called Gaustatoppen. It is 1,800 metres high. Between the mountains, in the narrow Vestfjord valley, is the small town of Rjukan. Long ago it was a strong, busy town. Eyde built it in the early years of the last century so workers could live near his factories.

Eyde used the 100-metre Rjukanfossen waterfall to make hydroelectric power. At that time, it was the biggest power plant in the world.

But Eyde could not change where the sun shines. Rjukan is deep in a valley with high mountains around it. So the town and its 3,400 people are in shadow for half the year. From late September to mid-March, it is not fully dark in the day, but it is not bright.

Now there is something new. On the mountain across from the town, 450 metres above the square, there are three big mirrors. They are solar-powered and controlled by computers. They follow the sun in the sky and reflect sunlight down to the square.

“It’s the sun!” says Ingrid Sparbo, smiling. She lifts her face to the light and closes her eyes. She is a retired secretary and has lived in Rjukan all her life. She says people get used to the shade and stop thinking about it. But the sunlight feels warm, not only on the body, but also in the mind.

Two young mothers bring their children into the square and stand in the sun. On a freezing day, an older couple sit on a new bench and smile as the warmth hits their faces. Children smile. Many people take photos. A shop assistant, Silje Johansen, says it is “awesome. Just awesome.”

Eivind Toreid, an electrical engineer, pushes his child in a buggy and is not so sure. “It’s a funny thing,” he says. “Not real sunlight, but very like it. Like a spotlight.”

Heidi Fieldheim says she heard about it on the radio. “But it’s far more than I expected,” she says. “This will bring much happiness.”

In the Nye Tider café across the road sits the person behind this idea. Martin Andersen is a 40-year-old artist who moved to Rjukan in the summer of 2001.

Andersen says he got the idea for an artwork called the Solspeil, or Sun mirror, as September went on. “Every day, we would take our young child for a walk in the buggy,” he says. “And every day, I saw we had to go a little further down the valley to find the sun.” By 28 September, the sun disappears from the market square. It does not come back until 12 March.

During the long months in between, Andersen says they could see blue sky above and the sun high on the mountain sides. But to feel it, they had to leave town. He says it is sad when people must leave a town just to feel the sun.

Twelve years after he first dreamed of the Solspeil, a German company that works with CSP, or concentrated solar power, brought in the mirrors by helicopter. There were three glass mirrors, each 17 square metres. Andersen says it took longer than they thought.

The mirrors really work. At first, many local people were against the plan. There were petitions, letters, and a Facebook page. Many people thought it was a vanity project and a waste of money. But now most of them seem to accept it.

“I was strongly against it,” says Nils Eggerud. Like others, he thought the money could be used in better ways, like more carers for old people, better schools, cycle paths, or fixing country roads.

“And I still have doubts about the maintenance costs,” he says. “What will they be, and who will pay? But it does feel nice standing here. And you just have to look at people’s faces.”

In his office looking over the square, Rjukan’s young mayor, Steinar Bergsland, thinks about the benefits. He is less focused on the cost.

Bergsland says more visitors are coming at this time of year. Shopkeepers say they are earning more money too. A hi-tech company is thinking about moving to Rjukan because of the new technology on the mountain and the attention it has brought.

“This is a powerful symbol for Rjukan,” Bergsland says. With some government grants and a donation from a local business, the town only had to pay 1m krone, or £100,000, out of the total 5m-krone cost.

Bergsland says that compared with the town’s 650m krone yearly budget, 1m krone was not much. He says the mirrors can help the town raise money for better schools and more nursing care. He tells people to look out of the window at the happy faces. Now that the mirrors are here, he says, people love them.
