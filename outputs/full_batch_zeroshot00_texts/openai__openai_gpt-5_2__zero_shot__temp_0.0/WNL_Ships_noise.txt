New research says noise from passing ships may bother killer whales and dolphins more than people thought. The noise can make it harder for them to talk to each other and to find food. People have known that the low rumble of ships can disturb large whales. But US researchers also found strong ship noise at medium and high pitches. This includes 20,000Hz, the pitch where killer whales, also called orcas, hear best.

This noise may make it harder for killer whales to communicate and to use echolocation. Echolocation is when an animal uses sound to bounce off objects, like prey, to find where they are. Dolphins and porpoises may have the same problem because they also use high pitches.

The findings may matter a lot for the endangered killer whales that live near shipping lanes. Only 84 of these killer whales feed along the US west coast and into Puget Sound.

Scott Veirs of Beamreach led the research. He said even a small increase in noise can make echolocation harder for whales. This is a worry because their prey, chinook salmon, is already scarce. He said hearing a small click from a salmon is very hard, and it is even harder when there is a lot of noise.

The researchers used underwater microphones to measure noise from about 1,600 ships as they passed through Haro Strait in Washington State. The study lasted two years. It recorded sounds from 12 kinds of vessels, including cruise ships, container ships, and military vehicles. About 20 ships a day passed through the strait.

Some ships were quieter than others. On average, the noise next to the ships was 173 underwater decibels. That is like 111 decibels in air, about as loud as a loud rock concert. Whales are usually not right next to ships. They would more likely hear about 60 to 90 decibels, like a lawnmower or a vacuum cleaner.

Veirs said scientists already knew underwater noise affects baleen whales, which include the largest animals on Earth. But this study shows a threat to smaller whales, dolphins, and porpoises too. He said ships were thought to mostly make low-pitch noise, like lorries or trains. Most noise is low, but ship noise can also raise the background noise of the ocean at high pitches. This could be a serious problem that needs more study.

A noisy ocean can cause other problems. Whales may need to swim closer together so they can hear each other. If they cannot find prey as well, they may need to use up extra blubber. This is a problem because blubber can hold manmade pollutants. These pollutants can be toxic to whales if they are released into the body.

Veirs said more work is needed to learn how much the noise is hurting whales and how to make ships quieter near these animals. He said it should be easy to reduce noise pollution. He said military ships are much quieter, and their technology could be used for commercial ships. Another way is to slow down. He said dropping speed by six knots could cut noise intensity by half.

Some whale species, like humpbacks and blue whales, have done better as whaling has gone down. But other whales are still in danger for many reasons. The US federal government recently protected nearly 40,000 square miles of the Atlantic to help avoid losing the critically endangered North Atlantic right whale, which has only about 500 individuals left.

In Europe, killer whales have dangerously high levels of banned PCB chemicals in their blubber. Scientists are still trying to find out if pollutants caused the deaths of five sperm whales that stranded on the east coast of Britain in January 2016. Around the coast of Australia, whales face more danger from ship strikes and oil and gas drilling. Japan has also pledged to resume whaling in Antarctic waters.
