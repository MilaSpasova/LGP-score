An octopus escaped from the National Aquarium in New Zealand. It got out of its tank, slid down a 50-metre drainpipe, and reached the sea.

The octopus was named Inky. He is a common New Zealand octopus. Workers think the tank lid was left a little open by mistake. In the middle of the night, when no one was there, Inky climbed out of the tank. He went down the side and moved across the aquarium floor.

Rob Yarrell, a manager at the aquarium in Napier, said octopuses are good at escaping. He said Inky was not unhappy or lonely, because octopuses like to be alone. He said Inky was very curious and wanted to see what was outside.

One idea is that Inky slid three or four metres across the floor and went into a drainpipe that led to the sea. The pipe was 50 metres long and opened into Hawke’s Bay, on the east coast of New Zealand’s North Island.

Another idea is that Inky squeezed into an open pipe at the top of his tank. This pipe went under the floor and into the drain. Yarrell said he was surprised when they came in the next morning and the tank was empty. He said the staff felt sad, but Inky often surprised them. Yarrell has not started a search for Inky.

Reiss Jenkinson, an exhibits keeper, said he was sure no one took Inky. He said he knows how octopuses act. He said he has seen octopuses on boats slip through bilge pumps. He also said the security is too strong for someone to take Inky.

Octopuses have no bones, so they can fit into very small spaces. They have been filmed squeezing through gaps the size of coins. Octopuses are also very smart and can use tools. In Wellington, at the Island Bay Marine Education Centre, one octopus used to visit another tank at night to steal crabs, then go back to its own tank. Another octopus there, named Ozymandias, was thought to have opened a jar very fast before it was released into the ocean.

A local fisherman brought Inky to the aquarium years ago. The fisherman found Inky caught in a crayfish pot. Yarrell said Inky looked rough and had scars and shortened limbs. He said Inky had been living on a reef and fighting with fish, so he was not in good shape. Yarrell said Inky was about the size of a rugby ball and was unusually intelligent. He was friendly, curious, and popular with visitors. The aquarium has another octopus named Blotchy, but Blotchy is smaller, and Inky had a bigger personality.

The aquarium does not plan to add more security because Inky’s escape was a one-off. But the staff now pay more attention to what octopuses can do. The aquarium is not looking for a new Inky, but it might take another octopus if a fisherman brought one in. Yarrell said there is always a chance Inky could come back.
