US researchers say that the government’s large collection of phone records is a clear danger to the privacy of ordinary people. They showed that even simple phone logs can help identify people and reveal private details about their lives.

Two scientists at Stanford University used anonymous “metadata” about calls and texts. This metadata did not include what people said or wrote. It only showed things like which numbers people contacted and when. Even so, the scientists figured out people’s names, where they lived, and the names of their partners.

The same data also helped them find very personal information. They found that one man owned a rifle. They found that another person had recently been told they had an irregular heartbeat. Other data suggested a new pregnancy and a person with multiple sclerosis.

The study shows how powerful phone metadata can be, especially when it is combined with public information from Google, Yelp, and Facebook. Phone metadata does not have the same legal protections as the actual content of messages and calls. Security services have known for a long time that this data is valuable. After Edward Snowden’s disclosures, Stewart Baker, former general counsel at the US National Security Agency (NSA), said: “Metadata absolutely tells you everything about somebody’s life.”

Patrick Mutchler, a computer security researcher at Stanford, said the public does not understand how powerful metadata is because few studies have shown how much it can reveal. He said this makes it harder for people who strongly oppose these programmes to fight them. He said the new study gives strong evidence that did not exist before.

For the study, the researchers recruited 823 people. The people agreed to let the researchers collect metadata from their phones using an Android app. The app also collected information from their Facebook accounts, which the scientists used to check their results. In total, the researchers collected metadata for more than 250,000 calls and over 1.2m texts.

In the NSA’s metadata system, analysts were first allowed to look up to three “hops” away from a person. A hop means one step in a chain of calls: a call from the target person to another number is one hop; from that number to another is two hops; and so on. Analysts could also look at records going back five years. Later, the rules were changed to only two hops and a maximum of 18 months.

Writing in the journal Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, Mutchler explained how he and another graduate student, Jonathan Mayer, found a lot of personal information using little money. With computer searches and manual searches, they identified 82% of people’s names. They also found the names of businesses people called. When they put these business locations on a map, they saw groups of nearby businesses. The scientists guessed these groups were near the person’s home. Using this method, they named the city where people lived 57% of the time, and they were almost 90% accurate in placing people within 50 miles of home. Mutchler said some mistakes may have happened because some people did not update Facebook after moving, such as moving out of their parents’ home to go to college.

Next, the scientists looked at people’s personal relationships. They used a simple computer program to study call patterns and guess who was in a relationship. After they knew someone had a partner, they said it was easy to identify who that partner was.

In the last part of the study, the researchers tried to see what sensitive information they could get from metadata. They looked at calls to and from a list of organizations, including hospitals, pharmacies, religious groups, legal services, firearms retailers, and repair firms. From this, they put together detailed stories about people’s lives.

For one person, the metadata showed a long call from a cardiology center, a short call with a medical laboratory, many short calls from a local pharmacy, and calls to a hotline for abnormal heart-rate monitoring devices. For another person, the metadata showed frequent calls to a local gun supplier that sold semiautomatic rifles, and later several long calls to a customer support hotline for a major gun manufacturer that produced the rifles. For two other people, the metadata suggested one had multiple sclerosis and the other had just become pregnant.

Mutchler said this shows what two graduate students can do with limited resources. He said the results should make policymakers think carefully before approving mass surveillance programmes. The scientists wrote that large metadata surveillance programmes, like the NSA’s, will expose highly confidential information about ordinary citizens. They added that future policymaking should be guided by relevant sciences to balance national security and civil liberties.

Ross Anderson, a professor of security engineering at Cambridge University, said the study gives numbers that people can use in discussions. He said that with the right analysis on nation-scale communications data, it is possible to guess huge amounts of sensitive information about everyone. He said people had suspected this, but now there is data to show it.
