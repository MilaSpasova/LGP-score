It is hard to think of many things worse than walking in the sunshine on Rio’s Copacabana beach on the way to watch a World Cup match. So England fan Anthony McDowell from Liverpool did not agree with the bad news people had talked about before the tournament in Brazil. “The place is lovely. The people are great. There’s a party atmosphere,” McDowell said. “The only thing that could be better is the England team.”

McDowell and about six friends were among many fans from around the world. They turned the beach into a party area with flags, colours, and songs. Some people danced, took photos, or drank. Most people just walked around and talked about football, waiting for the next game on the nearby FanFest big screen. The last time so many people were there during the day was when the pope was visiting.

The happy and mostly peaceful mood was very different from the protests, transport problems, and stadium troubles before the World Cup. Now that the football has started, visiting fans want to enjoy it. “If I had known, when I started planning, how complicated and costly it would be, I wouldn’t have come. But, now that we’re here, it’s great,” said Brian Hill, a retired chief executive from Sunderland.

The trip has had problems. Hill travelled for more than 20 hours to reach Rio. Then there were long delays on the road from the airport at 6.30am. His son, Andrew, had his sunglasses stolen almost as soon as he sat on the beach. They were also surprised that many bars did not have big screens for the games. But they, like many fans, said they loved the atmosphere, even if the planning and travel were difficult. They said the tournament has started in a spectacular way on the pitch.

Even people who usually complain may have been excited by Robin van Persie’s diving header for the Netherlands against Spain. There have also been many goals: 28 in the first eight games, almost three times as many as at the same stage in South Africa in 2010. Also, Latin-American teams won every game they played up until the time of writing.

So far, the tournament has avoided the worst “Doomsday” predictions, but it is not free of trouble. The stadiums were finished late, and some were not fully finished. But there were no reports of building problems or trouble getting into the stadiums since the kick

Like other World Cups, ticketing has been a problem. There were many empty seats at some games, including the Netherlands against Spain. FIFA spokesman Saint-Clair Milesi confirmed that only 48,000 of the 51,900 seats were filled. FIFA is also investigating security problems after Chilean fans set off fireworks during their win over Australia.

The Globo newspaper listed several problems in the 12 host cities. Almost all had worse traffic than normal. The worst transport problems were in Natal, where bus drivers have been on strike since 12 June. In Salvador, some trips took five times longer than usual. “Traffic was already bad but this week it is chaotic,” Jecilda Mello, president of the residents’ group the Association of Friends of the Historic Centre, told the paper.

But protests have become smaller since the opening day. On that day, small protests happened in several cities, and police used tear gas and pepper spray. After that, the main security problems were small theft and fans getting too excited. A street party of Argentinian fans was broken up with pepper spray after the fans blocked roads.

Because Brazil is so large, fans had very different experiences in different places. The tournament did not change São Paulo, South America’s biggest city, very much. But far away in Manaus, the remote Amazonian city where England played Italy, visitors said World Cup fever was strong. Streets were decorated, and flags were on many cars.

The FA chairman, Greg Dyke, said the change in feeling was easy to notice. “We’ve had a really warm welcome in Manaus. It’s a big thing for them, even if it is a bit strange to spend so much on a stadium with no one to play in it. But we were in São Paulo for four or five days before the opening match and you wouldn’t have known until the last day that there was even a World Cup on. It was weird.”
