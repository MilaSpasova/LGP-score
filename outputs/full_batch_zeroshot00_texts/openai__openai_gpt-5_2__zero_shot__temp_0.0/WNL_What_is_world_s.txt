If we ignore big disasters like volcanoes, hurricanes, and earthquakes, two natural things can slowly damage a city or even make it disappear: water and sand.

If the Earth were getting colder, ice would also be a danger. A huge glacier can crush a city as it moves down a valley. In 2013, a rare “ice tsunami” hit the Canadian town of Ochre Beach. Wind blew a wall of melting ice from Dauphin Lake onto the shore, and it broke every house in its way.

But Ochre Beach was unusual. In most places, melting ice makes cities less safe because it raises sea levels. A century ago, Venice used to flood about ten times a year. Now its lowest point, Piazza San Marco (only three feet above sea level), is flooded about 100 times each year.

Sea levels are not the only problem. In many places, the land is also sinking. In Venice, the ground under the city was squeezed down because of industry in the area. This lowered the city by 20 cm between 1950 and 1970. Ho Chi Minh City in Vietnam is sinking by about 2 cm a year. Jakarta is sinking much faster, by 10 to 20 cm each year. In the last 30 years, Jakarta has sunk about four metres, so its 40-year-old sea wall works less well.

Jakarta has pumped out a lot of groundwater to support its people. The land above dries out and packs down, making the city like a bowl. Rivers that once flowed down through the city to the sea have had to be moved, because they cannot drain uphill.

There are many plans to save Venice. Ho Chi Minh City and Jakarta are taking the problem seriously too. But in Miami, politicians will not admit the city has a serious environmental problem.

Miami has three big problems. It is less than ten feet above sea level. More tropical storms are flooding it. And it is built on porous limestone, which soaks up seawater. The water fills the foundations and comes up through drains and pipes. This pushes sewage up and pollutes fresh water. Miami may one day become unfit for people to live in.

In the Maldives, people on whole islands are thinking about leaving their homes. The capital, Male, has a population of 153,379 and is only four feet above sea level. With Japanese investment, it built a ten-foot sea wall that cost $63 million. But in the long term, only stopping sea levels from rising will save Male and the other islands.

The sea is not the only threat. Heavy rain is also putting cities in danger. In Detroit, one morning recently brought as much rain as an entire summer. The old sewer system could not handle it. Detroit became poorer after America’s car industry collapsed, and it is struggling to build a better system. Drinking water is also affected because Detroit is very vulnerable to germs carried by water.

In Africa, desertification is making the Sahara Desert spread south by 30 miles each year. This threatens settlements in northern Mauritania. In the last 20 years, the desert grew by more than 260 acres around Chinguetti, a trading and religious centre. Its population fell from 20,000 in the mid-1900s to only a few thousand now. Trading has almost stopped because sand is piling up in the streets.

In the United States, the resort city of Rancho Mirage near Palm Springs may have to be left within the next ten years. This problem is caused less by global warming and more by people growing too much in a place with limited resources. In 1870, California had about half a million people, so farming the desert seemed possible. Now California has 38 million people. Together they own 32 million vehicles, and they expect lawns and golf courses.

In Rancho Mirage, people use more than 200 gallons of water per person each day. This has helped create a human-made drought. The city has cut water use by 25%, but this may not be enough. The resort is surrounded by sand and dust. In the long run, some desert cities in California may be abandoned, while people move into other cities.

When people leave a city, it is a final sign that the city is falling apart. If we stop caring for a city, nature quickly takes over. Japan’s Hashima Island became a concrete city in 1887 to mine coal under the sea. In 1974 the mine closed as the coal ran out, and the island was abandoned. Now sea spray is making the concrete walls fall down.

Fire is also a growing danger to cities in America. After severe storms, forest fires cause the most damage. Between 1953 and 2014, 800 major fire disasters were declared there. A report by the USDA Forest Service shows more urban places that could be hit by wildfire.

In Australia, long droughts are making trees easy to burn. Some resorts in Victoria and several suburbs of Melbourne are on a list of the state’s 52 most vulnerable bushfire places. A single forest fire is unlikely to destroy a whole city, but many fires over time could make a city unfit to live in. Abandonment is one of the strongest causes of city collapse.

Many cities are losing their fight against nature. Can we name the most vulnerable big city in the world? Natural events are hard to predict, but Male looks especially at risk. Even if its sea wall keeps working, the islands around the Maldives capital are likely to disappear before long. If they disappear, Male loses its reason for being there.
