Brazil had one of its biggest protest nights in many years. More than 100,000 people went into the streets across the country. They were angry about tough police actions, bad public services, and the high cost of the World Cup.

Big protests happened in Rio de Janeiro, São Paulo, Brasilia, Belem, Belo Horizonte, Salvador, and other places. The protests began peacefully, but some turned into fights with police. Some people also set cars and buses on fire.

The number of protesters and how many cities joined showed the protests were growing fast. Earlier protests were smaller and were about higher bus prices. People said police reacted too strongly, using rubber bullets, tear gas, and violent beatings.

The protests happened as the Confederations Cup started, a test event for the World Cup. Many different kinds of people joined. They were upset about rising costs, poor public services, big spending on sports events, low health care standards, and worries about inequality and corruption.

In Rio, most protests were peaceful. But some police were hurt in clashes at the city’s legislative assembly. At least one car was flipped over and burned. Windows were broken at banks and notary offices.

Later that night, a large crowd set several fires outside the legislative assembly, broke the building’s windows, and wrote graffiti that said “Revolution”, “Down with Paes, down with Cabral [the mayor and state governor]” and “Hate police”. Police inside used pepper spray, and maybe more. The Guardian saw one protester passed out and bleeding badly from a wound in the upper arm.

Protesters had many reasons. Oscar José Santos, 19, from a group of hooded youths from the Rocinha favela, said: “We are here because we hate the government. They do nothing for us.”

Nadia al Husin said: “I’m an architect but I have been unemployed for six months. There must be something wrong with this country.” She held a banner asking the government to do more for education.

In Brasilia, a smaller group broke through police lines and entered the high-security area of the national congress. Several people climbed onto the roof.

In Belo Horizonte, police fought with protesters who tried to get past a barrier around a football stadium. The stadium was hosting a Confederations Cup match between Nigeria and Tahiti.

In Porto Alegre, people set a bus on fire. In Curitiba, protesters tried to force their way into the office of the state governor. There were also rallies in Belem, Salvador, and other places.

In São Paulo, where clashes had been very serious the week before and many people accused police of violence, large crowds gathered again. Early reports said the marches were peaceful.

Social networks helped spread news about the protests. In São Paulo, where the crowd was estimated between 30,000 and 100,000, some people carried signs saying, “We come from Facebook”.

Most protesters were young, and for many it was their first very large rally. Student Thiago Firbida said: “My generation has never experienced this. Since the dictatorship, Brazilians never bothered to take over the streets. They did not believe they had a reason to. But now Brazil is once again in crisis, with a constant rise in prices, so people are finally reacting.”

Some people compared Brazil’s protests to rallies in Turkey and other places. A few protesters wore Guy Fawkes masks, linked to Anonymous and the Occupy Wall Street protests.

People called the protests the “vinegar revolution” because police arrested people for carrying vinegar to reduce the effects of tear gas. They also called it the “20-cent revolution” because of the bus price rise, and Passe Livre because of the demand for free public transport.

Some people said the protests did not feel like Brazil, but they also felt free. Deli Borsari, a 53-year-old yoga instructor, said: “Our politicians need to see the strength we have as one people. Brazilians tend to be too nice sometimes – they enjoy partying rather than protesting – but something is changing.”

After many news reports about the cost of new and rebuilt stadiums, the Confederations Cup became an important focus of the protests. Before the opening match in Brasilia, riot police pushed crowds away. Video showed scared Japanese supporters running away with their children. People heard the sound of shots, maybe rubber bullets or tear gas.

Another march near Rio’s Maracana Stadium faced a similar strong police response.

Most rallies seemed peaceful at first, until they met security forces. These forces are mostly organized by region.

President Dilma Rousseff accepts the protests, her aides said. Helena Chagas from the president’s office said: “The president believes peaceful protests are legitimate and proper for a democracy, and that it is natural for young people to demonstrate.”

However, people booed the president at the opening ceremony of the Confederations Cup. The economy was doing badly and social unrest was growing. She faced a serious political challenge, now and in 2014, when Brazil would host the World Cup and also have a presidential election.
