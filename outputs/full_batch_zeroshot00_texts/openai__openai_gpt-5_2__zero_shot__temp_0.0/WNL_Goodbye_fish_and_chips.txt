In homes and cafes in the UK, tea, baked beans on toast, and fish and chips have been important foods for a long time. But a survey shows that British tastes have changed over time. The National Food Survey began in 1940, after the government worried about health and having enough food.

Even though people think the British love tea, people drink much less tea than before. Since the 1970s, tea use has fallen from 68g per person each week to 25g. A teabag or small amount of loose tea weighs about 3g. This means people now drink about eight cups of tea a week, down from 23 cups in 1974. Tea is still the most common hot drink in the UK, but households now spend more money on coffee.

The data was published by the Department for Environment, Food and Rural Affairs. It comes from 150,000 households in the survey from 1974 to 2000, plus information from 2000 to 2014. The data shows some healthier changes, like more low-calorie soft drinks, more skimmed milk instead of whole milk, and more fresh fruit. But people now eat a lot more chips, pizza, crisps, and ready meals each week.

People have also changed from white bread to brown and wholemeal bread. But they eat less bread overall: about 25 slices a week in the past, and about 15 slices a week now, using a 40g slice. People also eat fewer baked beans in sauce, down by a fifth. At the same time, other easy foods have become more common, especially Italian foods. Adults now eat about 75g of pizza each week, compared with none in 1974. Pasta eating has almost tripled.

Fresh potatoes are eaten much less. Since 1974, they have dropped by 67%. In 1974, adults ate about 188g of fresh potatoes each day. Other vegetables, like cucumbers, courgettes, aubergines, and mushrooms, are eaten more now. Takeaway food has almost doubled since 1974, from 80g per person each week to 150g. About 33g of this is chips and 56g is meat. Popular meats include kebabs (10g), chicken (7g), burgers (5g), and “meat-based meals” (32g).

Some changes look healthier. Fruit eating, fresh and processed, has gone up by 50% since 1974. In 2014, adults ate about 157g of fruit each day. That is almost two portions out of the government’s five-a-day advice. Bananas have been the most popular fruit since 1996. In 2014, adults ate 221g of bananas each week, more than apples (131g) and oranges (48g). In 2014, low-calorie soft drinks were half of all soft drinks for the first time.

The survey also shows social changes. Questions about owning chickens and getting your own eggs ended in 1991. In the same year, they also stopped asking the “housewife” to fill out the form. People in the UK now spend a smaller part of their pay on food: 11% today, compared with 24% in 1974.

The UK Environment Secretary, Elizabeth Truss, said this data shows what people ate 40 years ago and how culture changes led to big changes in food. She said shoppers now care more about where food comes from, and the internet can bring good food to homes quickly. She also said pop-up restaurants show new trends, and foods from around the world are now common, like fish and chips.

She also said that sharing this data helps people see when habits changed. She said there is still much more to learn from the National Food Survey. She looks forward to seeing the data used for things like local food maps, school projects, and predicting new food trends, and to help the UK food and farming industry in the future.
