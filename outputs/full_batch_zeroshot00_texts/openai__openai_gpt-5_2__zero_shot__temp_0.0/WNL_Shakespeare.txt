Many people around the world celebrate William Shakespeare. London also says he belongs to them. But Stratford-upon-Avon plans to spend 2016 celebrating him as their own man. Shakespeare was born in Stratford-upon-Avon in 1564, and he died there 400 years ago.

Paul Edmondson works at the Shakespeare Birthplace Trust. He says Stratford was very important to Shakespeare all his life. Some people think Shakespeare left Stratford, went to London to get rich, and only came back to die. But Edmondson says Stratford is where Shakespeare bought land and property. It is where he kept his library. It is where he lived, read, and thought. Stratford plans to remind people that Shakespeare was a man of Stratford.

A writer named John Aubrey wrote a diary in the 1600s. He was born 11 years after Shakespeare died. Aubrey said Shakespeare was not very special. Aubrey also said Shakespeare acted very well and that people liked his plays. But the world did not agree with Aubrey. People will mark the anniversary of Shakespeare’s death across Britain and around the world. Shakespeare is the most famous and most performed playwright in the world.

There will be many events. Macbeth will open in Singapore, and Romeo and Juliet in Brussels. Shakespeare’s Globe is finishing the first world tour in theatre history. It has taken Hamlet to almost every country. North Korea has not joined yet. In London, they are also making a pop-up cinema with 37 screens. Each screen will show one of Shakespeare’s plays. It will be along the South Bank.

The National Theatre, the Royal Shakespeare Company, and almost every theatre company in the country will mark the anniversary. Some shows will be very traditional. Others will be different, like Spymonkey’s Complete Deaths. It is a funny show about the 74 deaths in Shakespeare’s plays, or 75 if you count a fly squashed in Titus Andronicus. The deaths happen in many ways, like stabbing, poisoning, smothering, and smashing. There will also be hundreds of lectures, recitals, international academic conferences, films, concerts, operas, and big exhibitions.

Even though Shakespeare was famous while he was alive, there are not many documents about his life. His plays might not have survived without his friends and fellow actors. They collected every piece they could find, like drafts, prompt scripts, scribbled actors’ parts, and 17 plays not known in any other version. They put them into the First Folio, published in 1623, seven years after Shakespeare’s death.

Actor Mark Rylance has called the First Folio his favourite book in the world. Most of the surviving First Folios will be on display. These include copies from the British Library and the Bodleian Library, and a torn copy recently found in France. Some very important documents will be shown at Somerset House in London. This exhibition is organized by the National Archives and King’s College London. It will include four of Shakespeare’s six known signatures, and they are all a little different.

The exhibition, By Me, William Shakespeare, will include his will. It will also include court papers about a bold move. Shakespeare and his fellow actors took apart a theatre on the north side of the Thames and rebuilt it as the Globe on the South Bank. The exhibition will also show records of payments from the royal treasury for Boxing Day performances for James I and Queen Anne.

Dominic Dromgoole, the outgoing Globe director, joked that Shakespeare was a true Londoner. He also admitted Stratford-upon-Avon had “some spurious claim.” But Stratford says the town made and educated Shakespeare. His old school room is being restored with a £1.4m Heritage Lottery grant. It will open as a permanent visitor attraction.

Shakespeare bought New Place, a very fine house and the second best house in the town. He died there. A story says he died on St George’s Day, 23 April, the same day as his birth. Paul Edmondson said you do not buy a house like New Place and not live there. He says many people have underestimated how important Stratford was to Shakespeare.

Edmondson believes that after Shakespeare bought the house in 1597, all his thinking time was spent there. He thinks the late plays, including The Tempest, were at least planned in Shakespeare’s library and probably written there. The Shakespeare Birthplace Trust calls New Place “the jewel in the crown of the 400th anniversary celebrations.” But today, New Place is really a big empty space.

Shakespeare’s house was pulled down 300 years ago. The next house, which may have used some of the old building, was knocked down in 1759. A clergyman named Francis Gastrell did it during an argument about taxes. Before that, he cut down Shakespeare’s mulberry tree. People said Shakespeare sat under it and worked. Gastrell was annoyed by tourists looking into his garden.

The empty space in Stratford has never been filled. But a five-year archaeology project studied the site. News spread around the world that Shakespeare’s kitchen was found in cellars that still partly survive. For the anniversary, the site is being shown again. The foundations will be marked, and the garden will be restored. Edmondson said, “Without Stratford, there would have been no Shakespeare.”
