He is not the first person to doubt Mars One. Mars One is a very big private plan to send people to live on Mars from 2025. But Joseph Roche is different from most critics because he is on the shortlist of possible astronauts.

Roche is an astrophysicist at Trinity College Dublin. In February, Mars One said he was one of 100 people still in line for the mission. He wrote in the Guardian that he has serious doubts that Mars One can work.

Roche says the selection process was not strict enough, compared with normal astronaut programs. He also says the Dutch Mars One team is being naïve if they think they can succeed on their own. The mission is supposed to cost $6bn, and he says it is now very unlikely to happen.

He says Mars One should be more open and transparent. He also says there are problems with the selection process, and that Mars One does not want to work with the scientific community. He thinks Mars One should admit this plan is not likely and instead support other space missions that are more realistic.

Roche also worries about how the organizers promoted a “top ten” list of candidates. He says this ranking did not mean these were the most likely astronauts. Instead, it was based on how many “supporter points” each person got, such as by buying official merchandise.

He says these “supporter points” show how much support someone gives to Mars One’s mission. He says the points do not affect the astronaut selection. They only show how much money supporters have donated to Mars One.

Mars One’s timeline says they plan to send a lander and a satellite to Mars in 2018, then a rover in 2020, and cargo missions starting in 2022. People would start arriving in 2025. Crews of four would go every two years to build the settlement. They would not return to Earth.

In February, a well-known supporter, Gerard ’t Hooft, a Dutch Nobel Prize winner in physics, said he did not think this schedule was realistic. He said it would take much longer and cost much more. He said that when they first asked him to join, he told them: “You have to put a zero after everything.”

Roche also spoke to Medium, a US blogging platform that has also shown strong doubt about Mars One. Medium reported that the claimed 200,000 astronaut applications were actually 2,761.

Roche explained the selection process to Medium. He said he has not met anyone from Mars One in person. At first, Mars One said there would be regional interviews, with travel, several days of interviews, and tests. He thought that sounded closer to a real astronaut selection.

But later, he said they made candidates sign a non-disclosure agreement to be interviewed. Then the plan changed from a multi-day regional interview to a ten-minute Skype call.

Roche told the Guardian he did not want to do more interviews because he did not want to seem too negative about space travel.

In his article, he said he loves pushing the limits of science, and that is why Mars One’s big idea attracted him. He said Mars One was never likely to overcome the money and technology problems in the time they planned. Still, he said it was interesting to hear a new idea that makes people think about the future of space exploration.

He also said being part of the public debate about the ethics and morality of future missions has been one of the most interesting and enjoyable parts of being a candidate. He said if a one-way mission to Mars ever became possible, he would volunteer. But he said this does not matter much, because he does not think we will see a one-way mission in his lifetime.
