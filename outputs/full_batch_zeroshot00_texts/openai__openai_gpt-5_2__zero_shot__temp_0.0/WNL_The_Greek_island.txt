Agios Efstratios is a very remote Greek island. Banks and the government do not pay much attention to it. The mobile phone network cannot use data, and there is no ATM or credit-card machine on the island.

Before Greece had big money problems, people on this quiet island did fine. They did banking at the post office. Every summer, the few dozen rooms to rent were full. Visitors came because they heard from other people about the empty beaches, clear sea, and fresh seafood.

But the island still uses cash, so the closing of banks across Greece hurt it a lot. People had to travel nine hours there and back to the nearest big island to get cash. Greek visitors say they cannot get enough money to come. Mayor Maria Kakali said tourist numbers are down 80% this year. The main village has about 200 people. She said even people who were born on the island and now live in Athens are not coming, even if they have their own homes on the island.

Kakali kept pushing the government and a major Greek bank, and they promised an ATM within weeks. But she thinks it may be too late for this tourist season. Tourism is much bigger than the island’s other two ways to earn money: fishing and agriculture. She said there are almost no reservations in August, when normally people call and ask for a room and there are none left.

This winter might be a little better because 50 workers will stay in the village to expand the harbour. But a bigger problem may come soon. The government said it will end an old tax break for islands.

This lower sales tax was made long ago to help islands. It helped because everything had to be brought in, and it helped tourism be cheaper. Popular islands like Mykonos worry they will not be able to compete with Turkey without the tax break. For Agios Efstratios, it is an even bigger danger. Kakali said if they have to pay a tax of 23%, everyone will die on the island.

Food and fuel already cost more than on the mainland. The island is small, with only three shops, two restaurants, and no official hotel, even in summer. Provatas Costas, a 58-year-old fisherman, said it is an expensive island. He said even milk or bread is bought and sold three or four times before it reaches them, and each middleman takes a profit.

For Agios Efstratios and the nearby larger island of Lemnos, the timing is especially cruel. For years they were less well known partly because the ferries were slow and unreliable. In 2015, the government finally gave the ferry contract to a new, efficient company. More visitors started coming to see the islands, but then the bank controls began.

Atzamis Konstantinos, a travel agent in Lemnos, said it started as the best season in 30 years and, in one week, became the worst. He used to earn €15,000 a month as a captain of petroleum tankers and other large ships, but he came home because he missed Lemnos. He said he always loved the island. When he docked in Piraeus, the port in Athens, he would take his pay and then take the first boat to Lemnos. People called him crazy, but he only wanted to come there.

Lemnos has many wild beaches where you can be almost alone, even in the busiest part of summer. It also has a small nightlife and many cultural sites. It is the eighth largest island in Greece, so it may get the first tax increase in autumn 2015, but it is not as rich as many smaller islands. It has a little over 3,000 beds for visitors, compared with tens of thousands on Rhodes. Its councillors say they will fight the tax rise, but they do not know how. Lemnos Mayor Dimitris Marinakis said that when there is not enough money, people buy less, and then the whole economy gets worse.

The crisis has been very hard on people under 40. Many do two or three jobs, but still cannot afford a family. Young people fear a new wave of people leaving, like the one that sent their grandparents’ friends to America, Australia, and Canada. Katerina Fikari said she is very lucky to work for the local government in Lemnos. She asked what the future is for their generation. She said if you only work to pay bills, it is hard to have dreams.

Mayor Kakali warned that if taxes go up, even more young people will leave. She has used her year in office to improve education on the island, to help families want to stay. Because Agios Efstratios is one of the smallest islands, it has until 2017 before the tax rise is planned, so she hopes Greek politics might still stop it. If not, she plans to go to Athens to remind the government what the tax rise would cost.

She said the government does not pay much attention to the islands of the north Aegean. She said she would take all the school children to the gates of parliament to tell them: “There is still life in these islands”.
