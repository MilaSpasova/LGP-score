Cities do not usually pick up and move. But Kiruna, a town in northern Sweden in the Arctic, must move. If it does not, the ground could sink and the town could fall into the earth.

Krister Lindstedt from White, an architecture firm in Stockholm, says the town has a hard choice. A big iron-ore mine is eating away the ground under the streets. If the mine stops, many people will lose jobs. If the town does not move, it could be destroyed.

Kiruna was started in 1900 by the state-owned mining company Luossavaara-Kiirunavaara (LK). The town became rich because of the iron ore under it. Deputy Mayor Niklas Siren says the town exists because of the mine.

Kiruna is 145 km inside the Arctic Circle. Winters are very harsh. There is no sunlight in winter, and average temperatures are below -15C. But people stayed because the magnetite ore is valuable. The mine is the world’s largest underground iron-ore mine. It produces 90% of Europe’s iron, enough to build more than six Eiffel Towers a day. Demand is still growing.

In 2004, the mining company told the town that it was in danger. As the mine digs toward the city at a 60-degree angle, the ground will sink. Buildings could crack and collapse. Ten years later, cracks are starting to appear in the ground and are getting closer to town.

Viktoria Walldin, a social anthropologist working with the architects, says people have been waiting and worrying for ten years. Many have not made big choices, like buying a house, redecorating, having a child, or opening a business.

After years of delay, the city showed its main plan. Lindstedt says the town will move like a “walking millipede.” Streets and squares will slowly move east along a new main street. The town should be out of danger by 2033.

A new town square is already being built 3 km to the east. A new town hall is planned by Danish architect Henning Larsen. Twenty important buildings will be taken apart and put back together in the new place, piece by piece. The rust-red wooden church, built in 1912 and shaped like Sami teepees, will go into a new park. The cast-iron bell tower will stand again near the town hall. But not everything can be saved.

Walldin says small places also matter to people. She spoke to an old woman who walks past a bench where she had her first kiss. Places like that, or the hospital where your first child was born, help people feel who they are. Many of these places will disappear.

The project is called “the most democratic move in history.” The mining company will pay about £320m to build new facilities, like a high school, fire station, community centre, library, and swimming hall. But many people mainly worry about where they will live and how homes will be given out.

Lindstedt says these details are not decided yet. He says people are used to low rents and high incomes, but this may change. LK has agreed to pay residents the value of their homes plus 25%. Many locals say this is not enough to buy a new house at market prices.

To help decide home values, the architects studied housing rents in nearby cities for years. They also marked Kiruna homes by what they have, like size, gardens, and closeness to bus stops and the city centre. They also suggested a “Kiruna Portal,” a big place to save materials from houses that will be torn down, so they might be reused in the new town. But Sweden does not have a strong tradition of people building their own homes, so it may not happen.

The new town plan looks very different from the old Kiruna. The current town has many winding streets and separate houses with gardens. The new plan has more people living close together, in multi-storey apartment buildings around shared courtyards. The streets are straighter, and cold winds may blow strongly along them.

The architects say this is a chance for Kiruna to start again as a sustainable town. They hope it will attract young people, with new cultural places and ideas like a cable car over the main street. But many current residents may not be able to afford this new vision.
