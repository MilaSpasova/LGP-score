Europe will be the first place in the world to make car makers do “real-world” emissions tests. These new rules will check how much pollution cars make when they drive on real roads and in traffic. This is different from today’s tests, which happen in lab-like conditions.

The European Commission Vice President, Frans Timmermans, approved the plan. The tests aim to enforce a limit of 80mg of nitrogen oxide per kilometre. Researchers say only one car out of 16 meets this level. Other countries, like China and Korea, are also thinking about real-world tests, and they will watch what Europe does.

Pollution from diesel engines, such as nitrogen oxide, carbon monoxide, and tiny particles, is believed to cause at least one quarter of the 29,000 pollution-related deaths each year in the UK. This number may rise when the committee on the medical effects of air pollutants publishes “strengthening evidence” later in 2015 about harm to public health from nitrogen oxide.

The current lab test is called the “New European Drive Cycle.” It is 25 years old and has not kept up with new car technology. Studies say lab tests can be made easier in unfair ways. For example, people can tape up doors and windows to reduce air resistance, drive on very smooth roads, and test at unusually high temperatures. Campaigners also say car makers program some vehicles to go into a low-emissions mode during lab tests, when the front wheels spin but the back wheels do not.

Lucia Caudet, a Commission spokesperson, said the Commission is finishing a proposal for a new testing method that will properly check cars in real driving. The plan still needs approval from other commissioners and a technical committee, but an EU source said they do not expect major problems.

Greg Archer from Transport and Environment said a key reason why air pollution kills 400,000 citizens each year is that car makers cheat diesel tests, so cars pollute much more on the road. He said real-world tests are an important step to reduce city air pollution, and EU states should support the proposals.

Research from the International Council on Clean Transportation in 2014 said real nitrogen oxide emissions from cars are seven times higher than the 80mg/km standard. Some models are 22 times above the recommended limit. Only one car out of 16 met the target. About one third of all nitrogen oxide pollution comes from road transport, mostly diesel. In cities, this can rise as high as 64%, according to the European Environment Agency.

Campaigners say the car industry has tried to delay changes to test cycles. Industry groups deny this and say they need five years for technical and economic reasons. Cara McLaughlin from the European Automobile Manufacturers Association (ACEA) said Real Driving Emissions (RDE) is a new rule that could require big changes to emission control hardware during a car model’s production lifetime. She said ACEA accepts that RDE will apply to new types of cars from September 2017.

ACEA sent Timmermans its own draft regulation to consider. This happened after EU representatives agreed a regulation to enforce nitrogen oxide limits with stronger road trials and strict monitoring of exhaust fumes. ACEA’s draft would have covered fewer pollutants and delayed the step-by-step start until 2020. It would also have made tests shorter, from 1,300m to under 700m, raised the minimum temperature from -7C to -3C, and used more rural roads. Archer said lobbyists pushed hard, and he called it an attempt to weaken the lawmaking process.

After Timmermans seemed to reject ACEA’s proposal, the regulation will go to commissioners for a rubber stamp and then to member states for final changes, before a planned start in September. By 2017, the first real-world car emissions tests are expected to begin seriously. Because there will also be debate about CO2 testing at the end of 2015, and other countries have similar debates, many people will watch the EU’s new emissions tests closely.
