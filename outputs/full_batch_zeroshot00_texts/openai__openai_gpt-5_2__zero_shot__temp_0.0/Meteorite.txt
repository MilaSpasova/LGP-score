Galina Zaglumyonova woke up in her apartment in central Chelyabinsk because of a huge explosion. The blast blew in her balcony windows and broke clay pots with her few houseplants. When she jumped out of bed, she saw a long white trail in the morning sky. She also heard car alarms in the street.

“I didn’t understand what was going on,” Zaglumyonova said. “There was a big explosion and then many smaller ones. I first thought a plane had crashed.”

But she had really seen a ten-tonne meteorite breaking apart as it fell to Earth. It made several fireballs just after sunrise.

Officials said almost 1,200 people were hurt, and more than 40 were taken to hospital. Most were hurt by flying glass. The glass broke because of a loud sonic boom made by the meteorite as it came down. No deaths were reported.

The Russian Academy of Sciences said the meteorite entered the atmosphere at at least 33,000mph. It broke into pieces 18 to 32 miles above the ground.

People in Chelyabinsk panicked. The city has more than one million people and is south of Russia’s Ural mountains. Mobile phone networks quickly became jammed because so many people were calling. Videos from the area showed the glowing pieces getting brighter as they came closer. The trail in the sky could be seen for hundreds of miles, even in nearby Kazakhstan.

Tatyana Bets was working at the reception area of a hospital clinic in the city centre when it happened. “First we noticed the wind,” she said. “Then the room filled with a very bright light, and we saw a cloud of strange smoke in the sky.” A few minutes later, the explosions came.

The Ministry of the Interior said at least three craters were found and the military was watching them. One crater was more than six metres wide. Another piece of meteorite was said to have crashed through thick ice on a nearby lake. Local military officials said radiation levels at the impact sites were normal.

In Chelyabinsk, schools and universities were closed, and many workers were told to go home early. About 200 children were among the injured.

Many people with small injuries came to the clinic where Bets works, mostly with cuts from broken glass. Bets said a nearby college dormitory was hit especially badly, and many students were brought in because they were very scared. “Many girls were in shock,” she said. “Some were very pale and many fainted.”

Early estimates said more than 100,000 square metres of glass was broken and 3,000 buildings were hit. The total damage in the city was estimated to be over one billion roubles (£20m).

The meteorite came less than a day before asteroid 2012 DA14 was expected to pass close to Earth, about 17,510 miles away. Experts said the two events were only a coincidence.

In the first hours after the event, rumours and conspiracy theories spread. Russian state TV and local media suggested local air defence units shot at the meteorite and broke it apart more than 15 miles up.

Vladimir Zhirinovsky, the ultranationalist leader of Russia’s Liberal Democrat party, said it was not a meteorite. He said it was military action by the United States. “It’s not a meteorite falling – it’s a test of new American weapons,” he said.

Some people tried to make money from the confusion. Within hours, people were offering pieces of meteorite for sale on internet sites.

President Vladimir Putin and Prime Minister Dmitry Medvedev were told about the event. Putin held a meeting with the head of the Emergency Situations Ministry. Medvedev said at an economic forum in Siberia, “It’s proof that not only are economies vulnerable but the whole planet.”

Dmitry Rogozin, Russia’s Deputy Prime Minister and former Ambassador to NATO, wrote on Twitter that countries should work together to make a warning system for “objects of an alien origin”. He added that neither the US nor Russia could bring down such objects.
