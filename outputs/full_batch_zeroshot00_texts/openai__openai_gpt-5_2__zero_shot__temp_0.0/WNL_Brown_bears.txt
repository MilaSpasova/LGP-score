A new study says Europe’s forests, and even its suburbs, are again home to big wild predators. The study says brown bears, wolves, and lynx are doing well. Even though many people worry that these animals will die out as the human population grows, the study in the journal Science found that their numbers in Europe are stable or growing.

Brown bears, wolves, Eurasian lynx, and wolverines live in almost one-third of mainland Europe (not counting Belarus, Ukraine, and Russia). Most of these animals live outside nature reserves. This suggests that people’s attitudes are changing and that large conservation efforts are helping animals that humans have hunted and harmed for a very long time.

Bears are the most common large predator in Europe. There are about 17,000 bears, about 12,000 wolves, about 9,000 Eurasian lynx, and about 1,250 wolverines. Wolverines live only in the northern parts of Scandinavia and Finland. In mainland Europe, only Belgium, Denmark, the Netherlands, and Luxembourg, like Britain, have no breeding populations of at least one large predator species. The lead author and other conservationists said this wide spread, even in crowded places, suggests the British countryside could also support big predators.

Guillaume Chapron from Sweden’s University of Agricultural Sciences and other researchers found that some wolves live in suburban areas with up to 3,050 people per square kilometre. This is higher than the population density of Cambridge or Newcastle. On average in Europe, wolves live where there are 37 people per square kilometre, lynx where there are 21 people per square kilometre, and bears where there are 19 people per square kilometre. The Scottish Highlands have nine people per square kilometre.

Chapron said that to have wolves, people do not need to leave the land. The researchers said the return of big predators shows that a “land-sharing” conservation model can work. This means people and predators share the same areas. This is different from fencing off “wilderness” areas to keep people and predators apart, as happens in North America and Africa.

Chapron said it is not a perfect story, because living together can bring conflict. He said it is important to manage conflict, keep it low, and solve problems. He also said wolves can be hard neighbours. He said it is not only people versus predators; often it is conflict between people about predators. He said these animals represent hard questions about how people should use land.

The researchers said this “land-sharing” approach could work in other parts of the world too. They said it works in Europe because of political stability, more prey animals such as wild deer, and money to help farmers protect animals without killing predators, such as using electric fences. Chapron said the most important reason has been the EU Habitats Directive, which requires member states to protect and bring back rare species.

Chapron said that without the Habitats Directive, this recovery might not have happened. He said the recovery shows that if people want to protect nature and make strong laws like the Habitats Directive, wildlife protection can succeed.

The return of these animals was welcomed by author and commentator George Monbiot, who is starting Rewilding Britain. This is a new charity that encourages the return of wild landscapes and extinct species.

Monbiot said it is good to see numbers rising, but that Britain is unusual. He said Britain has lost more large mammals than any country except Ireland. He said that apart from the accidental return of boar, Britain has done very little. In much of Europe, bears, lynx, and wolves are coming back. He said this is a big change after centuries of persecution.

The survey found Eurasian lynx living permanently in 11 population groups across 23 European countries. Only five of these were native populations. This shows that reintroduction has worked. Monbiot said support is growing to reintroduce lynx to the Cairngorms in Scotland.

Monbiot said that if it works in the rest of Europe, there is no reason it cannot work in the UK. He pointed out that bears and wolves live within an hour of Rome. He said there is no population reason why wildlife cannot return in a similar way in the UK.
