Harvey Goldsmith, a top British rock promoter and manager, says the time of very big summer music festivals in Britain, including Glastonbury, may be ending.

Goldsmith has worked with many famous stars, such as the Who, the Rolling Stones, Queen, Madonna, Bob Dylan, and Luciano Pavarotti. He says the biggest problem is that there are not enough new, major bands to replace the older ones.

He spoke at the Hay Festival of Literature and Arts in Powys, Wales. He said the festival circuit has already reached its highest point, about two years ago. He said there are too many festivals, and not enough big artists to be the main act. He said the music business is not creating a new generation of headline bands like the Rolling Stones, Muse, or the Arctic Monkeys.

He said there were about 900 music festival events in the UK between May and September 2014, and they cannot all keep going. He thinks festivals will change into events that include more than music, such as poetry, books, or magic shows. He thinks there will be many smaller festivals that offer something extra, instead of huge fields full of people.

Goldsmith also said the way people listen to music has changed. He said many people do not want to listen to a full album now. He said rock bands used to build their name over several albums. But he said young people often choose one track, then quickly move on to the next. He said pop music is everywhere, and he thinks rock may return, but it will take time.

Goldsmith, 69, said he is working with Robin de Levita, a Dutch producer of the Who’s 1970s rock musical Tommy. They are involved with a new 1,100-seat theatre in Wembley. It is planned to be finished in time for the first stage adaptation of the teen book and movie series The Hunger Games in June 2016.

Goldsmith said de Levita will bring his SceneAround idea to London. In this kind of theatre, the audience seats are on a turntable that slowly rotates. The scenes are built in a circle around the audience, with projections on screens. This idea was first used in an aircraft hangar outside Amsterdam and was very successful.

Goldsmith said he is also planning to bring a production of the Anne Frank story to the new venue. He said it is a completely different way to produce shows.

At Hay, Goldsmith also shared stories from his career. He talked about Keith Moon putting dynamite down a toilet in a Sydney hotel room to try to unblock it. He also described John Lennon having very strong stage fright before a show at Madison Square Garden in 1974. Goldsmith said this fear is common for artists, but when the music starts, they are fine.

Goldsmith also explained why Elvis Presley never performed outside North America. He said Presley’s manager, Colonel Tom Parker, told him over tea that Parker was an illegal Dutch immigrant. Parker did not want to risk leaving the US, so Goldsmith’s efforts to bring Elvis to London failed.

Goldsmith said Freddie Mercury was the most powerful live stage performer he has seen. He said that at Live Aid, Mercury went on stage, saw the audience, and took control of it.

But Goldsmith said a new Queen is still not being formed. He said the industry is not creating the next generation of big, long-lasting artists. He said Coldplay is probably the last band to reach that level, and that was ten years ago.

Goldsmith said that without big headline acts, there cannot be big festival shows. He said even Glastonbury cannot find enough big acts anymore, and he called Glastonbury the top of the festival world. He said the big festival era is really over.
