The view from the visitors’ centre on the south edge of Donana National Park in southern Spain is amazing. It is a great place for people who study birds. The park has 200,000 hectares of salt marsh, and it is very important for birds in western Europe. Many birds from Britain stop here each year when they travel from Africa. These include house martins, swallows, cuckoos, and warblers. Donana is a UN World Heritage Site. It also has very rare birds like the Spanish imperial eagle. It also has mammals like the Iberian lynx, which is very endangered.

Donana is beautiful, but it is also in danger. In 1998, a dam at Los Frailes mine, 45 km to the north, broke. Almost two billion gallons of polluted, very acidic water mixed with waste metals flowed into the park. The poison rushed down the Guadiamar river and over the river banks. It left a thick metal layer over a large area. After this, workers collected more than 25,000 kilos of dead fish. Nearly 2,000 adult birds, chicks, eggs, and nests were killed or destroyed.

This was Spain’s worst environmental disaster. Cleaning it up cost 90m. Spain then decided to spend another 360m, including some EU money, to restore the land. In the 1950s and 60s, some places had been drained to make rice and cotton fields. Now some of this farmland is being changed back into wetland.

This work has been expensive, but it has helped. Still, Donana is not safe. Modern life brings more pressure. There are plans for an oil pipeline through Donana. Other developers want to grow nearby tourist resorts. New hotels and golf courses would need a lot of water, which could lower the local water table. Also, silt from nearby farms is filling up the channels that run across Donana.

A big worry for people who protect nature is a new decision by the Andalucian government. They want to reopen the Frailes mine, the mine that almost destroyed Donana in 1998. Laurence Rose of the RSPB says Donana is Europe’s most precious bird sanctuary. It is important for local bird species and also for birds that travel between Africa and Britain and other parts of north-west Europe. He says Donana already has many threats, and bringing back the cause of the old disaster is extremely worrying.

It may seem strange to reopen the mine after spending so much to repair Donana. But the local economy helps explain it. Spain’s bank crash in 2008 badly hurt the region. Unemployment in some parts of Andalucia is now more than 30%. Reopening the mine could provide more than 1,000 jobs.

Vicente Fernandez Guerrero, secretary-general of Innovation, Industry and Energy for Andalucia, said there are riches there that local people need. He said mining could help local people keep living in the area. He also said this is a mining area and people have dug metals and ores there since Roman times.

Fernandez also said the mine licence would require modern mining methods. These methods would avoid making poisonous wet waste. The government has also said the Canadian company that ran Los Frailes cannot bid for the contract. Sixteen years after the accident, the two sides still disagree about compensation. Fernandez insisted that the best technology will be used and that liquid will not be used.

Some people in the area support the plan, but many oppose it. One road sign had rude words about the workers’ union UGT, which supports reopening the mine. Carlos Davila, from the Spanish Ornithological Society in Donana, was also very worried. He said it is a very bad idea. He said people also said it was safe in 1998, but it caused Spain’s worst ecological disaster.

Davila and others worry that a new mine could harm eco-tourism in Donana. In recent years, people have invested a lot in nature tourism there. For example, at the restaurant Dehesa de Abajo, visitors can eat and drink among trees where storks and black kites nest.

Almost every visitor had a camera with a long lens or binoculars. People can make money from tourists who come to see the birds of Donana. This is not surprising because it is a special place. A huge sky stretches over the flat land. Birds of many kinds fill the air and sometimes stand on the road. During the visit, a stork stood calmly in front of the car until it decided to fly away.

The problem is that Spain does not have the same public resources it had 16 years ago. Rose said that if another toxic spill happened today, it would cause much more damage. Davila agreed. He said that after the disaster, Spain understood how important Donana was and worked hard to clean it up and protect it. He said that now people seem to be forgetting that lesson, and it is very depressing.
