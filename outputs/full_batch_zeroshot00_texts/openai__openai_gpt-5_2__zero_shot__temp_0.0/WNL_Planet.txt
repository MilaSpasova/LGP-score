A new science study says that if climate change is not controlled, Earth’s temperature could rise to the most severe levels scientists have predicted.

The lead scientist said that if we do not cut greenhouse gas emissions, the planet will warm by at least 4C by the year 2100. This is twice the level that many governments say is dangerous.

The study says that as the planet warms, fewer clouds form. With fewer clouds, less sunlight is reflected back into space. This makes the planet warm even more. How clouds affect global warming has been a big mystery.

Professor Steven Sherwood from the University of New South Wales in Australia led the work. He said the study helps explain what controls cloud changes. He also said it makes the lowest warming estimates less likely, and supports higher and more harmful estimates.

Sherwood said that 4C of warming would probably be catastrophic, not just dangerous. He said it could make life very hard, or even impossible, in much of the tropics. He also said it would guarantee that the Greenland ice sheet and some of the Antarctic ice sheet would eventually melt. That would raise sea levels by many metres.

Other scientists said the research is a big advance. They said it cuts the uncertainty about warming from carbon emissions in half. The study was published in the journal Nature. Hideo Shiogama and Tomoo Ogura from Japan’s National Institute for Environmental Studies said the explanation for fewer clouds was convincing. They agreed it points to more future climate change than expected. But they said it will still be hard to make temperature predictions even more exact.

Scientists often measure how strongly greenhouse gases warm Earth by asking what would happen if CO2 in the air doubled compared with pre-industrial levels. On current trends, this could happen within 50 years. For 20 years, estimates for this warming have ranged from 1.5C to 5C. The new research narrowed the range to 3C to 5C by closely studying clouds.

The researchers tried to make sure computer climate models show cloud formation more accurately. Climate models are the main tool scientists use to predict future temperatures. When water evaporates from the oceans, the vapour can rise over nine miles and form rain clouds that reflect sunlight. Or it may rise only a few miles and then sink back down without forming clouds. In real life, both happen. Models that include both processes predicted much higher future temperatures than models that include only the nine-mile-high clouds.

Sherwood said climate sceptics often say climate models are wrong, and he agreed they are not perfect. But he said the models that make the biggest mistakes are the ones that predict less warming, not more. He also said sceptics point to a “hiatus” in warming since the end of the 20th century, but evidence suggests this “hiatus” is not seen in other parts of the climate system and is almost certainly temporary.

Global average air temperatures have risen more slowly since a high point in 1998, which was caused by El Nino. But scientists see that greenhouse gases are still trapping more heat, and over 90% of that heat is going into the oceans. A study in November 2013 also suggested the “pause” may be mostly an illusion because there are not enough temperature readings from polar regions, where warming is greatest.

Sherwood said his team’s work cannot fully prove that lower temperature rises are impossible. But he said that for low rises to happen, there would need to be a major missing piece that we currently have no evidence for. He said this is possible but would take a lot of faith. He added that if global temperatures rise by at least 4C by 2100, it will strongly affect the world and the economies of many countries unless we urgently reduce emissions.
