Two islanders saw a small fibreglass boat on a far-away island in the Pacific Ocean. They went to look. Inside they found a very thin man with long hair and a beard. He said he had been drifting at sea for 16 months. He said he started his trip in Mexico, more than 12,500km away.

The man was wearing only torn underpants. He said he left Mexico for El Salvador in September 2012 in a 7.3-metre fibreglass boat. The boat’s engines had no propellers. He said another man who was with him died at sea months earlier.

Ola Fjeldstad, a Norwegian student doing research on Ebon Atoll in the Marshall Islands, said the man was not in good condition but was getting better. The man said his name was José Ivan. He said he stayed alive by catching turtles and birds with his bare hands. He spoke only Spanish, so the details were not clear. There was no fishing gear on the boat, but there was a turtle inside when the boat arrived. Fjeldstad said the boat was very scratched and looked like it had been in the water a long time.

The islanders took the man to the main island of the atoll to meet the mayor, Ione de Brum. The atoll is very remote. It has only one phone line and no internet. The mayor called the Ministry of Foreign Affairs in Majuro, the capital of the Marshall Islands. Officials said they were waiting for more information and expected the man to be taken to the capital.

The government airline’s only plane that can land at Ebon is being repaired and will not fly for several days. Officials may send a boat to pick up the man. Fjeldstad said the man was staying at the local council house and a family was feeding him. He had a basic health check and had low blood pressure, but he did not seem to have any life-threatening problems. He could walk with help from men on the island. Fjeldstad said they were giving him lots of water and he was getting stronger.

Fraser Christian, who teaches sea survival in Dorset, said the story, if true, would be amazing but not the only one like it. He said it is possible to catch turtles or small fish by hand, because they may come close to a small boat to hide under it. He told how some people eat turtles for survival.

Christian said the biggest dangers would be too much sun and not enough water. He said water is more important than food. If there is no fresh water and it does not rain for a few days, a person may not survive. He also said a person’s body and mind matter, and some people can handle hard times better than others.

Survival stories in the Pacific Ocean are not rare. In 2006, three Mexicans were found drifting in a small fibreglass boat near the Marshall Islands. They said they survived for nine months by drinking rainwater and eating raw fish and seabirds, and by reading the Bible for hope. But Cliff Downing, who teaches sea survival to sailors, said he did not fully believe this new story. He said many dangers would make survival for so long very unlikely, and people would need to know more.
