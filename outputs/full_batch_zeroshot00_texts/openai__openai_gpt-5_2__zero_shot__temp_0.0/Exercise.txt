Get fit in four minutes sounds like a silly promise. But when you try Dr Izumi Tabata’s workout, you learn it is very hard. You work as hard as you can for 20 seconds, rest for 10 seconds, and do this eight times. You will be out of breath and very tired.

Tabata has seen this before. He laughs about the first time he made his university students do it in the early 1990s. After four minutes they were exhausted. But after six weeks they saw results and were surprised.

Tabata studied Japan’s speed skating team in the early 1990s with coach Irisawa Koichi. They noticed that short, very hard exercise could work as well as hours of medium exercise. Tabata tested this with students.

One group rode a stationary bike for one steady hour, five times a week. The other group did a 10-minute warm-up, then four minutes of Tabata intervals, four times a week. They also did one 30-minute steady session with two minutes of intervals.

After six weeks, the Tabata group exercised only 88 minutes a week. Their anaerobic capacity went up by 28%. Their VO2 max went up by 15%. VO2 max is a key sign of heart and fitness level. The other group trained five hours a week. Their VO2 max went up by 10%, but their anaerobic capacity did not change.

Tabata also says they measured bigger heart size after three weeks of doing the protocol. He also says there is research coming that shows it lowers the risk of diabetes in humans. This is something they already showed in rats.

This workout is not something you do slowly while reading a magazine. It needs short bursts of very hard work on a bike or rowing machine, or with sprints and bodyweight exercises. Think of a 100m sprint at school. Now imagine doing eight of them with only a 10-second break.

Tabata says the rule is all-out effort at 170% of your VO2 max. If you feel fine afterward, you did not do it right. The first three rounds may feel easy, but the last two feel almost impossible. In the first plan the goal was eight rounds, but some people could only do six or seven.

One person on the exercise forum T-Nation says that if you do it correctly, you should “meet God.” They say most people cannot do it correctly and should not try.

Tabata does not fully agree. He says everyone can do it, but beginners should start with trained experts. This helps them work at the right intensity for them. He says he will soon publish research showing that doing it only twice a week, less than half of the original plan, still gives important health benefits.

Tabata also describes another new finding. It shows the protocol burns an extra 150 calories in the 12 hours after exercise, even while resting. This happens because of extra oxygen use after exercise. So it can help people get fit, and it can also burn fat.

It is surprising that the plan is still mostly used by serious athletes and gym fans. This may change because Tabata has made a deal with Universal Studios. This will create a network of instructors and a DVD range released near the end of the year. Tabata says he did this because he often sees people on YouTube doing it wrong and not understanding how hard they must work.

Should everyone add this to their workouts? Richard Scrivener, a former Assistant Strength and Conditioning Coach at Northampton Saints Rugby Club, says the benefits are clear. But Tabatas should be added, not used instead of a sport or training method. He says runners need good running economy, which comes from learning skill and running many miles. But runners could reduce long runs and total mileage by adding Tabata training. This can reduce stress on the skeleton and let joints rest and recover, especially for people with small pains or past injuries. Then they may get more benefit from long runs when they do them.

People who lift weights can do three strength sessions and three Tabatas each week. Other people can build up slowly, session by session and week by week. But it will not get easier, because each session needs maximum effort. That is why the protocol is so tough, and why it works.
