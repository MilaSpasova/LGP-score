Sometimes, in a man’s life, he feels sadder. He thinks more about death. He may feel like the only smart thing to do is to spend all his saved money on a sports car.

Big, and often unwise, life changes are often linked to a “midlife crisis.” Some people think it is real. If it is real, humans may not be the only animals that have it.

A team of scientists from several countries says they found a drop in happiness in middle-aged chimpanzees and orangutans. They say this drop is like a midlife crisis for apes.

The study was published in the journal Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences. Some scientists were surprised. But the authors say the results may mean the midlife crisis could come from biology that humans share with apes.

Alex Weiss, a psychologist at Edinburgh University, told the Guardian that many people think well-being goes down in middle age.

He said the team asked if the midlife crisis might not be only human, and not only caused by social life. It might be something that has evolved, so middle-aged individuals feel less well.

The team from the US, Japan, Germany, and the UK asked zookeepers and carers to fill out questionnaires about apes of different ages.

The questions were about each ape’s mood, how much they enjoyed being with others, and how well they reached certain goals. The last question asked how the carer would feel about being that ape for a week. Answers were scored from one to seven.

More than 500 apes were in the study, in three groups. Two groups were chimpanzees. The third group was orangutans from Sumatra or Borneo. The animals came from zoos, sanctuaries, and research centres in the US, Australia, Japan, Canada, and Singapore.

After studying the answers, the researchers found that ape well-being went down in middle age and went up again in old age. In captivity, great apes often live to 50 or more. The lowest point happened, on average, at 28.3 and 27.2 years old for chimpanzees, and 35.4 years old for orangutans.

Weiss said that in all three groups, well-being was lowest at an age that is like midlife in humans. On average, scores were lowest when animals were around 30 years old.

The team says this temporary drop could happen because apes may see depressed apes die younger, or because of brain changes with age that also happen in humans.

Weiss agreed that apes, unlike men, are not known to make big and often bad life changes in middle age.

Robin Dunbar, Professor of Evolutionary Psychology at Oxford University, doubted the results. He said it is hard to see what in an ape’s life would create this pattern. He also said apes are not very good at thinking far into the future, and that is a big difference between them and humans.

Alexandra Freund, Professor of Psychology at the University of Zurich, also doubted it. She said the idea of a midlife crisis is weak even in humans. She said many studies show no real evidence, or only a very small drop in well-being.

But Weiss thinks the results could help people understand the emotional crisis some men may have. He said that to understand the midlife crisis, we should look at what is similar in middle-aged humans, chimps, and orangutans.
