The Moroccan city of Ouarzazate is used to big movie and TV projects. It is near the Sahara Desert. Many famous films and shows were filmed there, like Lawrence of Arabia, The Mummy, The Living Daylights, and Game of Thrones.

Now Ouarzazate, called the “door of the desert,” is the place for another very large project. It is building four connected solar power plants. Together with hydro power and wind power, these will help Morocco get nearly half of its electricity from renewable energy by 2020. Morocco also hopes to have extra electricity to sell to Europe. Morocco wants to use its deserts to become a world leader in solar power.

When the whole project is finished, it will be the largest concentrated solar power plant in the world. The first part, called Noor 1, will start working in November 2015. It uses mirrors, not the usual photovoltaic panels seen on many roofs. This mirror system costs more, but it can keep making electricity after the sun goes down.

People have known for many years that deserts have huge solar power potential. After the Chernobyl nuclear accident in 1986, German physicist Gerhard Knies said the world’s deserts get enough sun energy in a few hours to cover all human energy needs for a full year. The hard part is collecting this energy and moving it to the places where people live.

As engineers finish Noor 1, 500,000 crescent-shaped mirrors shine in the desert. There are 800 rows. The mirrors slowly follow the sun and move a little every few minutes.

When all four plants are done, they will cover an area as big as Rabat, Morocco’s capital. They will produce 580 mega-watts (MW) of electricity, enough for about one million homes. Noor 1 can produce 160MW.

Morocco’s Environment Minister, Hakima el-Haite, says solar energy could change the region in this century the way oil changed it in the last century. She said the $9bn project started because of urgent needs. Morocco does not produce oil. It imports 94% of its energy as fossil fuels from other countries, which hurts the state budget. Morocco also used to pay subsidies for fossil fuels, which cost a lot. So, when Morocco saw the chance to use solar energy, it decided to do it.

By 2020, solar energy will be one third of Morocco’s renewable energy. Wind and hydro will each be one third too.

El-Haite said Morocco is very proud of the project. She called it the most important solar plant in the world.

Each curved mirror is 12 metres high. It points sunlight at a steel pipe. The pipe carries a heat transfer solution (HTF), which is heated to 393C as it moves along the pipe and then goes into a heat engine. There, it is mixed with water to make steam. The steam turns turbines that make electricity.

The HTF is a synthetic thermal oil. It is pumped to a heat tank with molten sands that can store heat for three hours. This lets the plant power homes into the night. The mirrors are placed in levels to reduce damage from sand blown by desert winds.

Technicians say Noor 2 and Noor 3, planned to open in 2017, will store energy for up to eight hours. This could allow solar power all day and night in the Sahara and nearby areas.

Project director Rashid al-Bayad said the biggest challenge was finishing on time and reaching the needed performance level.

Morocco also has bigger international plans. Ahmed Baroudi, from Société d’Investissements Energétiques, said they are already working on high-voltage lines to cover southern Morocco and Mauritania as a first step. He said the final goal is much wider, even as far as the Middle East. He said the king’s final goal is Mecca.

It is not clear if this will happen. But Masen, Morocco’s solar energy agency, says exporting solar power could help make countries more stable. Morocco is talking with Tunisia, and sending energy across the Mediterranean to the north is still an important goal.

Masen spokeswoman Maha el-Kadiri said exporting to Europe could be possible, but first they would need to build new interconnectors, because they do not exist yet. She said these connections would not use the existing one in Spain. After building them, Morocco could start exporting.

Spain has stopped new solar projects because it does not have enough interconnectors to send energy to France. The EU has a goal that by 2020, 10% of each member country’s electricity can be sent abroad by cable.

For now, Morocco is focused on using solar power to meet its own needs and be more independent. In the future, this could include desalination, to make fresh water from sea water. Morocco is being hit more often by drought as the climate warms. Officials know Morocco is leading the most advanced renewable energy program in the Middle East and North Africa region. El-Kadiri said Morocco is at the front of solar energy.

About $9bn has been invested in the Noor Complex. Much of it came from international groups like the European Investment Bank and the World Bank, with support from Moroccan government guarantees. The cost has not been passed on to energy customers because of undisclosed energy subsidies from King Mohammed VI, Morocco’s unelected ruler.

One month before Noor 1 starts, more than a thousand workers, mostly Moroccan, are still working fast. They are fixing electric wires, removing scaffolding, and wrapping insulation around steel pipes. They work 12-hour shifts with the Atlas Mountains behind them. They wear hard hats, safety shoes, and ear plugs.

Hajar Lakhael is 25 years old and works as an environment and security manager from Meknes. She says the preparation is almost finished.
