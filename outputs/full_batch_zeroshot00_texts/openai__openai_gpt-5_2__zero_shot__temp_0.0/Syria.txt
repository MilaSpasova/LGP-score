It is hard to know exactly where the noise comes from, but everyone in Damascus can hear it. Day and night there are dull booms from artillery, rockets, or planes hitting rebel areas. The war is coming closer to Syria’s capital. More than two years into the Syrian crisis, many people in Damascus have gotten used to hearing death and destruction nearby.

Over the weekend, men smoked water pipes in a park with palm trees. Children played near flowerbeds, and couples talked on benches. Loud explosions could be heard a few miles away. Smoke rose near the minarets of an old Ottoman-era mosque. No one seemed to notice.

“Actually you do get used to it after a while,” said George, an IT technician from a village on the coast. “But you never know exactly what they are hitting.” People often learn later from videos posted by opposition media outlets on YouTube.

The sounds are even more upsetting because the government tries to act like life is normal. An army officer said, “As you can see, everything here is fine but we have to hit the terrorists, these extremists.” An official, whose way home has been attacked by rebels in Daraya, said, “If I was afraid, I would just shut my door and stay inside. I have to work and I am not afraid. If I don’t defend my country, who will?”

In private, ordinary people speak differently. A shopkeeper in the center of town said sadly that his baby daughter cries when she hears shelling. Zeina, a student in her twenties, worries she is getting used to suffering, and maybe to danger too. “In the beginning, when there started to be explosions, I used to have nightmares,” she said. “Now I can sleep through anything.”

The danger is also getting closer to home. In Sabaa Bahrat Square, in what was supposed to be the safest part of Damascus, a car bomb exploded. It left blackened concrete, broken windows, twisted metal, and damage to the Syrian Central Bank building next door.

Notices of mourning for two of the 15 victims, Muhammad al-Sufi and Manal al-Tahan, are stuck to a wall nearby. Untidy militiamen with machine guns stand around the square. The square is often used for televised pro-regime rallies, where civil servants are brought in large groups to chant slogans under huge banners of President Bashar al-Assad.

That bombing was not the worst Damascus has seen as things have gotten worse. In February, 80 people, including schoolchildren, reportedly died near the ruling Ba’ath Party headquarters in Mazraa. The crater can still be seen, marked by a large patch of new asphalt on the main road going north. “I live nearby but luckily I wasn’t there,” said Munir, a university lecturer.

Mortar bombs, fired from rebel-held areas now close enough to reach the city easily, have become a frightening new danger. These bombs killed 15 students in a university cafeteria on 28 March. The target was thought to be a government building.

Security has become stronger since the bombing of the national security crisis cell in July 2012, when four of Assad’s most senior aides were killed. Concrete blast barriers, often painted black, red, and white like the Syrian flag, now protect many official places, not only military sites. The Iranian Embassy in Mezze, with a turquoise mosaic front, now looks like a fortress. A foreign diplomat said, “The regime did manage to set up a ring of steel round Damascus. But for whatever reason the perimeter is starting to be punctured and that brings home the reality of the war.”

Because of this, moving around the city is hard, uncertain, and slow. Checkpoints on main roads make traffic stop for ID checks and bag searches. Soldiers use handheld explosive detectors to try to stop future bombers. Only drivers with official security clearance can use special fast lanes to avoid waiting.

Many people are thinking about one big question: will there be a battle for Damascus, like the one that has badly damaged Aleppo? Parts of Damascus already feel like a war zone. The most expensive hotel is strangely empty, even though international agencies use many rooms as offices because of the growing crisis. Blue helmets and flak jackets are piled on Persian carpets in a decorated reception room. White UN vehicles are parked behind blast barriers outside. The streets become empty soon after 9pm.

Some people think the fight for the capital is coming, but not yet, maybe in the summer, after rebels strengthen their gains in the south. Others think neither side will win completely and hope for a political solution pushed from abroad. But few people in Damascus expect things to get better soon.
