Some people call it “the hotel of mum and dad”. This means living with your parents as an adult. Because of the housing crisis, many young adults are staying at home until they are at least 26. About one in five do not pay any money to live there.

A survey showed that the number of adults living at home is different in different places. It was just under 9% in the East Midlands. It was more than double that in London, where house prices and rents are the highest. The survey also found that 20% of adults living at home paid nothing at all.

Many young adults have low wages, and rents have gone up to record highs. People who want to buy a home often cannot save enough because renting costs so much each month. Research by the homeless charity Shelter showed that half of tenants could not save any money for a deposit. A quarter could only save £100 or less each month. Interest rates are very low, so mortgages can be cheaper than before, but the best mortgage deals are for people with large deposits.

Because of this, more young adults are moving back home to save money. Parents who cannot give their children a large amount of money still often want to help. The survey found that 28% of adults lived at home to save for a deposit. But it also found that 30% were not saving any money.

A spokesman for the company that did the survey said the “hotel of mum and dad” is staying open longer than many people expected. High rent costs and the need to save for a deposit mean some young adults have to wait longer before moving out. For some, moving out may never be possible.

Michael Day, 30, lives with his parents in Bristol. He says he feels stuck between paying high rent and saving for a deposit. Rents for a one-bedroom home in Bristol are between £500 and £800 a month. Buying a similar home would cost about £130,000. He said he does not want to rent because rent can cost more than a mortgage. But it is hard to get a mortgage because you need a big deposit.

Day does not want to live with strangers, so he has fewer choices. At home, he pays a small rent to help with bills. He keeps the rest of his pay from his job at a candle shop. He plays golf at county level. He says he spends extra money on golf and holidays instead of saving. He said he will need to save for a long time. Because saving has been so hard, he has been going on holiday and enjoying it.

Sue Green, from Saga, a company that sells insurance and products to people over 50, said many parents did not plan to have their children living at home into their 20s or 30s. She said many parents are happy to let them live at home without rent because it could help them buy a home sooner. She said some children who do not pay rent still help in other ways, like buying groceries, buying family takeaways, or doing small jobs around the home.

Angus Hanton, co-founder of a thinktank called the Intergenerational Foundation, said older generations were “the architects of the housing crisis” and young people should not be blamed for living at home. He said average incomes for under-30s have fallen about 20% since the 2008 downturn. He said rents and car insurance have never been so high. He also said mortgage lending rules have become stricter for young people, but not for older buy-to-let investors, who push young people out of the market. He said student-fee debt is rising quickly, and many jobs are zero-hour or short-term contracts. He said we should support young people so they can afford to build their own lives.

Jenna Gavin, 29, lives in Southport, Merseyside, in the home where she grew up. She moved out for one year to go to university, but she has lived with her parents since then. She works nearby as a medical receptionist and wants to stay in the area. Renting a one-bedroom flat would cost more than £420 a month before bills, which would take a large part of her pay. She said she does not want to rent because she would spend a lot of money and have nothing at the end. She has looked at buying and talked to mortgage advisers, but she cannot borrow enough.

Gavin is trying to save, but it is hard to save enough. She said even a 5% deposit is a lot of money, and she would like to pay more than that. Her parents are happy not to charge her rent. She said they want her to save, and she helps in other ways by bringing food home and doing things around the house.

Gavin gets along with her parents and has her own space in a room she moved into when she was 14. She said she always thought she would have her own home by the time she was 30. She said she does not think that will happen because she will be 30 next year. But she hopes she will move out in a couple of years.
