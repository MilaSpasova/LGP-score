We are not living in a time of flying cars like in the 1985 film Back to the Future II. But smartphones and other new technology are changing travel in ways that can feel just as amazing. Experts say that changes in the economy and population, new technology, and worries about the environment are changing transportation in a big way. Robert Puentes from the Brookings Institution’s Metropolitan Policy Program said this is a very active time. He said transportation is closely linked to the economic health of cities and affects people’s lives.

In the US, the average commute is getting longer, and roads and bridges are getting old and worn out. Because of this, many US cities must rethink what transportation means. More planners and scientists now see that old plans that focused mostly on cars and fixing traffic jams are not enough. Cities also need to deal with population growth and carbon emissions. Transportation is now a key part of a city’s plans to be more sustainable.

Big US cities like Los Angeles, Seattle, and Chicago are trying to use their streets better. They are adding more bus lanes, improving walking paths, and expanding rail options. At the same time, they are working with private companies on new technology. This technology could let a vehicle drive itself and “talk” to other vehicles and to things around it. This could make transportation more intelligent. Puentes said the most sustainable places to live have multi-modal transport systems, meaning many ways to travel. He also said a city cannot compete globally without a strong transportation network.

Experts say three key trends will shape transportation in the next few years.

Connectivity
Ride-sharing services like Uber and Lyft are like taxis that you book with a smartphone button. Apps like Waze use real-time traffic data to find the fastest routes. These tools are changing how people travel and even how traffic moves through a city. Allan Clelland, an expert in transportation technology, says communication between riders and drivers, between vehicles, and between cars and roads is creating a new age of transportation.

A recent study from UCLA’s Institute of Transportation Studies says that millennials (people born roughly from the early 1980s to the early 2000s) drive less than older generations did. The study says people born in the 1990s make 4% fewer car trips and travel 18% fewer miles per year, on average, than earlier generations did at the same age.

People who still drive may face less traffic because of Waze. Experts say the app has reduced congestion on motorways and shortened travel times. But it has also caused more cars to drive through residential neighbourhoods. Many residents are angry because the extra traffic on quiet streets hurts their quality of life.

This trend may continue as cars share more data with other cars and with roads and traffic systems. Right now, a traffic light can detect that a car is coming, but not much more. Companies are working on technology so a vehicle can tell traffic control systems that it is there, where it is going, and how fast it is moving.

Automation
Driverless cars have been in the news since Google began testing them on roads in 2012. But nobody knows when driverless cars will be common. Still, some automation is already starting. Alexandre Bayen from the University of California expects automation will grow step by step. First, there may be automated buses in special lanes. Later, there may be lorries in ports or mining towns. These would be vehicles that are connected electronically and travel one behind the other.

A fully automated system is interesting because it could make travel safer by reducing human error. It could also make travel more efficient, because car owners could do other tasks during a long commute. It may also reduce carbon emissions and traffic congestion, and give more people access to cars. But even if the technology were ready today, it would still take a long time to become fully automated, because the average age of cars on the road is 11.5 years.

Environmental concern
The world is trying to avoid disastrous climate change. Countries, states, and cities are making strong goals to cut emissions. These policies could change transportation a lot. They could push the use of zero- and low-emission vehicles. They could also support apps that encourage more walking, biking, and carpooling.

Sharon Feigon, executive director of the Shared Use Mobility Center, imagines a future with one transit pass for many kinds of travel in the same day. If a train is delayed, a person could use the same pass to get a city bike, take a shuttle, or rent a vehicle through a car share programme. She said these systems will be put together so it is easy for people to move from one type of transport to another.

When thinking about the future of transportation, it is also important to remember why people travel. They travel to go to work, to see friends or family, or to shop. Technology that reduces the need for trips, like virtual meetings or telecommuting, could also change transportation.

Flying cars once seemed like the top idea for the future. But the new technologies being developed today may be even more advanced. They may also be more useful for dealing with the social and environmental problems we may face in the coming decades.
