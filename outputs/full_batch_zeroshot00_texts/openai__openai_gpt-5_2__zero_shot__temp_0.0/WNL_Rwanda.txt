People from all across Rwanda, and even from nearby Burundi, travel to the southern town of Butare. They go to a small shop called Inzozi Nziza (Sweet Dreams). They come to try something many have never eaten before: ice cream.

In this first ice-cream shop in Rwanda, people can buy scoops in sweet cream, passion fruit, strawberry, and pineapple. They can add toppings like fresh fruit, honey, chocolate chips, and granola. The shop also sells black tea and coffee.

The signs say “ice cream, coffee, dreams.” The manager, Louise Ingabire, says the shop is also “changing lives.” She says ice cream is new for many Rwandans, so they still need to tell more people that they will enjoy it.

Ingabire says the job helped her. She says she did not have a job before and stayed at home. Now she has a plan for the future. She makes money and can help her family.

Butare has 89,600 residents and is 135 km south of the capital, Kigali. It is home to the National University of Rwanda. Inzozi Nziza has become a popular place for tired students who want something cool and different.

Kalisa Migendo, a 24-year-old agriculture student, says it brings people together. He says if you want to go out and talk to a friend, a girl, or a boy, you come to Inzozi Nziza for ice cream.

Most ingredients come from nearby places. The milk comes from a depot in nearby Nyanza. The vanilla beans and cocoa are imported.

The theatre director Odile Gakire Katese opened Inzozi Nziza. She met Alexis Miesen and Jennie Dundas, co-founders of Blue Marble Ice Cream in Brooklyn, New York. They partnered to open the shop in 2010.

Miesen says Katese believed an ice-cream shop could help people by rebuilding spirits, hopes, and family traditions.

At first, Miesen and Dundas owned the shop together with the staff. The business is a cooperative and non-profit. They did not set money goals. After 18 months, they gave their shares to the women, who had shown they could run the business.

Fatuma Ndangiza, deputy chief executive of the Rwanda Governance Board, says many small businesses are managed by women. But very few women are in big business and compete for big tenders. She says more women are becoming entrepreneurs, in and outside Kigali.

Ndangiza says ice cream is new in Rwanda, but she likes the idea of the shop. She says it takes many skills and it changes how people think, because selling and eating ice cream is not part of their culture. She says it is interesting to introduce it and make it.

The Butare shop employs nine women. In their free time, they practise with Ingoma Nshya, Rwanda’s first and only female drumming group. Katese started it ten years ago.

The drummers are Hutu and Tutsi women. Some are survivors of the 1994 genocide, when almost a million Tutsis and moderate Hutus were killed.

Some members are widows, and some are orphans. Others were affected by the killings in different ways.

Ingabire says that in the past, Rwandan women were not allowed to drum. Many people thought the drums were too heavy for women to carry. But she says drumming brings unity.

Ingabire says her father, two siblings, and many cousins were killed in the genocide. She says some women are survivors, and some knew someone who was killed. She says when she drums with them, it gives her power because they are still alive.

The shop is shown in a documentary by Rob and Lisa Fruchtman, who are brother and sister film-makers. The film is called Sweet Dreams. It tells how the women built a hopeful life after the genocide, and it also shows the female drummers.

The film has been shown in more than a dozen countries, including the US, UK, and several African states. It premieres in Rwanda in 2014. Lisa Fruchtman, who won an Academy Award for film-editing in 1984, says the film is about resilience, hope, bravery, resourcefulness, and the ability to change your own life.
