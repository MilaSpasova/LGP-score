The International Monetary Fund (IMF) asked Washington to fix its money problems. This came after the United States stepped back from a possible debt default. It also came after hundreds of thousands of federal workers went back to work after a 16-day government shutdown.

US President Barack Obama said, “We’ve got to get out of the habit of governing by crisis.” The IMF’s leader, Christine Lagarde, also asked for more stability.

Lagarde said it is important to lower uncertainty about government spending and taxes. She said the US should raise the debt limit in a longer-lasting way.

She also said the US should pass a budget for 2014. She said the US should replace the sequester with slower, step-by-step changes that would not hurt the economic recovery. She also said the US should make a balanced, complete plan for the next several years.

A deal written in the Senate was passed by the House of Representatives just hours before the deadline to extend the debt limit. The deal gave almost no concessions to conservatives who had pushed the country close to a new financial crisis.

The World Bank said it was relieved that the world economy had “dodged a potential catastrophe.” Its president, Jim Yong Kim, asked leaders in all countries to keep making and using policies that help economic growth and create jobs and opportunity for everyone.

Stock markets in Japan, China, Hong Kong, and South Korea first showed relief after Republicans gave up on their failed effort to weaken Obama’s healthcare reforms. But overall, markets in Asia and Europe reacted only a little. Traders seemed to expect another fight in Washington early in 2014.

Standard & Poor’s estimated the shutdown cost the US $24bn. China’s official Xinhua News Agency said Washington was putting other countries’ dollar assets at risk. China is the US government’s largest creditor.

Obama signed the needed law shortly after midnight on Thursday. This happened after Republicans were split in the House. The bill passed easily in the Senate with support from both parties, after Democratic and Republican leaders made the agreement. The bill was only a temporary fix. It funded the government until 15 January and raised the debt ceiling until 7 February.

But Obama said he did not expect another harsh budget fight and shutdown in 2014.

Before the House vote, Obama said he hoped the deal would “lift the cloud of uncertainty” over the country. He said, “Once this agreement arrives on my desk, I will sign it immediately.” He also said he hoped next time would not be at the last minute, and repeated, “We’ve got to get out of the habit of governing by crisis.”

After a Wednesday night press briefing, a journalist asked Obama if the crisis would happen again in a few months. Obama answered, “No.”

Earlier, Republican senator Mike Lee spoke in a strong, challenging way, which may mean more trouble later. He said the media asked, “Was it worth it?” He answered that it is always worth it to do the right thing. He also said, “This is not over.”

Still, the deal was one of the worst possible results for Republicans. They did not reach any of their goals, and polls showed voters mostly blamed them for the crisis.
