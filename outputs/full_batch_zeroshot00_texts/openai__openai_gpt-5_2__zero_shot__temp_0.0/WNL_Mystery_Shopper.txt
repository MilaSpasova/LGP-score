The customer next to you in the line looks normal. But she is not holding a shopping list. She has handwritten notes about how the store looks and how clean it is. She is timing how fast the line moves on her phone. You may even see a tiny camera lens in her purse. You may have seen a mystery shopper.

In the UK, about 50,000 mystery shopping trips happen every month, according to the Mystery Shopping Providers Association. More people now shop online, but the need for mystery shoppers is still growing. Simon Boydell from Marketforce says people who come into a real store want good service and a good experience that they cannot get online. Companies want to check if their stores give that experience.

Jill Spencer from the mystery shopping company ABa says they send shoppers to different stores and change the locations, so a shopper does not return to the same store within three months. Each day, shoppers may spend up to eight hours visiting five to ten stores. Then they spend another one or two hours writing detailed reports about every part of the visit. They can earn up to £155 a day. They also get money back for petrol and hotel stays, and money for car wear (they may drive up to 20,000 miles a year). Video mystery shoppers, who film visits with a hidden camera in a buttonhole or handbag, can earn more, about £300 a day.

Mystery shoppers are usually paid back for what they buy in the store. They may also be allowed to keep the items. Laura is a 50-year-old mystery shopper from Devon. She says she is usually given £5 to £20 to spend in each store to test the service at the till. She has been paid to visit about 7,000 shops since 2001. She is given a story to follow, like buying something from a certain department or a new product range. But within those rules, she can often buy what she wants and keep it.

Like many full-time mystery shoppers, Laura is self-employed. She takes jobs from ABa and other companies when they are available. She usually earns £30,000 to £40,000 a year, not counting the free items she gets. She says the extra benefits help her live. But she says she does not do it because she loves shopping. She says she now hates shopping, and it even hurts to buy a pint of milk when she is not working.

Laura does like it when she visits a store again and sees it has improved. She thinks this is because of her feedback. She says some stores win awards for customer service, and she thinks mystery shoppers help with that. She feels she is helping all shoppers.

But jobs that pay like Laura’s are not common. There is strong competition. She keeps her job secret, and even her friends and family do not know who she works for. Laura is not her real name.

It is estimated that more than 500,000 people have registered as mystery shoppers in the UK, but only 10% or less get regular work each month. Because of this, pay has gone down. Val, a 51-year-old former mystery shopper, says that in the past you got a fee, your purchase paid back, and money for miles. Now you often get only part of the cost of a purchase. Val worked for 40 different mystery shopping companies for almost 20 years. She stopped three years ago because she had bills to pay and very few jobs paid an acceptable amount.

Today, mystery shopping companies often use free items to attract workers. Boydell says Marketforce shoppers usually get only a couple of pounds for a visit as a small payment for their time. At most, they may pay £15 to £25, plus paying back the cost of something like a meal for two or a hotel stay. He says they do not directly employ shoppers, so they do not have to pay minimum wage.

Laura says she would go on a cruise for free, but she thinks some companies take advantage of people when they pay only a small fee for a job that needs travel to a restaurant and a meal. She says she does not do those jobs anymore. But many people still do. Hannah is a 41-year-old City lawyer. In her spare time, she has done nearly 500 visits for the Mystery Dining Company without pay or travel money. As a “platinum diner,” she is often chosen for the most special jobs. She gets £200 meals at Michelin-starred Mayfair restaurants, five-star hospitality at Ascot, and overnight stays at boutique hotels.

But Hannah says nothing is truly free. After each visit, she usually spends two to four hours writing detailed reports. She writes about the food and about specific moments with staff. She must name staff or describe them in a “subtle” way. The hardest part is that she must remember everything while she eats, because she cannot openly write notes.

Hannah says there is a lot to remember, and it can take away from the meal. She is expected to write feedback while it is fresh, so she has had to wake up at 5am to write a report before work. She says it is hard work, and you must be focused, able to write well, and good with details.
