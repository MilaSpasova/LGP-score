Coal may become as important as oil as the world’s biggest energy source in the next five years. This could be very bad for the climate, says a top energy economics group.

A big reason for more coal use worldwide is the huge rise of shale gas use in the United States.

New research from the International Energy Agency (IEA) says coal use is rising around the world, even in places that want to cut carbon. The main exception is the US, where shale gas has replaced coal. When the US used less coal, coal prices fell worldwide. This made coal seem cheaper and more attractive, even in Europe, where the Emissions Trading Scheme was meant to discourage coal.

Maria van der Hoeven, Executive Director of the IEA, said coal’s share of world energy keeps growing. She said that if current policies do not change, coal will catch up to oil within a decade.

Coal is common in many parts of the world. It is often cheaper to get than normal oil and gas. Because of this, coal provided almost half of the growth in world energy demand in the past ten years. The IEA says China and India will push coal use higher in the next five years. India will likely pass the US to become the world’s second biggest coal user. China is the biggest coal importer. Indonesia is the biggest exporter and has briefly passed Australia.

The IEA’s Medium-Term Coal Market Report says the world will burn 1.2 billion more tonnes of coal each year by 2017 than it does today. This is about the same as the current coal use of Russia and the US together. By 2017, world coal use is expected to reach 4.3 billion tonnes of oil equivalent. Oil use is expected to reach 4.4 billion tonnes by that time.

Coal makes more carbon emissions than other major fossil fuels. It adds a lot to climate change, especially when burned in old, inefficient power stations. If power stations do not have special “scrubbing” machines to remove chemicals, coal can also release sulphur, which causes acid rain, and other pollution like mercury and soot.

Van der Hoeven said coal use will not fall much unless there is a high price on carbon to push people away from coal and toward cleaner technology, such as renewable power. She said only cheaper gas could realistically lower coal demand. This happened in the US because shale gas production grew very fast in the last five years.

She said the US shows that a better gas market, with flexible prices and local unconventional resources made in a sustainable way, can reduce coal use, cut carbon dioxide, and lower electricity bills. She said Europe, China, and other places should pay attention.

To do that, much more shale gas would need to be produced, because easy-to-reach normal gas is running out.

In Europe, the Emissions Trading Scheme was meant to reduce high-carbon power by putting a price on carbon dioxide emissions. Power companies and heavy-using industries were given a set number of permits. They had to buy more if they wanted to emit more than allowed. But too many permits were given out. Also, the financial crisis and recession reduced emissions. This created a big extra supply of permits and made permit prices fall sharply. At today’s low prices, only a few euros per tonne of carbon, there is little reason to choose lower-carbon fuels, and coal use is rising again in Europe.

This means one of the world’s main market rules for cutting greenhouse gas emissions is not reaching its main goals. Because coal has the highest carbon emissions, this growing use of coal increases the risk of climate change.
