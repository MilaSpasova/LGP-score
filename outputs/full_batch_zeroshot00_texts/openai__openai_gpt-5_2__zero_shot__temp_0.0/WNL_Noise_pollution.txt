A senior US scientist warns that today’s children may not hear many sounds from nature. People often block out the sounds around them.

Kurt Fristrup, a senior scientist at the US National Park Service, says some places are getting louder. This can make people stop noticing nice sounds like birds singing, water running, and leaves moving in the wind. These sounds can even be heard in cities.

He says the problem is worse because many people listen to music on earphones. Then they do not listen to birds and other nature sounds. Nature sounds can be covered up by traffic, music, and other noise.

Fristrup says people can learn to ignore sounds. He says we may lose a special ability we are born with: to hear many things from far away. He also says we may lose the habit of listening to the world around us.

For ten years, the US National Park Service recorded sound levels at more than 600 places in the US. These places included Yosemite in California, Yellowstone, and Denali in Alaska. Every place had some noise from people, such as airplanes flying over, motorbikes, motorboats, or tour buses.

Fristrup’s team used park data and city data to model noise across the US. They predict noise pollution is growing faster than the US population. They say it more than doubles every 30 years.

Fristrup says it is not surprising that people use earphones or noise-cancelling headphones to try to make a quieter place for themselves.

He says loud background noise affects hearing like fog affects sight. When it is loud, you only notice sounds close to you instead of many sounds all around you. He says cities still have birds and other things to enjoy, but people are losing this.

Fristrup says people get used to louder places over time. He fears we will accept much worse conditions and forget that the world could be much quieter. He says many children may grow up without peace and quiet.

Other scientists at the same meeting reported that nature sounds may help health. Derrick Taff, a social scientist at Pennsylvania State University, described early experiments. He said listening to recordings from national parks, like waterfalls, birds, and wind, helped people recover from stressful events.

In one experiment, people had to give a short talk that researchers judged from behind a one-way mirror. Researchers measured heart rate and the stress hormone cortisol before and after the talk. People calmed down faster when they listened to nature recordings than when the same recordings also included sounds from road traffic, airplanes, or normal conversation.

Taff said natural sounds are important to people. They are one reason people visit protected areas. People want to hear quiet, birdsong, and wind and water. He says people may be losing this because they listen to iPods all the time, but he believes the public still likes these sounds. He advises people to visit protected areas and experience these sounds.

Scientists are not sure why nature sounds are calming. Fristrup suggests that over millions of years of evolution, humans may connect calm nature sounds with safety. He thinks these soundscapes may feel safe because they do not suggest a predator is nearby.
