Wales will be the first country in the UK to use a new organ donation rule. People will be treated as if they agree to donate their organs when they die, unless they choose to opt out.

The Welsh Assembly voted for this opt-out policy. This means hospitals can assume a person wanted to donate, unless the person had officially registered an objection.

The final vote on the bill passed by 43 votes to eight, with two people not voting. Some religious groups objected for moral reasons. Others worried it could upset families who are grieving.

Welsh Health Minister Mark Drakeford said, “This is a huge day for Wales, for devolution and, most importantly, for the 226 people in Wales waiting for an organ transplant.”

He said he is proud Wales will be the first nation in the UK to do this. He said it could increase organ donation and give hope to people waiting for transplants.

He said families often refuse donation, and the main reason is that they do not know what their loved one wanted.

He said the family of a possible donor has an important role. The bill aims to respect the wishes of the person who died. Relatives or friends may object if they believe the person would not have wanted to donate.

He said when families know the person wanted to donate, they usually agree. He said the new law should make people’s wishes clearer and increase agreement to donate.

He said this is a landmark day for Wales, and he expects the rest of the UK to watch closely when the law starts in 2015.

The issue is controversial. Opponents worry that the need for more kidneys and hearts could lead to the wishes of the dead person and their family being ignored. Ministers say there will be safeguards.

Some people may not get around to registering that they do not want to donate. Because of these concerns, the government said families would have a bigger role.

Relatives will have a “clear right of objection.” This gives them a chance to show that the person who died would not have wanted to be an organ donor.

Wales is doing this because there are not enough organs. Drakeford said there is a long-term problem of too few organs for people who need them. He said about one person every week dies in Wales while waiting.

He said about one third of people in Wales are on the organ donor register. But surveys show well over two thirds say they are happy to be donors. He said the other third often do not get around to signing up.

The new law would apply to anyone over 18 who lived in Wales for at least the year before their death. Donated organs would go not only to people in Wales, but to anyone in the UK.

Doctors are pleased. The British Medical Association has campaigned for an opt-out system for a long time. It is worried about the growing number of people needing transplants, which is linked to progress in transplantation.

The number of young donors fell a lot when seatbelt laws started. In recent years, people have worked hard to increase the number who carry an organ donation card, with good success. Hospitals also improved how they organize transplants, including important talks with relatives when the dead person’s wishes are not known. But there are still not enough organs.

Some religious groups strongly oppose the plan. They say it could cause more distress to families. Members of the Muslim Council of Wales and the South Wales Jewish Representative Council have concerns. The Archbishop of Wales, Barry Morgan, said donation should be “a gift of love, of generosity.” He said that if organs can be taken unless someone clearly registered an objection, it is not an expression of love, but more a medical use of a body.
