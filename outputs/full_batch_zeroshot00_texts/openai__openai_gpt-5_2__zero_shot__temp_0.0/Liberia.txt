Since Moses King, 48, found out he was HIV positive, he has had one big problem: food. He could handle the shame some people in Liberia put on HIV-positive people. He could also get antiretroviral medicine. The Global Fund to Fight AIDS, Tuberculosis and Malaria paid for the medicine, and the Liberian government gave it out. But King and his family, with six children, could not get the right food.

King is a farmer who grows food mainly for his family. He grew vegetables and bought rice. But meat and fish cost a lot in Liberian markets. They are important sources of protein, but his family could not afford them.

King said, “Farming helped us survive, but we had many problems. We could not get any protein, and we were not getting the nutrients we needed.”

Pate K Chon is a counsellor who works with people with HIV in Liberia. She had an idea to help. Years earlier, she watched a film about a fish farm in Thailand. She wanted to start a similar fish farm in Liberia. This could give people with HIV work and a steady source of protein.

Chon said she saw fish being raised in a cement pool and thought it was a good idea. Chon was diagnosed with HIV in 1992. She said many people she works with cannot eat a balanced diet with enough protein. She said fish is a clean source of protein and can be farmed.

Chon started building a pool to raise fish. In June 2012, she met John Sheehy. He is a “strategic philanthropist” and the founder of the consultancy Emerging Business Lab. He raised money for a non-profit fish farm in the northeast of Monrovia, Liberia’s capital. He also learned about fish farming by taking an online course through Cornell University and talking to other fish farmers in Africa.

Sheehy said he raised the money, built the farm, and learned how to set up the tanks and the water flow. He said he learned a lot on his own, and he would like to write a manual to share with other people.

The project has grown into the Grow2Feed Liberia Fish Farm. It has 12 tanks. When the tanks are full, each tank will have 5,000 fish. The farm can provide up to 200,000 fish per year. It serves a community of 1,200 people, mostly HIV positive, including King and his family.

The farm also uses waste from the tanks. It is collected and used to water crops. This also gives food and income to the community. The community has 503 women, 206 men, and 346 children.

Sheehy said the community members live near the farm and agreed to join the co-operative. Many people work on the fish farm or take care of the crops. When the fish are fully grown after 90 days, people receive fish. They can eat the fish and also sell fish in the market to get money for other basic items.

Sheehy said the fish farm helps people with HIV join society again. Now they trade with people in the market every week.

Liberia’s demographic health survey says 1.5% of Liberia’s 3.5 million people are HIV positive. Of those, 60% are women or girls. There is still stigma and discrimination, and this has led to about half of cases in Liberia going untreated.

Good nutrition is very important for people with HIV. Research shows they need much more protein to keep their health from getting worse and to support healthy growth. Chon said nutrition is key when taking antiretroviral drugs. She said the drugs are toxic, and if you do not have food, they can make you very sick. She also said food in Liberia is very expensive. People buy expensive imported rice, even though Liberia should be growing rice, and fish is too costly for many people.

Sheehy said Grow2Feed is the first co-operative fish farm in Liberia that works to help an HIV-positive community. The project has interested the Liberian government and the UN Food and Agriculture Organization. The organization has worked with Grow2Feed to provide training. It will also support teaching at the University of Liberia.

Fish farming experts say fish farming could grow a lot in Africa. Paul White owns the HydroFish fish farm in Ivory Coast, which produces 3,000 tonnes of fish each year. He said fish farming can work in Africa. He said a lot of fish in markets is frozen fish imported from China. He said its quality would not be allowed in Europe or America. He said there has not been enough money spent and not enough knowledge about fish production, but this is changing.

Some critics do not trust farmed fish. They worry about inbred fish and high levels of toxins. Sheehy said good practices can reduce these problems. He said some farmed fish is inbred, but Grow2Feed uses local fish from Liberia, not fish from another place. This helps them keep bringing in new broodstock using local fish. He also said they do not use closed-off parts of lakes, where fish can take in toxins. He said the tanks let them control the environment, test the water, and monitor it all the time.

Sheehy hopes to copy this model in more places in Liberia and nearby. He said a rice-growing co-op in Sierra Leone asked if Grow2Feed could do this on their land to feed workers. He also said there has been interest from Nigeria and Central America. But he said Grow2Feed is 100% non-profit and will keep its social justice focus.
