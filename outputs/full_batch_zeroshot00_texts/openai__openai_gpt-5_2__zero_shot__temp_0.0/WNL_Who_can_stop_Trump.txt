Donald Trump walked on stage and said something unusually humble for a politician who seems to break many political rules.

At a campaign rally in South Carolina, he warned the crowd: “Unless we win, it doesn’t mean a damn thing.” This was even though he had finished his fourth month in a row at the top of the opinion polls. He told the crowd of 11,000 people that he wished the election were sooner: “I want to pick my date for the election. I want it next Tuesday.” The crowd showed the kind of strong support he needs to keep going until March 2016 to win the Republican nomination, and then until November’s general election.

These moments of doubt do not last long. They are quickly replaced by the loud confidence of a billionaire who presents himself as a “winner.”

Trump is not the only person wondering if his unlikely campaign can last. This is especially true because recent controversies have not hurt him in the polls. They have seemed to make his lead stronger. Many experts believe that any one of these events would have ended most politicians’ campaigns.

First, he angered prisoners of war by doubting the heroism of Vietnam veteran John McCain because McCain was captured. Then, at the first television debate, he insulted Fox News moderator Megyn Kelly because she asked him difficult questions.

He also insulted Mexican immigrants to the US. He said a Black Lives Matter protester who was violently thrown out of a rally deserved to be “roughed up.” He appeared to mock a New York Times journalist for his disability. He also falsely accused Muslim Americans of cheering for the 9/11 attackers.

Trump said the political media exaggerated many of these incidents. He claimed 70% of them are “scum.” But he refused to take back his comments. In South Carolina, he said: “I could have said, ‘Oh, I misspoke’, but I am not big on that.”

Some rivals still hope that Trump’s supporters will eventually get tired of what critics call his constant attacks, especially on minorities. One poll shows his support among Republicans down by 12 points. But at 31%, he still leads.

Other opponents say Trump does not seem to follow one clear political belief system. Former presidential rival Bobby Jindal told the Guardian, shortly before dropping out, “He is an egomaniac; he’s a narcissist. He’s not a conservative, he’s not a liberal — he believes in himself.”

Liz Mair, a Republican strategist who is organizing an anti-Trump fundraising committee, worries that comments the liberal media call mistakes may be planned to raise his support with his main followers. She said people who do not like Trump and his policies should work to actively weaken him, and “not just pray for him to commit real political suicide.”

Still, Trump is not only about shocking statements. He often says that three issues he attacks most—immigration reform, free-trade deals, and Barack Obama’s national security policy—have become some of the most important issues in the election.

His plans to deport every undocumented immigrant in the US and demand that Mexico pay for a border wall—“A real wall. A very tall wall, taller than that ceiling.”—may sound unrealistic. But they have arguably harmed the campaign hopes of Jeb Bush, who supports immigration reform.

So what could stop Trump? Opponents point to evidence that polls far before election day are often about eight percentage points off, because many people have not decided yet. Among Americans who say they are Republicans, current polls suggest Trump has about 25–30% of the vote.

It will be hard to change the minds of Trump’s passionate supporters. To reduce his lead, another candidate would need to attract Trump’s supporters without losing their own supporters. Polling experts say this is unlikely. It is hard to imagine anyone doing Trump better than Trump, or copying him without losing support.

This can be seen in answers to one question: “Are there any of these candidates you would definitely not support for the Republican nomination for president?” While about 20–30% of voters say they would support Trump, another 20–30% say they definitely would not.

Steve Deace, an Iowa conservative, said Trump’s behavior is “both a good and a bad thing.” He said it creates a loyal group that likes Trump’s style and will not leave. But it also makes it hard for Trump to win support beyond that group.

Republican pollster Frank Luntz believes Trump speaks for voters who feel, for the first time, that someone is speaking for them and listening to them. Luntz said: “Trump says what they’re thinking and, the more outrageous he is, the more they agree with him. He’s saying what no politician would say and that’s another reason they like him.”

Many regular supporters at his larger and larger events say the same.

Sandra Murray of Dubuque, Iowa, said, “I like the way he speaks. This country is a huge mess and we need to get out of this and, honestly, he could be the man to do it.”

Another supporter explained it more simply: “He’s not afraid of anybody or anything. That’s pretty cool.”
