US researchers say a nasal spray with oxytocin, sometimes called the “love hormone,” might help children with autism deal with social situations better. Brain scans showed that one dose changed how the brain reacted to facial expressions. This change could make social time feel more natural and rewarding.

The researchers said that a course of oxytocin might make behavior therapies work better. These therapies already help people with autism practice social skills. Kevin Pelphrey, director of the Child Neuroscience Lab at Yale University, said that over time children might respond more appropriately in social situations, want to interact more, make more eye contact, and talk more.

Autism is a developmental disorder seen in more than 1 in 100 people. It affects people in different ways, but often includes trouble with social interaction and communication. There is no established treatment for the social problems caused by autism.

Yale researchers have studied oxytocin because it is important for bonding and trust. Results from other studies have been mixed. One recent study found no clear benefit when youths took oxytocin for several days. Pelphrey said oxytocin might help the brain learn from social experiences, and might work best together with therapies that encourage social engagement. He said it could act like a “social enhancer” when combined with behavioral treatment.

In this study, scientists used functional MRI to scan the brains of 17 youths ages 8 to 16 with autism. The youths looked at pictures of cars or at the eyes of people showing different emotions. The scans were done 45 minutes after the youths inhaled either a placebo or oxytocin through a nasal spray.

The scans showed that parts of the brain linked to reward acted more normally after oxytocin. These areas were more active when the youths looked at faces and less active when they looked at cars. The study was published in the journal Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences in the United States.

Pelphrey said that if other studies get the same result, oxytocin might help with a key problem in autism: social motivation. But he warned it is too early to use oxytocin as a treatment. He also warned people not to buy oxytocin online or try it at home, because they may not know what they are buying. He said more follow-up research is needed.

Simon Baron-Cohen, director of the Autism Research Centre at Cambridge University, said the study is important because it shows changes in brain activity in areas involved in social thinking after oxytocin is given. But he also said doctors should be careful until we know much more about the benefits and risks, in much larger studies.

A surprising result was that the oxytocin spray did not improve how the youths did on the social task. It is also not clear if oxytocin only helps people with autism or if it has unwanted side effects. The effects last only about 45 minutes, so it may be hard to use as a treatment.

Uta Frith at University College London said the study suggests oxytocin may make faces more interesting, because reward-related brain areas became more active. But she said this change was only seen in brain activity, not in behavior. Showing a change in behavior will be very important if the nasal spray is to be useful.
