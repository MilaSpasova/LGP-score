Many people do not get enough sleep. One simple idea could help: start school later.

At the British Science Festival, Dr Paul Kelley from Oxford University said schools should have different start times. He said this should match students’ natural body clocks. He said this could help students think better, get better exam results, and be healthier. Not getting enough sleep has been linked with diabetes, depression, obesity, and a weaker immune system.

In a 2014 paper, Kelley wrote that children’s natural wake-up time changes with age. Around age 10, it is about 6:30am. At 16, it is about 8am. At 18, it is about 9am. So a normal school start time may work for 10-year-olds, but not for 16- to 18-year-olds. For older teenagers, he said it may be better to start school at 11am or even later. He also said that a 7am alarm for older teenagers is like a 4:30am start for a teacher in their 50s.

Kelley said it is not as simple as telling teenagers to go to bed earlier. He said the body clock is controlled by light. The eyes have special cells that send messages to a part of the brain called the hypothalamus. This part controls our 24-hour body rhythm. Kelley said you cannot just control it by choice, like you cannot choose to control your heartbeat.

Kelley said workers could also benefit from later start times. He said the average sleep loss each night is more than two hours for ages 14 to 24. For people aged 24 to about 30 or 35, it is about an hour and a half. This can continue until about age 55, when sleep becomes balanced again. He said a 10-year-old and a 55-year-old naturally sleep and wake at the same times.

He said this may be why the traditional 9 to 5 work day is so common. Many bosses are in their mid-50s or older, and it works best for them. Kelley asked if workplaces should also have different start times. For example, people in their 50s could start at 8am, people in their 30s at 10am, and teenage interns or apprentices at 11am. He said matching work times to body clocks could improve performance, mood, and health. He said it could make people less ill, happier, and better at their jobs.

Kelley also said there could be fewer accidents because drivers would be more alert. Rush hour might become smaller if people started at different times. He said later starts could help everyone, especially families. Parents often try to wake teenagers who are waking up three hours too early, and this causes tension.

Kelley said he is 67, so he wakes naturally just after six. He said starting work is easier for him now than when he was younger.
