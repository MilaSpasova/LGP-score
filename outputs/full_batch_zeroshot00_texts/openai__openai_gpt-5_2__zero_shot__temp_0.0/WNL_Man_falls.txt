Benjamin Carle is 26 years old. He tried to live for 10 months using only products made in France. This was for a TV documentary.

The idea came after a French minister, Arnaud Montebourg, told people to buy French products to help factories and jobs.

At the start, Carle learned something surprising: only 4.5% of the things in his apartment were made in France. Many things had to go, like his lightbulbs from China and green beans from Kenya. Moving workers took away most of his things, and his home was almost empty.

Carle had to give up many items because they were not French-made. He lost his smartphone, TV, and refrigerator (made in China). He also lost his glasses (Italian), underpants (Moroccan), coffee (Guatemalan), and his David Bowie music (British).

His girlfriend Anaïs and his cat Loon stayed with him.

Carle made three rules. He would eat only food produced in France. He would avoid foreign-made goods. And he would spend only €1,800 per month.

Life was hard. France does not make refrigerators, so he cooled food on the window ledge. He had no nail clippers, so he used a penknife on his toenails. He bought French clothes, but they cost more: underpants (€26), socks (€9), a polo shirt (€75), and espadrille sandals (€26). He could not buy jeans because none are made in France.

He searched in supermarkets for 100% French products. He learned to cook fruit and vegetables grown in France. He brushed his teeth with the last toothbrush made in France by a company in Picardie that employs 29 people. He washed small clothes by hand until he found the last French-made washing machine, but it was top-opening and did not fit under the kitchen counter.

Going out with friends was difficult. He could not watch American films. He could not drink Belgian beer. He could not eat sushi or pizza. At home, he had no sofa for the first few months and no TV. He listened to Michel Sardou and read French novels. French wine was allowed. French-Canadian singer Céline Dion was allowed too. But advisers said he should not listen to French bands like Daft Punk because they sing in English.

He could not use his British-made bicycle. He also could not use a French car after learning that the only affordable Peugeot, Renault, and Citroën models are mostly made overseas. So he bought an orange Mobylette moped that gave off bad fumes.

He replaced his computer with a Qooq, a cooking tablet that connects slowly to the internet. He replaced his iPhone with an old Sagem phone. In the documentary, he smashes his iPhone with a new French-made Tefal saucepan, and his girlfriend yells that the pans are new.

Carle said he wanted to “save the French economy,” and he joked that he likes Mission Impossible. The project was partly serious and partly a joke. He even asked a French language expert if he should use words like “cool.” He was told to use the French word “chouette” instead.

Carle learned France makes no refrigerators (except wine coolers) and no televisions, but it does make aeroplane seats and windmills. He said those things would not fit in his apartment.

At the end, Carle took a bank loan to buy furniture and clothes again. A special auditor said he was 96.9% “made in France.” Montebourg visited and gave him a medal. Carle was not 100% because of six Ikea forks, a guitar from China, and wall paint with no clear origin.

Carle said it is not fully possible, and not always good, to live 100% made in France, especially with new technology. He said the goal was not nationalism. He wanted people to think about what they buy and to make different choices, in every country. He said buying local can help jobs and industries.

He said a T-shirt made in France costs more, but he can feel sure workers are paid correctly and have good working conditions. He said he cannot be sure about a cheaper T-shirt made in Asia or Morocco. He also said it is hypocritical to blame capitalists for economic decline if people do not try to act differently as consumers.

Carle said he wants to keep supporting French producers, but not 100%, because finding everything takes too much time.

When the experiment ended, he invited his friends over. They ate cheese and listened to David Bowie’s album Aladdin Sane. He said it was hard not to invite people before because there was nowhere to sit, but he would choose Bowie over a sofa any day.
