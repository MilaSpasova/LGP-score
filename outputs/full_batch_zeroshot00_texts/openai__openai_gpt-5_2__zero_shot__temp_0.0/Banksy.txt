A controversial auction of a Banksy mural was stopped just before it was going to be sold.

The artwork is called Slave Labour. It is spray-painted and shows a child making Union Flag bunting. Many people see it as social criticism about last year’s diamond jubilee. It was expected to sell for about $700,000 at a street and contemporary art auction in Florida.

The auctioneer, Frederic Thut, owns Fine Arts Auction Miami. All week he would not say who the seller was or how the artwork was put up for sale. Then he announced that Slave Labour, and a second Banksy work, were withdrawn from the auction.

Thut did not give a reason. Community leaders in Haringey were very happy. They had campaigned to stop the sale of the artwork, which had been taken from the wall of a Poundland in Wood Green.

Alan Strickland, a Haringey councillor, said, “One of our two demands was that it doesn’t sell and the other was that we get it back again, so we’re halfway there.” He said he would write to the auction house quickly to find out what happened and what will happen next. He said the pressure and strong views from people in Wood Green helped a London campaign affect what happened in the US.

Claire Kober, Leader of Haringey Council, wrote to Arts Council England and the Mayor of Miami, Tomás Regalado, asking them to help stop the sale. But it seemed the decision to withdraw the item came from the gallery owners after talking with their lawyers. The FBI would not confirm reports that they were asked to investigate.

After the auction ended, the auction house released a short statement. It said it had convinced the owners of the two Banksys to pull them from the sale. The statement said there were no legal issues with selling the two Banksy lots.

About 30 possible buyers attended the auction. There were 106 lots listed. The three-hour auction continued, and some other items sold for more than their asking prices.

Critics accused the auction house of selling stolen property. Thut said earlier that the seller was a “well known collector,” was the rightful owner, and that the sale was legal.

Thut said his gallery received many emails and phone calls from the UK, and some were abusive or offensive. He said he supported including the pieces in the sale because it would preserve them.

The second Banksy was a 2007 artwork called Wet Dog. It was removed from a wall in Bethlehem and was estimated to be worth up to $800,000. It disappeared from the auction house’s online catalogue at lunchtime on Saturday. But Slave Labour was still listed for sale up to the 3pm start time.

Thut said the two pieces came from different owners, and neither owner was British. He said the works were important in street art and should go to buyers who care about art and preserving it.

Thut said he would keep the seller’s identity private. He said, “We respect our clients and their confidentiality. It’s not our decision to have [the Banksy] returned. We only sell it. We do not have control of it.”

A spokesperson for Poundland said it did not know who removed the 4ft x 5ft slab from the side of the shop it rents in Turnpike Lane. Lawyers for the building owner, Wood Green Investments Ltd, would not say if the company was involved.

Banksy has not commented on the Slave Labour situation. But he has criticized people who try to sell his work. Before a planned 2011 auction in New York with five of his pieces, he spoke out. None of those pieces sold.

Stephan Keszler, the dealer behind that 2011 auction, believes it is legal to sell Banksy’s works without his permission. He said Banksy makes art on other people’s property without asking, and the property owner can do what they want with it.
