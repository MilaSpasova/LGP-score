“Some people are MOOC junkies,” says Dr Ben Brabon from Edgehill University with a grin. They take MOOCs only because they are free and they enjoy being part of the online group. Brabon is not insulting them. He is just saying that people join for different reasons. Because MOOCs have no entry rules and no fees, people on them often act differently from students at a normal university.

MOOCs are a new and popular idea to help more people get higher education. A lot of venture capital money is going into online platforms that can teach hundreds or even tens of thousands of people. Investors want to find a way to make money from MOOCs. So far, universities have paid for famous teachers to plan the course and teach online. One possible way to make money is to use the data about how people join, use the course, turn in work, talk to each other, and then leave.

A big problem is that nobody is sure who MOOCs are really for. Are they for universities to attract international students who will pay fees? Are they for students in developing countries who want access to top universities? Are they for workers who want to improve their skills? Are they for people without qualifications who want a step toward higher education? Or are they for hobby learners who just want to learn about a topic they like?

Brabon says MOOCs often start with many students, but very few finish. In his vampire fiction course, 1,000 people enrolled, and only 31 finished. He says almost all who finished already had a first degree or education at degree level. So MOOCs may not be reaching new groups of people.

David Kernohan from Jisc says online learning is different and needs strong study skills. In MOOCs there is little support. Students usually do not get personal attention. Instead, they may get help from other students in online discussion forums. This can make MOOCs hard for people without high-level qualifications. But he says MOOCs could be very useful for continuing education.

As degree costs rise and part-time student numbers drop, some people wonder if free open courses could give a new path to university-level learning. Could a whole degree be taught through MOOCs? Brabon says he does not think that is how MOOCs work. He suggests a blended approach, mixing time on campus with a MOOC. He also says it might be possible in the future to have a global degree, where students take courses from universities around the world.

For now, Brabon says employers will not care much about a MOOC completion certificate, because there may be no quality checks on the course or its marking. So accreditation is a main challenge for MOOCs. Brabon is in a Quality Assurance Agency working group that is trying to agree on standards and marking.

Some people like the idea that MOOCs can bring great teaching to students in less developed countries. But others are cynical and say universities might use MOOCs to advertise their on-campus courses to more students from outside the EU.

Mike Sharples, chair of Educational Technology, does not agree with that cynical view. He says MOOCs are mainly a way to share universities’ best teaching and to get interaction and feedback from students around the world. He thinks recruiting international students is a second goal, though MOOCs can still be smart marketing. He says if 20,000 people join a MOOC, you only need 20 of them to enrol later to run a master’s.

Matthew Poyiadgi, managing director at Pearson VUE, says these debates may not matter in the future. He thinks universities may have no choice. British universities may worry that if they do not have a presence in China, they will fall behind.

Sharples adds that in South America, China, and countries in Africa, many people want to learn, and some of the world’s best courses are now online. He says that if people love learning, that is a good thing. The real challenge is to help those countries not only use MOOCs, but also create them.
