It was a nice summer evening. I decided to swim near Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland. I moved there in 2012. A woman was in the water with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty often swims with a group of people.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Hundreds of people have swum with her, so she seemed very tame. She was even in an Irish tourist ad where a girl says she wants to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty’s belly. About 20 tourists and local people watched from the pier. After I got into the water, Dusty left the woman and suddenly went wild. Later I learned she can be very protective of her space when she is with someone. Her tail was flapping hard. At first I thought she was just showing off, but then I thought she might be angry.

I tried to swim back to the pier to get out. But very fast, Dusty hit me with her nose. It was very strong and it hurt a lot. I was thrown forward.

People on the pier stared at me. Dusty stayed close, still flapping her tail. That scared me the most. I thought if she hit me with her tail, I could go under the water.

I reached the pier, but I could not climb out because I was hurt. I was very scared. I shouted for help. A man reached down and pulled me out onto the steps. Then another man came. He said he was an orthopaedic surgeon who specialized in marine trauma. He had been driving into Doolin and saw what was happening.

I was very cold and worried. I did not know how bad my injuries were. I was most afraid of internal bleeding. The surgeon checked me and said he could not feel signs of internal bleeding, but he thought I might have broken bones. Later I learned I had six spinal fractures, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in the hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for weeks. I could not work for five months. I had trouble moving, and I had stiffness and pain. Then doctors said I had post-traumatic stress. I felt anxious about everything and overreacted. I thought people were looking at me the wrong way. Loud noises were hard for me. I also had memory loss.

Three months before the accident, I opened a health-food shop. But I had to give it up because I could not work. It was the hardest year. Now it is behind me. I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building my own osteopathy practice. I understand patients more because I have been a patient.

I am thankful I am healthy. I want to stop other people from getting hurt. Many people trust dolphins and think they are always friendly. Who would think a dolphin could attack a person? If you see a dangerous animal with its teeth showing, you feel scared. But dolphins have a wide smile.

I am not angry at Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her area, and she is a wild and unpredictable animal. People need to know that. Many people come to swim with her and do not understand the danger. My injuries were said to be one of several injuries that summer.

After the man pulled me out, Dusty swam away. Then she came back and floated upright next to me, looking at me. We looked into each other’s eyes. I felt she was sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin. The anger was gone. The people on the pier were amazed. After that moment, the terror ended for me. I made peace with her.
