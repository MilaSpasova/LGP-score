Why do they do it? When you ask men who may be top referees in the future, they smile. This season, people have criticized referees more than before. Some former referees have even complained about the standards. This is surprising because referees often try to support each other.

So why do it? Why spend many hours driving around the country to enforce the rules? Some calls will upset people. Referees try to move up to higher levels, until they make decisions on TV in front of millions of viewers. People then watch them closely, using many camera angles and slow-motion replays.

One answer is seen on Lee Swabey’s face after he blows the final whistle in a match where Grimsby beat Woking 2–1. The match was at level 5 in the English league system. Swabey gets what referees hope for. “Twenty-two handshakes,” he says later, proudly. That means all the players shook his hand, and both managers said “well done.” For him, that is the best feeling. He loves the “buzz” of a game that goes smoothly. He says, “I wouldn’t spend so much time away from my family if this didn’t mean the world to me.”

Swabey is well thought of by Professional Game Match Officials Limited (PGMOL). He knew people were watching him at that match. PGMOL’s chief, Mike Riley, was there. Steve Dunn was also there. Dunn coaches referees at this level. They took notes and used a stopwatch to record important actions by the officials.

A few weeks earlier, Riley, Dunn, and another former referee, Peter Jones, went to another level-5 match. They watched another referee expected to move up: John Brooks. Brooks says, “I hope to have the opportunity to get promoted to the Premier League and officiate some of the top games in this country.”

But the main thing they saw was how Brooks handled a hard situation. The match had to be called off because the pitch was frozen. This is part of what Brooks must learn before he is trusted with bigger games. There are many problems a referee must deal with. Clubs often do not want a late postponement. They may still have to pay staff who came to work, but they will not earn any gate money.

Brooks called his coach for advice. He made a difficult but correct decision. Later, the club secretary arrived with envelopes to pay the officials for their time. At level 5, the match fee is £95, so it is clear these men do not do it for the money.

Brooks, like Swabey, wants to move up. He knows he must develop a thick skin and learn how to deal with disappointment. How does he feel when he watches TV and sees a referee attacked with angry comments? “Erm … not great,” he says. He remembers making a wrong decision once. The deciding goal was just offside. He says your heart sinks, and you cannot stop thinking about it.

He also says he wishes people understood how much time and effort referees put in. It is easy to criticize a decision, but referees do everything they can to get it right. Sometimes a referee will be unpopular. If you cannot handle that, he says, you are in the wrong job.

The former referees say today’s referees have much better support, education, and tools than they had. When Riley was a young referee, he bought books on psychology and nutrition because no one offered him information.

Brooks, in comparison, can phone his coach. They talk every week, discuss how games went, watch video of key decisions, and plan how to improve. Brooks also gets regular support from a sports psychologist, Liam Slack. He follows an exercise plan to help him cope with the 11km he runs during a game.

Brooks says psychology is very important. He says they talk about forgetting decisions and moving on. A big decision might happen in the first 30 seconds. After you decide, you must stay focused for the next 89 minutes. You cannot keep worrying about the earlier decision. Liam teaches techniques to let go of the decision. Brooks says this helps build mental toughness.

Jones says this modern approach can only help. He says that even when he refereed in professional football, he was really an amateur. He also worked for British Telecom. He might referee at Newcastle on a Wednesday evening and then be in Leicester at 9am the next morning talking to customers. He may not have slept. Training was not well planned. He says referees were amateurs in a professional world, compared to now.

When they talk about technology, the three former referees all support it. Riley says they favour anything that makes the referee’s job better and helps them be more effective on the field. The goal is to make fewer mistakes. Jones jokes that a bad decision can stay with you for a long time—“the rest of your life.”
