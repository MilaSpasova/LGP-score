Opposition to Western Australia’s shark cull has grown. Thousands of people went to beaches across Australia to ask the state’s premier to stop the policy. RSPCA Australia and Virgin Atlantic owner Richard Branson also spoke against it.

The policy involves catching and killing sharks longer than three metres. It started after what the state government called an “unprecedented” number of shark attacks on Western Australia’s coast. A 35-year-old surfer was killed in November 2013. He was the sixth person to die from a shark attack in two years.

But the Shark Attack File says that across Australia, there has been an average of one shark-related death each year for the last 50 years.

Kate Faehrmann, from the Sea Shepherd Conservation Society, spoke at a protest in Perth. She said the policy will not work. She said drumlines are used to catch sharks, but they can catch and kill many animals. She said they can kill sharks of different sizes, and also dolphins, turtles, and other animals. She said this is why many people do not want the policy.

Thousands of people protested at Perth’s Cottesloe Beach and Sydney’s Manly Beach. Hundreds also protested at Glenelg in south-west Adelaide, and at beaches in Victoria and Queensland. Faehrmann said the protests showed many Australians want sharks protected. She said that even though people are often afraid of sharks, many people still respect them and do not want them killed.

Anthony Joyce, a surfer who once had his foot caught in a shark’s mouth, said killing sharks will not make a difference.

The state government has not given an ongoing count of how many sharks have been killed. There have been reports of sharks smaller than three metres being released after being caught. Drumlines are floating drums anchored to the sea bed, with bait on hooks under them.

Conservationists say there is no evidence the cull will reduce shark attacks on humans. They say no earlier cull used only drumlines. Researchers at the University of Western Australia say the recent increase in attacks may be linked to the state having the fastest-growing population in Australia, not to more sharks.

Richard Peirce, chairman of the UK charity the Shark Trust, said the cull would not work and could attract more predators to the coast. He said the drumlines can attract other predators, especially if the lines stay in the water overnight, because hooked and injured animals may draw them in.

Around the world in 2012, there were 80 unprovoked shark attacks, and seven were fatal. Humans kill nearly 100m sharks each year. RSPCA Australia said it believes the cull is not justified. It said there is no evidence the increase in attacks is because there are more sharks. It said it fits with population changes and human behaviour, meaning more people are in the water.

Richard Branson told Fairfax Radio the policy was backfiring. He said Colin Barnett may have thought it would help tourism, but he thinks it will do the opposite. Branson said it makes the problem seem bigger than it is and may stop people from wanting to visit Perth and the countryside around it. He said it will only make people worried for no good reason.
