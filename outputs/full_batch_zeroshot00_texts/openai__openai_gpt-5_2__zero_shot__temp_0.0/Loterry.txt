When the lottery balls fall and all six numbers match, many winners start spending. They may buy an Audi, book a holiday in the US, and call an estate agent. A study says this is common for lottery millionaires.

The UK National Lottery started in 1994. Since then, it has made 3,000 millionaires. Together they have won more than £8.5bn, about £2.8m each on average. The study by Oxford Economics says their money also helped make 3,780 more millionaires among their children, family, and friends.

Most winners (59%) stop working right away. But 19% keep their normal job, and 31% do unpaid volunteer work. The study says 98% of winners’ spending stayed in the UK. It estimates that each winner’s spending keeps six people in full-time jobs for one year.

Winners added almost £750m to UK GDP and created more than £500m in tax money for the Exchequer. Most money was spent on homes. Winners spent £2.72bn on their main homes, and £170m to pay off debts and mortgages.

Winners also wanted steady income, so they spent £2.125bn on investments. They gave £1.17bn as gifts to family and friends. They spent £680m on cars and holidays.

Camelot, which runs the UK National Lottery, paid for the study to mark 3,000 winners. The study used research from 100 winners who won £1m or more. It found that the 3,000 winners bought 7,958 houses or flats in the UK, about 2.7 each, and spent £3.3bn. Most winners (82%) moved to a new main home and spent about £900,000 on average.

Some winners added special things to their homes. Almost a third (29%) bought a hot tub. A walk-in wardrobe was important for 28%. Nearly a quarter (24%) chose a home behind electric gates. About 22% had a games room, and 7% put in a snooker table.

Bigger homes need help to look after them. About 30% hired a cleaner, and 24% hired a gardener. A small number (5%) hired a beautician.

Audi was the favourite car for 16% of winners. Range Rovers and BMWs were also popular (11% each). Mercedes was 10%, and Land Rovers were 5%. Winners spent £463m on 17,190 cars. The average price of their favourite car was £46,116.

Holidays were important too. Most winners (68%) stayed in five-star hotels overseas. The US was the top place to visit for 27%, and the Caribbean was next at 9%. In the UK, caravan sales also grew. In the past 18 years, 10% of millionaires bought a caravan, creating about £7.4m in sales.

Some winners used their money to start businesses. About 15% started their own business, 9% helped others start one, and 6% invested in or bought other people’s businesses. The study says these businesses employ 3,195 people.

Andy Logan, who wrote the report, said a win affects more people than they expected. It helps friends and family, and it also affects the UK economy because so much is spent in the UK. He said each win creates a ripple effect in this generation and often the next.
