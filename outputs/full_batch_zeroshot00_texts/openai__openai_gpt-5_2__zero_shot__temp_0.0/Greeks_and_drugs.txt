Nobody knows what came first in Greece: the economic crisis or shisha, a drug now called the “cocaine of the poor”. But people agree on one thing: shisha can kill. It costs €2 or less for one use, and it has spread in Greece, a country badly hit by Europe’s financial crisis.

“As drugs go, it is the worst. It burns your insides, it makes you aggressive and ensures that you go totally mad,” said Maria, a former heroin addict. “But it is cheap and it is easy to get, and it is what everyone is doing.”

This drug problem has put extra pressure on Athens’s health services, which were already struggling because of very severe budget cuts.

Shisha is used by many homeless Greeks who ended up on the streets because of poverty and despair. Addicts and officials say shisha is a type of crystal meth. They say it can make users very violent. It is often mixed with dangerous things like battery acid, engine oil, and even shampoo.

It is easy to find and easy to make, which fits a society that feels there is little hope.

“It is a killer, but it also makes you want to kill,” said Konstantinos, a drug addict. “You can kill without understanding that you have done it. And it is spreading faster than death. A lot of users have died.”

Charalampos Poulopoulos, head of Kethea, a leading anti-drug centre in Greece, said shisha shows how bad the crisis has become. The crisis has brought record poverty and unemployment. He called shisha an “austerity drug”. He said drug dealers make cheap synthetic drugs for people who cannot afford more expensive drugs like heroin and cocaine.

“The crisis has given dealers the possibility to promote a new, cheap drug, a cocaine for the poor,” said Poulopoulos at a centre for addicts in Exarcheia in Athens. He said shisha can be sniffed or injected. It can be made in home labs, without special knowledge. He said it is extremely dangerous.

Across Greece, six years of recession have caused serious harm. Depression has risen a lot, and so have drug and alcohol abuse. Crime has increased as austerity measures cut ordinary people’s income by 40%. Prostitution has also risen, because it is an easy way to get money to buy drugs.

“Desperation is such that many women agree to engage in unprotected sex because that way they’ll make more money,” said Eleni Marini, a psychologist with Kethea. She said shisha has been linked to a very strong sexual drive, but it also harms clear thinking. She said they are seeing more pregnancies among drug addicts who do prostitution. In 2012, two sex workers gave birth on the streets of Athens.

At the same time, suicides have increased, and HIV infections have spread so much that it has become like an epidemic. Drug addicts, a group believed to be about 25,000 people, have become more self-destructive. Experts say young Greeks are leading this trend. Youth unemployment is at 64%, the highest in the EU.

“The crisis has created a widespread sense of pessimism,” said Poulopoulos. He said people who might have stopped using drugs now have no reason to stop. He said there is misery, and people who think they will not find work are becoming more self-destructive. In Athens, where the crisis hit hardest, shisha is part of this.

Greece’s coalition government, led mainly by conservatives, has tried to deal with the problem by pushing drug users and other homeless people out of the city centre. Police operations cleared central streets, crowded doorways, and malls.

“But with such actions, authorities are only sweeping the problem under the carpet,” said Poulopoulos. He said this pushes people even further to the edge of society and into the hands of drug dealers who offer them protection.

Even though more people need help than ever, state-funded groups like Kethea have had their budgets cut by one third. This was requested by the “troika” (the EC, ECB, and IMF), which has been supporting Greece’s debt-hit economy.

Since the crisis began in 2009, Kethea has lost 70 of its 500 staff. The cuts happened even though studies show that for every euro spent on programmes like Kethea, the state saves about €6 in criminal justice and healthcare costs. “The cuts we have witnessed are a false economy, a huge mistake,” said Poulopoulos.

On the streets of Athens, where shisha has spread, people are more afraid. They fear that austerity does not only fail to help—it kills.
