Some famous people refused to do the Ice Bucket Challenge, like David Cameron, Barack Obama, and Pamela Anderson. Others did it, like George W Bush, Benedict Cumberbatch, and Stephen Hawking.

The Ice Bucket Challenge raised $100 million for a US charity for a motor-neurone disease, and £4.5 million for a British one. It also raised thousands more for charities in Hong Kong and Australia. But soon, many people began to criticize it.

Some people said celebrities were showing off their bodies. Some said people were having fun but not donating money. Others complained it wasted water. Commentators, animal-rights groups, and environmentalists made these complaints. The US ALS Association (not the same as Britain’s MND Association) was also criticized because its top staff were paid high salaries.

The challenge kept growing. Here is how it works: a person talks on camera about the charity, then pours a bucket of ice water over their head (or a friend does it). Then they name three people who must do the same or donate.

It started in the US in July, but people disagree about where it began. It first appeared on mainstream American TV on 15 July. The ALS Association tried to trademark the phrase “Ice Bucket Challenge,” but stopped after criticism. A spokeswoman, Carrie Munk, said they understood people’s concern and withdrew the trademark applications. The ALS Association had not yet answered the criticism about its high costs and wage bills.

At the same time, 31 August to 5 September was World Water Week. People met in Stockholm to discuss the world’s water crisis. The charity WaterAid asked people to use recycled bath water, water from garden butts, or sea water.

Douglas Graham, fundraising director of the MND Association, said criticism was expected. He said the donations were a wonderful surprise and they were very grateful. The money helps a small charity that supports people with a serious disease. The disease has no cure and kills five people a day in the UK.

Pamela Anderson, a longtime animal-rights activist, wrote an open letter to the ALS Association. She said she could not support it because of animal experiments. Some US stars rejected the challenge because of California’s drought. Actor Matt Damon used water from his toilets and said much of the world has less clean drinking water. Actor Verne Troyer used milk for environmental reasons. On the Scottish island of Colonsay, people blamed the challenge for a water shortage after many of the island’s 135 people joined in.

In Australia, TV anchorman Lincoln Humphries apologized for his strong “no, thanks” reply after being nominated. He said people should not waste water and ice. He shared a list of charities that help communities in great need. He said people who have more than they need should donate what they can to those who need it most.

Another criticism was that small charities might not be able to handle so much extra money. The MND Association disagreed. Graham said they could cope. He said they fund world-class research to find causes, and to find a treatment or cure. They also provide care and support for 3,500 people. He said the disease gets worse very fast and is costly to manage. More than 50% die within two years of diagnosis. He said it is heartbreaking to see people decline in just a few months.

For many people connected to the disease, the awareness from the challenge is as valuable as the money. Graham said it is priceless. Normally, the MND Association gets about 300,000 website hits in a year. On one recent day, it got 330,000.

Graham said they could not have created this attention on their own. He said all charities are worthwhile, and some people may want to give to a different one. In 2013, British people gave £62 billion to charity, and he said people should be proud. He said the extra money is wonderful for them, and they will plan how to use it in the best way. He said every penny will be used for good.
