Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8th April, 2014, she told people about an unusual moment. She received a message in a bottle, 101 years after her grandfather threw it into the Baltic Sea. People think it may be the world’s oldest message in a bottle. A museum in Germany, which is showing it, gave it to her.

Erdmann, 62, said it was a big surprise. She said a man came to her door and said he had mail from her grandfather. He explained that someone had found a message in a bottle, and the name on the card was her grandfather’s name.

The visitor was a genealogical researcher. He found Erdmann in Berlin after the letter was given to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg.

The bottle was a brown beer bottle. It had been in the water for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it in his catch in the Baltic Sea near the city of Kiel. Holger von Neuhoff, a curator at the museum, said this was the oldest bottled message he had seen. He said the museum has older documents, but not with the bottle. With both the bottle and the document, this is the oldest right now. He said it is in very good condition.

Researchers think Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913. He was hiking with a nature appreciation group. He was 20 years old then.

Most of the postcard could not be read. But people could read the address in Berlin on the front. They could also read his polite request: he asked the person who found it to send the note to his home address.

Erdmann said he also put two stamps from that time into the bottle, so the finder would not have to pay. She said he did not think it would take 101 years.

She said the message made her feel emotional. She did not know her grandfather because he died at age 54, six years before she was born.

She said she knew very little about him. Later she learned that he was a writer. She said he was open-minded, believed in freedom, and thought everyone should respect each other. She said he did a lot for young people. Later he travelled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful because she could see where her family roots came from.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and travelling around the world. She said she is open-minded too. She said what her grandfather taught his two daughters, her mother taught her, and she then taught her sons.

Even though she was happy to get the message, she said she hoped other people would not throw bottles with messages into the sea today. She said the sea is already full of bottles and rubbish.

The message and the bottle will be shown at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts will try to read the rest of the text. It is not clear what will happen to the bottle later, but Erdmann hopes it will stay at the museum.

She said they want to add some photos with the bottle. This way visitors can see the young man who threw the bottle into the water.
