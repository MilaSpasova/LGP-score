On a normal day, Starbucks shops are busy places. People meet friends, hold business meetings, and join reading groups. Many people go there for a morning coffee. But Starbucks may need to be careful.

A direct action group called UK Uncut plans to take over dozens of Starbucks branches in the UK. They want to turn them into crèches, refuges, and homeless shelters. They want to show what they say are Starbucks’ tax avoidance tactics.

UK Uncut announced this plan on the same day a Starbucks executive was questioned by the House of Commons Public Accounts Committee. The questions were about why Starbucks paid no corporation tax in the UK in the past three years. This happened even though senior US managers said the company’s business in Britain was profitable.

Starbucks’ Chief Financial Officer, Troy Alstead, appeared before the committee. He tried to improve the company’s reputation, which has been hurt by this controversy.

MPs also blamed officials at HM Revenue & Customs (HMRC). They said HMRC had cosy relationships with big businesses. Conservative MP Richard Bacon said about the Starbucks arrangements: “It smells. And it doesn’t smell of coffee – it smells bad.”

UK Uncut says government cuts, especially cuts that affect women, are linked to tax avoidance by big international companies.

UK Uncut activist Sarah Greene said refuges and rape crisis centres could face cuts unless companies paid their fair share of tax. HMRC estimates about £32bn was lost to tax avoidance in 2011.

Greene said the government could get billions to fund important services by clamping down on tax avoidance.

UK Uncut became well known after sit-ins at Vodafone stores, Topshop, and Fortnum & Mason. The group focused on Starbucks last month after a Reuters investigation. Reuters said Starbucks had paid only £8.6m in corporation tax since it started in the UK in 1998, even though total sales were £3bn.

UK Uncut campaigner Anna Walker said: “We’ve chosen to really highlight the impact of the cuts on women. So there is going to be a real focus on transforming Starbucks into those services that are being cut by the government … [such as] refuges and crèches.”

She said Starbucks is a good target because it is on many high streets across the country. She said this matters to UK Uncut because people can take action in their local areas.

Other international organizations have also been criticized for their UK accounts. Amazon, eBay, Facebook, Google, and Ikea have paid little or no corporation tax, even though they have big businesses in Britain.

But pollsters at YouGov’s BrandIndex say Starbucks’ image has been hurt the most.

Starbucks says it pays the correct amount of tax. Chief Executive Howard Schultz said in a statement: “Starbucks has always paid taxes in the UK despite recent suggestions to the contrary.

“Over the last three years alone, our company has paid more than £160m in various taxes, including National Insurance contributions, VAT and business rates.”

Margaret Hodge, who leads the Public Accounts Committee, told parliament last month that Apple, eBay, Facebook, Google, and Starbucks had avoided nearly £900m of tax. Prime Minister David Cameron replied: “I’m not happy with the current situation. I think [HMRC] needs to look at it very carefully. We do need to make sure we are encouraging these businesses to invest in our country as they are, but they should be paying fair taxes as well.”

A Starbucks spokeswoman said tax law can be very complex. She said Starbucks respects and follows tax laws and accounting rules in each of the 61 countries where it does business, including the UK. She said Starbucks is committed to the UK for the long term, and that the company has posted facts about its UK tax practices on its website.

The spokeswoman also said Starbucks helps the UK economy in other ways. She said Starbucks spends hundreds of millions of pounds with local suppliers on milk, cakes, sandwiches, and on store design and renovations. She said that when indirect employment is included, Starbucks’ extended economic impact on the UK economy is more than £80m each year.

She said Starbucks hopes UK Uncut will respect the wellbeing of its employees and customers. She also said Starbucks creates jobs and apprenticeships, and pays its fair share of taxes in the UK.
