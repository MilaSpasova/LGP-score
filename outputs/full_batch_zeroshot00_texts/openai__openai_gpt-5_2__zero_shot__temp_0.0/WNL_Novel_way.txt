Many governments in Europe want a “magic” answer to rising unemployment. But in the parts of the EU that are doing the worst, unemployment keeps going up. Strong words do not help people get jobs faster.

In one struggling part of Italy, a mayor thinks he has an answer for his town’s long problem of not having enough work. Some people say it is not really a solution, but more like giving up. Valter Piscedda is the centre-left mayor of Elmas, a small town near Cagliari, the capital of Sardinia. He wants to pay residents to leave. The town council will pay for ten unemployed local people to take intensive English lessons, buy a cheap flight, and look for jobs elsewhere in Europe.

“This is, above all, an idea born of common sense and experience,” he told the Guardian. He said that for the past year and a half, and especially in recent months, many young people have come to him almost every day. They feel hopeless about finding work. Some ask for help to find work in Elmas. Others have tried everything and feel so discouraged that they do not want to stay and wait. They want to go abroad to get work experience and life experience.

He said his idea was to do everything the council can do, so that people who want to get experience abroad can do it.

Italy’s national economy is still weak. Sardinia, like much of southern and central Italy, has very high unemployment. The overall jobless rate was 17.7% in the second quarter of 2014, according to Italy’s National Institute of Statistics, Istat. More than 54% of people under 25 are out of work.

For the Adesso Parto (Now I’m leaving) programme, Elmas’s council has set aside Û12,000. Money is given on a first-come, first-served basis. Applicants must be between 18 and 50. They must be out of work and must have lived in the town for three years. They do not need a university degree. Their yearly income must be no more than Û15,000.

Encouraging people to leave is a sensitive idea now, because many Italians leave their country every year, including many smart young graduates. But Piscedda, who is in the Democratic Party of Prime Minister Matteo Renzi, says he is not causing a “brain drain.” He believes the people he sends away may return “and give me back 100 times what they were given.” Most of all, he wants the scheme to help the people who need it the most.

He said it is for people who have no other help and it is their last chance. He said it lets them keep their dignity, so they do not have to ask friends for money or put pressure on families who cannot afford it.

He added that earlier in 2014, the council started another plan. Businesses were given financial rewards to hire young workers from Elmas. He said they advertised 20 jobs and received 120 applications.

People in Elmas have different opinions about the new plan. Alessandro Macis said there is little work in the town. He said going abroad to learn about work and other cultures can be very valuable. He gave an example: the son of a friend did not study much, but he went to London and is doing well. He started as a waiter. Now he is a cook and is learning English.

Other people were confused. Consuelo Melis, who works behind the bar in a local cafe, said she heard about it and thought it was strange. She asked why the town does not use the money to help people stay and work in Elmas. On Twitter, many people reacted with shock. Marco Patavino called it “the state’s admission of defeat.” Carlo Mazzaggio said, “Institutions are raising the white flag.”

Piscedda said he is not stopping. He said his online critics are probably people who are not in need. He said he deals with people’s problems every day and must try to solve them. He said if people had another choice, they would not be asking for help.

He said the work he can create as mayor is only temporary. He can pay for a piazza to be cleaned, and cleaned again, and for streets to be cleaned. But he said these are short-term jobs that only give a little money for a few months. He said he wants to do more than that.
