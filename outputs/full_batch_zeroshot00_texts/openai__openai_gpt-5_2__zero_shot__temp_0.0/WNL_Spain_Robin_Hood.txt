People call Enric Duran the “Robin Hood of the banks.” He took many bank loans, almost 500,000 euros in total. He did not plan to pay the money back. Instead, he gave the money to projects that supported alternatives to capitalism.

Duran hid for 14 months. He says he is not sorry, even though he could go to jail. In a Skype interview from a secret place, he said, “I’m proud of what I did.” He said the money created chances for people. He said it helped start a movement and build a strong network of groups working on these ideas.

From 2006 to 2008, Duran took 68 business and personal loans from 39 banks in Spain. He gave the money to social activists. He paid for talks against capitalism and for TV cameras for a media network. He said these social movements were building new ways to live, but they did not have enough money or good ways to communicate. He also said the economic system depended on constant growth and created money “out of nothing.”

He said taking the loans was his way to challenge and speak against this situation. He started slowly. He first filled out some loan forms using his real information. The banks said no, but he wanted to learn what they asked for.

Later, the former table-tennis coach made a complicated system of accounts, payments, and transfers. He said he was always learning. By summer 2007, he learned how to make it work. He applied for loans using the name of a fake TV production company. He said he then got a lot of money: 492,000 euros.

In 2009, Duran was arrested in Spain. Six of the 39 banks made charges against him. He spent two months in prison. Then he got out after paying bail of 50,000 euros. In February 2013, he faced up to eight years in prison. He decided to run away instead of going to trial. He said he did not see the court system as legitimate because he did not accept its authority.

He said his actions were part of a worldwide debate about the economic crisis. The timing helped the anti-capitalist movement get more attention. Many people in Spain were looking for other ways because the system had badly harmed their lives.

He said people in Spain today might understand his actions better. But he also said they would not be needed now. He said the anti-capitalist movement has grown from a small group to one supported by thousands of Spaniards. He pointed to popular movements like the Indignados.

Duran said the movement has become able to support itself. “We now have the capacity to generate resources,” he said. He added, in an ironic way, that this is what banks give credit for: to help you move forward and become independent. Duran is also focusing on Spain’s justice system by supporting restorative justice. He said some people in Spain believe banks do not work and think he does not owe anything. He said, “I’ve already done my work.” But he also said some people do not agree with him, and he thinks he should answer them.

He said he might offer something back to banks in a different way. He said he learned a lot from getting loans by fraud. He said he could share ideas about what works and what does not work, for the general public and for bank workers.
