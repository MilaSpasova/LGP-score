Junior Smart knows a lot about gangs. He is 36 now. He says his life has two clear parts.

When he was in his late teens, his mother died. After that, he joined a gang in south London. He says the gang filled an empty space in his life.

He says, “They became my new support group. At first it was just a bit of fun. Then it became more serious and more about making money. They got involved in crime.”

At school, Smart failed his GCSEs. He retook them at college and passed them all. He got a full-time job in administration and also worked as a DJ. But he was also making money illegally with the gang.

Later, he was arrested for serious drug-related offences. He was sent to prison for 12 years. He says this shocked him. He says the first night after his arrest was a big wake-up call. He says he was living two lives: one with his friends and another with their enemies. He says he spent a long time trying to change himself.

Today, Junior Smart runs a team with 12 full-time workers and six volunteers. Their goal is to help young criminals and gang members leave crime. Many workers are ex-offenders. A few are still serving their sentences, but they can go out in the day to help. The team works with the police, the probation service, and other voluntary organizations. They help people who feel trapped and scared in violent gangs across London.

Smart says his change from gang member to helper started in prison. He saw a prison system that was failing prisoners. He remembers a drug addict he became friends with who kept coming back to prison. Smart says many people knew what they needed to change, but the prison system only dealt with the main offence.

He gives an example of a man with a £300-a-week cocaine habit. The man paid for it by burglary. He would even go into houses when he knew people were there. Smart says the drug addiction was not dealt with. He says it was like a “revolving door,” with people going in and out of prison.

Because of cases like this, Smart became a prison “listener.” This is a prisoner who helps new prisoners feel calmer and get through their first days. He then made a plan to start his own program after prison, using ex-offenders to help others stay out of prison. He was released early, after five years. He says he then got the chance to use his plan.

Smart is asked if things are getting better after the riots of last August and the government promise to crack down on gangs. He gives some praise to the police and says some of their work has been good. But he criticizes the government approach. He says the government often thinks that arresting a gang leader ends the problem. He agrees with a report that says arresting leaders can even make things worse. He says removing the leader can upset the gang.

He explains it like this: when the top person is arrested, people fight for power. They ask who was most loyal and who had the most respect. He says it is like a family. He says this can lead to more violence. He says arresting leaders may not have a long-term effect, and it may not even give the community a break.

He says the streets can become more dangerous. He says when a leader is gone, gangs may recruit more young people. Other gangs may think the gang is weak, and fights can start between areas. He also says the leader can make alliances in prison, or try to take control again after release, and violence can happen.

Smart says gangs are now recruiting many more children in primary schools. The youngest members are called “tinies.” He says they can be as young as eight to eleven. He says young children are used to protect older gang members from risk. He says the young ones may be the ones who do street dealing or even stabbing.

Smart says the work is very hard because there is not enough government funding and there is not enough long-term support. He says it is hard to help a young person who has been earning £300 a week illegally. He says it is even harder now because unemployment is high and benefits have been cut.

Still, his project has well over 1,000 clients and is getting results. Fewer than 20% of the people who get help offend again. Smart says he will not give up on anyone. He says, “I don’t think that about anybody.”
