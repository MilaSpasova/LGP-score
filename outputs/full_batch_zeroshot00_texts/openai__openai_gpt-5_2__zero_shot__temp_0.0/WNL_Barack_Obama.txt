Barack Obama told young people not to be hopeless. He said they should talk with people who have different beliefs if they want to change the world.

On the last day of his final trip to Britain as US president, Obama spoke to 500 youth leaders at a town hall meeting in London. He said they should not believe that some forces cannot be controlled. He quoted JFK, saying that human-made problems can be solved by people.

He told A-level students and UK–US exchange students that they have better tools than ever to make a difference. He said to reject pessimism and cynicism, and to believe that progress is possible. He also said progress is not guaranteed, and it takes struggle, discipline, and faith.

Obama said he knew young people face hard times, with big changes like 9/11 and 7/7, and with constant bad news on the internet and Twitter.

People cheered when he was introduced. He spoke about his policies, including healthcare and education.

He encouraged the audience to talk with people who have different political beliefs. He said this can help people learn to compromise.

Obama said social attitudes are changing. He said it is not happening fast enough, but young people should keep pushing, and that brave young people have helped cause change.

When asked what he is most proud of, Obama said he was proud of healthcare reforms, and the US response to the 2008 financial crisis. He said it helped save the world from a great depression. He also named diplomatic deals with Iran and the response to the Ebola crisis as important parts of his presidency. He said he felt he had stayed true to himself.

He told young people not to give up if poverty is not ended after five years. He quoted Dr Martin Luther King, who said the moral universe is long but bends toward justice.

When asked about the Transatlantic Trade and Investment Partnership (TTIP), Obama said the answer to globalization is not to shut off from the world. He also said it was important to pay attention to workers’ rights.

Before Obama arrived, Tanya Williams, a community engagement officer, told the Guardian she liked Obama and was excited to hear him speak.

Oliver Sidorczuk, 26, said people were very excited. He said he wanted to ask Obama about electoral rights and about joining a campaign to ask David Cameron for automatic registration.

Obama ended the session by answering a question from a young Sikh Londoner. The young man asked about racial profiling at airports and being mistaken for a Muslim.

Obama said that even though some people have “crazy ideology,” pluralism is important. He said he had visited a mosque a few months earlier and said America’s greatest allies are American Muslims who are well integrated and doing well economically.

Furqan Naeem, a campaigner from Manchester, said he visited the States on a US embassy community leaders programme. He said he saw important work Obama did to celebrate America’s diversity and bring communities together.

Kenny Imafidon, the managing director of the youth organization ClearView Research, said the event was a great opportunity. He said he would remember Obama’s advice about meeting people with different politics and making compromises. He also mentioned what Obama said about being a good leader and finding great talent.

Later, Obama met Jeremy Corbyn. Corbyn said they had an “excellent” 90-minute discussion. Corbyn said they talked about challenges in postindustrial societies, the power of global corporations, the growing use of technology, and its effects around the world. Corbyn said Obama’s comments about Britain’s membership of the EU were also discussed briefly.

After the meeting, Obama joined Cameron to play golf at the Grove in Chandlers Cross, Hertfordshire. Obama ended the day with a dinner with the prime minister and the US ambassador, Matthew Barzun, at the ambassador’s home, before travelling on to Germany.
