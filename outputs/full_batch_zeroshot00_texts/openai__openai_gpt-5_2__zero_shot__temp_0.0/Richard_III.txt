At a special press conference at Leicester University, experts shared new details about Richard III. They did not only talk about finding his body in a car park. They also described his terrible last moments and the humiliating way his naked body was treated after he died.

People cheered when Richard Buckley, the lead archaeologist, said the team was sure “beyond reasonable doubt” that they had found Richard III, the last Plantagenet king. His spine was badly bent by scoliosis, and his body had been pushed to fit into a quickly dug grave in Grey Friars church. The hole was a little too small.

Many kinds of experts explained their evidence. Scientists did DNA tests. Others used computer images to look closely at the bones. Genealogists found a far-off descendant with matching DNA. Academics read old writings about the king’s death and burial.

Philippa Langley from the Richard III Society said, “What a morning. What a story.” She had pushed the project for years, even when many people did not believe it. She also helped find money from around the world when funds were almost gone.

Work has begun on a new tomb in Leicester Cathedral, only 100 yards from where the body was found. A ceremony will place him in the new grave there, probably next year. Leicester’s Museums’ Service is planning a new visitor centre in an old school building looking over the site.

Richard died at Bosworth on 22 August 1485. He was the last English king to die in battle. The researchers showed a picture of the base of his skull cut by one powerful blow. They think it was from a halberd, a medieval weapon with a sharp iron axe blade weighing about two kilos on a wooden pole. It was swung very close to him. The blade likely went several centimetres into his brain. Bone expert Jo Appleby said he would have passed out at once and died soon after.

This injury supports old reports that he died fighting closely in the middle of the battle and after being knocked off his horse. Shakespeare later wrote the line: “A horse! A horse! My kingdom for a horse!” Another sword cut also went through the bone into the brain and could have killed him. But many other wounds happened after he died. This suggests people carried out a cruel ritual. His body was stripped, mocked, and cut as it was brought back to Leicester.

One very bad injury was a stab through the right buttock into the pelvis. It happened after death, because armour would have protected him in battle. This supports the story that his naked body was carried back over a horse and abused. Bob Savage, a medieval arms expert from the Royal Armouries, said this wound was likely made by a sharp knife or dagger like a workman might carry, not a war weapon.

Michael Ibsen, a furniture maker born in Canada, was shown to be a descendant of Richard’s sister. He heard the confirmation on Sunday and listened quietly. He said, “My head is no clearer now than when I first heard the news.” He also said many hundreds of people died on that field. Richard was a king, but one of the dead. Times were very violent, and deaths would not have been pretty or quick.

Mathew Morris first found the body in the first hour of the first day of digging. He did not think it was the king. A machine was still removing the tarmac from the council car park. Local historians and the Richard III Society had studied for years and believed this was the place where Grey Friars church once stood. The priests there had bravely taken the king’s body and buried him in a quickly dug grave, probably still naked, but in a place of honour near the high altar. The leg bones showed through the soil, and they were covered again.

Ten days later, on 5 September, more digging showed Morris had found the right spot. He returned with Lin Foxhall, head of the archaeology department, to dig out the body. Morris said they lifted the arms, legs, and skull first, and then moved slowly to the body. Only when they saw the twisted spine did he think, “My word, I think we’ve got him.”

Langley believes Richard was the true king, the last king of the north, and a brave leader. She thinks he was hurt by strong Tudor propaganda, helped by Shakespeare. There is still the dark story of the princes in the tower. In Richard’s time, people said the children Edward V and his brother Richard were called illegitimate when Richard III took the throne. They were put in the Tower of London and were never seen alive again. It is not certain that bones found at the tower centuries later are theirs, but there may be more DNA work to do.
