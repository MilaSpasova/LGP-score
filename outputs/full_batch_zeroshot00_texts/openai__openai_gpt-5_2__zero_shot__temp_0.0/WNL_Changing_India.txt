Every morning, before it gets hot in India’s capital, a small group of old friends meet. On the dry grass near the India Gate monument in the centre of Delhi, they stretch, breathe, and meditate.

“It is the only healthy way to start the day. Much better than an egg or a sandwich or a cup of tea,” said Arvind Singh at 6.15am as he did breathing exercises on a bench.

Singh is a 42-year-old salesman. He and his friends are not the only ones. Across India, in crowded cities, people do the same thing in the green spaces that are left.

On 21 June, the new International Day of Yoga, Narendra Modi, India’s prime minister, hopes the world will join. The grass near India Gate will become the place for what they hope will be the biggest single yoga session ever. Up to 45,000 people may do a 35-minute routine.

The group will include 64-year-old Modi, most of his government, and, they hope, some celebrities. Officials have been sent to find volunteers from many countries to show it is an international event.

Getting Indians, and other people, to do yoga has become important for Modi. He led his Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) to a big election win in 2014. In May 2015, schools were told to make sure students went to yoga events planned for the big demonstration in Delhi, even though it is on a Sunday.

Officials have said yoga may become required for India’s police officers, who are known for being out of shape. They also said free daily yoga lessons would be offered to three million civil servants and their families. Air India, the national airline, said it will add yoga for trainee pilots. Some senior politicians also said that doing more yoga could reduce high levels of sexual violence in the country.

Modi is an ascetic. He is a committed vegetarian and he also practises yoga. He suggested an international yoga day when he spoke to the United Nations in New York in 2014.

He said, “Yoga is an invaluable gift of India’s ancient tradition. It brings together mind and body, thought and action, restraint and fulfilment, and harmony between people and nature. It is not only exercise. It is about feeling oneness with yourself, the world and nature.” He also said yoga could help in tackling climate change.

Yoga is between 3,000 and 6,000 years old. It began somewhere on the Indian subcontinent, possibly among religious ascetics. Its meditation and physical exercises have long been linked to local religions, including Buddhism and Jainism, and also Hinduism, which is practised by 80% of Indians.

Modi once worked as an organizer for a hardline Hindu nationalist organization. He has been criticized before for supporting a view of Indian culture that leaves little room for other traditions. One commentator called the 21 June event “a mix of cultural nationalism, commercialization and subtle coercion”.

Novelist Ajaz Ashraf wrote on India’s Scroll website that there is hope of bringing “modern-day gurus and their many followers” into the BJP, and that these followers are mostly the urban middle classes.

Other people point to a court ruling in the United States that said yoga is not always linked to religion. A court in California said that yoga may be religious in some situations, but yoga classes in the San Diego district were “devoid of any religious, mystical or spiritual trappings”. This happened after two parents said yoga in schools promoted Hinduism and held back Christianity.

Amish Tripathi, a writer of best-selling novels set 4,000 years ago in India that retell stories from Hindu mythology, said people in his books practise yoga.

He said that in ancient India, yoga was part of daily life, including both the body and the mind. He also said every culture gives something to the world, and yoga is India’s gift.

Suneel Singh, a guru in south Delhi, agreed that yoga does not belong to one religion. He said, “Is t’ai chi just Chinese? Is football just English? Same with yoga. It is a complete package for everybody’s body and a cheap way to keep you healthy.”
