In 2005, Pope Benedict XVI said he came as “a simple, humble worker in God’s vineyard”. On a grey, cold, windy Monday in February, he ended his time as Pope in a similar way. He was like an old worker who could not ignore his back pain and could not rely on his strength anymore.

Because he was a very traditional pope, he gave his message in Latin. He was the first German pope in modern times, and he gave the exact time he would leave. He told the cardinals in the Vatican: “From 28 February 2013, at 20.00 hours”, the seat of Rome, the seat of Saint Peter, would be empty, and the people in charge would need to call a conclave to choose a new pope.

A Mexican church leader, Monsignor Oscar Sanchéz Barba from Guadalajara, was there. He was in Rome to hear the date for a canonization that he helped with. He said they were all in the Sala del Concistoro on the third loggia of the Apostolic Palace. After the Pope gave the canonization date, 12 May, he took a sheet of paper and read from it.

Sanchéz Barba said everyone was left speechless. The cardinals looked at each other. They could not believe the man they saw as God’s representative on earth was giving up the job.

Angelo Sodano, the Dean of the College of Cardinals, spoke next, and he seemed to have known before. He said they had listened with confusion and could hardly believe it. He then promised the Pope the cardinals’ loyalty. At the end, the Pope blessed them and left. Sanchéz Barba said it was very simple. Then everyone left in silence. There was complete silence and sadness.

John Thavis, who reported on the Holy See for 30 years, said he had a feeling the Pope might resign. He planned his return to Rome from the US because of this, and another Vatican watcher confirmed it. Thavis said that in a long interview from 2010, published as Light of the World, Benedict had said it would be right to leave if he felt he could not do the job anymore. Thavis tried to guess when Benedict would resign. He thought 22 February, the Feast of the Chair of St Peter, was most likely, but he was wrong.

Soon after the announcement, the Vatican said the Pope’s choice was brave. They said Benedict was facing reality: modern medicine can keep people alive long after they are strong enough to lead a huge worldwide organization. Thavis agreed. He said it was courageous because Benedict was leaving even though he was not sick, and because he was tired, not because he was seriously ill. But some people wondered if there was more to the story, and if the Pope knew more about his health than the Vatican had said.

Benedict explained that he thought about both his body and his mind. He said that to lead the “bark of Saint Peter” and preach the Gospel, a person needs strength of mind and body, and his strength had gotten worse in recent months. He said he had to admit he could not do the job well.

More ideas and rumors were expected around the Vatican in the coming days and weeks, like after the death of Pope John Paul I in 1978, only 33 days after he was chosen. Some people were already guessing that something might soon be revealed about Benedict’s past. The Vatican would likely reject these ideas. Still, the writer says it is understandable, because what Benedict did was extremely important.

Outside St Peter’s Basilica, Julia Rochester from London, who called herself a lapsed Catholic, was still thinking about what the resignation meant. She asked, “If you’re God’s chosen one, how do you choose not be chosen?” Many practicing Catholics would ask their priests the same question. In his first speech as Pope, Benedict had said he was not fully fit for the task, but that God could work even with “insufficient tools”. Over the last eight years, it seems he stopped believing that.
