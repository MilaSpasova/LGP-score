Introduction
Did you know that in the UK, restaurants do not have to give tips to staff by law?

A government review, released in May 2016, asked workers, employers, and customers what they think about tipping. After this, ministers said they wanted to change the rules so low-paid workers get the money customers leave for them. The government announced a two-month consultation with ideas to stop unfair tipping.

The review also found that some waiters are charged up to a 15% administration fee on tips paid by credit or debit card. In some restaurants, the government saw evidence that waiters were forced to give their own cash if they did not collect enough tips to cover these fees. The government said it would think about banning employers from charging this admin fee or making other deductions from tips.

The government also said customers should know that tips are voluntary. It asked for a clearer system, with restaurants showing their tipping policy clearly.

We asked waiters around the UK what they think about tipping, including how much tip money they take home, whether it is fair, and what would make it better.

1. Rodri, 37, London: “Around 50% of a waiter’s income is tips”
Average tips: £60 per eight-hour shift
I think they treat waiters best in ... the US

Things have become fairer since the tipping scandal in summer 2015. At that time, it was revealed that many restaurants were taking tips from staff as administration fees. But you still hear bad stories from new staff at some chains. I have heard of restaurants not giving chefs a share of the tronc, which is a system used to share out tips. Some restaurants take 4% of all sales to pay the chefs and as an admin fee. So if you sell £1,000 of food, the company takes £40, no matter how much you make in tips. This means if a table has a £100 bill and does not tip, the waiter has to pay £4 from their own money. This can create a culture of trying to grab money and can lead to bad service. If a table does not tip, the waiter can feel the company is taking from them and may start to resent the customers.

I average about £60 in tips per shift. On a good night it can be over £100, and on a bad night it is around £40. About 50% of a waiter’s income is tips. All restaurants should have to publicly say exactly how tipping works. In America, it is good because everyone tips.

2. Elle, 22, Edinburgh: “We never know whether it’s fair”
Average tips: £20 per eight-hour shift
I think they treat waiters best in ... France

I work three part-time jobs on zero-hours contracts in catering and hospitality. My main day job is in a cafe, where the small team works both front of house and in the kitchen, so all tips go into one pot and are shared equally. My main evening job is in a restaurant where we do not get tips, but we are paid minimum wage plus an extra £2.50 per hour. This is said to be worked out to cover average tips when tips are shared with kitchen staff. My third job is events catering, and nobody tips there.

In restaurants, many customers tip by card, so staff do not see how much is being tipped. Because of this, we do not know if we are getting a fair share. The system seems better in France, where people do not tip much, but waiting tables is seen as a real job with job security and a decent income.

3. Ashley, 22, London: “Tips go towards customer breakages”
Average tips: £10-15 per eight-hour shift
I think they treat waiters best in ... Australia

I work evenings in a pub in south-east London and day shifts in a local restaurant. In both places, tips are collected and shared at the end of the night. Money is also taken from the tip jar to pay for breakages caused by staff or customers. If someone finishes early, they often do not get their tips because tips are shared just before the pub closes.

I think it is very unfair that tips are shared, especially when one person does not work well. Also, when someone works very well, they do not get what they earned. It is also unfair that tips are used to pay for things customers break. Managers should find other ways to pay for broken glasses and plates without taking from our tips. I might make around £20 in tips in a shift, but I may only be given £10-15.

When I get good tips, it really affects my money situation. Sometimes I earn over £50 in one night, which almost doubles what I earn that day. But after tips are shared, I never get more than £30. I rely on tips because I am paid only £7 an hour.

I would rather we had a good basic wage, like in Australia, so we did not have to depend on tips to survive.
