In October 2012, the Taliban sent a gunman to shoot Malala Yousafzai. She was riding home on a school bus. They wanted to stop her from speaking up for girls’ education.

Nine months later, after many surgeries, she spoke at the United Nations on her 16th birthday. She said, “They thought that the bullet would silence us. But they failed.”

Her birthday was not like most birthdays. Instead of a cake, she sat in a big UN meeting room, in a seat often used by world leaders.

She listened as Ban Ki-moon, the UN secretary-general, called her “our hero, our champion.” Gordon Brown, a former British prime minister and a UN education envoy, told her, “happy 16th birthday, Malala.”

The day was called Malala Day. It came after four very difficult years for Malala, a girl from Mingora in Pakistan’s Swat valley. People learned about her when she wrote a BBC Urdu blog about trying to get an education while the Taliban were gaining power.

When she was 11, she showed strong determination. She called Richard Holbrooke, the US special representative to Pakistan, and asked him to use his influence to fight the Taliban’s attack on girls’ education. When she was 14, Archbishop Desmond Tutu supported her for the International Children’s Peace Prize. When she was 15, she became the youngest Nobel Peace Prize nominee in history.

But being famous also brought danger. She received death threats. On 9 October 2012, after a meeting of Pakistani Taliban leaders, the gunman was sent to kill what they called the “symbol of infidels and obscenity.”

After the attack, she had many operations in Pakistan and the UK. Doctors put a titanium plate on her left forehead and a cochlear implant to help her hear again. Now she lives with her family in Birmingham. She does what the Taliban tried to stop her from doing: she goes to school every day.

At the UN she said she was not against anyone. She said she was not speaking for personal revenge against the Taliban or any other terrorist group.

She answered violence with words. She said she does not even hate the Talib who shot her. She said that even if she had a gun in her hand and he stood in front of her, she would not shoot him.

She spoke clearly and confidently. Only her injured eye and the drooping left side of her face showed what she had been through. She wore a white shawl that belonged to Benazir Bhutto, a former prime minister of Pakistan who was also targeted by extremists and did not survive.

Malala said extremists are afraid of books and pens. She said they fear education and the voices of women.

She talked about an attack in June on a hospital in Quetta, the capital of Baluchistan, and about female teachers being killed in Khyber Pakhtunkhwa. She said extremists blast schools every day because they are afraid of change and afraid of equality.

She also spoke about Islam. She said the Taliban are wrong when they say God would send girls to hell for going to school. She said terrorists misuse the name of Islam and Pashtun society for their own benefits. She said Islam is a religion of peace, humanity, and brotherhood. She said Islam says education is not only a right for each child, but also a duty and responsibility.

Many people responded to her message. A petition called “Stand with Malala” asks for education for the 57 million children who do not go to school. It has more than four million signatures, and more than a million were added soon after her speech.

At the start of her speech, Malala said she did not know where to begin or what people expected her to say. But she did very well.
