Rebecka Singerer got tired of hearing the same thing again and again: that the beer she wanted was “too dark and too strong” and that she should choose something sweeter.

She said, “No, I don’t want a fruit beer. Women can drink whatever we want.”

Singerer is a childminder. She joined FemAle in Gothenburg, a group of women who like beer. They helped launch Sweden’s first beer made by women.

The beer is called We Can Do It. It is a bottled pale ale, and it is now sold in stores across Sweden. The label shows Rosie the Riveter, a picture from a US Second World War campaign that later became a symbol of women’s power at work.

FemAle was started by Elin Carlsson, 25. She paints cars at the Volvo factory near the city. She says, “We Can Do It is not a female beer. It is a beer brewed by women that anyone can drink. It’s not about feminism; it’s about equality. We wanted to show we can do it.”

FemAle is facing old prejudice in the beer world. Big brewers like Carlsberg have spent millions trying to sell beer to women by making it seem more “pink” and smaller. Beers like Carlsberg’s Eve and Copenhagen, Foster’s Radler, and Coors’s Animée were lighter and flavored, and some were even called “bloat-resistant.” But they did not sell well.

FemAle does something different. They hold women-only tastings. This helps women try beer flavors and styles they might not normally try. The group says this learning is how to “get more girls into the beer world.” They say, “Bring your mother, sister, girlfriend, aunt and grandmother so we can all learn more about beer.”

The idea for FemAle began because the women kept meeting each other at beer festivals.

We Can Do It was the idea of Felicia Nordström, a bar worker. She was tired of beer snobs saying, “What do you know about beer, sweetie?” She contacted FemAle. Together they worked with Ocean, a local independent micro-brewery. One weekend they made the recipe, and the next they brewed 1,600 litres.

Thomas Bingebo, the head brewer at Ocean, says, “This beer is not aimed at women. It’s our hoppiest brew. When big breweries target women, it usually fails. This is completely different.”

The first batch of We Can Do It sold out almost before it was brewed. Other breweries have already asked FemAle if they can make new beers together.

We Can Do It uses three malts: Maris Otter, Amber, and a Thomas Fawcett wheat malt. It uses Galaxy and Cascade hops. Its bitterness level (IBU) is 65, and its alcohol content is 4.6%.

Carlsson says, “Women choose wine because they don’t know what beer is about. They don’t know what to order. We open up new worlds to them.”

Singerer, 38, says she used to not like stout, because she only drank Pripps, a light Swedish lager. Now she says Guinness tastes like water to her. She says some imperial stouts are like drinking biscotti dipped in espresso.

These women are part of a big rise in brewing in Sweden. Many people now like “craft” ales, in bottles and on tap. In Gothenburg, the usual stor stark lager is now “almost extinct,” they say. Many pubs and bars are replacing big brands with many specialist beers.

Emma Henriksson, 22, a FemAle member who works at a garden equipment company, says, “All girls are different. There is no typical woman beer-lover. Anyone can do it.”

Singerer adds, “Every pub wants to learn how to reach women. And Elin has found the way. It’s awesome. We feel so proud.”
