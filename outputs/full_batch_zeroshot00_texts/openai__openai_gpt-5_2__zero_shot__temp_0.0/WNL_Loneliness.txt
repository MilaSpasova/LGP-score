Loneliness is now a big issue. The Office for National Statistics says Britain is the loneliest country in Europe. People in Britain are less likely to have strong friendships or know their neighbours than people in other EU countries. Many also say they have no one to rely on in a crisis. Professor John Cacioppo at the University of Chicago found that loneliness is very bad for older people’s health. He said it is twice as bad as obesity, and it can cause death almost as much as poverty.

But these studies can miss a big problem: loneliness in young adults. In 2010, the Mental Health Foundation found that loneliness worried young people more than older people. In the survey, people aged 18 to 34 were more likely than people over 55 to feel lonely often, worry about being alone, and feel depressed because of loneliness.

“Loneliness is a known problem among older people – there are day centres and charities to help them,” says Sam Challis from the mental health charity Mind. “But when young people reach 21, they’re too old for youth services.” This is a problem because loneliness is closely linked to mental health. It is linked to more stress, depression, paranoia, anxiety, addiction, cognitive decline, and it is a known factor in suicide. In a new essay, Paul Farmer from Mind and Jenny Edwards from the Mental Health Foundation say loneliness can both cause mental health problems and be caused by them.

So what can young people do about loneliness? Dr Grant Blank at the Oxford Internet Institute says social media and the internet can help, but they can also cause problems. They help when they let us talk to loved ones who are far away. They are not helpful when they replace meeting people face to face. Challis says people often show an “ideal” version of themselves online, and we expect our lives to look like what we see in the media. When we compare our life to friends’ perfect-looking lives, we may pull away from other people.

Meditation and apps like Headspace are popular and often suggested for mental health problems. But they may not help loneliness, because they encourage us to sit alone with our thoughts. Challis says it is better to look at the real reasons you feel lonely first, and ask what is stopping you from going out and seeing people.

A 2013 University of Michigan study about social media found that Facebook can reduce life satisfaction. But using technology to help you meet new people can help. Also, if you cannot go outside, the internet can bring comfort. Mumsnet was “an absolute godsend” for Maddy Matthews, 19, a student with a two-month-old daughter. After the birth, she rarely sees her university friends, and her partner works most evenings. She says that in the first days she was up late feeding the baby and worried she was doing something wrong. Posting on Mumsnet helped her feel less alone.

Helplines can also reduce loneliness, at least for a short time. One in four men who call the support charity Samaritans mention loneliness or isolation. Get Connected is a free confidential helpline for young people, where they can get help with emotional and mental health issues linked to loneliness. Websites like Mind’s also offer support services to remind you that you are not alone.

At work, it can help to tell your employer how you feel. John Binns, who advises businesses on mental health and well-being, went to hospital for stress-related depression in 2007 and took two months off work. He felt there was no one to talk to, and he was not close enough to his colleagues for them to notice changes in his behaviour. Being more open with his employer and colleagues made it easier to return to work. He says colleagues are often more supportive than people expect. His colleagues reached out, asked him to lunch, and told him that things had not changed too much since he had been away.

Small talk in the office may seem like a waste of time, but it can protect us from some of the emotional and mental effects of work stress. Rick Hughes from the British Association for Counselling and Psychotherapy (BACP) says that if you connect with your team, you might feel stressed but not isolated.

Nicky Forsythe, a psychotherapist and the founder of Talk for Health, says the social networks we have may seem unimportant, but they are key to our well-being. She says the most important thing is to have a regular time and place to think about your life, and to have a kind, understanding listener.

Counselling can help people learn skills like empathy. The BACP website lets you search for counsellors near you. Hughes says that sharing a problem can help, and sometimes you need to talk to someone who is separate from your friends and family. Most universities offer counselling to students, and many also run group sessions that focus on loneliness.

If recent research is right, loneliness is killing older people. Because the population is getting older, we should try to reduce loneliness before it is too late. Ruth Sutherland, the chief executive of the relationship counselling service Relate, says getting older does not have to mean getting lonelier. But she says this depends a lot on building good, strong relationships earlier in life.
