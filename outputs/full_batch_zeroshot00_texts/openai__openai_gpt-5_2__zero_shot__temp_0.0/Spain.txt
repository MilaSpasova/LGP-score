Valdevaqueros is one of the last clean, natural beaches in southern Spain. The sand is golden, and the sky is full of kites that pull surfers over the waves. There is only a small road to the beach. Many camper vans from Germany, France, Italy, and Britain park there. Windsurfers and kitesurfers come because the wind is strong.

For many years, this beach was very different from the built-up beaches in places like Torremolinos and Marbella. But on 29 May, the local council in Tarifa approved a plan to build a tourist complex next to the beach. It would have 1,400 hotel rooms and 350 flats. Environmental groups say it will hurt the homes of protected animals. Many councillors say the main reason to support it is jobs. Tarifa has 18,000 people, and 2,600 do not have work. Spain is in a very bad economic crisis, and it has raised questions about the future of the euro.

Sebastián Galindo, a councillor from the Socialist Party, said fishing is no longer a good way to earn money because fishing fleets are being taken apart and there are fewer fish. He said tourism is the only way out, as long as it is sustainable. His party is in opposition in Tarifa, but it voted with the governing People’s Party to approve the plan.

Tarifa’s Mayor, Juan Andrés Gil, did not comment. Galindo said the plan follows environmental rules. The buildings would be 800 metres from the coast. The law says buildings must be at least 200 metres from the coast. This law was made to stop more ugly building like the building that damaged much of Spain’s coast in the 1960s and 1970s.

People against the project say Spain already has a million empty homes, so it does not need more flats. Last week the central government suggested a plan to sell homes by giving non-Spaniards residency permits if they buy property worth at least €160,000.

The Socialist opposition in Madrid attacked this idea. Galindo said it is unfair to migrant workers who came to Spain during the boom years, many from Morocco. Morocco is only 14km away and you can see it from Tarifa. He said the plan helps rich people more than the people who came to work.

Surfers worry that new buildings in Valdevaqueros could make the local Levant wind weaker. They also think the place may not attract normal package tourists. Henning Mayer, from Augsburg in Germany, has visited for 20 years. He said it is not really a place for families, and the Levant wind can be very strong. He also said that 10 years ago people talked about building a new highway there, but it did not happen, so he thinks it will be impossible to build new hotels.

Tarifa is at the southernmost tip of Spain. It is an important place between Africa and Europe, where the Mediterranean Sea meets the Atlantic. Campaigners say it is also important for animals that migrate.

A campaign to save the beach started a few hours after the council vote. It has a Facebook page. Groups supporting it include Greenpeace, the World Wide Fund for Nature, and the Spanish branch of Birdlife. Noelia Jurado, who works with multimedia, said the project is like putting a shopping centre in the middle of the Alhambra. She also said the resort would be near the ancient Roman town of Baelo Claudia, and it might be built on top of more Roman ruins.

The Andalusian College of Geographers also opposes the plan. In an early study on its website, it said “free areas,” including car parks, may overlap part of Alcornocales National Park. The geographers also say the site goes into two wildlife conservation areas. One area in Valdevaqueros is home to two bat species that are in danger.

Raúl Romeva, a member of the European Parliament and Vice-President of the Greens group, said money is being put before planning laws and European environmental rules. He also said the area does not have enough water. Tarifa already has water shortages in summer in Andalusía. A lack of water recently made the Andalusía Supreme Court support an appeal against building a different complex in the region, which would have had golf courses, hotels, and luxury homes.

Many local people ask why the resort should be built 10km away, instead of on empty land near Tarifa’s old centre. The old centre has white walls, small winding streets, and a 10th-century Moorish castle. Cristóbal Lobato, who has worked at the same beach bar for 30 years, said he agrees with the complex if it creates jobs for the town, but he is against it only helping a few people. He said if it was built in the centre of Tarifa, visitors could go to shops, tapas bars, and restaurants.

Biologist Aitor Galán, who does environmental impact studies, pointed to one of only two seaside breeding places for vultures in Europe. He said that in other parts of Europe, this place would have very strong protection, but here people want to cover it with buildings. He said they want to turn it into Benidorm, but people come for the wildlife and the wind. He said builders and mayors use the current crisis and unemployment to justify a lot of destruction.
