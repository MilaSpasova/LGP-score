The rule says the 8-hour work day is finished. The most important work is done, and the rest can wait until morning. Many workers would think it is time to go home.

But for millions of Japanese workers, leaving on time and being home for dinner can lead to people saying they are not loyal.

For many years, companies in Japan have expected workers to do as much work as possible. Now the government is challenging this overwork culture. It is thinking about a new law that would require workers to take at least five days of paid holiday each year.

Japanese workers are supposed to get an average of 18.5 paid holiday days a year. That is only two days less than the world average. They must get at least 10 days, and there are also 15 one-day national holidays. But in real life, few workers take all their days. The labour ministry says they usually use only nine days out of the 18.5 days. Many British workers see a two-week summer holiday as a basic right. In Japan, some workers think even a four-night trip to Hawaii is too self-indulgent.

This plan will be debated in the current parliament session. It comes after companies started telling workers to nap at work to improve performance.

By the end of the decade, the government hopes the law, if it passes, will push Japanese workers to act more like British workers, who use about 20 paid leave days a year, and French workers, who use about 25.

Japan’s strict work culture may have helped it become a strong economy. People around the world respect Japanese workers for their commitment to their companies. But this often leaves little time for anything else.

Japan has a low birth rate, and experts say the population may fall quickly. Part of the blame is that couples do not have enough time to start families. More workers are getting sick from stress. Some die from karoshi, which means death from overwork.

Studies suggest that longer hours at work do not always make people more productive. Still, many workers keep doing very long hours, like in the 1980s.

About 22% of Japanese workers work more than 49 hours a week. In the US it is 16%, and in France and Germany it is 11%, based on Japanese government data. South Korea is worse, with 35%.

Erika Sekiguchi works 14 hours a day and gives up many paid holidays, and she is not even an extreme example. She is 36 and works at a trading company. In 2014, she used eight of her 20 paid vacation days, and six of those days were sick leave. She said, “Nobody else uses their vacation days.”

She has the same problem as many workers in Japan. If they take time off to rest, they may be criticized. If they do not, they cannot recharge.

Yuu Wakebe is a health ministry official who works on policy about working hours. He admits he does 100 hours of overtime each month. He says there is strong pressure to match co-workers hour for hour. He said, “It is a worker’s right to take paid vacations. But working in Japan involves quite a lot of volunteer spirit.”

People say fear of being left out at work is causing more stress-related illness, early death, and suicide. Official data says about 200 people die each year from heart attacks, strokes, and other karoshi events caused by very hard work schedules.

The prime minister, Shinzo Abe, is not known for taking long vacations. But even he has spoken against unreasonable demands that companies put on workers as they try to survive in a more complex global market. Abe recently said Japan’s work culture “falsely beatifies long hours”.
