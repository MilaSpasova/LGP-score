A new, very detailed report says low-income countries will be hit first and hardest by climate change caused by people during the next 100 years. They may face slowly rising sea levels, stronger storms called cyclones, hotter days and nights, less predictable rain, and bigger and longer heatwaves.

A major United Nations (UN) report in 2007 warned that temperatures could rise by 6 °C or more by the end of the century. Scientists now think this is unlikely. But they still expect land and sea temperatures to keep rising this century, possibly reaching 4 °C above today’s levels. That could ruin crops and make many cities dangerously hot.

The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) report, released in Stockholm and published online in September, says warmer oceans and higher temperatures will change rainfall in tropical and subtropical places.

East Africa may get more short rains. West Africa may get heavier monsoon rains. Burma, Bangladesh, and India may get stronger cyclones. In other parts of southern Asia, heavier summer rains are expected. Indonesia may get less rain from July to October. But coastal areas around the South China Sea and the Gulf of Thailand may get more extreme rain when cyclones reach land.

The report’s authors say it is almost certain that, over time, global rainfall will change. High-latitude places, such as parts of Europe and North America, are expected to get more rain. Many dry and semi-dry subtropical regions will likely get less rain. In wet tropical regions, very heavy rain events are very likely to become stronger and happen more often as the world warms.

They also say monsoon seasons may start earlier or change little, but monsoons are very likely to end later. This would make the monsoon season longer.

Scientists in developing countries and other commentators welcomed the report. They said it matches what they have already seen.

Saleemul Huq, director of the International Centre for Climate Change and Development in Dhaka, said the IPCC shows climate change is real and happening more strongly than before. He said people in Bangladesh and across south Asia are already seeing the effects. He added that many developing countries are facing climate change now, and they do not need the IPCC to tell them the weather is changing.

Scientists have also reduced their estimates for sea-level rise. Depending on future greenhouse gas emissions, sea levels are expected to rise by an average of 40–62 cm by 2100. However, sea-level rise will differ by place. Many millions of people in large developing-world cities, including Lagos and Calcutta, are at risk.

The report says weather disasters are more likely in a warmer world. The total number of tropical cyclones worldwide may go down or stay about the same, but the storms may become stronger, with faster winds and heavier rain.

Life in many cities in developing countries could become very hard because cities are already hotter than nearby countryside. The report says much higher temperatures could shorten the time crops can grow in some parts of Africa by up to 20%.

Dr Camilla Toulmin, director of the International Institute for Environment and Development, said climate models are not strong enough yet to predict exact local and regional effects. But she said it is clear that everyone is vulnerable in some way.

The charity Oxfam said hunger around the world will get worse because climate change will damage crops and disrupt incomes. Oxfam suggested the number of people at risk of hunger could rise by 10% to 20% by 2050, and the daily calories available per person could fall worldwide.

Oxfam said climate change is already putting progress against hunger in danger and is likely to make it worse. They said, “A hot world is a hungry world.” They also warned that if the rest of the 21st century is like its first decade, people will soon face climate extremes far beyond what humans have experienced.
