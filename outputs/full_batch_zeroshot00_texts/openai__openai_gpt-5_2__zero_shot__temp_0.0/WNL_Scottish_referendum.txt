David Cameron said there was a “clear result” in the Scottish independence vote. Scotland voted against leaving the United Kingdom by a 10.6-point margin, keeping the 307-year-old union with England and Wales. Cameron said there should be “no disputes” and “no reruns.” He promised more powers for different parts of Great Britain. He also said there should be votes on English issues by English MPs at Westminster.

Alex Salmond, Scotland’s first minister, spoke at a Scottish National Party rally in Edinburgh. He said he accepted that Scotland had not, “at this stage,” chosen independence. He called the vote a “triumph for democratic politics.” He said he would work with Westminster for Scotland and the rest of the UK. He also warned the leaders of the three main parties to keep their promises to give Scotland more powers. He said more people had taken part in politics than before.

The yes campaign won in some places. It got 53% in Glasgow, 57% in Dundee, and 51% in North Lanarkshire. But the no campaign won in 28 areas. It won strongly in places like Edinburgh, Aberdeenshire, and Borders. It also won in the Western Isles. In the final count, no won 2,001,926 votes (55.3%), and yes won 1,617,989 votes (44.7%).

Cameron said big changes to the UK system would not happen until after the general election. He said changes for Scotland would happen along with changes in England. He said, “We have heard the voice of Scotland and, now, the millions of voices of England must be heard.”

Cameron said he was happy Scotland stayed in the UK. He said it would have “broken my heart” to see the UK end. He said the UK should now come together and move forward. He said there should be a fair plan for Scotland and also for England, Wales, and Northern Ireland.

Ed Miliband, the Labour leader, said the vote was a call for change. He said Labour would give stronger powers to a stronger Scottish parliament. He also said there should be change across England, Wales, and the whole UK.

Nick Clegg, the deputy prime minister, said the vote starts a new chapter for Scotland in the UK and also wider changes across the union. He said voting no was not a vote against change. He said the UK must deliver the new set of powers promised to Scotland.

Some Conservative MPs threatened to oppose Cameron. They were unhappy about his late promise to quickly increase the Scottish parliament’s powers while protecting its spending.

Nigel Farage, leader of the UK Independence Party, said Cameron’s plan for England did not go far enough. He said England is 86% of the population of the union. He said English people have been left out for 18 years. He said Scottish MPs can still vote on English-only issues in the House of Commons. He said English people want a fair plan.

Cameron and the Queen planned to make statements to calm tensions. Cameron planned to explain how he would increase Scotland’s devolution, including giving the Scottish Parliament more power over tax and welfare.

The Queen would make a written statement. It was understood her words would focus on bringing people together again. Cameron wanted to act fast to show the three main UK party leaders would keep their promises. Former prime minister Gordon Brown called this “home rule” within the UK.

Ministers said it was important to move quickly to avoid a repeat of the 1980 referendum in Quebec. They said behaviour in Ontario helped separatists, and the cause almost won in a second referendum in 1995.

People in the no campaign felt relieved. In the last days, polls said the vote was very close, and Yes Scotland seemed near to winning after a big rise in support.
