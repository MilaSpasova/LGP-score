To tourists, Amsterdam still seems like a liberal city. The Labour Mayor recently said the city’s marijuana-selling coffee shops would stay open, even though there is a new national law to fight drug tourism. But Amsterdam may lose its name for tolerance because of plans to send very troublesome neighbours to places called “scum villages,” made from shipping containers.

The Mayor, Eberhard van der Laan, says his new policy costs £810,000. He says it is meant to stop antisocial behaviour and to protect victims of abuse and homophobia from harassment. People who behave very badly could be moved for three to six months. Some people call these places “scum villages” because the idea sounds like a plan from Geert Wilders, a far-right populist. Last year, Wilders said “repeat offenders” should be “sent to a village for scum.”

The Mayor’s spokesman, Bartho Boer, says the plan is not against liberal values. He says Amsterdam wants everyone to be who they are, including gay and lesbian people, and wants to protect people who are harassed. Boer says the plan is not for normal neighbour problems, like loud music on a Saturday night. He says it is for people who are extremely violent and intimidating, when a victim is repeatedly harassed.

People found guilty of causing “extreme havoc” will be evicted. They will be put in temporary homes that are very basic. These may be converted shipping containers in industrial areas of the city. Boer says they call it a “living container.” The units have showers and kitchens and have been used for student housing. Boer says the goal is to make sure these people are not “rewarded” by being moved to better housing.

Dutch newspaper the Parool said that in the 19th century, troublemakers were moved to villages in Drenthe and Overijssel, and these quickly became slums. But Boer says the city has learned from the past and will not put antisocial people together.

Boer says “scum houses” is a more accurate name than scum villages, because the city does not want to put more than one of these families in the same area. After up to six months in these houses, which will be spread around the city, the tenants will get permanent homes. The city expects to move about ten families a year into this programme, starting in 2013.

The temporary homes will have heavy police presence. But the tenants will also be able to get help from doctors, social workers, and parole officers. Boer says they will be supported so the same problems do not happen again in their new homes.
