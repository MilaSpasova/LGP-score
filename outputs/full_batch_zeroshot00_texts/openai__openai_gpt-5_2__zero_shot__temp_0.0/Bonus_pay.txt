Male bosses get bonuses that are about twice as big as the bonuses for women doing the same jobs. Over a whole working life, this can mean men get £141,500 more than women in extra pay.

The Chartered Management Institute (CMI) shared these numbers. It said that in 2012, men in UK management jobs got an average bonus of £6,442. Women got £3,029.

In the most senior jobs, female directors got bonuses of £36,270 in the past 12 months. Male directors got £63,700.

These figures led equality campaigners to call for action.

Ann Francke, the CMI chief executive, said this issue should be part of normal management. She said workplaces should have more flexibility, less “masculine” culture, more focus on results instead of time in the office, and clearer information about performance and rewards. She said fixing this could improve how organizations work and help people feel better at work.

Dr Ruth Sealy, a senior research fellow at Cranfield School of Management, said this is not surprising. She said bonuses can be given based on personal choice, so the system should be more open and clear.

Some statisticians warned that some data may be affected by things like women working in jobs where bonuses are less common. But the bonus differences seem to make the UK pay gap worse. The government says the pay gap is getting smaller, but full-time men still earn 10% more than women.

Maria Miller, the Minister for Women and Equalities, said the CMI figures show women still do worse than men at work, and things are not equal yet. She said the pay gap is closing, but more work is needed.

She said the government has taken steps. It made pay secrecy clauses illegal. It gave tribunals power to make employers who break equal pay laws do equal pay audits. It also got 120 companies to join the Think, Act, Report scheme, which encourages companies to improve how they recruit, promote, and pay women. She also said the government has tried to help with work and family duties by introducing shared parental leave and the right to request flexible working for all employees.

Big companies like Tesco, BT, Unilever, and the international law firm Eversheds have joined Think, Act, Report. The scheme has 120 supporters after almost two years. It had 54 participants in November 2012.

CMI data also gave some support to Miller’s idea that the overall pay gap is getting smaller. The difference between the average salaries of male and female bosses seemed to drop from £10,060 in 2012 to £8,502 in the most recent CMI figures.

But the institute warned that the 2012 and 2013 groups are not the same, even though each survey asked about 40,000 managers.

A smaller group of 17,000 managers was tracked over several years. It showed that for the first time in five years, men’s earnings are rising faster than women’s. When salary and bonuses are added together, men’s total earnings rose by 3.2%, compared with 2.8% for women.

At the highest level, male directors’ earnings rose by 5.3% in the past 12 months, compared with 1.1% for female directors.
