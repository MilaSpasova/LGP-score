In the past, not sleeping much was seen as a good thing. It was a sign you were busy and important. People said that sleep was for weak people. Now things have changed. Arianna Huffington’s book, The Sleep Revolution, is a New York Times best-seller. It is a “call to bed” that says it can change your life “one night at a time”. Huffington tells people to “sleep their way to the top”.

At the same time, the sleep business has grown a lot. Many start-ups are changing where, when, and how we sleep. They also want to change how much money we will pay for sleep. For rich customers, luxury hotels offer “sleep retreats”. For more than $1,000, you get dinner and a movie about sleep. If you stay at home, you can buy new things for your bedroom. You can buy a mattress cover with a sensor that tracks your sleep ($249). You can also buy a sleeping mask that monitors brainwaves and says it helps you nap better ($299).

Sleep is not only a business now. It is also part of work life. Some companies already have nap pods. Huffington says nap rooms in offices will become “as common as conference rooms” in the next two years. So why did this happen? Why did sleep suddenly become fashionable?

For some people, getting enough sleep fits with a lifestyle where paying $10 for green juice and $34 for a SoulCycle class is normal. Also, wearable technology has become popular. People track their bodies with devices and try to improve them. Sleep has become another set of numbers to track. Huffington says sleep does not just rest you; it restores you. She says sleep is the best performance enhancer. Getting eight hours of sleep has become a status symbol.

How does Arianna Huffington get her eight hours? A recent New York Times profile says she has “nine or so” assistants. She calls them her “A-Team”. They run errands, plan travel, and load The Huffington Post on her computer in the morning. The Times says most of them can only do the job for about 12 months because it is so hard. The low pay also means many take second jobs. Basically, they do not sleep so that Huffington can, and can sell books about it.

Getting enough sleep is not only about choosing to go to bed on time. It also depends on where you live and what life is like for you. Many studies show that poor people are more likely to sleep badly. It is hard to sleep if you worry about safety or do not have enough to eat. It is hard to sleep if you are one of the 15 million American shift workers with irregular hours. Research has also found a black/white sleep gap. One study shows white people sleep an average of 6.85 hours, while African Americans sleep an average of 6.05 hours. African Americans also have lower quality sleep. Researchers say this is partly because of the stress of discrimination.

Who gets the most sleep and the best quality sleep in America? Wealthy white women. That is probably the same group Huffington is aiming her book at. She calls her sleep message a “revolution”, but it is really a rebranding. The real sleep crisis is not because a few rich people think sleep is a waste of time. It is because the 99% cannot afford to spend time sleeping.

Sleep is popular right now, but it may not last long. Sleep can improve performance, but it is not efficient. The bigger goal is to find a way to stop the harm from sleep deprivation, so people can work well with less sleep. The military is leading this research. In 2008, the Pentagon published a report called “Human Performance”. It looked at a future where soldiers could do their best with only a couple of hours of sleep. It said: “Suppose a human could be engineered who slept for the same amount of time as a giraffe (1.9 hours per night). This would lead to an approximately twofold decrease in the casualty rate. An adversary would need an approximately 40% increase in the troop level to compensate for this advantage.” The report also looked at the effects of ampakines, a type of drug that changes neurotransmitters in the brain, to remove the effects of sleep deprivation.

The writer says that one day humans will find a way to get rid of sleep. Then spending a third of your life unconscious will not be seen as a luxury. It will be something only poor people are forced to do. At that point, we may need a very different kind of sleep revolution.
