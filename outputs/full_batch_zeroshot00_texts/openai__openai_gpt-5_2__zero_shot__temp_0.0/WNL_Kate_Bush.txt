The last time Kate Bush performed on stage, the mobile phone was still being tested. Now, 35 years later, she is performing again in a very different world.

At many concerts today, people hold up phones and tablets. But Kate Bush does not want fans to watch her show through a screen.

Before her concerts at the Hammersmith Apollo in London, Bush wrote a message to fans. She asked them to put away their phones at her gigs. She said she chose a small, close theatre, not a big stadium. She asked people not to take photos or film during the show. She said she wants to connect with the audience, not with iPhones, iPads, or cameras. She knows it is a big request, but she thinks it will help everyone share the experience together.

Bush also likes theatre and fancy costumes. She may also want to stop unclear videos going on YouTube, so the show stays a surprise for the thousands of fans who bought tickets for the 22 dates.

Other artists have also complained about phones at concerts. Roger Daltrey from The Who said it is “weird” when people watch the screen instead of the artist on stage. He said he feels sorry for them, because they are not fully in the moment.

In 2013, Beyoncé told a fan to stop filming. She said he could not sing because he was too busy recording. She told him to put the camera down and enjoy the moment.

This debate is not only about music. Sports fans also talk about it. Dutch fans at PSV Eindhoven protested against wi-fi in their stadium. They held banners that said “No wi-fi. Support the team,” “You can sit at home,” and “Stand united”. Manchester United also told fans to leave “large electronic devices” at home, and they banned filming on tablets this season.

Jarvis Cocker said phones in the audience made him “insane at concerts”. He said it seems stupid to look at a small screen when something is happening right in front of you.

Johnny Marr said in 2013 that people miss the full feeling of live music when they only try to record it. He said watching through your phone is a wasted chance. He told people to put their phone down and enjoy the gig. He said gigs are also about the people around you, and watching on a little screen is a waste of time.

The Yeah Yeah Yeahs put up a sign at one venue. It asked fans not to watch the show through a screen. It told them to put devices away as a courtesy to the person behind them and to the band.

Phones have also caused problems in classical music. In June 2013, the pianist Krystian Zimerman walked off stage because a fan was filming on a smartphone. He came back and said: “The destruction of music because of YouTube is enormous.”

But Sam Watt of Vyclone, an app that encourages filming at concerts, said these artists are fighting a losing battle. He said filming is now part of concerts, and it can improve the experience. In the app, people upload their videos, and the app mixes them with other people’s videos to make one crowd video. He said this can create great content.

Watt also said filming adds to the experience, not takes away from it. He said he could have an interesting discussion with Kate Bush and might change her mind. He said people want memories, and artists should accept that fans will film.
