Anitta is a famous Brazilian funk singer. She has gained millions of fans by bringing music from the favelas into the mainstream. But people are debating her skin colour.

Some anti-discrimination groups and writers say she had to give up part of her black identity to become popular with Brazil’s mostly white middle-class market.

The debate grew after “before and after” photos were shared. In the first photo, from when she was not well known, she looks darker. In the second photo, a marketing picture after she became famous, she looks lighter. People wonder if this happened because of skin-whitening products, cosmetic surgery, or more likely Photoshop. The photos started a new discussion about whether people need to be pale to succeed in Brazil.

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote that Anitta’s image change shows hidden discrimination in media and marketing. She said many people do not admit they are racist and think Brazil is a country where all races are treated equally. But she said the numbers show that is not true. She wrote that society often connects white skin with being rich and successful, and black skin with the opposite. She also said this can hurt people’s self-esteem. She gave examples like feeling forced to straighten curly hair or change a nose with surgery.

Anitta’s real name is Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

She became very famous with an album and her hit song “Show das Poderosas.” The song reached the top of the charts and got 52 million views on YouTube.

Many fans love her as a pop star with strong messages and catchy songs. The people who support her also present her as a bridge between the mostly black and mixed-race shanty towns on Rio’s hills and the richer and whiter areas below.

She has also changed her style. She toned down sexual dancing, gangsta references, and explicit lyrics from baile funk. Now some people ask if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract a richer audience.

Race and colour are sensitive topics in Brazil. Brazil was one of the last big countries to ban slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. In Brazil, skin colour often matters more than ancestry. There is also a clear link between skin tone and inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Until 2011, black or mixed-race students spent about two fewer years in school on average.

The government says the gap is getting smaller because of university quotas and other programs. But big differences still remain. Most business and government leaders are white, while many lower-paid jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In rich areas like Ipanema and Gávea, it is common to see black nannies pushing strollers with white children.

Colour categories can be complicated. In the 2010 census, people who called themselves white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million said black, two million said Asian, and 0.5% said indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology lecturer at the Federal University of Pernambuco, said Anitta won middle-class fans by taking a rebellious sound and making it calmer and easier for everyone to accept. He said it was not racial whitening. He said it was a change in where she made her art, from the periphery to the centre.

Some others also say the colour issue is too big. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who knew Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot but not her colour. He said makeup, hairstyling, and clothing can explain the difference. He also said he did not think she was being pressured, because she always liked to sing, dance, and show off, and that natural style helped her succeed.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador, said the problem is not the public but music producers and media leaders. He said people showed they care about music itself when they chose Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, to win the talent show The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for a comment. But in a recent interview, she said identity should be decided by the person. She said a powerful woman does not need to be beautiful. She said she tries to show people that we can be who we want.
