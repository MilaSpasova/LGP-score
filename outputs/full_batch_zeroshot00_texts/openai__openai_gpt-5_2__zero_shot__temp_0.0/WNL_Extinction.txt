Many people know about animals in danger, like tigers in India, orangutans in Indonesia, and pandas. People do not want these animals to disappear. But scientists now worry that the biggest danger may be closer to home. Even small animals like ladybirds could be very important for nature.

Climate change, fewer animals, more humans, and many species dying out are making scientists say we are in the Anthropocene. This means humans strongly control Earth’s natural systems.

WWF’s Living Planet Index (LPI) 2014 showed a very serious problem. It said the number of vertebrates (animals with backbones) fell by 52% over four decades. Biodiversity loss is now at “critical levels”. Some groups have lost even more. Freshwater species fell by 76% over the same time.

Scientists are especially worried about animals that help nature work. Three quarters of the world’s food production is thought to depend on bees and other pollinators, like hoverflies. Worms help by breaking down waste and turning it into nutrients in the soil. Bats catch mosquitoes and help keep malaria rates down. A North America study said the loss of pest control from declining bats could cost more than $22bn in lost agricultural productivity.

Dr Nick Isaac, a macroecologist at the Centre for Ecology & Hydrology in Oxfordshire, said losing common species will affect people more than losing rare ones. He said Britain’s insects and other invertebrates are declining as fast as vertebrates, and this could have “serious consequences for humanity”. He also said it is not only about how many species go extinct, but also about how many animals are left in each population.

He said that 23 to 36% of all birds, mammals, and amphibians used for food or medicine are now threatened with extinction. In many places, wild animals are an important food source, especially for poor people.

Many people agree the main cause is unsustainable human consumption. This damages ecosystems, creates climate change, and destroys habitats faster than people thought. It is not only big animals we should worry about, but also smaller creatures we depend on, like insects and worms. They may not die out soon, but if their numbers drop, it will affect everyone. Isaac said the problem is not simple.

Sir David Attenborough said in 2013 that humans are a “plague on earth”. But WWF says there is still time to stop the damage. WWF UK Chief Executive David Nussbaum said this report should be a wake-up call. He said politicians, businesses, and all people must act to protect a healthy future for people and nature.

Nussbaum said humans are cutting down trees faster than they can regrow. We are catching more fish than the oceans can replace. We are taking water from rivers and aquifers faster than rain can refill them. We are also sending out more carbon than the oceans and forests can absorb.
