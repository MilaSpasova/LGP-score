People call it the “Richie Rich Club,” and it may get richer soon. A report says India’s richest people will make their total wealth about four times bigger by 2018. Many new business owners and people who get money from family will become multimillionaires. The report is based on interviews with 150 very rich people. It also says business confidence is coming back in India.

In recent years, India had slow growth, higher food prices, and a weaker currency. But the Bharatiya Janata Party (BJP) won a big election in May 2014 and promised to improve the economy. Even with the slowdown, there are almost one-sixth more Indians worth more than $3.75m than in 2013, says the Kotak Mahindra Bank report. Murali Balaraman, a co-author, said more cities are growing, the middle class is growing, and there are more chances to make money. He also said politics has improved in recent months.

India’s rich people together own assets worth about $1 trillion. That is about one-fifth of all the wealth in India. By 2018, the report says total wealth could reach $4tn, and there could be three times as many multimillionaires.

A luxury market is growing to sell to new rich people and old rich families. Balaraman said rich people want to show their wealth in a quiet way, and buying luxury goods helps them do that. Abhay Gupta, the CEO of Luxury Connect, said the market for expensive goods and experiences will get bigger. He said many people want to be like the very rich and copy what they do.

Cars are one of the most popular luxury items, the report says. In 2009, rich people showed off SUVs made in India. Now, only foreign cars get attention. Mercedes had a 47% jump in sales in India in 2013. BMW launched a new $200,000 model in Delhi.

But India’s bad roads and traffic can reduce demand for some cars. In 2013, Lamborghini’s Chief Executive, Stephan Winkelmann, said India’s roads and traffic “are not so suitable” for $450,000 sports cars. Lamborghini sells two models in India: the Gallardo and the Aventador. The Aventador can reach 217mph. Winkelmann said Lamborghini buyers in India are younger than in Europe, usually in their 30s. Still, the most popular investments are real estate (mostly in India) and jewellery.

India’s super-rich are known around the world for very big spending. Mukesh Ambani, India’s richest man, built a very expensive home in Mumbai. The 27-storey tower has helicopter pads, indoor cinemas, and more than 600 staff. It is estimated to be worth $1bn.

A three-day wedding for the niece of Lakshmi Mittal, a UK-based steel tycoon worth an estimated $16bn, was reported to cost $80m. Hundreds of guests were flown to Barcelona. The ceremony and party were held in a museum.

The report says luxury buyers now want even more special items. One person ordered nine cases of Japanese whisky that cost over $750 a bottle for a wedding reception. The report says the whisky was attractive because guests would not know how to buy the same drink in India. Another big spender bought pairs of Louis Vuitton bags, then cut up half of them to make clothes that matched her accessories.

Traditional weddings are also changing fast. Gifts like silver plates, dried fruit, or sweets, once sent with wedding invitations, are being replaced by gifts from top western designer brands. Gupta says, “These days, it’s Rolex watches and Louis Vuitton bags.”

Almost half of new ultra-high-net-worth individuals live in smaller provincial cities. Many give large amounts to charity, but the report says charity spending has not grown as fast as their wealth.

Co-author Balaraman says having more rich people will not cause social tension because a big gap between rich and poor is accepted in India. He said people know some are rich and some are poor, and they continue with their lives.
