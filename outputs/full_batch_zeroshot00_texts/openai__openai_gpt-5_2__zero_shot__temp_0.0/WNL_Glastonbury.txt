Glastonbury Festival wants to fight the problem of plastic water bottles. This is part of a long-term plan to become the world’s most environmentally friendly outdoor music festival.

Organizers are focusing on single-use bottles. They are a clear sign of “throwaway” culture. Each year, the 900-acre site in Somerset is left covered with plastic. About one million plastic bottles are used during the festival.

The festival will give stainless-steel reusable bottles to 2,000 road crew and band members. Thousands more will be sold to people at the festival. The goal is to help people stop using plastic bottles. The 140,000 ticket-holders are also being asked to bring reusable bottles. They can fill them at 400 drinking-water taps around the site.

Lucy Smith, the festival’s organizer for green issues, said: “We have amazing water quality in the UK but everyone is obsessed with drinking bottled water.”

She said this action comes before a plan for Glastonbury 2015. The festival wants to replace all plastic beer glasses and cutlery with reusable items. This is to remove plastic waste left on the large rural site.

Environmentalists say there are about 150 million tonnes of plastic waste on Earth and in the oceans. It harms ecosystems and kills wildlife.

In the end, organizers hope to make Glastonbury the world’s greenest greenfield festival. They want to copy America’s Burning Man festival in the Black Rock Desert of Nevada. Burning Man is a “leave-no-trace” event, where people must take away everything they bring.

People going to Glastonbury are also being asked to use public transport or share cars with friends. Smith said, “We want to be as sustainable as we can. We do everything we can, but coping with the litter of 140,000 people is a challenge. We can’t put bins everywhere.”

Campaigners say plastic water bottles can take hundreds or even thousands of years to fully biodegrade. Making them also makes the problem worse. Millions of barrels of oil are used to make plastic bottles. Moving mineral water around the world creates even more carbon emissions.

About 13 billion plastic water bottles are sold in the UK each year, but only one in five is said to be recycled.

Smith said that instead of buying bottled water, people should use the tap water at the festival. It comes from huge underground reservoirs, not old water tanks that gave heavily chlorinated water. The charity WaterAid will also set up water kiosks around the site. They will sell reusable bottles and cups and give free refills. In 2015, these kiosks, based on ones found in Africa, will also be DJ booths at night.

Organizers say almost half of the rubbish left on site was recycled in 2013. They also say there will be 15,000 recycling bins across the festival grounds in 2014.

Even with its greener image, some critics still attack Glastonbury. They say it is becoming more corporate. One critic, Iron Maiden’s Bruce Dickinson, said he will never bring his band to Glastonbury. He called it “the most bourgeois thing on the planet”.

The weather forecast was good. The festival was expected to be sunny and dry. Experts said it would not repeat 1985, the windiest year; 1997, the muddiest; or 2005, known as the “year of thunder”.
