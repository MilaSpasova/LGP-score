The British government expects thousands of legal claims. This is after the High Court in London said three elderly Kenyans can sue Britain for money. They say they were jailed and badly treated during the Mau Mau rebellion in the 1950s, near the end of the British Empire.

On Friday, the court rejected the government’s argument that too much time had passed and a fair trial was not possible. In 2011, the same judge, Mr Justice McCombe, also rejected the claim that the three people should sue the Kenyan government instead. Kenya became independent in 1963.

Human rights activists in Kenya say more than 5,000 of the 70,000-plus people held by British colonial authorities are still alive. Many of them may also sue the British government. The ruling may also help victims in other former colonies sue.

The Foreign Office said the ruling could have big legal effects and it plans to appeal. A spokesman said the usual time limit for a civil case is three to six years, but this case is more than 50 years later. He said key decision makers have died.

The three claimants are Paulo Muoka Nzili, 85, Wambugu Wa Nyingi, 84, and Jane Muthoni Mara, 73. They won after a three-year court fight. Their lawyers say they suffered very cruel acts, including castration, beatings, and severe sexual assaults. A fourth person left the case, and a fifth, Susan Ciong’ombe Ngondi, died two years ago at age 71.

In Nairobi, Nyingi and Mara heard the news by mobile phone while sitting with supporters in a hot garden. They were very happy. They hugged, danced, and then raised their hands to the sky to pray.

Nyingi said he was held for about nine years, beaten until he lost consciousness, and still has marks from leg shackles, whipping, and caning. He said he wanted the truth to be known, even by his grandchildren’s children. He said people should be paid so they can begin to forgive the British government. Mara said she was very happy and felt her heart was clean. She said she would tell her four children: “I will tell them I won.”

In 2011, McCombe said there was plenty of evidence that detainees may have been tortured in an organized way. On Friday, he said a fair trial was possible. He noted that thousands of documents appeared in 2011 after the Foreign Office admitted it had a secret archive of colonial-era files.

During the government’s efforts to end the case, the claimants’ lawyer, Martyn Day, said those efforts were “morally repugnant.” The British government’s lawyers accepted that all three Kenyans were tortured by the colonial authorities.

Day said the British government admitted the three were brutally tortured, but used legal arguments for three years to avoid responsibility. He said victims of colonial torture in places from Malaya to the Yemen, and from Cyprus to Palestine, would read the judgment carefully.

Some people watching closely include veterans of the Eoka insurgency in Cyprus in the 1950s. One has already met the Mau Mau lawyers. Cypriot claimants could use British documents and the archives of the International Committee of the Red Cross in Geneva. These files are kept secret for 40 years and then opened. The Red Cross recorded hundreds of torture cases in Cyprus. Reporters there called British interrogators HMTs, meaning Her Majesty’s Torturers.

There may also be claims from Malaysia, where many people were detained during a 12-year war that began in 1948. It was against communist fighters and their supporters. Relatives of 24 unarmed rubber plantation workers killed by British troops are now in British courts, asking for a public inquiry. Former prisoners in Aden may also have claims. But because Aden is now part of Yemen, British lawyers may find it hard to contact possible clients there.
