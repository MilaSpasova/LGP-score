Two mothers in South Africa found out they are raising each other’s daughters. The babies were accidentally switched at a hospital in 2010.

Now there is a hard problem. One mother wants to fix the mistake and take back her biological child. The other mother does not want to give up the girl she has raised.

A lawyer named Henk Strydom speaks for one of the mothers. A court order says the mothers cannot be named. He said the mistake is very sad and may not end happily.

Both women gave birth at Tambo Memorial Hospital in Boksburg, east of Johannesburg, on the same day in 2010. At the time, no one noticed anything was wrong.

In 2013, one mother, who is 33 and unemployed, went to court to ask her ex-partner for money to help support her daughter. The man said he was not the father. A DNA test showed he was not the father, and the child was also not the mother’s biological child. The mother was very upset and did not know what to do.

Later, she met the other mother. Since December 2013, they have gone to counselling together, set up by the hospital. They also met their biological daughters.

Strydom said his client saw that the other girl looked like her. He said it was very traumatic for her. She is caring for a child who is not biologically hers, while her own child is with someone else.

The woman became unhappy with the process and went to the children’s court to try to get custody of her biological child. The other mother refused to give up the girl. Strydom agreed to represent the woman for free. The woman also has one older child.

Strydom said it is a tragedy. He said the other mother has raised the girl for four years, so it is understandable she does not want to give her up.

The High Court in Pretoria asked the University of Pretoria’s Centre for Child Law to investigate what is best for the children. In South African law, what is best for the children is the most important rule. The centre must report back within 90 days.

Strydom said it is hard to know what the court will decide. He said fixing this after four years is very difficult. Waiting longer may make it more traumatic. He also said that whatever happens, someone will be unhappy.

He said that he and his client do not want to sue the hospital or the government health department right now. They are helping with the case and providing counselling.

The Centre for Child Law will interview the mothers and fathers, and other people who have an important relationship with the girls. The children and mothers will have full clinical assessments and may see a psychologist.

Karabo Ngidi, a lawyer at the centre, said the decision must be in the children’s best interests. She said biology matters, but it is not the only thing that matters.

She also said the families are Zulu, so Zulu tradition, culture, and customary law will be considered. It is also possible that the ex-partner of the mother taking legal action could be the biological father of the other girl.

This is not the first baby-swap case in South Africa. In 1995, two mothers received money after their sons, born in 1989, were accidentally switched at a Johannesburg hospital.

Other cases happened in other countries. In 2009, in Oregon in the United States, Dee Ann Angell and Kay Rene Reed found out they were mixed up at birth in 1953 when a nurse brought them back after bathing. In 2013, in Japan, a 60-year-old man who was swapped at birth was given compensation. He was raised by a poor family instead of his rich biological parents. He grew up on welfare and became a truck driver. His biological siblings, and the boy raised in his place, went to private secondary schools and universities.

Bruce Laing, a clinical psychologist in Johannesburg, said the long-term effects of a baby swap can be very strong and scary. He said parents might start to feel resentment toward a child who is not theirs. He also said parents might keep thinking, “What if?”
