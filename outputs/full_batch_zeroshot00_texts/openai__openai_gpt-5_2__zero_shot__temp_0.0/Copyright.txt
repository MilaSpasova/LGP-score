Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is “moral squalor” and is theft. He says it is as much theft as reaching into someone’s pocket and stealing their wallet. Pullman wrote this in an article for Index on Censorship. He is the president of the Society of Authors. He strongly supports copyright laws. He is very critical of people who think it is OK to download music or books without paying.

Pullman writes that the technology is so impressive that some people do not see that what they are doing is wrong. He says it is outrageous that someone can steal an artist’s work and not be punished. He repeats that it is theft, like stealing a wallet.

His article came after music industry leaders met British Prime Minister David Cameron at Downing Street. They talked about web piracy, which means illegal downloading.

Pullman is the writer of the His Dark Materials trilogy. He says authors and musicians often work for years in poverty and without attention to create work that delights audiences. Then, when they finally succeed, their chance to make a living can be taken away. He ends with a simple idea: if we want to enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

In the next issue of the group’s magazine, Pullman is in a dialogue with Cathy Casserly. She is the chief executive of Creative Commons. Creative Commons offers open content licences that “lets creators take copyright into their own hands”. Casserly says there are many problems with copyright, because it was created “in an analogue age”. She says copyright can block many ways people can share, build on, and remix other people’s work, ways that were not imagined when the laws were made.

Casserly says artists need to think in new ways about how they share and make money from their work. She quotes the science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees with her. The magazine’s editor, Rachael Jolley, said: “Existing copyright laws don’t work in the digital age and risk criminalizing consumers. We need new models for how artists, writers and musicians earn a living from their work.”

The debate is active, and illegal downloading is very large. Data collected by Ofcom (the independent regulator and competition authority for the UK communications industries) suggests that, between November 2012 and January 2013 in the UK, 280 million music tracks were pirated. It also suggests 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files were pirated.

Ofcom has said 18% of internet users aged over 12 admit they have recently pirated content, and 9% say they fear getting caught. Pullman writes that the speed and ease of getting music as MP3 downloads is still surprising to people who have been building their iTunes list for some time.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron chose the Conservative MP Mike Weatherley to be his adviser on this issue. A spokesman for the BPI, the record industry trade body, said: “Mike Weatherley is a strong champion of copyright and the artists and creative producers it’s there to protect. We hope his influence and the prime minister’s endorsement of copyright will be brought to bear on the approach of the UK’s intellectual property office.”
