He liked the things many millionaires like. He owned a gold and silver Rolex and many expensive cars. He also liked modern art. But this Chinese businessman, who had several companies and a very large villa near Madrid, had almost no money in the bank. This made Spanish officials interested.

Gao Ping had lived most of his adult life in Spain. He controlled supplies for 4,000 Chinese bazaars in Spain. But officials thought he was not paying taxes on clothes, furniture, and other goods he brought in from China.

In 2012, police raided his warehouses. They found piles of cash. Bundles of €100, €200, and €500 notes were tied with rubber bands. About €12m was taken away in trolleys. It was the biggest cash seizure ever by Spanish police. The gang, with Gao as the suspected leader, is accused of washing up to €300m a year. They are also accused of selling fake goods and toys with false safety marks. A government prosecutor said Gao’s illegal business was so big it was hurting Spain’s ability to compete. Gao is out on bail. The case has not yet gone to trial.

Police have long worried about €500 notes. They are small and easy to carry for their value. People who avoid taxes, wash money, and drug bosses often use them. €1m in €500 notes can fit in a small laptop bag. The same amount in €50 notes would need a small suitcase. Some cash carriers have folded the notes into plastic pellets and swallowed them. A safer way is to hide the notes inside a car.

The UK stopped giving out €500 notes in 2010. It said people wanted them “almost entirely for criminal purposes”. In 2009, Italy’s central bank said mafia money washers and terrorists used the notes a lot. Other countries have limited large notes because of links to organized crime. Canada stopped its $1,000 note in 2000 after advice from law enforcement.

Today many people use electronic payments and contactless cards. More people now ask if printing these big notes makes sense. Peter Sands, the former head of Standard Chartered Bank, has asked to end high-value notes. He included the €500, the $100, the 1,000 Swiss Franc note, and the £50. In a report for the Harvard Kennedy School of Government, Sands said it was time to get rid of high-value notes that help “bad guys” with tax cheating, financial crime, terrorist funding, and corruption. He said criminals might switch to smaller bills, or to gold or diamonds. But those choices are bigger and easier to track, so criminals are more likely to be caught.

At a conference in London about terrorist funding, the Head of Europol, Rob Wainwright, asked the European Central Bank (ECB) to review if it “should continue to produce and circulate these notes” that make it easier for criminals and terrorists to hide their work and pay for illegal acts. Europol says the purple €500 note is 30% of the total value of all euro notes in use, even though most people have never seen one.

The €500 note started in 2002, when the euro began. It replaced the 1,000 Deutschmark, the 10,000 Belgian franc, and the 500,000 Italian lira. Some European countries liked big banknotes. Pia Hüttl, an affiliate fellow at the Bruegel thinktank, says this preference has existed for a long time. She says it comes from the idea that cash costs less and is accepted everywhere.

Cash is still used a lot in Germany and Austria. More than half of all payments there are made with notes and coins. Hans-Jürgen Papier, former president of Germany’s constitutional court, said limits on cash go against personal freedom. The tabloid newspaper Bild started a petition to defend cash, including the €500 note, saying “hands off my cash”.

Police are less sure about keeping these notes, because there are many reports of suspicious cash. In one case, two men went into a bank and tried to deposit €200,000 in torn and muddy €500 notes. In the same week, €1.3m in €500 notes was found in the false bottom of a suitcase. But suspicion alone is not enough to keep people in custody. Jennifer MacLeod, a specialist in Europol’s financial intelligence group, says it is often impossible to give judges enough proof that suspicious cash is linked to a crime. She says it is even harder because time is short and cooperation and information sharing are broken up.

Europol wants central banks to take more responsibility for strange patterns in €500 note use. For example, Luxembourg issued more than twice its yearly GDP in banknotes in 2013, even though it is one of Europe’s countries that uses cash the least. Europol asked Luxembourg’s central bank to explain. MacLeod says Luxembourg replied that it only gives out the notes people ask for and has no explanation for the demand. She says she is surprised the bank does not think it has responsibility here.

This may be changing. Mario Draghi, head of the ECB, said he does not want the money the bank earns from issuing notes to be “a comfort for criminals”. Other ECB leaders, like Yves Mersch, say there is no evidence about criminal use of the €500 note. But because fears about terrorism are stronger, this view may not convince people now. EU finance ministers asked policymakers to look for “appropriate restrictions” on high-value notes and report back by 1 May 2016.
