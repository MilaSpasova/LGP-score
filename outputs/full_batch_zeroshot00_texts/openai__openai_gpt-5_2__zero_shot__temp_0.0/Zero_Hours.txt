New information suggests that more than one million British workers may be on zero-hours contracts. This is much higher than people thought before.

A poll of more than 1,000 employers by the Chartered Institute of Personnel and Development (CIPD) found this. It led to new calls for the government to investigate these contracts. Many organizations, from retail chains to Buckingham Palace, were criticized for hiring staff but not promising any work or pay each week. Workers on zero-hours contracts often do not get holiday or sick pay. They may also have to ask permission before getting another job.

The CIPD found that 38% of people on zero-hours contracts say they work full-time, usually 30 hours or more each week. One-third of voluntary sector employers use these contracts, and one in four public sector organizations use them.

The new numbers also raise questions about official data. The Office for National Statistics increased its estimate by 25%, to about 250,000 UK workers on zero-hours contracts.

Peter Cheese, chief executive of the CIPD, said there should be a closer look at what a zero-hours contract means, the different types, and clearer guidance on good and bad use. He said the review should look at both the benefits and the downsides for businesses and workers.

Sports Direct became controversial when it was reported that about 20,000 of its 23,000 staff are on zero-hours contracts. Other employers were also named, including Cineworld and Buckingham Palace, which uses them for 350 summer workers. Pub group J D Wetherspoon has 24,000 staff, or 80% of its workers, on these contracts.

Vidhya Alakeson from the Resolution Foundation said that if around one million people are on these contracts, it affects a large part of the workforce. She said the government needs a deep review of zero-hours contracts and their effects on workers’ rights, how much they can earn, and their well-being.

Unions and poverty campaign groups say some employers pressure staff to sign these contracts to avoid responsibilities and cut benefits.

Dave Prentis from the union Unison said most workers are on these contracts because they have no choice. He said a few people get flexibility, but employers have more power and workers find it hard to complain.

Workers on zero-hours contracts are often told their hours only when weekly or monthly rotas are made. They are expected to be ready for extra work at short notice. They should get holiday pay based on the hours they work, but they do not qualify for sick pay.

The National Trust, which uses zero-hours contracts for many seasonal workers, said it pays them the same as full-time staff, in proportion to the hours they work. It said it needs flexible workers because it depends on the weather. A spokeswoman said it is important to change rotas quickly when the weather changes.

Politician Chuka Umunna said these contracts should not be common. He said some workers like the flexibility, but many feel insecure because they do not know when work will come.

Some people say zero-hours contracts may have helped the UK avoid higher unemployment during the economic downturn. CIPD research also says only 16% of workers on zero-hours contracts report that their employer often does not give them enough hours each week.

The CIPD also found that 17% of private sector employers use zero-hours contracts. This is lower than the 34% in the voluntary sector and 24% in the public sector.

The industries most likely to have at least one person on a zero-hours contract were hotels, catering and leisure (48%), education (35%), and healthcare (27%).
