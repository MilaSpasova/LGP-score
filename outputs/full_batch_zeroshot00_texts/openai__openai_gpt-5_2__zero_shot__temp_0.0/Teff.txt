At Addis Ababa airport, visitors see pictures of golden grain, tiny red-brown seeds, and men around a very large pancake. Signs say: “Teff: the ultimate gluten-free crop!”

Ethiopia is one of the poorest countries in the world, and many people worry about having enough food. But Ethiopia is also the home of teff. Teff is a very healthy old grain. More and more, it is being sold in health-food shops and supermarkets in Europe and America.

Teff seeds are tiny, about the size of poppy seeds. They have a lot of calcium, iron, and protein, and many amino acids. Teff is naturally gluten free. People can use it instead of wheat flour to make bread, pasta, waffles, and pizza bases. Like quinoa, teff’s strong nutrition could bring new markets in the west.

In Ethiopia, teff is very important. About 6.3 million farmers grow it. Teff fields cover more than 20% of all farm land. People grind it into flour and make injera, a soft, fermented flatbread that is a basic food in Ethiopia. Teff is also used in many religious and cultural events. In Ethiopia and nearby Eritrea, people eat together around large injera. They use it to pick up stews, and they sometimes feed each other to show friendship or loyalty. This tradition is called gursha.

For many years, outside diaspora communities in the west, most people did not notice teff. Now, more people want traditional crops, and health-food and gluten-free markets are growing. Some people call teff Ethiopia’s “second gift to the world”, after coffee.

Sophie Kebede is a London entrepreneur with a UK company that focuses on teff. She says she was very surprised when she learned how nutritious it is. She says she is Ethiopian and has eaten injera her whole life.

But higher demand for “ancient grains” does not always help poor communities. In Bolivia and Peru, the global quinoa trade has raised incomes for some people. But there have also been reports of malnutrition and fights over land, because farmers sell all their crop to meet western demand.

In Ethiopia, the growing middle class also wants more teff. Prices inside the country have gone up, and the poorest people cannot always afford it. Today, many small farmers sell most of what they grow to city buyers.

This may raise income in some rural areas, but it can also hurt nutrition, the government says. Teff is the most nutritious grain in the country. Estimates say people in cities eat up to 61kg of teff each year, but people in rural areas eat about 20kg. Rich people mostly eat the more expensive magna and white teff. People with less money often eat red and mixed teff, which are less valuable. More than half also mix teff with cheaper grains like sorghum and maize.

The Ethiopian government wants to double teff production by 2015. A 2013 plan says teff could help school meals and emergency aid, and could reduce malnutrition, especially for children and teenagers.

Ethiopia’s economy is growing fast, but the country is still on the UN list of least-developed countries. About 20% of children under five are malnourished or have stunted growth.

The government’s Agricultural Transformation Agency wants higher harvests. It plans to create better types of teff, use new planting methods, and use tools to reduce losses after harvest.

Since 2006, the government has blocked exports of raw teff grain. Only injera and other processed teff products can be shipped out. But this could change. The government’s plan says Ethiopia wants enough teff for people at home and also a strong export market.

Mama Fresh is a family company that has sold injera to top restaurants and hotels in Addis Ababa for years. It also ships injera to Finland, Germany, Sweden, and the US, mainly for diaspora communities. Now the company is also looking at the gluten-free market. It plans to double exports to America in 2014 and soon will make teff pizzas, bread, and cookies.

David Hallam, a director at the UN Food and Agriculture Organization, says these products pass through many hands before they reach stores like Sainsbury’s. Each step takes some profit, and small farmers may not be able to bargain well with big traders. He says quinoa is a warning that exports can be good and bad for poor countries.

Regassa Feyissa, an Ethiopian agricultural scientist and a former head of the National Institute for Biodiversity, warns that if teff exports grow without careful planning, farmers may stop growing other important crops. He also warns that attempts to raise production could help businesses more than small farmers.

Because there is little Ethiopian teff on the world market, farmers in the US have started growing it. Farmers in Europe, Israel, and Australia have also tried it.

Kebede says she buys teff from farms in southern Europe, but she would rather buy it from Ethiopia. She says teff is natural for Ethiopians, so Ethiopian farmers should be the best suppliers. She asks why Ethiopian farmers cannot benefit from this popular grain.
