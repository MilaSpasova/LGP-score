You can no longer legally smoke a cigarette inside a bar in New Orleans, Louisiana. Many cities have banned indoor smoking, but New Orleans is different. The city attracts tourists by advertising a “let the good times roll” attitude. So a smoking ban in New Orleans may have special effects.

The Wall Street Journal reported that since Hurricane Katrina in 2005, the New Orleans city government has tried to calm things down. With help from neighbourhood groups, the city has policed bars and nightclubs more strictly. At the same time, the city has tried to create a new “noise ordinance” (a rule about music and noise).

Bar owner William Walker dislikes the anti-smoking law. “This is just the wrong time for them to have pursued something like this,” he says. He thinks making people go outside to smoke will make tensions worse.

Many of New Orleans’s best bars and some live music places are in quiet residential neighbourhoods. Living side by side like this is part of what makes the city charming. But lately, some people do not accept this arrangement, and people worry about the future of New Orleans’s music culture.

Martha Wood lives next to a loud bar with live music. She says the bar was one reason she bought her house, so she will not complain about noise. She also manages a live-music bar. After noise problems in 2013 (including complaints about loud smokers outside), her bar became one of the first in New Orleans that was legally not allowed to serve drinks to go. Wood says, “Now, any small infraction could get us shut down.” She says they installed a “smoke eater” to deal with smoke because they cannot open the doors at night because of the “noise.”

The Maple Leaf club chose to go smoke-free in 2014. Another club did the same after artists asked for smoke-free nights. Zalia BeVille, manager of the All Ways Lounge, says many performance places were already doing this for performers. She wishes the city had let it happen naturally instead of forcing the ban on every corner bar, even bars without music. The All Ways Lounge went smoke-free in 2013 because, she says, “The horn players and the singers, they asked us for it.”

All Ways has an outdoor patio. Lost Love Lounge does not. Its owner, Geoff Douville, likes the ban. Before, he felt he had to allow smoking so his bar could make enough money. He says he could not ban smoking unless the whole city did it too. If he banned it alone, smokers would go to another bar nearby. He wanted the rule to be the same for everyone.

Many small business owners fear they will lose money without smokers. Smoker Neil Timms owns an English pub. He saw a smoking ban before in England. He remembers that in Coventry, every pub where he was a regular closed within a year of the UK ban that began in 2007. To avoid the same problem, he is spending money to build a patio.

Douville thinks the ban could help business. He says many people would like to come for the bar and food, but did not want to smell like smoke for the next seven days. Now those people may come. Douville also is not worried about noise complaints the way Walker is. He says a court will not call a bar a “nuisance” after the city has approved a smoking ban that makes people go outside. He says, “I want to see a judge reconcile ‘no loitering’ with ‘no smoking inside.’”

Councilwoman LaToya Cantrell introduced and pushed the ban. She disagrees with Douville. She says bar owners must keep their customers respectful outside too. Owners and bartenders should tell people they can smoke outside but must respect the community. She says it is silly to say the city cannot have clean air because of noise. She believes it can be a win-win, with better communication and partnerships between communities and businesses.

Cantrell says New Orleans is unique, but still needs the ban. She says New Orleans is known as “the City that Care Forgot,” but the city should say, “We care about our people.” She says the most vulnerable people are workers in smoky places. These workers are important to the hospitality industry, which drives Louisiana’s economy. She adds that in New Orleans, people can take their drink outside when they go out to smoke.

Many people worried that the New Orleans Police Department, already under strain, should not have to police smokers. Instead, the health department will handle warnings and fines. Customers are encouraged to fill out a form or call 311, and to include date-and-time-stamped photos of illegal smoking. Neil Timms says he will follow the ban for this reason. He does not want someone to take a photo of a person smoking, and then the smoker gets beaten up.

Geoff Douville says he is used to noise complaints, so he is not worried about the ban. He says the neighbours who complain about noise now may be the same ones who wanted the smoking ban. In the end, he agrees with Cantrell’s win-win hope. He says people will complain, but that does not mean they will win.
