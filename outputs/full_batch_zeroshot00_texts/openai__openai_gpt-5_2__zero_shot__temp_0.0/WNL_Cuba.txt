Young people in Cuba are rushing to use the first known free internet service that anyone can access. It is in Cuba, a communist island nation. A small cultural center in Havana has become a rare place with free wi-fi. The service is offered by a famous Cuban artist named Kcho. His spokesman said the state phone company, Etecsa, approved it.

The internet is very slow, especially when many people are there. But in Cuba, only about 5% of people are thought to have internet with no limits. So a place that is free and has no restrictions is seen as something very special.

People are excited because they can read world news, talk to family and friends in other countries, and use Facebook and Twitter. Adonis Ortiz, who is 20, said, “I come as often as I can,” while he video-chatted with his father in the US. He has not seen his father in nine years.

The US and Cuba are slowly easing a long freeze in talks and trade. People expect that American tech companies like Google and Apple may enter the Cuban market when they are allowed. Cuba has also put a high-speed fiber-optic cable under the sea from Venezuela. Internet users also have some access to Chinese equipment.

Another estimate says about one quarter of Cubans have internet access. But this includes people who use a restricted Cuban intranet with only certain websites and limited email.

Kcho has let the public use his own personal internet connection. Some people think he may be an approved figure for the government as Cuba slowly accepts that people want the internet.

Kcho has close ties to the Cuban government. He said the Ministry of Culture approved what he is doing. He said he wants Cubans to learn to use the internet. He told the Associated Press, “It is expensive but the benefit is tremendous. I have something that is great and powerful. I can share it and I am doing so.”

Kcho’s real name is Alexis Leiva Machado. He became famous around the world for his paintings, sculptures, and drawings after winning the grand prize at an important art biennial in South Korea. He is now getting ready for the Havana biennial in May. He was born on one of Cuba’s outlying islands. He is known for modern art with rustic, seaside, and patriotic themes.

In the center’s courtyard, many young people sit in wicker chairs under a white canopy, or outside when it is full. They use laptops, tablets, and smartphones.

Cuba has some of the lowest internet connection rates in the world. Dial-up accounts are tightly limited. Home broadband is almost never available, except for foreigners who pay hundreds of dollars each month. In Cuba, the average salary is about $17 to $20 a month. Kcho is estimated to pay $900 a month to provide the free wi-fi.

Since 2013, the Cuban government has opened hundreds of internet salons. One hour online costs $4.50, and the speed is much slower than at Kcho’s studio, which is around 2mbps. A 2014 report by Akamai Technologies said average internet speeds were about 10.5mbps in the US and 23.6mbps in South Korea. The world average was about 3.9mbps.

Because dozens of people use Kcho’s wi-fi at the same time, the signal gets weaker. One user said he sometimes goes in the middle of the night, when nobody else is there, and then it feels unbelievably fast.
