Robert Mys_ajek stops and looks closely. On a muddy mountain path, between two paw prints, he finds what he wants. “Droppings!” he says happily. People rarely see wolves, so finding their droppings is a good day, even for an expert.

But wolves are becoming easier to find in Poland. There are now about 1,500 wolves there. The number has doubled in 15 years. Wolves, along with the brown bear, the lynx, and the wolverine, are Europe’s last large meat-eating predators. Conservationists from Britain, Germany, and the Netherlands come to Poland to learn how the country has saved this protected animal, which has a bad name even in fairy tales.

The black droppings have bits of bone and hair. “It ate a red deer,” says the University of Warsaw biologist. “In my lab, I can learn a lot about this wolf: what it ate, if it is male or female, its age, its health, and its family.”

DNA tests show that Polish wolves travel far. “One wolf reached the Netherlands, but it was hit by a car,” says Mys_ajek. “They need a lot of space. The average area for a Polish wolf pack is 250 square kilometres.”

“Could we ever bring wolves back to Scotland?” asks student Alex Entwisle, 23. He is on a field trip from a UK college to southern Poland. The students spend the day looking for droppings and paw prints in the spruce forests of the Beskidy mountains in the Polish Carpathians.

They talk a lot about bringing wolves back to the British Isles, for the first time since the 18th century.

Mys_ajek visited the Scottish Highlands in 2015 as a guest of the British charity Wolves and Humans Foundation. People there asked him about Poland. “The big difference is that in Poland we eat pork. We do not have many sheep here,” he says.

“The similarity is that we have many wild animals: 300,000 red deer and more than 800,000 roe deer. We also have too many wild boar, about 200,000. They damage farmers’ cereal crops. Here, wolves help with this problem.”

Mys_ajek, who is often on Polish television, says wolves can travel up to 30 km during one hunt. “The Beskidy pack is strong, with eight or nine wolves. This year we recorded five cubs, two yearlings, and two adults.”

“We track them with cameras that turn on when something moves, and by following prints in mud and snow. In each family group, only one pair has pups, once a year. All pack members help care for the young.”

Mys_ajek, whose father was a shepherd, does not understand why wolves have such a bad reputation. “Why do people say ‘lone-wolf gunman’? Why did we need Little Red Riding Hood?”

He is interested in wolves because they stayed wild 33,000 years ago, while others became domestic dogs.

Wolves are not pet dogs. Mys_ajek says only scientific reasons can save Europe’s wild predators, especially the unpopular wolf. Wolves help forests grow again and control the number of wild animals. “Natural predators balance nature. They stop plant-eating animals from eating too much, so trees can grow tall and birds can nest in them.”

What about shooting deer instead? “It is only a partial solution,” he says. “In a varied natural area, animals also change where they go because they fear predators. They do not stay all day eating soft grass near rivers. They move away to avoid being trapped. This gives plants a chance to grow.”

Poland banned wolf hunting in the western Carpathians in 1995 and across the whole country in 1998. Now there are wolf packs in almost all major forests. Wolves live near people instead of being kept behind fences, like in African safari parks.

The Polish government pays farmers when wolves kill livestock. Mys_ajek advises farmers on using electric fences. He has also helped bring back two old ways to scare wolves: red bunting hung around sheep pens, and the barking of the fluffy white Tatra Mountain Sheepdog.

Poland’s wolves have also been helped by the country’s slower building of roads and motorways. In 1989, when the communists lost power, Poland had only one motorway. Big road projects started after Poland joined the European Union in 2004, and they required studies about wildlife. Poland now has one of the highest numbers in the world of wildlife bridges and underpasses.

People’s attitudes have changed too. “For many years, hunting was part of culture. In 1975, there were fewer than 100 wolves in Poland. From the 1950s, the authorities encouraged people to kill wolves. They paid a reward worth a month’s salary. It was slaughter,” says Mys_ajek.

Mys_ajek says wolves are doing much better now, but their situation is still fragile. Packs cross borders, and wolves are still hunted in nearby Russia, Lithuania, Belarus, Ukraine, and Slovakia.

He says Poland’s government, elected in October 2015, is against wolves. “The Environment Minister, Jan Szyszko, is openly a hunter. There are 120,000 licensed hunters in Poland, and they have influence in parliament.”

“Hunters say wolves are pests and claim there are 4,000 wolves in Poland. He says this number is false and comes from an unscientific count. He says the government could undo the progress.”

Supporting wolves is not easy. “You cannot tell politicians that wolves bring many tourists,” he says. “Most tourists want to see them, but wolves avoid people. Their sense of smell is very strong.”
