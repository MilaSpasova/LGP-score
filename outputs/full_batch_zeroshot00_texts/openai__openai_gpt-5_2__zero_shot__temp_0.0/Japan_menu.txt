The problem started with a fake “scallop” dish. Now many famous hotels and department stores in Japan are in trouble for lying on their menus. This could hurt Japan’s good name for great food around the world.

One luxury hotel chain admitted that it lied about where some ingredients came from. After that, Japanese news reported new cases almost every day.

The Hankyu-Hanshin hotel chain in Osaka said it gave false menu descriptions for many items at some restaurants from 2006 to October 2013. About 78,000 diners were affected.

One main example was a dish called red salmon “caviar.” It was not salmon eggs. It was flying fish eggs, which are cheaper.

The company president, Hiroshi Desaki, went on TV and said he would take a 20% pay cut, and other executives would take 10% cuts. This did not calm angry customers.

A few days later, Desaki resigned. He said the group had “betrayed our customers,” but he also said, “We never had the intention to deceive them.” Later, one head chef refused a medal of honour he was supposed to get from the government.

The company has refunded more than 10,000 customers, paying about 20m yen so far. The total cost may reach 110m yen.

The scandal spread to other well-known food businesses, like Japan’s version of the UK horsemeat scandal. No one has become sick from the wrongly labelled food, but people are still very angry.

For example, some customers thought they were eating expensive kuruma shrimps. They were actually eating cheaper black tiger shrimp.

The first case was almost ignored. The Prince Hotel in Tokyo admitted a problem after a diner wrote a blog post. He said a “scallop” dish he ordered used a similar but cheaper shellfish.

The hotel investigated and corrected more than 50 menu items at many restaurants. This report pushed Hankyu-Hanshin and other hotels to admit they also tricked diners who thought they were paying for top ingredients.

The Hotel Okura chain, which has hosted guests including Barack Obama, admitted many problems. It said it injected beef with fat to make it juicier, and it called tomatoes organic when they were not.

It said, “We deeply apologize for betraying the expectations and confidence of our clients.”

More examples of false menu items were found: carton orange juice sold as fresh juice; Mont Blanc desserts topped with Korean chestnuts instead of French chestnuts; bought chocolate cream sold as home-made; imported beef sold as high-end wagyu.

Japan’s top government spokesman, Yoshihide Suga, commented. He said these cases appeared one after another, and the wrong labels caused people to lose trust. He said these were “clearly cover-ups.”

The scandal came at a bad time. Japan is trying to get South Korea and other countries to remove bans on food imports after the Fukushima nuclear accident. Also, UNESCO is thinking about adding Japanese cuisine to its intangible cultural heritage list.

One local newspaper wrote the headline, “Japan’s proud food culture in tears.” The newspaper Yomiuri Shimbun said it was “astonished by the industry’s lack of morals.”

The newspaper warned that the scandal could harm “brand Japan,” including products and services that foreign countries and tourists praise for safety and security.

Experts said the 2008 global financial crisis forced luxury hotels to cut costs, but they still tried to attract diners with detailed menu descriptions.

Hiroshi Tomozawa, a hotel and restaurant consultant, told Kyodo News that menu descriptions were made because consumers like brand products. When hotels could not get the ingredients listed, they used food from different places instead.

Even so, the hotels and restaurants may not face legal action. Menus are not covered by the agricultural standards law or a new food labelling law planned for 2015. The government’s only legal tool is a law that bans misleading claims about goods and services.

The biggest problem for the industry may be Japan’s careful and demanding customers. In a 2009 poll by an online restaurant guide, 72% said where food comes from was the most important thing when choosing menu dishes. Next were calorie and nutrition details.
