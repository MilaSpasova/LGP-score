Dan Sullivan was late to breakfast with the Guardian. He was late because police stopped him while he was riding his motorcycle.

Sullivan says he works too much. He misses dinner dates. He forgets to give presents.

So the 27-year-old venture capitalist made a start-up to help: BetterBoyfriend.me. This service sends girlfriends and wives a present every month for about $70.

Sullivan works in finance. He has been testing the program quietly. He says about 350 boyfriends have signed up. This brings in about $17,000 each month. He says most members are his Harvard and investor friends, like venture capitalists, founders, and workers at companies like Apple, Google, and Facebook. Their girlfriends have been getting presents from Sullivan for the last year.

Each month, members pick from a list of seven gifts, such as chocolate, tea sets, and manicures. The package is sent to the boyfriend without a brand name and without a label.

Sullivan tests ideas by buying Facebook ads to see what results he gets. BetterBoyfriend.me grew quickly. Sullivan said, “BetterBoyfriend hit something. It’s a pain point everyone has.”

BetterBoyfriend.me was not his first idea. For a few days, he thought about a dog-sharing idea. He said he saw rich guys looking miserable while walking dogs. He said a dog is only valuable 5% of the time. He wondered if someone could babysit the dogs and other people could rent them.

Another start-up is called InvisibleBoyfriend. It lets users design a believable social partner who is always available to talk. The result is a “boyfriend” who can text with you all day.

Founder Kyle Tabor launched it to give users a fake boyfriend to stop unwanted advances. But he found that people became attached to their computer-made partners. He said more users want companionship through conversation, not just outside “proof” of a relationship to make people leave them alone.

For Sullivan and Tabor, a surprise was the real relationships they formed with customers. Sullivan says he has started to see himself as a kind of relationship consultant for the boyfriends.

Sullivan admits he made mistakes. At first, the packages included receipts with his name, Dan Sullivan. He said one boyfriend wrote, “Listen, dude, she’s not mad but Cynthia found out.”

Sullivan says about 50% of the women who get packages know he is involved. He says this is connected to age. He thinks that after people have been married for a while, they do not keep many secrets.

Over the year, Sullivan says he has gotten to know the boyfriends well. They even delivered a gift to a hospital delivery room. He said the key is to remember that his relationship is with the boyfriend.

When he first launched the company, he put BetterBoyfriend.me tags on flower bouquets. He went to Dolores Park, a popular place in the Mission District.

He looked for couples and gave the bouquet to the girl. But the boyfriend did not like it. Sullivan said it made sense. So he changed his plan and started giving the bouquets to the boyfriends.
