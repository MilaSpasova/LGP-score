A new report says the average six-year-old understands more about digital technology than a 45-year-old adult. Many children may not know who Steve Jobs was, and some cannot tie their shoelaces, but they still know a lot about devices.

Ofcom, a group that checks standards in the UK communications industries, says broadband in 2000 helped create a generation of “digital natives.” These children were born in the new millennium. They never used slow dial-up internet. Some learn to use smartphones or tablets before they can talk.

Jane Rumble, Ofcom’s media research head, said young people are changing the way we communicate. She said they have different habits from older people, even compared with “early adopters” aged 16 to 24.

Ofcom made a “digital quotient” (DQ) test. They tested 800 children and 2,000 adults. The test is not like an IQ test. It checks how well people understand and feel confident using gadgets like tablets and smart watches. It also checks knowledge of superfast internet, 4G mobile-phone networks, and mobile apps.

Children aged 6 to 7 scored an average DQ of 98. Adults aged 45 to 49 scored 96. Digital understanding is highest at ages 14 to 15, with an average DQ of 113. After that, it slowly drops in adulthood and drops faster in old age.

People can now take a shorter version of the test. It gives them a DQ score and advice on how to improve and stay safe online.

Experts say millennial children communicate and enjoy entertainment in very different ways from older generations. Forecasters think these children’s choices may show the future better than the choices of trendsetting young adults.

The biggest change is phone calls. Twenty years ago, teenagers spent hours at night talking on the home phone.

Now, for ages 12 to 15, phone calls are only 3% of their communication time on any device. For all adults, phone calls are 20%. For young adults, they are 9%. Children mostly socialize from far away by sending written messages and sharing photos and videos. Ofcom says the millennium generation is “losing its voice.”

Over 90% of children’s device time is messaging. They chat on social networks like Facebook, send instant messages with services like WhatsApp, or use traditional text messages. Only 2% of children’s time is email, compared with 33% for adults.

Children aged 12 to 15 also use other media differently. A seven-day digital diary shows that live TV is only about half of what they watch. For all adults, live TV is nearly 70%. Children spend 20% of viewing time on short video clips, such as on YouTube, or news clips shared on Facebook and other social sites. The rest is DVDs, streamed content through Netflix or iTunes, and recorded TV programmes.

Young adults aged 16 to 24 use a lot of different media. But live radio and print media are almost gone from what they use each day.

Younger people are moving from live TV to streaming and catch-up services. Even for adults, TV is becoming less important. TV viewing for 16 to 24-year-olds has gone down each year since 2010. In 2013, viewing fell across all age groups for the first time. Researchers think tablets helped, because many older people used the internet more. Tablets have big screens and simple touch controls. They are linked to more internet access for people over 65.

James Thickett, Research Director at Ofcom, said people over 65 resisted using the internet for years. He said this changed in the last three years, and Ofcom thinks tablets are the reason.

In Britain, people of all ages are using internet devices more. Screen time is now more than sleep time. The typical adult spends 8 hours and 41 minutes a day communicating or using media, including books and newspapers, and sleeps 8 hours and 21 minutes.
