Canadian tennis player Frank Dancevic criticized Australian Open organizers. He said they made players play in “inhumane” heat. He collapsed on court when the temperature reached 41C.

Dancevic collapsed in the second set of his first-round match against France’s Benoît Paire. It happened on uncovered court six at Melbourne Park. He passed out for about a minute. He said the conditions were dangerous. He also said the heat made him hallucinate: “I was dizzy from the middle of the first set and then I saw Snoopy.”

Dancevic said it was not fair to players, fans, or the sport when people were pulling out of matches or passing out. He said he had played many long matches, and passing out after about a set and a half was not normal. He said players complained it was too hot, but the tournament still kept matches going. He said he and many other players did not think it was fair.

Other players agreed. Britain’s number one, Andy Murray, said it should be looked at. He said one bad event could happen, and it looks terrible when players, ball kids, and people in the stands collapse. He said the heat was very tough when he practiced before his match around 2.30–3pm. He said he did not know if it was safe, and he mentioned problems in other sports like heart attacks.

Caroline Wozniacki said she put a water bottle on the court and it started melting underneath the plastic. John Isner said it felt like opening an oven. Victoria Azarenka said it felt very hot, like “dancing in a frying pan.”

The tournament used its “extreme heat” plan for women’s matches on Tuesday. This allowed an extra ten-minute break between the second and third sets. But under a 2014 rule change, the tournament director, Wayne McKewen, decides whether to stop matches.

Organizers did not only use the Celsius temperature. They preferred the Wet Bulb Globe Temperature, which also includes humidity and wind. Organizers said temperatures peaked at 42.2C early Tuesday evening. They said the conditions never reached the level where play would be stopped.

McKewen said they must reach a minimum level and have a forecast that the heat will last long enough. He said that did not happen. He said it was hot and uncomfortable, but low humidity meant play would continue.

After medical help, Dancevic continued the match but lost 7–6, 6–3, 6–4. He said he was close to stopping. He said he was not tired, but his body temperature felt too high.

Earlier, a ball boy needed medical help after collapsing during Milos Raonic’s match on uncovered court eight. Raonic beat Daniel Gimeno-Traver 7–6, 6–1, 4–6, 6–2. After that, the tournament shortened ball boy rotations to 45-minute shifts. China’s Peng Shuai said the heat made her cramp and vomit. After losing 7–5, 4–6, 6–3 to Japan’s Kurumi Nara, she had to be helped off the court.

Officials said the health risks were not as serious as they seemed. They said most matches finished without medical calls. Tim Wood, the tournament’s chief medical officer, said a few players had heat illness or discomfort, but none needed major medical treatment after finishing.

Many players followed Roger Federer’s view that the conditions were hard but the same for both players. He said it is “a mental thing,” and if you cannot handle it, you quit.

Dancevic disagreed. He said it is not only about fitness. He said some players’ bodies handle heat better than others. He said it was dangerous. He also said that an hour and a half after his match, he still could not pee.
