Insects are crunchy and full of protein. People often forget them as food, except in a few places. But in spring 2013, more people will try to make insects seem less “yucky.” Insects may appear not only on special restaurant menus but also in supermarkets.

In April, London will have a festival called Pestival 2013. It is supported by the Wellcome Trust. The event is about learning to like insects, and eating them is an important part. There will be a two-day “pop-up” restaurant by the Nordic Food Lab. This group is linked to the Danish restaurant Noma. Noma served ants at Claridge’s hotel in Mayfair in 2012 for ten days, and it sold out.

Restaurant magazine named Noma the world’s best restaurant for three years in a row. Its chef, René Redzepi, says ants taste like lemon. He says a purée made from fermented grasshoppers and moth larvae tastes like strong fish sauce. Bee larvae can make a sweet mayonnaise used instead of eggs. Scientists keep finding new ways to use small creatures as food.

In March, a BBC documentary will show food writer Stefan Gates looking for and eating deep-fried locusts and barbecued tarantulas. Some people may laugh about this, like jokes about flies in soup. But there is also a serious message. Many experts think eating insects can help the environment.

The UN’s Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) has funded projects since 2011 to support eating and farming insects in south-east Asia and Africa. About two billion people already eat insects and caterpillar larvae regularly. In 2012, the FAO published a list of 1,909 edible insect species. With support from the Dutch government, it planned a major international conference on “this valuable food source” in 2013.

Insects are very common. Around the world, for every human there are 40 tonnes of insects. So they are not likely to become endangered, and they are unlikely to have been given chemicals. Redzepi said it is taboo to eat bugs in the western world, but asked why not. He said that in south-east Asia it is common, and that people eat bugs in many parts of the world. He also said that if you like mushrooms, you have eaten many worms without knowing it. He added that we eat honey, and honey is the vomit of a bee, and said to think of that next time you put it in tea.

Redzepi said the basic idea behind Nordic Food Lab was: “Nothing is not edible.”

Insects are important for life on Earth. There are more than a million species, and insects are the most varied group of creatures on the planet. But people often misunderstand them, hate them, and kill them just because they are there.

In the next 30 years, the human population will grow to nine billion. Already one billion people do not have enough food. More people will put more pressure on farmland, water, forests, fisheries, biodiversity, nutrients, and energy.

Meat is becoming more costly. This is not only about money. Rainforest is also destroyed to make land for grazing or to grow food for cattle. There is also methane released by cows. Livestock farming adds a lot to greenhouse gas emissions: 35% of the planet’s methane, 65% of its nitrous oxide, and 9% of its carbon dioxide.

Edible insects produce fewer gases. They have high-quality protein, vitamins, and amino acids. They also turn food into body mass efficiently. To produce the same amount of protein, they need about a quarter of the food that sheep need, and half of what pigs and chickens need. They create fewer greenhouse gases and less ammonia than cows, and they can be raised on organic waste.

China is setting up large maggot farms. Zimbabwe has a strong mapone caterpillar industry. Laos received nearly $500,000 from the FAO for an insect-harvesting project. In the UK, it is already big business, though not always official. A man was detained by Gatwick customs after a flight from Burkina Faso with 94 kilos of mapone in his luggage, worth nearly £40,000.

A study by FoodServiceWarehouse.com said that replacing pork and beef with crickets and locusts could cut greenhouse gas emissions by up to 95%. The text ends with a dark joke: when we die, worms and insects will get a chance to eat us back.
