On Tuesday, the Duke and Duchess of Cambridge won the first part of their fight for privacy. A French magazine was told it could not sell or reuse photos of the couple taken at a private chateau in Provence.

But the case is not finished. French prosecutors must now decide if there will be a criminal case against the magazine editor and the photographer or photographers. The photos showed the duchess sunbathing topless while on holiday in the south of France.

A court in Nanterre, near Paris, ordered the gossip magazine Closer to give the digital photo files within 24 hours. The court also ordered the magazine not to share the photos again, including on its website and tablet app.

The ruling only affects Mondadori Magazines France, the company that publishes Closer. It must pay €2,000 in legal costs. If it does not follow the order, it can be fined €10,000 for each day it fails. The couple did not ask for damages.

The judges said the photos were very intrusive. They showed a couple partly naked on the terrace of a private home, in a park hundreds of metres from a public road. The couple could reasonably think they were protected from people passing by.

The judges also ruled that any future photo published in France by Mondadori would bring a €10,000 fine for each breach.

But the ruling only talks about the 14 pictures already published. Closer’s editor has suggested she has other, more private pictures.

St James’s Palace said the couple welcomed the ruling. A source said they believed the law was broken and they had a right to privacy. Maud Sobel, a lawyer for the couple in Paris, called it a wonderful decision and said they had been proven right.

Even though they won this civil case, the couple also want a criminal case for breach of privacy against the magazine and the photographer or photographers.

The prosecutor must decide who would be charged. The complaint names “persons unknown.” But it is understood the couple want a case against Closer’s editor, and whoever took the photos at the chateau. The chateau belongs to Lord Linley, the son of the late Princess Margaret.

Paris police started a preliminary investigation on Tuesday. Under French law, breach of privacy can lead to up to one year in prison and a €45,000 fine.

The couple hope this criminal action will send a strong message and stop paparazzi in the future.

Their lawyers did not ask for copies of Closer to be taken off shop shelves.

On Saturday, the Irish Daily Star printed the photos. Its editor was suspended on Monday night while an internal investigation happens. Also on Monday, the Italian celebrity magazine Chi, owned by Mondadori, published a special issue with 26 pages of the photos.

The couple’s lawyer, Aurélien Hamelle, told the court it was necessary to stop the “highly intimate” photos of the duchess without her bikini top. He said she is a young woman, not an object.

Delphine Pando, defending Closer, said the legal action was not fair compared to what happened. She also said the magazine could not control people reselling the photos because it did not own the original images.

Copies of Closer were selling quickly on the auction site eBay. One sold for £31.01. eBay later removed all listings after strong feedback from its community.
