“I got a Dyson vacuum cleaner, but I don’t even know if I want it. I just picked it up,” said Louise Haggerty. She is 56 and works as a hairdresser and waitress. She went to the Black Friday sales at 1am. “It was mental in there. It was crazy. It was absolutely disgusting,” she said.

Haggerty went with a friend to the 24-hour Sainsbury’s supermarket in Harringay, north-east London. They hoped to get a cheap flat-screen TV. “But so many people pushed in the queue we didn’t have a chance,” she said. “The poor woman who was second in the queue was pushed out by a crowd of youths. She didn’t get anything. People were behaving like animals. It was horrible. I only saw two security guards.”

Haggerty could not buy a Blaupunkt 40" TV that was reduced from £299.99 to £149.99. So she rushed to pick up a Dyson Animal Vac, down from £319.99 to £159.99. “I don’t even know how much it costs. I don’t even know if I’m going to buy it. I just wanted something,” she said. “There are lads in there with three, four, five tellies. It’s not fair.”

One of those lads was Andy Blackett, 30, an estate agent. He had two trolleys full of bargains. “I got two coffee makers, two tablets, two TVs and a stereo,” he said. “I couldn’t tell you the prices but I know they’re bargains.” But his friend Henry Fischer, a 19-year-old student, did not do as well. “Someone snatched my telly from me. It’s cos I’m the smaller one.”

Blackett, Fischer, and some friends drove to Sainsbury’s at 12.45am. They left Tesco’s 24-hour Lea Valley supermarket because it was “bedlam.” The Black Friday sale there started at midnight. “Tesco was scary so we came here instead,” Blackett said.

More than a dozen police officers went to the Tesco store on Glover Drive, Upper Edmonton. Fights broke out between shoppers. People were seen tearing down cardboard barriers that were meant to hold back sale items until midnight. Tesco delayed selling its most popular sale items, TVs, for almost an hour until police got control. One police officer was heard criticizing the manager for not having enough security. The officer suggested stopping the sale. This Tesco is across the road from an IKEA store where there were riots when it opened with a midnight sale in 2005.

Police also stepped in at other stores, including Tesco in Willesden and Surrey Quays, just before doors opened at midnight. Greater Manchester Police said at least two people were arrested at Black Friday sales that morning. On Twitter they said: “Keep calm, people!” South Wales Police said staff at Tesco stores made calls because they were worried about how many people came to the sales.

When TV sales started just before 1am, one of the first buyers was James Alled, 30, a businessman. He bought two flat-screen TVs and started trying to sell one to someone farther down the queue. “I bought them for £250. I’ll sell it to you for £350, £300 cash,” he said. Farther back, Christine Ball, 62, was angry. “I got here at 10.15pm and I’m further back now than when I got here,” she said. “None of that lot know what a queue is.”

Ball, like most shoppers, had not heard of the US-inspired Black Friday sales before now. She said she came to buy her grandson a TV for Christmas. “Not one of those massive ones; just a normal one at £100 or so,” she said. In her basket she had a pint of semi-skimmed milk and a loaf of bread. “Telly, milk and bread – the necessities,” she said.

Mel Mehmet, 23, had been to Black Friday sales in 2013 and expected queues. But she said the feeling in Tesco scared her this time. “It’s crazy, really, having it at midnight. The police must have more important things to do at night than be called to sales. We’re going to PC World first thing. Their sale starts at 8am.”
