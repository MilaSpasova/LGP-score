Clay Cockrell sits in his office at Columbus Circle. Across the street is 1 Central Park West. It has Trump International Hotel and Tower. Central Park is in front of it. Cockrell does his popular “walk and talk” therapy in Central Park.

Cockrell used to work on Wall Street. Now he is a therapist. He spends a lot of time walking in Central Park. He also walks in Battery Park near Wall Street. He listens to and helps some of New York’s richest people.

He said he became an expert in wealth therapy naturally. He said the extremely wealthy, the “1% of the 1%,” often feel their problems are not real problems. But he says they are real. He says many therapists do not take their issues seriously enough.

What problems do America’s 1% have? Cockrell said some feel guilty about being rich. Some feel they must hide that they are rich. He also said being in the 1% can be lonely. The writer F Scott Fitzgerald said the very rich “are different from you and me.”

Some counsellors say things got worse after the financial crisis. They say the debate about income inequality also made things worse. Movements like Occupy Wall Street and the Fight for $15 fair wage campaign helped start this debate.

Jamie Traeger-Muney is a wealth psychologist. She founded the Wealth Legacy Group. She said Occupy Wall Street had important things to say about income inequality. But she said it pointed at the 1% and made them seem bad as a group. She also said the media partly makes rich people feel they must hide or feel ashamed.

Traeger-Muney works with inheritors, people who get money from family. She said they often get a bad reputation in the press. She said there are many negative names for inheritors, like “spoilt brat,” “born with a silver spoon in their mouth,” and “trust-fund babies.” She said it is easy to blame the rich.

She said she is sometimes shocked by what people say. She said people talk about rich people in ways they would not use for other groups.

Barbara Nusbaum is an expert in money psychology. She said it can feel very lonely to have a lot of money. She said people’s reactions can be scary. She said people are taught not to talk about money because it is not polite. She said it is harder to talk about having money than not having money. It is more accepted to say, “I am broke. Things are hard.” But you cannot easily say, “I have a ton of money.” She said rich people have to keep much of their life private.

Because of this, Cockrell said rich people often spend time with other rich Americans. He said it is not because they think they are better. It is because they want to be with people who understand their problems.

The gap between rich and poor is happening around the world. Oxfam said the richest 1% increased their share of global wealth from 44% in 2009 to 48% in 2014. Oxfam said they were on track to own more than the other 99% by 2016.

In the US, over the last three decades, the top 0.1% of households increased their share of wealth from 7% to 22%. At the same time, the wealth of the bottom 90% of households went down.
