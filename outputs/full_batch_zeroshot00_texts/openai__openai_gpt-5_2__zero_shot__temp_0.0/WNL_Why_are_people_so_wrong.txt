A new international survey in 33 countries shows that many people are wrong about important facts in society.

In Britain, people think the richest 1% own 59% of the country’s wealth, but they actually own 23%. In the United States, people think 33% of the population are immigrants, but the real number is 14%.

In Brazil, people think the average age is 56, but it is 31. In Russia, people think 31% of politicians are women, but it is 14%.

In Britain, people think 43% of young adults aged 25–34 still live with their parents, but the real number is 14%. In India, people who are online think 60% of the whole country has internet access, but only 19% do.

Why are people so wrong? One reason is that many people find basic maths hard. Some people also misunderstand the questions. For example, people in many countries guess that 37% of people have no religion, but the real average is 18%. This may be because people think about who practises religion, not what people write on census forms.

Another reason is that people use quick guesses. They may think about what is easy to picture, even if it does not fit the question. For example, people often overestimate how many people live in rural areas because rural land takes up a lot of space.

People also think from their own experience. In the survey, many answers came from online users. In developing countries, online users are often richer and more connected than the whole population. Even though people with more education are often more accurate, many still guess based on their own lives and forget that they are not typical.

People can also guess with feelings, not just facts. Sometimes their answers show what they worry about. Worry can make people guess wrong, and wrong guesses can also create worry.

In Britain, this may help explain why people overestimate how much the richest own, how many young people live with parents, and how many immigrants live in the country (people guess 25%, but official estimates say 13%). These are issues people worry about, so they make the numbers seem bigger.

But the survey also shows some problems where people may not worry enough. In many countries, people underestimate how many people are overweight or obese. In Saudi Arabia, people think 28% are, but 71% are. In Britain, people think it is 44%, but the real number is 62%.

The study also shows that countries are very different from each other. The richest 1% in Russia own 70% of the nation’s wealth, but in New Zealand they own 18%. Half of Italians aged 25–34 live with their parents, but in Norway it is only 4%. The average age in India is 27, but in Japan it is 47. Only 10% of politicians are women in Brazil, Hungary, and Japan, but in Sweden it is 44%.

Because the real world is so different and sometimes surprising, it is not strange that many people get these facts wrong.
