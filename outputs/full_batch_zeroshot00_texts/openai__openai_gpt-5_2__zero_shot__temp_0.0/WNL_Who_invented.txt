Sometimes life is not fair. Mark Zuckerberg made Facebook and is now worth about $48bn. James Goodfellow invented the cash machine (ATM), which millions of people use every day. But he did not become rich. He got only £10 from the patent and has earned no more money from it.

Goodfellow says it feels strange when he sees bankers getting £1m bonuses. He wonders what they did to deserve that much money.

He is also upset because people do not see him as a good example for inventors and engineers. His invention helped make billions of pounds, but he got almost nothing. He asks why someone would want to copy his path if they only get £10.

For many years, people argued about who should be called “the inventor of the ATM.” In 2005, a man named John Shepherd-Barron got a UK honour for services to banking as the “inventor of the automatic cash dispenser.” But now the UK government says Goodfellow invented the ATM. So it seems his place in history is now secure.

In the mid-1960s, Goodfellow was a development engineer. He was asked to find a way for customers to take out cash on Saturdays. Many people could not go to the bank during the week. He worked on a machine that could give cash to a customer who was recognized. To make it work, he invented the PIN (personal identification number) and a matching coded token.

The token was a plastic card with holes in it. The patent described a system with a card reader and ten buttons on the outside wall of a bank. A customer would put in the card and press the buttons for their personal number to get banknotes.

Except for the hole-punched cards, this is similar to ATMs today. After Goodfellow showed a model, prototypes were made, and the first machines were installed in 1967.

Around the same time, Shepherd-Barron was making another cash machine. His machine did not use plastic cards. It used cheques with carbon-14, a mildly radioactive material. The machine detected the carbon-14, checked the cheque with a PIN, and then gave out cash.

Many people accept that Shepherd-Barron’s ATM was the first one installed and used by the public. The first one, in north London, opened on 27 June 1967. This was one month before Goodfellow’s ATM was shown to the public. But Goodfellow’s patent was filed on 2 May 1966, which was 14 months before the London machine started.

The rivalry became stronger when Shepherd-Barron got the official honour. Goodfellow says he did not complain until Shepherd-Barron received the OBE in 2005. He says that honour was given because Shepherd-Barron was seen as the inventor, and that upset him.

Shepherd-Barron is no longer alive to reply now, but in a 2005 interview he spoke sharply about Goodfellow. He said banks bought their system because they thought through the whole idea. He called Goodfellow’s invention “an elegant failure,” like the hovercraft.

Cash machines have become very popular around the world. New things like contactless payments, bitcoin, and wearable technology have not stopped their growth. There are now 3m ATMs worldwide, and the number was forecast to reach 4m by 2020.

Goodfellow says he did not invent the idea of a cash machine, but he did invent a way to do it. He compares this to the Wright brothers. They did not invent the idea of flying, but they made it work and got credit. He thinks he should get credit for the cash dispenser.

Now he is starting to get that credit. The website ATMInventor.com says: the idea of an ATM was from Luther George Simjian. The ATM as we know it was James Goodfellow in Scotland, because he had a patent date of 1966. The ATM design people recognize today was John D White for Docutel in the US.

Goodfellow also gets official recognition in a guidebook called Life in the United Kingdom. In a section on “great British inventions of the twentieth century,” it says: “In the 1960s, James Goodfellow (1937-) invented the cash-dispensing automatic teller machine (ATM) or ‘cashpoint’.”

So after many years, Goodfellow is now mentioned with other famous inventors, like John Logie Baird (television), Alan Turing (the Turing machine), Sir Frank Whittle (the jet engine), and Sir Tim Berners-Lee (the World Wide Web).

When asked what he did with the £10 in the 1960s, Goodfellow says he thinks he spent it on a wild night out. He says it did not change his life. But he says he had a good working life and he was happy doing his job.
