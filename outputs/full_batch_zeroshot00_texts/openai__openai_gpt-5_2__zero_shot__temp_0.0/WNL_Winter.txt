Maria sits on a black plastic chair in a community centre on a cold Tuesday afternoon. She is waiting for her number to be called. She is number 34.

When it is her turn, Maria goes forward. She gets a brown paper bag of basic food. It has pasta, eggs, and corn flakes. She can choose butternut squash or carrots for vegetables this week.

Maria is the 34th client today at the East Hampton Food Pantry. This food pantry is only a few streets away from some of the most expensive homes in the world.

By the end of the day, the organizers expect more than 400 families to come. They will pick up their weekly food package to help them during the cold, dark winter on Long Island.

In summer, the Hamptons is a place where billionaires play. The Hamptons is a group of old towns and villages on the ocean, 100 miles from Manhattan. But after Labor Day in early September, rich people close their mansions and go back to places like Manhattan or Beverly Hills. Then life is hard for the mostly immigrant community that lives there all year.

“The people who come here are rich and famous, but we who live here are not,” Maria says. In the summer she works 14-hour days cleaning mansions. In the winter she can go for months with no work.

Maria laughs when asked if she has enough money. “There is no work in the winter, only in the summertime,” she says. Like many workers in the Hamptons, she is from Latin America. “Here, lots of people live in a single room because they can’t pay the rent.”

She says some families with up to five children live in basements. They still pay more than $1,000 a month in rent. “People come here looking for work but, in the winter, there is nothing.”

She says many friends cannot pay for heating or medicine. Many would be hungry without the East Hampton Food Pantry. It is one of several food pantries in the town.

Vicki Littman is the chairperson of the East Hampton Food Pantry. The pantry gave more than 31,000 food parcels in 2015. She says more and more people need help.

“After Labor Day, when the season is over and people’s hours are cut, our numbers go up to about 400 families a week,” she says. “On Tuesday, they get two to three days of food. Without us, they would struggle much more.”

Littman says outsiders may not see the problems in a place famous for luxury. She says the summer visitors are shocked to hear about food pantries. They think of the glamorous Hamptons with parties, beaches, and mansions.

But she says many people work in service jobs, like landscapers, nannies, and waitresses. They depend on summer pay to get through the winter. Visitors do not see that when they come on holiday.

She says some older people must choose between paying for medicine, paying bills, or buying food. She says this should not happen.

Littman says the town has lost many workers in important jobs, like teachers, police officers, and even doctors and dentists. They cannot afford to live there. The food pantry board wants to do more so people can stay.

Housing is the biggest cost in the Hamptons. The nation’s most expensive property costs $147m. It is hedge fund manager Barry Rosenstein’s 18-acre beachfront estate at 67 Further Lane, near Maidstone Golf Club.

Larry Cantwell is East Hampton’s town supervisor and a lifelong resident. He says homes often sell for more than $25m. These high prices also raise the cost of smaller apartments.

“Finding your first home is a challenge in an area like this,” Cantwell says. It is not only poor people. Working-class and middle-class families also struggle. He says if you can buy a home in East Hampton for $500,000, you are very lucky.

Cantwell says more than half the town’s homes are empty most of the year. In winter the population can drop to 10,000. In August it can be 80,000.

“It’s like two cities,” Cantwell says. There is a lot of wealth, but most of it is in second homes used only in summer. Other people live there all year.

He says there are famous rich people, and also hard-working poor people who struggle. He says the community started as a farming and fishing place, a working-class community.

Cantwell says it is almost impossible now for a farmer or fisherman to save enough to buy a first house in East Hampton. He says it is not only the poor. Police officers, teachers, young professionals, and others also struggle to find a place to live. Many cannot afford to own a home.

Being homeless in the Hamptons often means riding a bus. Different religious groups work together so homeless people have a place to sleep in winter. Churches across Long Island take turns. About 50 homeless people are bused between churches every day.

Eddie Vallone is 22 years old. He is one of the people on the bus each night. He says people think the Hamptons is rich and has no problems, but there are many problems, especially drugs.

At Maureen’s Haven, the charity that organizes the shelter program, Vallone says it is hard when summer ends. He worries about what to do in winter. He wants to work, but there is no work.

Vallone cleans pools and does small jobs on luxury estates. He says if he saves carefully and does not buy things without thinking, his summer money can last until November. But work does not start again until May or early June.
