A subway system is like a city’s veins. It carries many tiny living things, especially bacteria. These bacteria can come from food, the human body, and sometimes from germs that can cause sickness. For the first time, scientists started to count and map the bacteria in a city’s subway. They found many interesting results.

Dr Christopher Mason, a scientist at Weill Cornell Medical College, led a team in New York City. For 18 months, they used swabs to collect bacteria from turnstiles, seats, ticket booths, and stations. They found meningitis at Times Square. They also found a very small trace of anthrax on a train handhold, and bacteria that can cause bubonic plague on a rubbish bin and a ticket machine in uptown Manhattan.

The scientists said people should not worry. They said the anthrax trace was extremely small. They also said rats likely carried the plague bacteria. No one has gotten plague in or around New York for years. The study said plague and anthrax are not common, and New York residents are not at risk.

Most of the bacteria they found are not harmful. Some bacteria may even help, like bacteria that break down waste. This is like bacteria inside the human body that help with digestion.

Some findings were expected. Mason said they found some bacteria linked to fecal matter, and this should remind people to wash their hands. They also found bacteria from the same group as helpful bacteria used to make cheese.

The bacteria also seemed to match food habits in different neighbourhoods. Around the subway, they found bacteria linked to cheeses like brie, cheddar, parmesan, and mozzarella from New York pizza. Swiss cheese bacteria were more common in midtown Manhattan and the financial district. Bacteria used to ferment cabbage for kimchi and sauerkraut showed up in the financial district and Bay Ridge. The computer also found cucumber DNA all over the city.

Bacteria linked to illness were common too. They found bacteria that can cause diarrhoea and nausea. They found E. coli (mostly harmless types). They found bacteria that can cause skin infections and urinary-tract infections all over the city. The bacteria that produces tetanus appeared in Soho. Bacteria that cause dysentery appeared at one station in the Bronx and another in Harlem.

The team collected more than 1,000 samples from all 466 open subway stations. They used a DNA sequencer and a supercomputer with genetic databases. They identified 15,152 different species, and almost half were bacteria.

The researchers wrote that these possible infectious agents do not seem to be spreading disease in New York. Instead, they seem to be normal organisms living with people, and may even be important. They said this is a normal, healthy genetic picture of a city.

The researchers said the subway and the city are about as safe as people thought.

Mason said people should not worry about getting urinary-tract infections from subway seats. He said people should wash their hands, get sleep, eat salads, and go to the gym, just like before. He also said he feels more confident riding the subway now.

Other results also made sense. Busy stations like Grand Central and Times Square had more bacteria and more kinds of bacteria. The subway had many bacteria linked to human skin. The Bronx had the most variety of bacteria. Staten Island, with only three stops, had the least.

The researchers found marine bacteria at South Ferry, which flooded during Hurricane Sandy. They were surprised that some of the species are usually linked to Antarctica and fish.

Next, Mason said they will study other cities. Studies have begun in Paris, Sao Paulo, and Shanghai. They will also keep studying New York to see how the microbiome changes with the seasons. He hopes this research will give a baseline for health officials and geneticists, and help health officials be more ready to prevent and track diseases and pathogens.
