David Mitchell is a British writer. He is often in the running for the Man Booker Prize. Critics often study his novels closely. Mitchell says it is a relief that his newest work will not be seen by anyone until the year 2114.

Mitchell is the second writer to join the Future Library project, made by Scottish artist Katie Paterson. In 2014, 1,000 trees were planted in the Nordmarka forest near Oslo, Norway. The first writer was Margaret Atwood. In 2015 she gave a manuscript called Scribbler Moon. Each year for 100 years, one writer will give a piece of writing. No one will read these texts until 2114. In 2114 the trees will be cut down to make paper, and the 100 texts will be printed.

Each year the next writer’s name will be announced. A group of experts and Paterson choose the writers while she is alive. The writers travel to a place in the forest above Oslo and hand over their manuscripts in a short ceremony.

Mitchell said the project gives him hope during a time of very sad news. He said it suggests civilization might still be here in 100 years. He said many things make people feel doomed, but the Future Library shows a possible future. It brings hope that humans are stronger than we think, and that there will still be trees, books, readers, and civilization.

Mitchell said writing the book felt freeing because he will not be alive to see what people think of it. But he said he is between Margaret Atwood and other great writers, so his work should be good. He said he would look like a historic fool if people opened it in 2114 and it was not good.

Mitchell was shortlisted for the Man Booker Prize for his novels number9dream and Cloud Atlas. He said he usually polishes his writing again and again, and even too much. But this time was different. He wrote until the last minute. The first two-thirds were polished, but he did not have time to polish the final third. He said that was also freeing.

Paterson asked the writers to write about imagination and time, in any way they wanted. At the ceremony in the Norwegian woods, Mitchell shared only the title of his manuscript: From Me Flows What You Call Time. The title comes from a piece of music by Japanese composer Toru Takemitsu. Mitchell would not say more, except that the work was more substantial than he expected.

In the forest, Mitchell gave his text while it rained on and off and he used an umbrella. Around him were small pine trees growing. He read the audience, including children and adults, a short story and the poem A slumber did my spirit seal by William Wordsworth. The poem ends with the lines “Rolled round in earth’s diurnal course / With rocks, and stones, and trees”. This felt right in that small part of the forest, with blueberry bushes on the ground. The forest will be cared for during the next 98 years, before it is used to make the Future Library manuscripts.

In a piece he wrote for the Future Library, Mitchell said it seems foolish to think his writing will matter to future generations. But he also said it is quiet and modest to work so hard on a manuscript when nobody will ever praise you and say, “Nice one,” or “I loved the bit where she did that and he did this”.

Mitchell has now delivered his manuscript. It will be sealed and stored with Atwood’s in a wood-lined room in Oslo’s new public library, which will open in 2019. A trust of experts will watch over it until it is printed. Mitchell said it is now as far from him as “a coin dropped in a river”.
