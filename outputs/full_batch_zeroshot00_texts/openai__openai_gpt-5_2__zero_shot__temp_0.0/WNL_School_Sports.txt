Do you want your child to do well in sport, make the school team, and maybe compete in big events one day? A study says children born in November, or in October, may have an advantage. It found that pupils born in these months are fitter than others in their class.

The study was led by Dr Gavin Sandercock from the Centre for Sports and Exercise Science at Essex University. He and his team found that children born in October and November were fitter, stronger, and more powerful than children born in the other months, especially April or June.

The research tested 8,550 boys and girls aged 10 to 16 from 26 state schools in Essex. The tests happened between 2007 and 2010. The children did three fitness tests: stamina, handgrip strength, and lower-body power. The study said the month you are born can make important differences in heart and lung fitness, muscle strength, and how quickly you can speed up. These skills matter in many sports.

Children born in November were the fittest overall. They had the most stamina and power, and they were the second strongest. Children born in October were almost as fit. They were the strongest and came third for power. Children born in December were close behind.

Sometimes the differences were large. Dr Sandercock said a boy born in November can run at least 10% faster, jump 12% higher, and be 15% more powerful than a boy of the same age born in April. The study said small differences like this can affect who gets chosen for top sports teams.

When the three test scores were added together, children born in April were the least fit, then children born in June. Dr Sandercock said this could mean these children are not picked for school teams and may do less well in sport.

The study suggests children born early in the school year may have an “autumn advantage.” They were already known to do better in school work, and now they also seem to do better in sport. Dr Sandercock said it is not only because these children are older in the school year, because the fittest children were not the tallest or the heaviest.

The authors think one possible reason is vitamin D from sunlight. They believe babies whose mothers were pregnant during summer may get more vitamin D near the end of pregnancy.

John Steele, chief executive of the Youth Sport Trust, said a good first experience of PE and sport at school can strongly affect how well a child develops in sport. He said it can help children build agility, balance, and coordination, and enjoy being active.

UK Sport could not say if many of the 1,300 athletes across 47 sports it funds were born in October and November. Natalie Dunman, head of performance pathways, said the differences seen in teenagers in junior competitions were gone in adult competitions. She said many things help make a champion, and their work has not shown that birth month is a key reason.
