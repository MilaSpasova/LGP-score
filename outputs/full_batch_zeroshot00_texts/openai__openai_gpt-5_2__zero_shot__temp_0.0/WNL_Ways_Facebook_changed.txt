On one day in August, 1 in 7 people on Earth used Facebook. That is about 1 billion people, said Facebook founder Mark Zuckerberg. In 10 years, Facebook has changed how people have relationships, how they think about privacy, how they do business, and how they get news. It has helped bring down some governments. It has even changed the meanings of common words.

“A more open and connected world is a better world,” Zuckerberg wrote when he shared the numbers. He said it can bring stronger relationships, a stronger economy with more chances, and a stronger society.

Here are some ways Facebook has changed things, for better or worse.

1 Facebook changed what “friend” means  
Now “to friend” is a verb. And it is easy to “unfriend” someone on Facebook.

Facebook also changed the feeling of words like “share” and “like.”

Reunions at school and university can feel less important now. You often already know what people are doing. You may know who broke up, and you have seen many photos of their babies. You may also already know if an ex has a new boyfriend or girlfriend because of selfies.

But Facebook does not show different levels of friendship. A person you knew long ago and your closest friend can both be “Facebook friends” in the same way.

Professor Robin Dunbar says people can only have about 150 people in their social group. He believes Facebook has not changed that. But he worries that if it is so easy to end friendships on Facebook, people may stop learning how to get along.

2 We care less about privacy  
There is a saying: if you are not paying for it, you are the product. Facebook is like that. It makes money from user information, and many users give that information willingly.

Pew Research Center found that many young people share personal details. 91% post a photo of themselves. 71% post the city or town where they live, up from 61%. More than half give email addresses. One fifth give their phone number.

Because so much life is shared online, Facebook lets people create an image of themselves and build fans. Researchers wrote about a new idea called the “Facebook self.”

More than 80% list their interests, which helps brands target them. Many younger users do limit who can see their profiles, with 60% allowing friends only.

3 Facebook created millions of jobs, but not in its own offices  
Facebook helped create a new kind of work. Many people now have jobs helping brands use Facebook.

“It is a tool like no other,” said Michael Tinmouth, a social media strategist who has worked with brands like Vodafone and Microsoft. He said marketers can learn a lot about customers through data and analytics, such as who the customers are, who their friends are, and how they interact with a brand.

Advertisers pay a lot for this. Facebook said its ad revenue rose 46% to $3.32bn.

But Facebook can also be risky for brands. Complaints used to happen on the phone or in small online forums. Now angry customers can post complaints where hundreds of friends can see, or on the brand’s own page. A story about unfair treatment can spread very fast.

4 Facebook helped organize revolutions  
The Arab Spring was sometimes called the Twitter revolution, but Facebook also changed how protests are organized. Researcher Olga Onuch from Manchester University found that Facebook was key for reaching half of the Euromaidan protesters in Ukraine.

Onuch found that Facebook posts helped start the Maidan protests after it was announced that Ukraine would not sign a free trade and association agreement with the EU. The posts helped organize real actions, not just online anger. Activist Mustafa Nayyem posted: “If you really want to do something, don’t just ‘like’ this post. Let’s meet near the monument to independence in the middle of the Maidan.”

Many people in the research said they depended on Facebook to learn what was really happening, because they did not trust traditional media.

5 Facebook makes news, breaks news, and decides what news people see  
Pew Research Center says about 71% of people aged 18 to 24 say the internet is their main news source. It also says 63% of users overall say the same. About one third of Facebook users post about politics and government.

Many people first see news through Facebook or other social media, often on mobile phones.

People may not need to leave Facebook to read news. Instant Articles lets stories appear inside Facebook. News companies can sell ads around their articles and keep 100% of that money. Facebook can also sell ads around the article, and 70% of Facebook’s ad money from that goes to the news companies.

Facebook has also changed how journalists work. Many reporters use it to find, contact, and learn about ordinary people who may suddenly become part of a big news story. Facebook also has its own newswire, sharing useful reactions from users, including pictures and videos.

6 Users are changing Facebook  
Facebook started as a site for students, and at first only elite US universities could use it.

In 2014, 10 years after it started, 56% of internet users aged 65 and older had a Facebook account. And 39% were connected to people they had never met in person.

Groups have been replaced by pages. Writing on each other’s walls is now old-fashioned. Carefully made photo albums have been replaced by quick mobile uploads. More than ever, Facebook is a gateway not only to friends but also to the rest of the internet.

David Kirkpatrick, author of The Facebook Effect, said people should get used to it. He told BBC Radio 4’s Today programme that Facebook might go away one day, but something this big takes a long time to disappear. He said Facebook has shown it can change, and it will continue to be a very major player.
