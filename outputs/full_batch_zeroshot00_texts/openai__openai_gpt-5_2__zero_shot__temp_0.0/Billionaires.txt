Oxfam says the very large fortunes of the world’s richest 100 billionaires are increasing inequality and making it harder to fight poverty.

Oxfam says wealth and income are growing for a few people on a scale never seen before. This often happens while poor people lose secure jobs and fair wages. This makes it harder for people who live on aid or low pay to improve their lives and escape poverty.

Oxfam says the world’s poorest people could be lifted out of poverty several times if the richest 100 billionaires gave away the money they made in 2012. The charity says the $240bn net income these 100 billionaires made in 2012 could end extreme poverty four times over.

Charities rarely criticize rich people, because rich people often give money to charities. Bill Gates and Warren Buffett are among 40 US billionaires who promised to give much of their wealth to aid projects. But there is little information about how much they donate each year. Oxfam says Russian, Middle Eastern, and Chinese billionaires have not done the same.

In a report called The Cost of Inequality: How Wealth and Income Extremes Hurt Us All, released just before the World Economic Forum in Davos, Oxfam asks world leaders to limit extreme incomes and reduce inequality to at least the levels of 1990.

The report says the richest 1% increased their incomes by 60% in the past 20 years. It says the financial crisis made this happen faster, not slower.

Barbara Stocking, Oxfam’s Chief Executive, says extreme wealth is bad for the economy, politics, society, and the environment. She says studies show countries have lower investment and growth when workers get a smaller share of total income. She says we can no longer believe that wealth for a few will always help everyone else, because often the opposite happens.

The report says inequality is a problem all over the world. In the UK, inequality is quickly returning to levels not seen since the time of Charles Dickens. In China, the top 10% now get nearly 60% of the income. China’s inequality is now similar to South Africa’s. South Africa is the most unequal country on Earth and is much more unequal than at the end of apartheid.

In the US, the share of national income going to the top 1% has doubled since 1980, from 10% to 20%. For the top 0.01%, their share is higher than it was in the 1920s.

The World Bank and the International Monetary Fund say extreme income inequality slows growth. They have tried to connect their loans to programmes that limit the growth of inequality.

Oxfam says the richest 1% may use up to 10,000 times more carbon than the average US citizen.

Oxfam says world leaders should learn from countries like Brazil, which has grown quickly while reducing inequality.

Stocking says the world needs a “global new deal” to reverse many years of rising inequality. She says as a first step, world leaders should formally promise to reduce inequality to the levels seen in 1990.

She also says tax havens should be closed. The Tax Justice Network says tax havens hold as much as $31 trillion, which may be as much as a third of all global wealth. She says closing them could bring in $189bn more in tax money.
