A new World Health Organization (WHO) study says that more than one third of women worldwide (35.6%) will face physical or sexual violence during their lives. It is usually done by a male partner. The report says 30% of women are attacked by a partner. It also says that 38% of murders of women are done by an intimate partner.

Dr Margaret Chan, director general of the WHO, said this shows that violence against women is a global health problem like an epidemic. She said health systems can and must do more to help women who experience violence.

The highest levels of violence against women are in Africa. There, nearly half of women (45.6%) will face physical or sexual violence. In low- and middle-income Europe, the number is 27.2%. In high-income countries, about one third of women (32.7%) will experience violence at some time in their lives.

Among women who suffer violence, 42% get injuries. This may bring them to doctors and nurses. The report says this is often the first time the violence at home is noticed and the woman can be offered help. Violence can badly harm women’s health. Some women come to hospital with broken bones. Others have problems during pregnancy and mental illness.

The WHO made two reports: one about how common violence is, and another with guidelines for healthcare staff on how to help women. The work was done by Dr Claudia Garcia-Moreno at WHO and Professor Charlotte Watts from the London School of Hygiene and Tropical Medicine.

Garcia-Moreno said that, for the first time, they compared data from all over the world about partner violence and sexual violence by non-partners, and how this violence affects health. The health effects include HIV and other sexually transmitted infections, depression, women turning to alcohol, unwanted pregnancies, and low-birthweight babies.

Rates of violence were different in different regions, but Garcia-Moreno said the level was unacceptably high everywhere. Data from 81 countries shows that even in high-income countries, 23.2% of women will face physical and/or sexual violence from a partner in their lives. The global figure for women attacked by partners was 30%.

The report says that in high-income countries, more sexual assaults and rapes by acquaintances or strangers are reported than in other places. It says 12.6% of women in wealthy countries will be sexually attacked by a non-partner in their lives. This is higher than the African rate of 11.9%. But the report also says that data on these crimes is not collected well in all regions.

The authors say earlier research shows that women with more education are less likely to suffer violence. Women who have jobs are also less likely to suffer violence, but not in all regions. Watts said societies need to change social norms. She asked what society thinks is acceptable, because in some societies, some kinds of violence against women are seen as OK.

Garcia-Moreno said the numbers should be a wake-up call. She said people have paid more attention to this problem in the past decade, but it is complex. She said there is no vaccine or pill to fix it.

The new WHO clinical and policy guidelines say healthcare staff should be trained to recognize signs of domestic violence and sexual assault. But the guidelines do not support general screening, which means asking every woman at a clinic if she has faced violence.

Garcia-Moreno said that if a woman comes back many times with injuries that are not explained, healthcare staff should ask about domestic violence. She said that when she was in medical school, this was not something students learned. Later, she sometimes felt something else was going on with a woman she was talking to, but did not think about domestic violence. Now she says she would handle the interview very differently.
