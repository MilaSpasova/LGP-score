Race engineer
A race engineer is the link between the driver and the mechanics.

Typical salary: New graduates start at £25,000 to £30,000. After a few years, junior engineers can earn more than £40,000. Senior race engineers earn £50,000 to £90,000. Promotion often leads to six-figure salaries.

What the job involves: Race engineer Jamie Muir says, “A race engineer acts as the interpreter between the race-car mechanics and the driver.” The engineer listens to the driver, studies the data, decides how to set up the car for the best performance, and then tells the mechanics what to do.

Qualifications: A university degree, usually in automotive engineering, mechanical engineering, or motorsport technology. Hands-on experience is very important.

To succeed as a race engineer, you need to handle pressure.

Worst thing about the job: The long hours. Chris Aylett, CEO of the Motorsport Industry Association, says, “Race engineers work 24/7.”

Ethical hacker
Typical salary: £60,000 to £90,000 at team-leader level. A newly qualified hacker can expect £35,000 to £50,000.

What the job involves: A company pays an ethical hacker to try to hack into its computer system. This tests how well the system can stop a real attack.

Qualifications: You do not always need a computer science degree. The industry accepts people with many different qualifications and skills.

To succeed as an ethical hacker, you need to love technology and details. You also need strong analytical skills and enjoy solving difficult problems.

Worst thing about the job: You may be asked to test a new customer’s network and find out they have already had a data breach.

Bomb-disposal diver
Typical salary: In the private sector, you can earn up to £100,000 by working two months out of every three.

What the job involves: You go down to the sea bed and search for unexploded ordnance, such as bombs, shells, grenades, and landmines. Then you either safely recover them or safely destroy them.

Qualifications: To dive offshore, you need diving-inspection and medical-technician qualifications. To dispose of bombs safely, you also need an explosive ordnance disposal qualification and years of experience.

To succeed as a bomb-disposal diver, you need to stay calm in stressful situations. You work alone deep underwater, with no visibility. You also need to be okay living in small, crowded spaces.

Worst thing about the job: You should expect to be away from home at least six months a year.

Social engineer
Typical salary: Between £50,000 and £80,000 on average. Graduates start on £25,000, but pay rises quickly with qualifications and experience.

The job: A company pays a social engineer to try to trick employees into giving confidential information. This can let the engineer access sensitive company data or the company’s computer network.

Qualifications: Social engineers often have a degree in IT. Knowledge of psychology is helpful. Backgrounds in marketing, teaching, and customer service can also help.

To succeed as a social engineer, you need the confidence to lie in a believable way and to fit in almost anywhere. You also need strong personal ethics and knowledge of the law.

Worst thing about the job: People may misunderstand your job. Social engineers are not spies, but many people think they are.

Power-line helicopter pilot
Typical salary: £65,000

The job: You fly a helicopter close to high-voltage power lines so the lines can be checked with a camera. The power company uses the pictures to find possible faults and problems.

Qualifications: You need a private helicopter pilot licence, a commercial pilot’s licence, and about 2,000 hours of low-level flying experience in the type of helicopter the company uses.

To succeed as a power-line helicopter pilot, you need a steady hand and a calm mind. Pilots may fly beside the power line, sometimes only 20 feet away and 30 feet above the ground.

Worst thing about the job: Robin Tutcher, chief helicopter pilot for Western Power Distribution overhead-power network, says, “There are no negatives.”

Private butler
Typical salary: £60,000 to £90,000

The job: A private butler does many tasks for an employer. This can include managing clothes, driving, and caring for pets. Other duties can include managing staff, serving meals, running errands, looking after guests, booking restaurants, keeping the house secure, cleaning, cooking, and anything else the household needs.

Qualifications: You do not need specific qualifications. You can take a course at the British Butler Academy or the British Butler Institute.

To succeed as a butler, you need to truly enjoy looking after other people.

Worst thing about the job: Long hours and an unpredictable schedule can make family life hard. Butlers can also feel isolated, face cultural differences with the employer, and have to work for people who are not always nice, says Sara Vestin, director of the British Butler Academy.
