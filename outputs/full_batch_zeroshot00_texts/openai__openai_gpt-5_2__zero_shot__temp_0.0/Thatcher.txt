Margaret Thatcher has died. She was one of the strongest British prime ministers since Winston Churchill in 1940. She supported free market economics. The British government said she would have a ceremonial funeral with military honours at St Paul’s Cathedral.

Prime minister David Cameron said he was very sad. He said Britain had lost a great leader and that she was the first woman prime minister. He said she led the country and that many people would remember her as a great peacetime prime minister.

President Barack Obama said many Americans would remember her working closely with President Ronald Reagan. He said she showed that people can shape history with strong beliefs and courage.

Lady Thatcher was the first woman elected to lead a major western state. She served 11 years in a row at No 10 Downing Street, the longest time since 1827.

Thatcher was 87. For some years she had poor health and dementia. After several small strokes in 2002, she stopped going to public events.

Many people from different political parties paid tribute to her.

The Labour Party said it would stop campaigning in local elections for a short time as a sign of respect. Labour leader Ed Miliband said she was a special person who changed politics for a whole generation. He said Labour disagreed with many of her actions and she was controversial, but they could still respect her achievements and personal strength.

Deputy prime minister Nick Clegg said she was one of the most important people in modern British politics. He said she made a lasting mark on the country.

Former Conservative prime minister Sir John Major said she showed courage and determination in politics, and kindness in private life.

Thatcher was called the “Iron Lady.” She was a key ally of US president Ronald Reagan during the cold war. The Soviet Union later broke up during changes led by Mikhail Gorbachev. Thatcher said she could “do business” with Gorbachev. Many people in former Soviet bloc countries saw her as a strong supporter of freedom. Many people in the US also shared this view, but not everyone in Britain or among key EU partners agreed.

Thatcher was a middle-level Conservative politician until she became party leader in 1975. Within 10 years, people around the world knew her. Some admired her and some hated her. She supported market reforms at home and took a hard line in foreign policy. She also fought the IRA. The IRA almost killed her in 1984 with a bomb at the Grand Hotel in Brighton.

In Britain, “Thatcherism” meant selling state-owned industries to private owners. The word “privatization” became widely used in many countries. It also meant defeating powerful trade unions, especially the National Union of Miners. The miners’ strike in 1984–85 lasted a year and was very bitter.

Money from Britain’s North Sea oil fields gave Thatcher more freedom to change the economy. She used this chance to fight her opponents, including some moderate people in her own party.

She also made strong verbal attacks in the European Union to get a British rebate, which she called “my money.” She was less successful in stopping the European Commission from gaining more power. She called it the “Belgian Empire.” This was especially true when the Commission was led by Jacques Delors.

Some tabloid newspapers supported her strongly. Later, the British economy improved after a severe recession. Her strict economic policy was meant to control unions and reduce inflation. For a short time, she seemed impossible to beat.

But when she had more power and fewer allies to restrain her, she made mistakes and became less popular. Sir Geoffrey Howe, her deputy in name, resigned after a major disagreement, mainly about Europe. His resignation speech led to a challenge for her leadership. Thatcher stepped aside for John Major instead of risking losing in a vote.

After she retired, she wrote very successful memoirs in two volumes. She also worked hard for the Thatcher Foundation, which tried to spread her values around the world.
