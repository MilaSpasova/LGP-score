Flappy Bird
Be careful what you wish for, especially when you make something new. Dong Nguyen made the mobile game Flappy Bird. He later removed it from app stores. He said the game’s success had ruined his simple life. It had been downloaded more than 50 million times. It was making him about £30,000 a day from ads. He wrote on Twitter, “I cannot take this anymore.”

Regretting a game is not the same as regretting a rifle. But Nguyen is one of many inventors who wish they had not made something that caused problems.

The labradoodle
The labradoodle is cute. But the problem is how crossbreed dogs have been bred and sold since Wally Conron created the labradoodle in the 1980s. He told the Associated Press, “I’ve done a lot of damage. I’ve created a lot of problems. There are a lot of unhealthy and abandoned dogs out there.” Conron made the labradoodle while working for the Royal Guide Dog Association of Australia. He wanted a dog for a blind woman. Her husband was allergic to dog hair. Conron did not expect the labradoodle, and other poodle-cross dogs, to become so popular. Many of these dogs have health problems.

The AK-47
Six months before he died in December 2013, Mikhail Kalashnikov wrote to the head of the Russian Orthodox Church. Kalashnikov designed the AK-47 assault rifle. He wrote that his suffering was very strong. He asked if he was responsible for deaths caused by his rifle, even if the people killed were enemies.

Electronic tagging
Electronic tags were first planned in the 1960s. They were meant to track former prisoners at school and work. They were also meant to reward good behaviour. The inventors were Bob Gable and his brother Kirkland. Later, they were shocked that tags became a way to control and punish people. In 2010, Kirkland Gable told the Guardian, “It’s not pleasant, but I’m not in control of the universe. I have to realize there are some things out of my control.”

Pepper spray
In 2011, police used pepper spray on peaceful protesters at a University of California campus. After this, one of the scientists who helped develop pepper spray in the 1980s spoke against how it was used. Kamran Loghman told The New York Times, “I have never seen such an inappropriate and improper use of chemical agents.”

The office cubicle
In the late 1960s, a new kind of office was created. It was meant to give workers privacy and help them work better by giving them more space. Instead, companies used cubicles to squeeze workers into smaller spaces. Cubicles became a sign of all workers being the same and work feeling empty. In 1997, the inventor Bob Propst said, “the cubiclizing of people in modern corporations is monolithic insanity.”
