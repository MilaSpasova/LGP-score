Researchers were shocked by how far people would go to avoid a simple task. The task was to sit in a chair and do nothing but think.

Some people found this so boring that they chose to give themselves mild electric shocks just to stop the boredom.

In one test, men and women sat alone for 15 minutes. Two-thirds of the men pressed a button to give themselves a painful shock. One-quarter of the women did the same. Scientists think this may be because men often look for stronger feelings than women.

Psychologists from Virginia and Harvard Universities did more than 11 studies. They found that people did not like being left alone with their thoughts. This was true no matter their age, education, income, or how much they used smartphones or social media.

Timothy Wilson, who led the research, said the results may not be because modern life is so busy. Instead, people may like phones and social media because they feel a strong need to do something rather than do nothing.

In the first experiments, students were led into a plain room alone. They had no phones, no books, and nothing to write with. They had to stay seated and not fall asleep. They were told they would be alone for six to 15 minutes.

After the time ended, the students said they did not enjoy it. They had trouble focusing. Their minds wandered even though nothing was distracting them. Even when they were given time to plan what to think about, it did not help.

The researchers then tried the experiment at people’s homes, in case the room was the problem. The results were similar, but people felt even more miserable. Some cheated by standing up or checking their phones.

To see if this was only a student problem, the scientists recruited more than 100 people, ages 18 to 77, from a church and a farmers’ market. They also disliked being alone with their thoughts.

Then came the most surprising result. The researchers wanted to see if people would choose something bad instead of doing nothing. They gave students the choice to give themselves a mild electric shock.

Earlier, students had rated how unpleasant the shocks were. They also rated other unpleasant things, like looking at pictures of cockroaches or hearing the sound of a knife rubbing against a bottle.

After trying a shock, every student said they would pay money to avoid mild electric shocks.

But in the test, 12 of 18 men gave themselves up to four shocks. Six of 24 women did too.

The scientists wrote in the journal Science that being alone with their thoughts seemed so unpleasant that it made many people shock themselves, even though they had said they would pay to avoid it.

Jessica Andrews-Hanna at the University of Colorado said some students might shock themselves to make a boring lecture feel better. But she said we need to know more about why people pressed the button in this study.

She asked us to imagine the setup: a person sits in a chair with wires on their skin and a button that gives a harmless but uncomfortable shock. They are told to sit and entertain themselves with their thoughts.

As time passes, she said, the person’s mind may wander to the shock and wonder: was it really that bad? She also suggested the person may be curious about what the researchers really want to learn. She said it might be a case where curiosity caused the problem.
