When the children at a primary school in Stirling hear the words “daily mile,” they put down their pencils and go outside to run laps around the school field. For three-and-a-half years, all pupils at St Ninian’s Primary have walked or run one mile each day. They do it at different times during the day. They seem happy to do it. Even though childhood obesity is rising across the UK, none of the children at this school are overweight.

The daily mile has helped the children’s fitness, behaviour, and concentration in class. Because of this, many nursery and primary schools across Britain are now doing it too. Pupils leave their desks for about 15 minutes to walk or run around the school or a local park.

Elaine Wyllie, the headteacher of St Ninian’s, said she gets at least two emails a day from other schools and local authorities asking how they do it. She said it is amazing to think children across the country might run every day because of what her school started.

Figures from the Health & Social Care Information Centre say that one in ten children are obese when they start school at age four or five. In summer 2015, a study found that schoolchildren in England are the least fit they have ever been. Many primary schools have noticed the benefits of the daily mile. Schools in London, Gateshead, Wales, and other parts of Scotland have started it. Other schools planned to start during the 2015-6 academic year. In Stirling, 30 schools have started or plan to start the daily mile.

Wyllie said it is a common-sense way to improve children’s fitness, and it is free and easy. She said the most important thing is that children enjoy it, or they would not keep doing it. She said they come back looking healthy and full of energy. At St Ninian’s, teachers take pupils out of lessons and onto a special path around the playing field when it fits the day’s timetable best. Only ice or very heavy rain stops them.

Researchers from Stirling University have started a study to find clear evidence of the physical, thinking, and emotional benefits of the daily mile. Dr Colin Moran, who leads the study, said the children at St Ninian’s do not seem to have obesity problems, they seem happier, and staff say they settle into lessons faster. He said there is a lot of personal stories about the benefits, but no scientific facts yet. The pupils at St Ninian’s will be compared with children from another school in Stirling that has not started the scheme yet.

Kevin Clelland, a primary school teacher from Leeds, visited St Ninian’s and then told his colleagues it was a great idea. He said it is simple but seems to have a big effect. He said his school wants to improve pupils’ fitness beyond the two hours of PE they are expected to provide. His school is now building a track.

Active Cheshire, a group for sports and fitness in Cheshire and Warrington, is taking senior people from the local authority to Scotland to look at the results of the daily mile. They hope to bring it to all 450 schools in the region if a trial goes well.

Paralympian Tanni Grey-Thompson, chair of ukactive, said children need 60 minutes of activity every day. This can be during lessons, walking to school, or playing. She said it is good to see the daily mile growing. She said it shows strong leadership from education to improve children’s fitness and behaviour, and to help schools, teachers, parents, and young people. She said sitting still is harmful, and moving helps children learn skills they will use for life.

The Scottish government also supports it. A spokesperson said PE learning is improved by projects like the daily mile. They said it can help parents teach healthy habits to children from a young age. They said they are pleased that many Scottish schools are taking part or planning to do so.
