Takahito Iguchi says the problem with Google Glass is that it is not cool. Many people online share photos of people wearing it and looking silly or smug. Google first gave 10,000 pairs to software developers. Some people think it would have looked better on supermodels or Scarlett Johansson. If you search online, you can even find a photo of a large naked man wearing it in the shower. Iguchi, a Japanese business owner, hopes this hurts Google. He is making a competitor that looks more stylish, like something from a science-fiction movie.

Iguchi’s product is called Telepathy One. It is not really a pair of glasses. It is one piece of metal with a camera and a tiny projector. He showed it at the South by Southwest festival in Austin, Texas. After that, it got $5 million in venture capital. Like Google Glass, Telepathy One is planned to launch in 2014.

Iguchi says Telepathy One is a simpler version of Google Glass. He calls Glass “an egotistical device” because it can do many things, like use the internet, read emails, take photos, and use apps. He says Telepathy One will be more for communication. It connects to your phone by Bluetooth. It will focus on sharing what you see and hear right away. You can post photos and videos from your view on Facebook or send them by email. You can also see and talk to a floating video image of a friend.

Iguchi says it will help people feel closer to friends and family. He has worked in Japanese technology for 20 years. His most recent work was a location-based phone app called Sekai Camera.

Some people may not want to get closer to a person wearing a futuristic headset. Iguchi says he is a visionary. He says when he goes to London, he is a stranger and sometimes feels afraid. He believes everyone wants to understand others and be understood. He says this device can help you learn information about people before you even speak to them.

Compared to Google, Telepathy is very small. But Iguchi is not worried. In his shared office in San Francisco, a converted warehouse full of young startup workers, he quotes Sun Tzu’s The Art of War. He says even small armies can sometimes beat big ones. When he was young, Japanese technology was very strong, like how the Sony Walkman was very popular. Now, to compete, he has left Tokyo for Silicon Valley.

Iguchi says Tokyo has a lot of fashion and culture, but it is still like an island and feels isolated. He says Silicon Valley has people from everywhere and helps you connect with the world. The hardware will be made in Japan. He is building a team of software engineers in the US to make the apps. When the writer meets him, a Japanese news crew is following him. They are interested in Japanese business owners who feel they must leave Japan.

The reporter, Takashi Yanagisawa, says Japan is losing confidence and needs a way to get power back. He hopes Iguchi could be part of a new answer. Iguchi says building the Telepathy One prototype was easy because Tokyo has many kinds of technology. The hard part is showing it to the world. He says big manufacturers want to work with him because they have good technology but find it hard to sell. He says a product needs a strong story. He gives Apple as an example, like “1,000 songs in your pocket” for the iPod. He also mentions the story of David and Goliath, and says Steve Jobs was inspired by Akio Morita of Sony, and Morita inspired him too.

Iguchi may have strong confidence, like Jobs. But because of his thick Japanese accent, he sometimes has trouble being understood. This may have helped give him the idea for Telepathy One. He tells a story about London, where he stayed in an Airbnb. He talked with the house owner for three hours, and then they became friends. He says that is how long it can take to understand each other and share feelings, background, and career. He hopes Telepathy can make that faster. He says if you get information from the cloud and social networks, it will be easier. He says the house owner works on getting investment from the UK to Africa. The man was excited about Telepathy and thought it could help educate people about Africa by showing other people’s point of view.

This is Iguchi’s biggest hope: that seeing what someone else sees will help you understand how they think and feel. As a student, he studied philosophy in the day and taught himself coding at night. He says one day he opened his apartment door and realized that everything is code. He says everything can be shared between humans, and everything can be encoded and decoded. He believes that if people can exchange code, it will end all war against each other.
