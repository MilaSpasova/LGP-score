A big report warns that up to 1 billion people could still live in extreme poverty in 2030. This may happen unless countries work on inequality and on social, economic, and cultural problems that stop people from escaping poverty or push them back into it.

The report is by the Chronic Poverty Advisory Network (CPAN). It says some people can rise above the poverty line of $1.25 a day, but then fall back into poverty. This can happen when they face shocks like drought, illness, or insecurity or conflict.

Using household surveys, the report found that in parts of rural Kenya and in South Africa, 30% to 40% of people who escaped poverty later fell back. In some areas of Ethiopia, the number was 60% between 1999 and 2009. Even in countries doing well, like Indonesia and Vietnam, about 20% fell back.

Some stories show how easily this can happen. Amin, 61, from rural Bangladesh, has seen his work and income slowly get worse. This happened because he and his wife were ill, his son’s marriage cost money, his father died, and he lost goods like fishing nets. Lovemore, 74, from Zimbabwe, is now one of the poorest people in his village. He lost his job as a car-park attendant because of poor health. He also had to take in his five grandchildren after his daughters died.

Andrew Shepherd, a research fellow at the Overseas Development Institute and lead author of the report, said governments must help people stay above the poverty line permanently. He said too many families make progress and then lose it. He said governments should not think the job is done just because someone’s income reaches $1.25.

A UN high-level panel said ending extreme poverty by 2030 is possible. But this report says that doing “more of the same” will not bring poverty down to zero.

Extreme poverty fell from 1.9 billion people in 1990 to 1.2 billion in 2010. But the report says the next 15 years will be harder. Big progress like China’s may not happen in many other places. Climate-related shocks and very deep poverty in parts of sub-Saharan Africa may slow progress.

The report says we should focus more on the chronically poor. These are people who are poor for many years or for their whole lives. The report also says it is important to stop impoverishment, which means stopping people from falling into poverty. Shepherd said governments often help people just cross the poverty line because that is easier, but they avoid the harder work of ending chronic poverty.

The report says past progress has helped people who were already close to the poverty line more than it has helped the chronically poor. It says poverty will not reach zero unless policies put the chronically poor first.

The report gives three connected goals: tackle chronic poverty, stop impoverishment, and help people stay out of poverty. It also suggests three main policies, which need very large global investment.

First, it calls for social assistance, or a safety net, to help the poorest people reach a decent standard of living. One example is Ethiopia’s productive safety net programme. It helped many vulnerable households survive drought without cutting spending on education.

Second, it calls for education from early childhood until people start work. Education can help people escape poverty and stay out of it. The report says education is also a “portable” asset, because people can carry what they learn anywhere.

Third, it calls for economic growth policies that help the very poorest people the most, so they get a share of the country’s increasing prosperity.

Shepherd said economic growth is important, but it must create decent jobs, not only low-paying and insecure jobs. He gave India’s rural employment guarantees scheme as an example, saying it has raised wages in urban areas as well as rural areas. The report also points to South Africa extending the minimum wage to groups like domestic workers.

The report says all of this costs money. It says countries will need stronger tax systems and higher tax revenues. Aid will also be needed to pay start-up costs for social assistance, universal health coverage, and education, including scholarships for the poorest children. Shepherd said aid will still matter in the next 20 years because many developing countries spend less than $500 per person each year. He said Nigeria, even with oil wealth, spends only $650 per person.

With crises in Syria, South Sudan, and the Central African Republic, the report says conflict and environmental disasters must be dealt with. It says governments need to reduce the risk of conflict and create inclusive peace settlements.

The report says making “reducing (income) inequality” a goal after 2015 would help. It says lowering or controlling inequality makes economic growth reduce poverty faster. It also says that if we fix inequalities that hurt the poorest people, such as access to land, labour markets, and power between men and women, we can reduce chronic poverty and inequality at the same time.

The authors ask governments to make inclusive national development plans and work with civil society. They say the poorest people should be represented in politics. They also say governments should challenge difficult social norms, such as dowries and witchcraft, that can contribute to extreme poverty. They say this would mean changing the status quo and moving away from “business as usual.”
