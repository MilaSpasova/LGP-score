From glow-in-the-dark trees to bike storage under the ground and solar-powered bins, people are trying unusual ideas to make cities better places to live.

Living in a city can be good, but it is often hard to live in a sustainable way. City people deal with pollution, traffic, and less green space. So inventors are trying new ideas. Here are some of the wackier solutions.

Pop-up parks
Some cities look like they are built more for cars than for people. A pop-up park is a simple idea. You take an empty car park, some coins, and one or two plants, and you make a small park for a short time. The PARK(ing) project started as an art experiment in San Francisco and then spread around the world. People have also made temporary city farms and ecology demos. The movement has a day of action every September.

Subterranean storage
Not everyone in cities uses a car. Bikes are becoming more popular. But where can you keep a bike safe? A Tokyo-based engineering firm called Giken made an idea called an “eco-cycle, anti-seismic underground bicycle park.” It is a round storage space that is only seven metres wide, and it goes down into the ground. It can store 204 bikes. Owners can get their bike back by pressing a button. The automatic system brings it up in about 13 seconds.

Glow-in-the-dark trees
When many people think of trees that glow, they think of Christmas lights. But Dutch designer-artist Daan Roosegaarde made a “bioluminescent” plant. This experimental technology mixes DNA from glowing marine bacteria with the chloroplast genome of a plant. This makes a glow like a jellyfish. Trials are happening to make a large-scale version. Roosegaarde hopes it could one day replace normal street lights.

Footfall harvesting
In West Ham in east London, many commuters and shoppers walk across an elevated walkway near the underground station. Most people do not notice that the ground feels a little springy. The rubber surface bends about five millimetres. This helps power the streetlights above. The floor has smart tiles that capture energy from footsteps and turn it into electricity. The UK firm Pavegen made this idea. Pavegen has also installed a similar system at London’s Heathrow Airport and other places.

Supertrees
Singapore’s Gardens by the Bay has man-made trees called “supertrees.” They are steel-framed and can be up to 50 metres high. Flowers and ferns grow on them. Their metal tops can absorb and spread heat. They can collect rainwater and help provide air ventilation for two “climate-controlled biomes” below (large conservatories). Eleven of the 18 trees also have solar panels on their “branches.”
