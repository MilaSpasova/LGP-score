The UN says that if the world wasted 25% less food, there would be enough food to feed all people who are malnourished.

Each year, 1.3bn tonnes of food is wasted. That is about one third of all food produced. This includes about 45% of fruit and vegetables, 35% of fish and seafood, 30% of cereals, 20% of dairy products, and 20% of meat. At the same time, 795 million people suffer from severe hunger and malnutrition.

Many people are trying to reduce food waste. For example, France has laws that require supermarkets to give unsold food to charities. The UN says food waste is a major challenge for food security. By 2050, food production may need to increase by 60% compared with 2005 to feed a growing population. If we waste less food, we can use fewer resources.

Food waste happens in different ways in different places. In developing countries, there is a lot of “food loss.” This is unplanned waste, often caused by poor equipment, transport, and roads or storage systems. In wealthy countries, “food waste” is high. People may throw food away because they buy too much. Stores may reject food because it does not look perfect.

In developed countries, consumers and retailers throw away 30% to 40% of the food they buy. In poorer countries, only 5% to 16% is thrown away. A 2011 report says that in Europe and North America, each person wasted 95 to 115kg of edible food each year. In sub-Saharan Africa and south and south-east Asia, each person wasted only 6 to 11kg.

Robert van Otterdijk from the UN Food and Agriculture Organization’s Save Food programme says food waste happens mostly where people can afford to throw food away. He says consumers in industrialized countries waste 222m tonnes a year. This is almost the same as the total net food production of sub-Saharan Africa, which is 230m tonnes. He also says food losses are very common in developing countries because conditions are not well developed, from managing production to transport and distribution.

Food loss and waste also harm the environment. The carbon footprint of food that is produced but not eaten is about 3.3 gigatonnes of CO2. If food waste were a country, it would be the third highest producer of greenhouse gases after the US and China. About 1.4bn hectares, close to 30% of available farm land, is used to grow food that is later wasted. More surface and groundwater (“blue water”) is used to produce wasted food than any single country uses for agriculture, including India and China.

Van Otterdijk says climate change is linked to an economy that produces and consumes more than the Earth can provide. He says food production is one of the biggest sectors in the world, and wasting one third of it has a huge impact on land, water, energy, and greenhouse gas emissions.

The highest food waste is in the US, Canada, Australia, and New Zealand, where consumers waste 39% of the food they buy. Europe is next, where about 31% is thrown away. In the UK, 15m tonnes of food is lost or wasted each year. British consumers throw away 4.2m tonnes of edible food each year. This means 11.7% of all food bought is wasted even though it could be avoided. It costs each family about £700 a year, or almost £60 a month.

In the UK, the foods most often thrown away are bread, vegetables, fruit, and milk. By weight, bread is the most wasted food: 414,000 tonnes, or 22.4% of all bread bought. By percentage, lettuce and leafy salads are wasted the most: 38%, or 64,000 tonnes.

For meat, poultry is wasted the most by percentage and weight. About 13% of edible parts (not including bones) is wasted. For fruit, melon is wasted the most by percentage, with more than a quarter of each melon thrown away.

The UK has improved in the last ten years after a strong campaign to reduce waste. Van Otterdijk calls it “one of the great successes.” From 2007 to 2012, avoidable food waste in UK homes fell by 21%, from 5.3m tonnes to 4.2m tonnes, mostly because people became more aware. Less wasted food is going into landfill. In 2000–01, only 14% of household waste was recycled or composted. By 2011–12, composted food had risen to 43%.

Van Otterdijk says there is strong and growing interest in food waste, which helps local campaigns around the world. He says much more must be done, and both public and private groups must take part. He says that if the same effort continues, the world may see important results after ten years.
