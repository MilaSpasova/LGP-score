The roof of the school is made of plastic and held up by a crooked tree trunk. The desks are mixed old chairs. But inside the Chemin des Dunes school, the students study very hard. They hope for a new life in France.

“The French language is very difficult but we try hard. If we come every day, maybe we can touch our dreams,” says Kamal, a refugee from Sudan’s Darfur district, which has been hurt by war. He comes to class three or four hours every day. “It’s a good thing to keep your brain active.”

Kamal is 29 years old and an electrical engineer. He is one of many refugees living in the “jungle” camp outside Calais. They have applied for asylum in France and want to learn French for what they hope will be their new home.

Kamal is upset because the media often talks only about people who try at night to sneak onto lorries or trains to cross the Channel.

“I need to tell people in the UK, they think that everyone wants to go there. But there are a lot of people here who want to stay in France,” Kamal said.

France already has more than a quarter of a million refugees, according to United Nations data. France has taken in more than twice as many as the UK, even though the countries have similar populations.

There are also 56,000 asylum seekers waiting for their case to be processed in France, the second highest number in Europe. In the UK, there are 36,000.

While people wait, France does not give them money support and does not let them work. The process can take many months. The jungle camp gives one free meal a day and a plastic roof. So many people stay in the hard conditions for a few more months, instead of working illegally and possibly hurting their asylum request.

Some people had the idea for the school at the start of the summer. They were bored from waiting and worried about starting life in France without being able to talk to people. The school became real within weeks and opened on 11 July.

“We did it to reunite the ‘brothers’ and, at the same time, they can learn French,” said Zimarco Jones, the school’s founder from Nigeria. He arrived in Calais in 2013 and is still waiting for his asylum claim to be processed. “Now, we need to build another one,” he says with a grin.

When it is busiest, the small classroom has 30 pupils. They sit in five rows in front of a big green chalkboard. There are pictures of cartoon animals for each letter of the French alphabet. There are also classes in English, art, and t’ai chi. But the most popular classes are French lessons, taught by volunteers from Calais and other places.

“French is not as easy as English but, two weeks ago, I decided there was no way to get to the UK,” says George, another refugee from Darfur and a serious student. He wanted to cross the Channel because he speaks English very well. But with language classes, he says he is happy to live in France.

“Anywhere there is peace, I can stay, no problem,” he says. He is already waiting at the classroom more than half an hour before his teachers arrive.

He says he does not know much about France. But the classes are slowly helping him understand the country and the language.

Many volunteers at the jungle school are local teachers who are giving up their summer holidays.

Jenny Flahaut, 33, works at a children’s home. She decided to volunteer after seeing an ad on Facebook. “I saw these people in Calais every day and I wanted to do something for them,” she said.

She is also upset about how the media shows migrants. She is upset with politicians who have never visited the camp, including David Cameron, the British prime minister. In a speech that many people criticized, he talked about “swarms” of people trying to reach the UK.

“They don’t know them and have a bad vision but they are not like that,” Flahaut said as she got ready for an afternoon lesson. “Most of them are very good people. They are welcoming and friendly. They want to improve their life and make it better, and learning is part of that.”

Now the teachers and Zimarco are working to set up a separate classroom for about 200 women and two dozen children. Volunteers say there are about ten men for every one woman in the Calais camp. Many women feel uncomfortable going to classes with male students they do not know.

After that is done, Zimarco has more dreams to make the camp a place to live, not only to survive. People in the camp call it “Zimarco’s school.” He wants to start a football team for migrants. He also dreams of changing the camp’s name. He hates “the jungle” because he says it makes it sound like the people there are not human.

“We have a discotheque, a house, a mosque, a school, shops,” he says. “We are not animals.”
