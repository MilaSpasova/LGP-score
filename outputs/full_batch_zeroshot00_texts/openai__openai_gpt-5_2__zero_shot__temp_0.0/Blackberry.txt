In 2005, BlackBerry brought instant messaging to mobile phones. The company was starting to do very well. The iPhone did not exist yet, and BlackBerry phones became one of Canada’s big exports.

Six years later, in summer 2011, there were riots in London and also in Birmingham, Nottingham, Liverpool, and Manchester. BlackBerry Messenger (BBM) helped people organize very quickly. Some politicians said BBM should be shut down for a short time. But two years later, many users were leaving BBM on their own.

Fewer people were buying BlackBerry phones, so fewer people were using BBM. Many other messaging apps appeared. These include messaging from Facebook and Apple, and other apps like WhatsApp and Kik (which is also Canadian). These apps are free to download and use. They use the internet to send text messages, pictures, voice clips, stickers, and even videos (in WhatsApp).

BlackBerry tried to keep users by making BBM available on Android and Apple phones. Many people downloaded it, and BlackBerry said there were more than 20 million downloads. But some people think this came too late. James Gooderson, an 18-year-old student who writes a technology blog, says bringing BBM to the iPhone is four or five years too late. He says WhatsApp has taken away the reason young people used a BlackBerry.

BBM says it has 80 million users each month after an upgrade, but WhatsApp has 300 million. Other apps can do things BBM cannot. BBM does not yet have video or voice calls like Skype and Viber. It does not share your location like Path. It does not have video sharing like iMessage. It also does not have stickers, which many kids like. Even features BBM had on BlackBerry phones, like sharing contacts and calendars, are not on the Apple and Android versions.

Messaging is changing from mostly words to more pictures. Photos on Instagram get many comments. Snapchat lets people send pictures that delete after ten seconds. Like BBM, these services are free on any phone with internet. But in 2011, BBM was seen as very powerful. Some people said it helped start a revolution in Egypt. During the London riots, BBM could spread news faster than television.

Jean-Pierre Moore, 28, manages a youth club in Stockwell, south London. He said they could see messages about where rioters were going next, while TV news was four hours behind. He mostly uses an iPad now. He does not think stopping BBM would have stopped the looting. He says people were angry for a long time. He says that if you mention “stop and search” in his area, young men quickly get angry.

A research firm called Enders Analysis says almost 80% of young smartphone owners regularly use a social networking app. But two-thirds use more than one. Among 16- to 24-year-olds, 60% use Facebook every day, but 46% use other options too. Benedict Evans, a digital media specialist at Enders, says the phone itself is now the main platform. Apps connect to your contacts and photos. Apps can become popular and then lose popularity quickly. Some, like Instagram, stay popular, and others disappear.

A 13-year-old named Bennett has three devices, all given by family members. He keeps his BlackBerry for messaging. He uses an iPhone on wi-fi to play games. He uses an HTC Android phone to make calls. His friends still use BBM. There have been four phone thefts at his school this term, and all were BlackBerrys. With BBM, one message can be sent to everyone in your contacts, which can be several hundred people. On WhatsApp, a broadcast message can go to 50 people. But Bennett says Instagram is now very important. He says Instagram is like Facebook without parents. He says older people have taken over Facebook. He says that when he saw his mum on Facebook, he deleted his account.

For families who may struggle to pay heating bills this winter, BlackBerry can still seem good because it is cheap. With a second-hand device and a £7-a-month deal from T-Mobile, people can send unlimited BBM messages. But people trust BBM privacy less now. Many business people, revolutionaries, demonstrators, and rioters believed BBM messages were encrypted and could not be traced. After the riots, prosecutions ended that belief.

In South Kensington, outside a private school, older students all have phones with Apple logos. They all use WhatsApp. For many of them, BBM is something from the past. One teenager says he still has a BlackBerry, but he is the only one. He says that makes him feel isolated.
