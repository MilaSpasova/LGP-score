Sweden is the best country for older people, and Afghanistan is the worst, says the first global index on ageing. But being a rich country does not always mean life is better for people over 60. Sweden is number 1, followed by Norway, Germany, the Netherlands, and Canada. The Global AgeWatch index also has some surprising results.

The US, the world’s richest country, is only in 8th place. The UK is not in the top ten and is 13th. Sri Lanka is 36th, much higher than Pakistan at 89th, even though their gross domestic product (GDP) levels are similar. Bolivia and Mauritius do better than their economy size might suggest. Brazil, Russia, India, and China have mixed results. Brazil and China rank fairly high, while India and Russia rank much lower.

“This survey shows that history counts,” said Mark Gorman, director of the HelpAge International advocacy group. He said the top countries are what people might expect, but Scandinavian countries were not wealthy when they started universal pensions. Older people in Sri Lanka today benefit from good basic education and health care because of past policy choices. He said resources are limited, but countries should also think about old age when they decide how to invest.

The index was developed with the UN Fund for Population and Development. It covers 91 countries and 89% of the world’s older people. The report comes during a big change in population: by 2050, there are expected to be two billion people aged 60 and over. That will be more than one fifth of the world’s population.

Population ageing means older people make up a bigger share of the population. This is happening fastest in developing countries. More than two-thirds of older people live in poor countries. By 2050, this is expected to be about four-fifths. In France, it took 115 years for the older population to double from 7% to 14% (from 1865 to 1980). In Brazil, the same change is likely to happen from 2011 to 2032, in only 21 years.

The index shows that the fastest ageing countries—Jordan, Laos, Mongolia, Nicaragua, and Vietnam—are in the lower half of the ranking. In these countries, the number of older people is predicted to more than triple by 2050. This suggests policymakers need to deal with ageing directly so they can support their people.

There are also differences between men and women. Women usually live longer than men. In 2012, for every 84 men aged 60 and over, there were 100 women. Many women are at a disadvantage in old age because they have less paid work (so less savings), less power in family decisions, and more risk of violence.

The report says that if the right steps are taken, an ageing population does not always mean much higher health care spending. It says long-term investment in education and health care is important. Bolivia is ranked 46, even though it is one of the poorest countries. Bolivia has a national plan on ageing, free health care, and a non-contributory universal pension. Nepal is ranked 77. Nepal started a basic pension in 1995 for people over 70 who have no other pension income. The pension is limited in value and eligibility, and coverage is uneven, but it shows how a poor country can begin to address poverty in old age.

Good basic health care, introduced decades ago in Chile and Costa Rica, has helped their ageing populations. A good education system also helps later in life. Basic literacy is important for older people when they deal with pensions bureaucracy. In the Philippines, older people benefited from education reforms after independence in 1946, which made elementary and high school education compulsory. The same is true for Armenia, which, like other former Soviet Union countries, benefited from a strong education system. South Korea is a surprisingly low 67 on the ageing index. It did worse than similar countries by GDP per head, partly because it started a pension only recently.

The ageing index uses 13 indicators in four areas: income security, health care, employment and education, and an enabling environment. All indicators have equal weight, except pension income coverage, life expectancy at 60, healthy life expectancy at 60, and psychological well-being. These were given more weight because the data quality was better. Countries were included only when there was enough data.

Professor Sir Richard Jolly, who created the human development index, said this new index helps people understand the needs and opportunities of older people in a wider way. He said it challenges countries around the world to aim higher and see what is possible.
