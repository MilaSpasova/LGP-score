Felix Baumgartner stood near the edge of space above the deserts of New Mexico. He paused for a moment. He was just one small step from the capsule, but he would fall 24 miles back to Earth. Mission control said, “Our guardian angel will take care of you,” and Felix jumped.

About ten minutes later, the Austrian landed safely. He reached speeds up to 725 mph. He broke three world records. One record was becoming the first person to skydive faster than the speed of sound. He broke the sound barrier at Mach 1.24. People in the control room cheered, “We love you Felix,” and his mother, Ava Baumgartner, cried. Felix raised his arms to thank his team.

He wore a special survival suit. It protected him from big changes in air pressure during the fall. Without the suit, his blood would have boiled and his lungs might have exploded. Later he said, “When I was standing there on top of the world, you become so humble, you don’t think about breaking records.” He said he mostly thought about getting back alive. He also said, “Sometimes you have to go up really high to see how small you are.”

The week before, two attempts were stopped. On Sunday morning, the weather was good, so the mission went ahead. A huge balloon carried him up into clear skies. The balloon was 30 million cubic feet. Its skin was one-tenth the thickness of a sandwich bag. A capsule hung under the balloon, and Felix sat inside it in his suit.

When he reached the right height, he went through a 40-item checklist with his mentor, Joe Kittinger. Kittinger was the previous record holder for the highest altitude manned balloon flight.

There was a problem with a heater for Felix’s visor. The visor could fog up. He told Kittinger, “This is very serious, Joe. Sometimes it’s getting foggy when I exhale. ... I do not feel heat.” But they decided to continue. About 8 million people watched the jump live on YouTube.

The ride up took two and a half hours. During the ride, Felix could see the curve of the Earth, and the sky slowly turned black. Coming down was much faster.

Three cameras on his suit recorded his free fall. He fell for a little over four minutes, but he did not beat the record for the longest free fall. Then his parachute opened.

The mission and the suit may help astronauts in the future. It suggests astronauts might survive a high-altitude disaster by leaving their spacecraft. The space shuttle Columbia had such a disaster in 2003. The top medical doctor for Felix’s jump was Dr Jonathan Clark. His wife, Laurel Clark, died in the Columbia accident. He now works to improve astronauts’ chances in a high-altitude disaster.

Felix is known for dangerous stunts. He used to be a paratrooper. He has jumped off buildings and mountains. He once jumped into a cave that was 600 feet deep. He did two practice free falls for this event: one from 71,000 feet in March and one from 97,000 feet in July 2012. His big jump was above Roswell, a town known for UFO stories.

Felix tried to break five records: first person to break the sound barrier in free fall, highest free-fall jump, highest manned balloon flight, longest free fall, and a jump platform that is believed to be the largest manned balloon in history. The stunt took seven years to plan. Red Bull sponsored it. Felix beat two of Joe Kittinger’s old records. Kittinger, a retired US air force colonel, had jumped from a balloon 19 miles above Earth in 1960. During Felix’s climb, the only voice guiding him on the radio was Kittinger, who was 84.

After the jump, Felix said, “I want to inspire a generation.” He said he hoped that in the next four years he could be like Joe Kittinger, giving advice to a young person who wants to break Felix’s record. He said his most exciting moment was standing outside the capsule “on top of the world.” He also said the best moment was after he landed, when Mike Todd, the life support engineer who helped him put on the suit, came over smiling like a little kid.

Felix said he felt like Todd’s son, and that Todd was very happy he was alive. Todd said, “The world needs a hero right now, and they got one in Felix Baumgartner.” Kittinger joked that he wanted to give a “one-fingered salute” to people who said Felix would come apart when he went supersonic.

Felix said this would be his last jump. He said he will settle down with his girlfriend, Nicole Oetl. He will fly helicopters on rescue missions in the US and Austria.
