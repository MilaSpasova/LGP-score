A group of experts and charities says some allergy tests are fake or wrong. These tests make thousands of people get treatments they do not need. They can also make people, or their children, stop eating many foods. This can lead to poor diets and sometimes malnutrition.

Allergies and food intolerances are increasing. But many people mix them up. Many people are also wrongly told they have an allergy. Sense About Science made a guide with allergy specialists and charities to help.

Tracey Brown from Sense About Science said there is a big problem with myths, wrong ideas from studies, and fake medicine. She said this costs a lot. Some people do things they do not need to do, and other people do not get the help they truly need.

Experts worry about restaurants and caterers. They see many people say they have allergies. But some of these people really have food intolerance, which is not dangerous. Then staff may stop being as careful. This can be very dangerous for a person with a real allergy.

Moira Austin from the Anaphylaxis Campaign said this is very important. If a caterer thinks someone is only avoiding a food because it makes them feel bloated, they may be less careful. There have been deaths when people told a restaurant about an allergy, but the meal still contained that food.

The guide says most allergy tests bought online or in shops do not have scientific proof. One home test checks for IgG (immunoglobulin G) antibodies in blood. These antibodies are part of the immune system’s response to infections. But the best medical evidence shows high IgG levels do not mean a person has an allergy. Many people without an allergy or food intolerance get a positive result.

The guide also says another test is not valid. It mixes acupuncture and homeopathy. It tries to measure electrical resistance on the skin while a person holds the food. Hair follicle testing is also useless. Hair is not involved in allergic reactions, so hair tests cannot show if someone has an allergy. People should also not believe that allergies are caused by an “energy blockage” found by muscle testing and cured by acupuncture.

Paul Seddon, a consultant paediatric allergist, said he often sees children on diets that are too limited. Parents believe the children are allergic to many foods because of these tests. He said this must stop.

Adam Fox from Guys and St Thomas’ Hospital in London said he also sees patients who sent hair for testing or removed many foods from their child’s diet. He said it is hard to fix this later. Children need proper diets. Avoiding foods that are not harmful can also hurt quality of life. A child who cannot eat wheat or drink milk may not be able to go to parties.

Some parents believe a child’s long-lasting tiredness, headaches, or eczema is caused by an allergy. It can take time and many tests to find out if this is true or not. It can be tempting to go to an alternative therapist who gives one quick test and a fast answer, but the answer can be wrong.

Allergies are rising in developed countries. In the last 30 years, the percentages of children diagnosed with allergic rhinitis and eczema have tripled. Better diagnosis is part of the reason, but the rise is real. More people now think allergies may be causing their own health problems or their children’s. The guide lists myths about what causes allergies, like E numbers in food colourings, “toxic overload”, and fast food.
