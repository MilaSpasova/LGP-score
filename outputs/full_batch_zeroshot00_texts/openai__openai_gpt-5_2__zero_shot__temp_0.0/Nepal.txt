A new count shows there are more tigers in Nepal now than at any time since the 1970s. This gives conservation workers hope. Tiger numbers have been falling across South Asia for 100 years, but people think tigers can still be saved.

Nepal now has 198 wild royal bengal tigers. This is a 63.6% rise in five years, the government survey said. “This is very encouraging,” said Maheshwar Dhakal, an ecologist with Nepal’s Department of National Parks and Wildlife Conservation.

The count used photos from more than 500 cameras. The cameras were placed in five protected areas and three wildlife corridors. More than 250 conservationists and wildlife experts worked on the survey. It cost about £250,000. Dhakal said India did a similar survey. Results from both countries will be published later in 2013. India has 1,300 big cats in several large protected areas and needs a few more months to finish its data. Nepal has promised to double its tiger population by 2022, from 121 in 2009, when the last full tiger count happened.

As Asia has become richer, prices for tiger skins and tiger body parts have gone up. Some body parts are used in traditional Chinese medicines. International gangs pay poor local people in Nepal a lot of money to kill tigers. The skin and bones go to middlemen. The middlemen can cross the loose border to India easily, where the main dealers are.

One big problem is that some senior officials work with mafia networks in this trade. Experts say the tiger increase comes from better policing in national parks, stronger efforts to stop poaching, and better care of tiger habitats. In Nepal, forests cover 29% of the land. But experts say Nepal must do more to protect tiger habitat and the animals that tigers hunt, so tigers have enough space and enough food.

As tiger numbers have grown, conflicts with villagers have also grown. Park officials said seven people were killed by tiger attacks near national parks in 2012, compared to four in 2011. Villagers want better protection. “The government is making conservation plans for tigers. But it should also come up with plans to protect people from tigers,” said Krishna Bhurtel, a village headman in Chitwan, to the Nepali newspaper Nagarik. Wildlife authorities caught a tiger in Chitwan after it killed two people, including a villager pulled from his bed in May.

Long ago, thousands of tigers lived in the forests of Bangladesh, India, and Nepal. Now their total number is about 3,000. This is a 95% drop over a century. Chitwan National Park in central Nepal has the most adult tigers, with 120. Bardiya National Park has 50, and Shukla Phanta Wildlife Reserve has 17.

Diwakar Chapagain, who leads a World Wildlife Fund Nepal unit that watches wildlife trade, said tiger skins are wanted in Tibet. Rich people there use them as festival clothes.

In Nepal, kings used to stand on tiger skins in front of stuffed tigers at special events. Some rich Nepali people have put tiger heads on the walls of their living rooms. Tiger bones are also wanted for traditional Chinese medicines. Chapagain said selling tiger parts can bring thousands of dollars in illegal markets, and this is a serious threat to tigers.
