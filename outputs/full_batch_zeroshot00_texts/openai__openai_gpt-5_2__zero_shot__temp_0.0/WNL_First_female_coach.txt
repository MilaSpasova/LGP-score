Women were often kept out of important roles in football. This changed when France chose its first female professional team manager. It did not matter that the club was in the second division. Some men said it was only a trick to get attention for a small team, Clermont Foot 63, which was 14th out of 20. But the key point was that Helena Costa got the top job.

Her appointment made football history. She became the first woman to be hired as a manager in the top two divisions of any professional European league. Véronique Soulier, who leads the club’s supporters’ group, said she was happy. She said she was surprised at first, but then most people agreed it was good news. She said a woman leading a group of men can be a good thing.

Clermont Foot 63 plays at Clermont-Ferrand in the Auvergne region of south-central France. The average home crowd is about 3,800. Costa used to work as a talent spotter for Celtic in the Scottish Premiership. Costa is 36. She was born in Alhandra, on the River Tagus in southeast Portugal. She has a master’s degree in sports science and is a UEFA-licensed coach. She coached Benfica’s male youth teams. She also coached the Qatar women’s team and led them to their first international win in 2012. More recently, she coached the Iranian women’s national team, which she left in September 2013.

Costa got a two-year contract from the club president, Claude Michy, who often gets attention for his club. In 2013, he said the team had signed Messi. They did sign someone named Messi, but it was not Lionel Messi. It was Junior Messi Enguene, a 20-year-old midfielder from Cameroon.

France’s women’s minister, Najat Vallaud-Belkacem, tweeted: “Bravo to Clermont Foot for understanding that giving women a place is the future of professional football.”

Carolina Morace, an Italian, was the only woman before Costa to coach a men’s professional team. Morace said she did not know Costa, but if a team hired her, it means she can do the job. She said she hopes one day this will seem normal. Morace was also a great player. She was the top scorer for 12 seasons in Italy’s Serie A and played 153 international games for Italy. In 1999, she became coach of Viterbese in the men’s Serie C1. After two games, she quit after a conflict with the club owner, Luciano Gaucci, who also owned Perugia at the time.

Morace said she refused Gaucci’s demand that she fire her assistant and the team’s trainer. Gaucci said she could keep working with who she wanted, but Morace said trust was gone and she did not want to work in that uncertain situation. Today, Morace works as a lawyer in Rome and as a football commentator on TV and for the daily Gazzetta dello Sport.

Morace said there are still too many men working in football, even in the women’s game, even when they do not have the same skill as women who are not working. Raymond Domenech, former manager of the French national team, said women know how to play football and how to manage teams. He asked why women should not manage men’s teams. He said the reverse happens and no one complains. He said it is a natural choice and shows that women are equal to men. He praised President Michy and said that if he managed a club again, he would hire a woman as his number two, but Michy did it first.

Clermont Foot 63’s website said Costa’s appointment would bring “a new era.” On the supporters’ website, people reacted with surprise and some doubt. One fan said it was only a publicity trick and she would not last the season. The fan said it was hard to believe she would earn the players’ respect, especially since she is the same age as the oldest players. Another fan said her experience was not bad, but asked if she would be good enough. A third fan wished her well but said it might be hard for a woman to succeed in such a macho business, and asked if the president had made a media move.

Soulier had hope. She said the club might find the motivation it is missing. She said the boys on the team can be hard to manage, and with a woman in charge, maybe they will be less demanding.

But Costa may be the one who makes demands. She once did work experience at Chelsea during José Mourinho’s first time as manager there, from 2004 to 2007. Someone reportedly called her “Mourinho in a skirt.” Costa rejected that sexist comment. She said that like Mourinho, she always wants to win. She said she was happy to be compared with him in that way.
