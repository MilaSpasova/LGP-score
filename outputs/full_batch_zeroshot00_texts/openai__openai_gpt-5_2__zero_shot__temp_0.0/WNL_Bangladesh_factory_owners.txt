A world agreement to make Bangladesh clothing factories safer may face a court case. Some factory owners want money back for the cost of closing factories and fixing buildings. They say repair work can take months. During that time, they cannot pay workers while the factory is closed, and they also have to pay for big repairs.

These repairs began after the Rana Plaza building in Dhaka, Bangladesh, collapsed in 2013. In that disaster, 1,138 people died.

Now, hundreds of factories each month are checked for fire safety and building problems under the Accord on Fire and Building Safety in Bangladesh. More than 170 international brands support it, including Primark and Marks & Spencer. International trade unions also support it, including IndustriALL.

One factory owner in Dhaka, from Softex Cotton, has threatened legal action after his factory was closed because of building problems. He is asking for a reported $100m in compensation.

Another factory owner, who did not give his name, said a factory cannot close “just for a short time.” He said that even closing for a few months could make a factory lose orders and shut down forever. He said the Accord was unclear about who should pay the costs when factories close.

Jenny Holdcroft, a policy director at IndustriALL, said brands promised to keep placing orders with suppliers for two years, so factories would not lose orders during closures. She said 12 factories have been found so far that need major work. But she said many only need to partly close, so work can continue on other floors.

She also said the Accord legally requires brands to make sure workers are paid during factory closures. She said the Accord left open the details of who pays, so factory owners who can afford repairs and worker payments will pay their share.

Holdcroft said payment was always going to be negotiated. She said brands do not want to pay if rich factory owners have kept profits and did not spend money on their factories for years. She said there would be disruption, and that without disruption, there would be no change. An Accord spokesman said talks about payments, and even legal action, would not stop the work to improve factory safety.

Pressure on the Accord has increased because another safety group, the Alliance for Bangladesh Worker Safety, has offered money to help pay workers. The Alliance is backed by US retailers including Walmart and Gap. It set aside $5m to help pay workers for up to two months while building work is done. It has found five factories in four buildings where work needed to stop.

Shaidullah Azim, a director of the Bangladeshi Garment Manufacturers and Exporters Association, said the Alliance is paying part of workers’ salaries with factory owners, and this has caused confusion. He said they met with the Accord and told them they need to help, or workers will not get support.
