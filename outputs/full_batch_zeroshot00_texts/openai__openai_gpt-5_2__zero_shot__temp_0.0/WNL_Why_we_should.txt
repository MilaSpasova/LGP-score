James Hamblin is a senior editor at The Atlantic magazine. He tried a challenge: he stopped showering for a while. He did this for his series, “If Our Bodies Could Talk.” He wanted to see what happens when we clean our bodies too much. He took fewer showers. When he did shower, he did not use shampoo or soap.

He found something many others have found. When we use lots of soap and body wash, our skin tries harder to get back to normal. Showering can wash away the skin’s natural oil and bacteria. Hot water and cleaning products can make this happen even more.

After a shower, you might feel tight and dry. That is because your skin’s natural moisture was washed away. Our skin also has millions of helpful bacteria, like our gut does. Frequent rubbing and scrubbing can destroy these bacteria. When the bacteria grow back, the new mix may include more microbes that make smell. So showering too often may make you smell more. If you stop showering and stop using soap, your skin may go through an early adjustment period that is likely gross. After that, the skin often returns to balance, makes less oil, and healthy bacteria can grow.

After his challenge, Hamblin felt the human body can be quite lovely on its own. It is not only about smell or looks. Dermatologists suggest that using less soap can help acne and eczema. Taking fewer showers and using fewer products can also help the environment. The average shower lasts seven minutes and uses 65 litres of water. That is 65 litres of clean, drinkable water mixed with soap and sent down the drain each day, sometimes more than once.

Clean water is becoming harder to ignore as California has another summer of drought. It is becoming clear that clean water is one of the world’s most valuable commodities, and it may soon be in short supply. There is also waste from body wash bottles. These are strong reasons to let your body be more natural.

If this idea makes you feel nervous because you have been close to people who do not shower much and still smell, do not worry. Many people who reduce washing still use deodorant if they need it. People’s natural scent is different, and it can change with diet, hydration, and exercise. Also, washing your hands with soap is still recommended to help stop infectious diseases from spreading.

You do not have to stop all at once like Hamblin did. But there is a middle place between cleaning many times a day and never showering. You can reduce. Skip a few showers, use less soap, and let the helpful bacteria grow a little.
