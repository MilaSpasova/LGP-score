Swedish prisons have been known around the world for being modern and fair. But some people ask: are Sweden’s prisons too easy?

In November 2013, Nils Oberg, the head of Sweden’s prison and probation service, said four prisons would close. This was because the number of prisoners had dropped in an “out of the ordinary” way.

Crime rates had not fallen. But from 2011 to 2012, Sweden’s prisoner number fell by 6%. It was now a little over 4,500 people. A similar drop was expected in 2013 and 2014. Oberg said he was surprised, but he hoped it was because prisons were helping people change. He said they hoped their work in rehabilitation and stopping people from committing crime again had helped.

Kenneth Gustafsson runs Kumla Prison, Sweden’s most secure prison. It is about 130 miles west of Stockholm. He said Sweden’s prison service is very different from when he started as a young officer in 1978. But he does not think it has become soft. He said prisoners were treated well, maybe too well, some people said. After some famous escapes in 2004, Sweden put more focus on security.

Even with more focus on security, Sweden still tries to be humane. Prison sentences rarely go over ten years. People with life sentences can ask the courts after ten years to change the sentence to a fixed time, usually about 18 to 25 years. Sweden was the first country in Europe to use electronic tags for convicted criminals. Sweden also tries to avoid short prison sentences when possible. It uses community measures instead, because they have been shown to reduce reoffending better.

In Sweden, about 30 to 40% of people offend again within three years. This is about half the UK rate. One possible reason is the age of criminal responsibility in Sweden is 15. In the UK, children aged 10 to 17 and young people under 21 have the highest reoffending rates. Many of them later go to adult prisons. In Sweden, no one under 21 can get a life sentence. Sweden also tries hard to keep as many young offenders as possible out of prison.

Another reason prisoner numbers may be dropping is strong support after prison. A government probation service supervises people on probation. It also must provide treatment programmes for people with drug, alcohol, or violence problems. About 4,500 volunteer supervisors from the public also help by supporting offenders.

Gustafsson said that in 2013 and 2014, the main focus would be young offenders and men convicted of violent behaviour. Sweden has had programmes for drug addiction for many years. Now it is also developing programmes to deal with aggression and violence. He said this matters for society when people are released.

The writer spoke to a former prisoner, Peter Soderlund, who runs a social enterprise called X-Cons Sweden. Soderlund served almost three years of a four-year sentence for drug and weapons offences, and was released in 1998. He got help from a new group called Kris (Criminals’ Return Into Society), which was run by former prisoners.

Soderlund said a big difference between Kris and X-Cons is that X-Cons lets people join even if they are still taking addiction medicines. Both groups want to help prisoners return to society after release. Soderlund said he was treated well at Osteraker Prison, where he served his sentence, because the governor was “enlightened.” But he said not all prisons were like that. He met many people who needed help. X-Cons meets people when they leave prison, helps them find accommodation, and gives them a support network.

Gustafsson said Sweden believes strongly in rehabilitation, but not in a naive way. He said some people will not or cannot change. But he believes most prisoners want to change, and prisons should help them. He also said change does not always happen in one prison sentence.

He said rehabilitation is not only done in prison. It is often a process that includes probation and wider society. Prisons can give education and training, but when people leave prison they also need housing and jobs.
