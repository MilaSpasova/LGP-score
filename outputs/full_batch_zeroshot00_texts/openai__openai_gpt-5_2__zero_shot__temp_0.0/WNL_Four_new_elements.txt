Being a chemist today can be surprising: you wait a long time for a new element, and then several appear at once.

Researchers in Japan, Russia, and the United States found four new chemical elements. These were the first new elements added to the periodic table since 2011, when elements 114 and 116 were added. The four new elements live for a very short time and are highly radioactive. They finish the seventh row of the periodic table, so many science textbooks now need updates.

The International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry (IUPAC) is a world group based in the US that checks and approves element names and measurements. IUPAC confirmed the four elements on 30 December 2015, after studying research going back to 2004.

For now, the elements have temporary Latin-style names: ununtrium, ununpentium, ununseptium, and ununoctium. These names match their atomic numbers: 113, 115, 117, and 118. An atomic number is the number of protons in the center (nucleus) of an atom.

IUPAC said a Russian-American team at the Joint Institute for Nuclear Research in Dubna and at Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory in California provided enough evidence for elements 115, 117, and 118. IUPAC gave credit for element 113 to a team from the RIKEN Institute in Japan, even though Russian and American scientists also claimed it. This means Japan is the first Asian country that will get to name an element. IUPAC rules say new elements can be named after a myth idea, a mineral, a place or country, or a scientist.

When elements 114 and 116 were officially named in 2012, they were called flerovium and livermorium. The names came from the Flerov Lab in Dubna and the Lawrence Livermore Lab in the US.

Kosuke Morita, who led the RIKEN research, said his team plans to look for element 119 and beyond. Jan Reedijk of IUPAC said chemists are excited to see the periodic table completed down to the seventh row.

The Japanese team is thought to be choosing between three possible names for ununtrium: japonium, rikenium, and nishinarium. The last name comes from the Nishina Center for Accelerator-Based Science, where the element was found. Polly Arnold, a chemistry professor at Edinburgh University, said this kind of work takes great care. She said learning about radioactive decay helps scientists improve their models, and may help with problems like nuclear waste. It can also lead to new technology for building the equipment needed to make these measurements.

The scientists must also suggest two-letter symbols for the elements. After IUPAC gets the suggestions, they will be open for public review for five months so people can object. In 1996, scientists suggested the symbol Cp for copernicium (element 112), but it was changed to Cn because other scientists said Cp already referred to another substance.

To make the new elements, researchers at the three labs smashed lighter atomic nuclei into each other. Then they looked for special patterns of radioactive decay that should happen if a new element was made. Ununtrium and ununpentium are thought to be metals. Ununseptium might be a metalloid, which means it has some metal-like properties. Ununoctium may be a noble gas, like helium, neon, and argon in group 18. But it is hard to be sure because only a few atoms of each element have ever been made.

Paul Karol, who led the IUPAC group that checked the elements, said the results will mostly help scientists who study very heavy nuclei. These scientists are looking for an “island of stability” near element 120, or maybe 126. Some elements there might live long enough for detailed chemistry studies. He also said any practical uses for these new elements are far away, because they are very hard to make.
