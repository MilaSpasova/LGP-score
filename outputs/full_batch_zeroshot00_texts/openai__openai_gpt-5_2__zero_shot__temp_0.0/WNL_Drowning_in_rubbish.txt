Some cities have pigeons. Lima has black vultures, also called gallinazos. They fly in circles above the city in groups. They sit on famous buildings, like old churches and old buildings in the city centre. With their wrinkled heads and small eyes, they remind many people of things they do not want to see: neglect, poverty, and dirt.

But now these birds are being used to help the city. Environmental authorities are trying to improve the birds’ image. They put GoPro cameras and GPS trackers on them. The birds get a new job: helping fight illegal rubbish dumping.

Samuel is one of ten certified disease-free black vultures (Coragyps atratus) in the project. He does what vultures do well: find rubbish. With his tracker, he is released above the city. He finds secret rubbish dumps and records their GPS locations on a live map.

Samuel’s trainer at Lima’s Huachipa Zoo is Alfredo Correa. He says vultures can eat dead animals because their bodies protect them from viruses and bacteria. He says they have some of the strongest gut flora in nature.

This work is a project between USAID and the Peruvian Environment Ministry to deal with Lima’s rubbish problem. Other vultures have names like Capitan Huggin, Capitan Fenix (named after a mythological creature that rises from ashes), and Capitana Aella (“Whirlwind”). A playful video uses a dramatic voice and says the vultures are fighting sickness and disease while “humanity is calmly ignoring the danger.”

The project has a serious message. Lima has nearly ten million people but only four landfills. Because of this, there are many illegal dumps. The Environment Ministry says one fifth of the rubbish ends up in these illegal places. Water from the waste pollutes Lima’s main water source, the Rimac river. It also pollutes the Chillon and Lurin rivers, which flow into the Bay of Lima.

The environmental supervision agency, OEFA, says three poorer neighbourhoods have the most fly-tipped rubbish, even though they have only 12% of Lima’s population. These areas are Villa Maria del Triunfo (39.4%), Villa El Salvador (25.3%), and El Agustino (18.3%).

Part of the problem is unpaid taxes. Many residents, especially in the barrios, do not pay. This means some of the 43 district municipalities do not have enough money for basic services like rubbish collection.

It also means that even when vultures find illegal rubbish, it may not get cleaned up. “We share the vulture’s GPS coordinates with the municipalities,” says Javier Hernandez, the project director. “It’s their job to collect the rubbish and to try and change the habits of their residents.”

The project also wants citizens to be “vultures on the ground.” It asks people to report illegal dumping, make less waste, and recycle. Some residents are helping by posting photos of illegal dumps on the project’s Twitter feed and Facebook page.

The idea began at the 2014 United Nations Climate Change Conference in Lima. Hernandez says they wanted ways to involve not only authorities but also citizens, to build environmental awareness. Artist Cristina Planas put huge vulture heads on top of 25 dead palm trees in a wetland reserve in the south of the city. Rich residents disliked it and thought it was ugly, but Planas asked citizens to “adopt a vulture” to support conservation and recycling.

Planas said people are a little scared of how vultures look. But she said the vulture is really helping people. She said it is “recycling” in the last place where people threw their rubbish.
