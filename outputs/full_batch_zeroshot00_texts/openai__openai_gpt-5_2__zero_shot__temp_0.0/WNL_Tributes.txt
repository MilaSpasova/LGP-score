Most of B.B. King’s work was blues, and much of his best work was blues. But he was always willing to play in other places too. He was friendly and skilled, and he helped connect different kinds of music and different cultures. It may be too soon to call him “the last of the bluesmen,” but it is hard to imagine another blues artist having as much influence as King. He influenced thousands of musicians and millions of listeners during a 65-year career.

Riley B. King was born near Itta Bena, Mississippi. The “B” did not seem to stand for a name. He grew up with few chances in life because he was an African-American farm worker. Little by little, he worked past this barrier. A family friend taught him the basics of guitar. He also improved his singing by singing with a quartet of gospel singers. In his early 20s, he moved to Memphis.

After a couple of years, he played often at a bar in West Memphis, Arkansas. He also became a disc jockey on a Memphis radio station. He was called “The Beale Street Blues Boy.” This was shortened to “Blues Boy King,” and then to “BB.” After one recording session in 1949 for a Nashville label, King began recording for Modern Records in 1950.

His first hit was in 1952. It was a new, dramatic version of “Three O’Clock Blues.” It was number one on the R&B chart for 15 weeks. More successful songs followed, such as “Please Love Me,” “You Upset Me Baby,” and “Sweet Sixteen.” On these and many other recordings, most of them his own songs, King built a style that was new but still clearly part of blues history. He often praised the musicians who influenced him and usually named T-Bone Walker first.

King told Guitar Player magazine, “I’ve tried my best to get that sound. I came pretty close but never quite got it.” In an interview in 2001, he said, “If T-Bone Walker had been a woman I would have asked him to marry me.” He also named older blues guitarists Blind Lemon Jefferson and Lonnie Johnson, and jazz players Charlie Christian and Django Reinhardt.

King said part of his guitar style came from what he could not do well. He said he started bending notes because he could not play in the bottleneck style, like Elmore James and Booker White. He loved that sound but could not do it. He also spoke modestly about his singing. His singing mixed smooth singers like Nat King Cole with loud blues singers like Joe Turner and Dr Clayton. He especially liked the composer and singer Louis Jordan, and he honored him in the 1999 album Let the Good Times Roll.

During the 1950s, King was the top blues artist playing in black-supported theatres and clubs. He played many one-night shows. In 1956, he was said to have played 342 engagements. In 1962, he tried to change his work life by signing with a major label, ABC. But the first records under that contract tried to make him a mainstream pop singer. His fans did not like them, and ABC’s accountants did not either.

His 1965 album Live at the Regal showed how strong his blues music was. It became a famous album and an important early influence for many younger musicians. He had more R&B hits with blues songs like “How Blue Can You Get” and “Paying the Cost to Be the Boss.” In 1969, he reached high on the pop charts with “The Thrill Is Gone,” something blues artists had not done for many years.

It took time for him to become popular with rock audiences. Many rock fans thought of blues mainly through Chicago blues artists like Muddy Waters and Howlin’ Wolf. But rock musicians who admired King helped more people notice him. In 1969 he said that kids started calling him the greatest blues guitarist in the world, and when he asked who told them that, they said “Mike Bloomfield” or “Eric Clapton.” He said he owed his new popularity to these young people. He gained more rock respect with the 1970 album Indianola Mississippi Seeds, where he worked with Carole King and Joe Walsh.

After that, King was firmly known as “the chairman of the board of blues singers.” With help from his manager, Sidney Seidenberg, he toured internationally, including Japan, Australia, China, and Russia. He also played concerts for prisoners at the Cook County jail in Chicago and at San Quentin. These experiences led to his long involvement in rehabilitation programmes.

In 1990, King was diagnosed with diabetes. He toured less, but he still toured enough that fans outside the US could see him every year or two. He often performed seated, but his singing and guitar playing stayed strong and only slowly weakened. For his 80th birthday in 2005, there were big celebrations. They included an award-winning album with collaborations with Eric Clapton, Mark Knopfler, Roger Daltrey, Gloria Estefan, and others. Many musicians, including Bono, Amadou Bagayoko, and Elton John, also paid tribute. There was also a “farewell tour,” but it did not end up being a real farewell.

In 2009, King won a Grammy award for best traditional blues album for One Kind Favor. In 2012, he performed at the White House. The US President, Barack Obama, joined him to sing “Sweet Home Chicago.” King was married twice and divorced twice. He had 11 children with different partners. Four other children died before he did.
