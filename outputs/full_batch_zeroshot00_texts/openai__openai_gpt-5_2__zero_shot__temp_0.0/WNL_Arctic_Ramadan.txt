In Canada’s Arctic, summer days are very long. Bright sunlight covers the treeless land for more than 20 hours each day. Many people like this after the dark winter. But for Muslims in Iqaluit, Nunavut, it can be hard during Ramadan. During Ramadan, Muslims usually do not eat or drink from sunrise to sunset.

Abdul Karim is 29 years old. He moved from Ottawa to Iqaluit in 2011. He follows the local sun to fast. This year, he eats at about 1:30 a.m. before sunrise. He eats again at about 11 p.m. when the sun sets. He said he has not fainted. He said he would stop only if fasting hurt his health. He also joked that he does not think fasting will hurt him.

As Ramadan nears its end, Muslims around the world focus on community work, prayer, and thinking quietly. In the Arctic, the long days have changed how some people fast.

Most Muslims in Iqaluit follow the Ramadan times used in Ottawa, about 1,300 miles south. Muslim scholars have said that Muslims in the far north can follow the timetable of Mecca or the nearest Muslim city. Atif Jilani moved to Iqaluit from Toronto in 2015. He said this still means fasting for about 18 hours each day, but it is easier to manage.

Many people in the community, about 100 people, break their fast together. They meet at the city’s new mosque, finished in February. At that time, the temperature with wind chill dropped as low as -50C. They eat nightly suppers. They eat foods like dates and goat or lamb curry, while the sun still shines through the windows.

This also happens in other northern mosques in Canada. In Yellowknife, in the Northwest Territories, about 300 Muslims have a few choices for fasting, said Nazim Awan, president of the Yellowknife Islamic Centre. There are exceptions for people who are pregnant or ill. He said some people might want to fast for 23 hours. Others can follow nearby cities or follow the times of Mecca and Medina.

In recent years, many people there have followed Edmonton’s timetable, in Alberta. Awan follows Mecca’s timing. He has two young children, including a 12-year-old who recently started fasting. He wants to help his son by using a more manageable fast of about 15 hours, compared with about 18 hours in Edmonton. He said if he followed Yellowknife or Edmonton times, his son might say, “Papa, you are really insane. What are you doing?”

In Inuvik, a town 125 miles north of the Arctic Circle, it is impossible to follow the local sun at some times. About 100 Muslims there follow Edmonton’s timetable too. Ahmad Alkhalaf said they currently have 24 hours of sunlight, with no sunrise or sunset.

Alkhalaf said this plan was already used in 2001 when he moved from Toronto to Inuvik, a community of 3,500 people. His first Ramadan there was in December. At that time there was no sun, and it was dark day and night. So they used Edmonton time.

Alkhalaf said it can feel strange to follow the clock instead of the sky. He said people should break the fast at dusk, but there they sometimes eat when the sun is still up. He said it is not usual to have iftar, the meal that breaks the fast, when the sun is up.

In Inuvik, many people are Inuit. The Muslim community tries to respect Ramadan and local culture. The iftar meal includes dates and rich curries, and also local meat such as reindeer. The reindeer is prepared according to Islamic law. Alkhalaf said they make soup or curry, but use reindeer instead of beef.

In Iqaluit, as Muslims prepare to end Ramadan, some say the timing in 2016 was hard because it happened during some of the longest days of the year. This is especially true for people like Karim who follow the local sunrise and sunset. But Karim said it will be easier in future years because Ramadan follows a lunar calendar. One day it will happen in winter. In Iqaluit in winter, the sun rises and sets within a few hours each day. Karim said he will follow those hours too, and he laughed and said, “Oh yes, definitely.”
