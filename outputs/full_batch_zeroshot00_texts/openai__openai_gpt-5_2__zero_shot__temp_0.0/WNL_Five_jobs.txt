At the start of the year, many people feel unhappy about work. The next holiday feels far away, and it is dark in the morning and when you get home. If you are in a job you do not like, you might want to update your CV and look for a new job. But before you do that, try to think about things in a different way. What other jobs could you be doing instead? We asked five people with unusual jobs how much they are paid, what the worst parts are, and why they enjoy their work.

1. Dog-food taster  
The job: Tasting dog food to check it meets a premium brand’s quality standards.

What it involves: Opening sample tins from each new batch of dog (or cat) food, smelling it, and eating it. Philip Wells, the chief taster for Lily’s Kitchen pet food, says taste is an important check to make sure the ingredients are balanced in the right way. He says tasting also helps him notice small differences in how the food is cooked, especially the dry food.

Typical salary: £20,000 for an entry-level job in the quality department. Wells says £50,000 or more is possible for an experienced technical director. This person tastes products, develops new recipes, and advises the business on technical and regulatory matters.

Worst part of the job: The deadlines. Wells says he actually likes the food. Under the Animal Feed Regulations 2010, the meat used in pet food must come from animals passed as fit for human consumption. He says the firm uses “human-grade freshly prepared raw food” in its recipes. He adds that some pet foods in the market are very unpleasant, and the smell can make him feel sick when he does market research.

Job satisfaction: “No two days are ever the same.” Wells says it feels good to work on projects that help pets become happier and healthier. He also says another important member of the tasting team is Lily, the border terrier.

2. Hygiene technician  
The job: Disinfecting places that might have been exposed to bio-hazardous situations.

What it involves: Cleaning up crime scenes, road accidents, and suicides. Cleaning hoarders’ houses full of rubbish, rats, and excrement, and other things. Richard Lewis, a hygiene technician for Rentokil, says the job is about keeping people safe. He says they work in very dirty places.

Typical salary: Around £14,500 for entry level, and up to £22,000 at the top.

Worst part of the job: Cleaning up after suicides. Lewis says you get used to the job being disgusting, but the emotional side is still hard. He says you learn not to take the work home with you, and you need a sense of humour because some days are tough.

Job satisfaction: Lewis likes that the work is different every day. One day he might clean up after a dead body, and another day he might work in a prison cell or be 100 feet in the air being lowered into a silo to clean it. He also feels proud to make a dangerous place safe again, and he says it helps society.

3. Biogas engineer  
The job: Setting up biogas plants in developing countries.

What it involves: Connecting digesters to toilets to make biogas for cooking and lighting. The digesters can be filled with human excrement, animal dung, and other waste. Baburam Paudel, chief technical officer in Nepal for the charity Renewable World, says you must know the right size and shape for the mixing pit, how to create the best temperature, and where to put the biogas plant. He says you also must convince poor communities that poo can be useful, because many people do not like the idea of connecting toilets to kitchens.

Typical salary: Around £10,000 for entry level. Around £30,000 for a chief technical officer.

Worst part of the job: Paudel says it is hard to see people trying to live on very little money. He also says the job is difficult if you do not like dirty work. You must be willing to get your hands dirty during building and inspections. Anaerobic digestion, when bacteria break down waste and produce methane, smells like rotten eggs. He says it can be disgusting and mistakes are not allowed.

Job satisfaction: Paudel says it feels very good to help people earn more money and to help girls attend school by replacing the need to collect firewood. He says his work improves health and hygiene for whole communities.

4. Eel ecologist  
The job: Protecting the critically endangered European eel.

What it involves: Ecologists go into the Thames and other London rivers and marshes to check the number of eels. Sometimes they wade in up to their armpits and reach into a net with up to 20 adult eels to grab one with bare hands. Stephen Mowat, an eel conservationist and ecologist for the Zoological Society of London, says adult eels can be a metre long or more and can weigh up to 2kg. He says they are not dangerous, but they are very strong and can be slimy. They have to weigh and measure them, and the eels wriggle a lot. He says it can be hard to look professional while chasing an eel across the grass.

Worst part of the job: Mowat says eels are hard to work with, and it can be embarrassing when an eel outsmarts you. He says you must also be ready to move from one project to another. Once he had to dissect a tub of dead eels to look for parasites, and then quickly go to a meeting with government officials. He says he remembered to wash his hands.
