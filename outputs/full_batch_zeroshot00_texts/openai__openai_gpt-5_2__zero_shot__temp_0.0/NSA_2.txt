The Senate Intelligence Committee approved a bill about the National Security Agency (NSA) collecting many US phone records. The bill would make the program more open to the public, but it would still let the program continue. The bill was sponsored by Chairwoman Dianne Feinstein. It would let the NSA keep collecting phone “metadata” (information about calls, like numbers and times) from millions of Americans for renewable 90-day periods, and let the government keep the data. Some lawmakers suggested that phone companies should hold the metadata instead. The bill passed the committee by a vote of 11-4, and it can now go to a full Senate vote.

The bill says analysts can search the data if they have a “reasonable suspicion” that a person is connected to international terrorism. It also says the NSA can keep watching a foreign person who was being watched outside the US if that person comes into the US for a short time, up to 72 hours.

The bill challenges a different bill by Senator Patrick Leahy that would stop collecting US phone records inside the country. Mark Udall, a leading member of the committee, opposed Feinstein’s bill. He said it does not change enough. Udall said the NSA’s surveillance of Americans’ private information does not respect constitutional values and needs major reform, not small changes.

Another committee Democrat, Ron Wyden, said the bill keeps “business as usual” and is not meaningful reform. Feinstein defended the bulk collection program, but said public trust needs to be rebuilt. She said the program is legal and watched by Congress and courts, and she believes it helps national security. But she also said more should be done to increase transparency and public support for privacy protections.

Feinstein said the bill would improve transparency and oversight in several ways. It would require a public report each year with the total number of searches of the phone metadata database and how often the program leads to an FBI investigation or a probable cause order. It would require the foreign intelligence surveillance court to limit how many people at the NSA can approve or search the call-records database. It would create criminal penalties of up to ten years in prison for intentionally and illegally accessing data collected under the Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act (FISA). It would require the FISA court to limit how many contacts an analyst can get back from a search of bulk communication records.

After the hearing, Feinstein strongly supported the NSA’s main domestic program. She said there is a big misunderstanding about the program and that it is vital for protecting the country.

Independent legal experts also raised concerns. They said this could lead to a fight with the USA Freedom Act, a bill by Leahy and Jim Sensenbrenner that would ban bulk collection of Americans’ phone records. Elizabeth Goitein of the Brennan Center for Justice said the two bills show two different ideas about the relationship between law-abiding Americans and the national security state. She said the key question is whether the government should have a reason to suspect wrongdoing before collecting very personal information. She said Leahy and Sensenbrenner say yes, and Feinstein says no.

Wyden said recent news about the NSA spying on foreign leaders took attention away from the bigger issue of mass domestic surveillance in the US. He said collecting information about foreign leaders has long been understood and affects foreign policy. But he said his top priority is ending mass digital surveillance of millions and millions of law-abiding Americans.

Feinstein unexpectedly said she was “totally opposed” to spying on foreign leaders like German Chancellor Angela Merkel. But she has strongly supported the NSA’s bulk collection of Americans’ phone records.

Wyden said Americans are making it clear that they never agreed to give up constitutional liberties just for the appearance of security. He said the fight will continue and it will take a long time.

In a separate issue, Feinstein said James Clapper, the Director of National Intelligence, agreed to give her a written statement about a Washington Post story. The story said the NSA intercepted data while it was being sent between Google and Yahoo data centers. Feinstein said she would not decide what she thinks about the story until she sees Clapper’s response. Her strong support for collecting domestic phone records suggests she is not ready to expand her criticism of the NSA beyond opposing spying on allied foreign leaders.

Wyden did not comment on the Washington Post report about Google and Yahoo. But the senators suggested it could affect the privacy of Americans’ communications. Wyden said that in the past, countries had separate communication systems, but now communication is global. He said this makes spying on foreigners harder and can also affect US citizens.
