Early in the day, police carried out new dawn raids at the Baur Au Lac hotel in Zurich. Later that day, 16 football officials were charged in the United States with corruption crimes. This included five current or former members of FIFA’s executive committee. They included the former Brazilian federation chief Ricardo Teixeira and his replacement, Marco Polo Del Nero, who recently stepped down from the FIFA executive committee.

The 16 people were accused by the US Department of Justice of fraud and other crimes. The new charges were written in a new 240-page document that replaced the earlier one from May 2015. The total number of defendants charged by the US became 27. The document also mentioned 24 unnamed “co-conspirators,” including former FIFA executive committee members. US Attorney General Loretta Lynch said the betrayal of trust was outrageous and the corruption described was completely unacceptable.

That same day, FIFA’s executive committee had hoped to show new reforms during an ongoing corruption crisis. Instead, Swiss police led away Juan Ángel Napout, president of the South American football confederation, and Alfredo Hawit, head of the North and Central American and Caribbean body. Hawit replaced Jeffrey Webb in May 2015 after Webb was arrested in the US operation. That operation pushed FIFA into crisis and led to the downfall of Sepp Blatter. Webb’s predecessor, Jack Warner, was also seized in May.

The Swiss Federal Office of Justice said the arrested men were being held while waiting for extradition. It said the US suspects them of taking bribes worth millions of dollars. Webb and Luis Bedoya, a former FIFA executive committee member from Colombia, were among those who pleaded guilty in the US. Lynch said that eight people had pleaded guilty since May, and five of them were not named in the original charges.

So far, 11 current and former members of FIFA’s executive committee have been charged in the investigation. The investigation says there were $200m in bribes. Most were kickbacks from TV and marketing contracts, and some were linked to FIFA development programmes. The last three presidents of the regional bodies CONCACAF and Conmebol have all been charged.

Lynch warned that people still hiding should not think they can avoid the investigation. Teixeira, who was once the son-in-law of former FIFA president João Havelange, was charged along with Del Nero and José Maria Marin, who was charged in May.

In May 2015, 14 men were charged, and four more guilty pleas were entered. A few days later, Blatter won a fifth term as president, but later agreed to step down as the crisis grew. He was then suspended, along with UEFA President Michel Platini, over an alleged £1.3m “disloyal payment” to Platini. Both men could face life bans when their case is heard by the FIFA ethics committee in December if they are found guilty.

Others charged on Thursday included Rafael Salguero from Guatemala, who left the executive committee in May; Eduardo Deluca, a former Secretary General of the South American Confederation; Manuel Burga, Peru’s former football federation president; and Carlos Chaves, Bolivia’s football president, who was already in jail in his own country.

Lynch said the Department of Justice is committed to ending the corruption described in international football leadership. She said this is because of the size of the schemes, how wide the operation was, and because this behaviour goes against international principles.

Acting FIFA President Issa Hayatou did not comment on the details of the arrests. But he said neither he nor the organization was corrupt. Speaking to the media for the first time since becoming acting president in September 2015, when Blatter was suspended, Hayatou said the crisis was caused by only a few people. “FIFA is not corrupt. We have individuals that have shown negative behaviour. Do not generalize the situation,” said Hayatou, who has been president of the Confederation of African Football for more than 25 years. He said many people have been in FIFA for 20 or 30 years and have not been accused of anything.
