For 85 years, Pluto looked like a small grey blur on school maps. But on 15 July, Pluto was seen clearly for the first time. The new pictures showed big mountain ranges made of solid water ice, as large as the Alps or the Rockies.

These special pictures of Pluto and its big moon, Charon, were sent about 4bn miles back to Earth from the New Horizons spacecraft. This was the high point of a mission that has been running quietly for almost ten years.

Alan Stern, the mission’s main scientist, said the pictures were a “home run” for the team. He said the results were amazing and that Pluto and Charon were “mind-blowing.”

One big surprise was that there are mountains in the Kuiper belt. The Kuiper belt is the solar system’s “third zone,” where Pluto sits with about 100,000 smaller icy objects. John Spencer, a mission scientist, said the mountains seem to be about 11,000ft high and several hundred miles wide.

A close picture of one edge of Pluto showed no craters at all. This suggests the surface was covered over not long ago by geological activity. This activity could include geysers that shoot ice into the air, or cryo-volcanoes that erupt with ice instead of melted rock.

At a NASA press conference, people talked about Pluto’s old status as the ninth planet. Pluto was changed to a dwarf planet in 2006. NASA spokesman Dwayne Brown said they had brought a blurry point of light into focus.

Stern said these pictures were only the start of what scientists will learn in the next year. Scientists think the mountains are made from water ice, with only a thin top layer of “exotic” ices: methane and nitrogen. Spencer said you cannot make mountains from methane and nitrogen.

Spencer said water ice is strong enough to hold up large mountains. The methane and nitrogen are just a coating. The mountains on Pluto may have formed no more than 100m years ago, which is very recent compared to the 4.56bn-year-old solar system. The area seen up close is about 1% of Pluto’s surface, and it may still be geologically active. These are the first pictures to show ice mountains outside the moons of giant planets. Scientists now wonder what process made this mountain land.

The mountain shapes, and the smooth surface, suggest that activity is filling in holes made by asteroid hits. Scientists think this “paving” could come from heat inside Pluto that softens rock and ice, or from snowfalls that cover the ground. The pictures are so clear that, if the spacecraft were flying over London, we could see the runways at Heathrow airport.

Pluto is very far away, about 5bn km. Because of this distance, New Horizons needs hours to send just one picture back to Earth. It will take 16 months to send all the data from the fly-by. The team also said the heart-shaped area on Pluto will be called the Tombaugh Regio, to honour Clyde Tombaugh, who discovered Pluto in 1930.

The new view of Charon shows many different kinds of land. An area of cliffs and long dips that stretches about 1,000km suggests big cracks in Charon’s surface. This could also be caused by geological activity. The picture also shows a huge canyon that may be 7 to 9km deep.

Cathy Olkin, a mission scientist, said the new Charon image greatly excited the team. She said there is a lot of science in just this one picture. Scientists think Pluto is about two thirds rock, covered in a lot of ice, with surface temperatures near minus 230C. The mission cost £460m. As New Horizons continues into the Kuiper belt, scientists hope it will help them learn about the ancient solar system and how planets formed, and it may help explain how Earth formed.

Andrew Coates, head of planetary science at the Mullard Space Science Laboratory, said Kuiper belt objects are the building blocks of the outer solar system. He said they are very cold, like a deep freeze, which helps keep solar system history.

In August 2015, mission scientists will pick which of two objects to visit next. NASA says the spacecraft may keep recording and sending data until the mid-2030s. After that, its plutonium power will run out. It will shut down and drift toward the edge of the solar system and into deep space.

New Horizons also watched smaller parts of the Pluto system. Pluto has four other moons: Nix, Hydra, Styx and Kerberos. A new early picture of Hydra showed its uneven shape and its size, about 43 by 33km. John Grunsfeld, from NASA’s Science Mission Directorate, said New Horizons is a real mission of exploration and shows why basic scientific research matters.

He said the mission had nine years to build hopes about what it would see near Pluto and Charon. Now scientists have the first sample of the scientific “treasure” collected at the closest time, and it is even better than they expected.

The observations also suggest Hydra’s surface is probably covered with water ice. Future pictures will give more clues about how this moon, and the other moon, formed billions of years ago.
