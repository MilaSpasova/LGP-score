After a vote in the European Parliament, new EU rules on tobacco are closer.

The plan includes big health warnings on cigarette packs and a ban on menthol cigarettes across the EU. Menthol and other flavours will be banned from 2022.

But MEPs decided that most electronic cigarettes (e-cigarettes) do not need to be regulated like medicines. This was bad news for the UK government.

Health officials and the e-cigarette industry in Britain want to understand what the vote means. For example, they want to know if e-cigarette companies will face the same bans on sponsorship and promotion at sports events as tobacco companies.

The Department of Health said it would not comment on advertising until it had studied the decision. It said it was pleased about tougher action on tobacco, including a ban on flavoured cigarettes and stricter health warnings on the front of packs. But it said it was disappointed that the plan to regulate nicotine-containing products (NCPs), including e-cigarettes, as medicines was rejected. The Department of Health said it still believes these products should be treated as medicines.

The Department of Health also said smoking levels in England are at their lowest since records began: 19.5%. It said it wants to reduce smoking even more.

The UK e-cigarette industry mostly welcomed the vote. It said it was already talking with the Advertising Standards Authority. It also said it would not be sensible to ban all advertising.

MEPs said e-cigarettes should only be treated as medical products if companies claim they can stop people from smoking tobacco. The government’s main medicines regulator criticized this decision.

By 2014, an estimated 1.3 million people in Britain were expected to use these products. Some people want e-cigarettes to be treated like nicotine gum, patches, and mouth sprays that help smokers quit. But the industry says the licensing process would be expensive and could push alternatives to tobacco off the shelves.

MEPs voted for health warnings to cover 65% of each cigarette pack, not 75% as first suggested. Right now, warnings cover at least 30% on the front and 40% on the back. The UK government has delayed deciding whether to copy Australia’s standardized packaging, until there is evidence it reduces tobacco use.

This was the first reading of a draft EU tobacco directive. It could become law in 2014. Next, there will be negotiations with the EU Council of Ministers.

The Medicines and Healthcare Products Regulatory Authority had asked manufacturers to choose voluntary regulation in June 2013. It still hopes regulation will become compulsory across Europe. A spokesman said the law-making process is not finished, and the UK still believes regulating NCPs as medicines is best for public health.

Linda McAvan, a Labour MEP for Yorkshire and the Humber, said children start smoking, not adults. She said World Health Organization figures show worrying increases in young smokers in some EU countries. She said tobacco companies should not be allowed to target young people with gimmicky products, and packs should have strong warnings.

Martin Callanan, a Conservative MEP for North East England, said forcing e-cigarettes off the shelves would be crazy. He said they have helped many people stop smoking more harmful cigarettes, and some MEPs wanted to make e-cigarettes harder to make than tobacco.

Katherine Devlin, president of ECITA, said the decision not to support medicines regulation was very important, and meant it was now off the table.

British American Tobacco said the larger health warnings go beyond what is needed to inform consumers about health risks. It also said banning menthol cigarettes would increase demand for black-market goods.
