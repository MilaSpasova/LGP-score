A thick layer of bird droppings covers a shiny railing at one of Britain’s most expensive homes. Pigeon bones lie near broken mirrors, and water runs through broken parts of the ceiling. This home is called The Towers. It is a £30m house on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London. Its serious damage was kept secret until now.

The Towers is one of ten big houses in the middle of The Bishops Avenue. This street is a key part of London’s rising property market. These houses have been almost empty since they were bought about 25 years ago. People believe they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. The Greek-style columns are breaking. Swimming pools with mosaic tiles are full of broken pieces. Nature has taken over, and owls have moved in.

This sad picture is seen all along the street. Lloyds Bank says The Bishops Avenue is the second most expensive street in Britain. While more people struggle to buy a home in London, house prices rise 11.2% each year. On the most expensive part of the avenue, 16 big houses are empty. Many sit behind locked gates. Windows are covered with steel bars, and the gardens are overgrown. Guard dogs patrol the grounds.

Across the street is another empty house worth £18m, with broken windows and walls covered in anti-climb paint. Another house has metal bars on the windows and was sold for £20m.

Still, prices keep rising. Dryades, once owned by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007 and is believed to be worth about £30m today. Heath Lodge, where fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou was murdered in 1984 by a silver bullet, is worth £13m today. It was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

For people who struggle to pay for housing in one of the world’s most expensive cities, these empty, falling-apart houses can feel upsetting. A security guard on the avenue said it was frustrating to see so much space—enough for dozens of people—being wasted and breaking down.

In the past, royals with oil wealth from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia were among the first to come to this curving road near Hampstead Heath. Iranians moved here after the fall of the shah. Now, Chinese buyers are coming after Russians and Kazakhs. Estate agents tell them the address is as famous as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two houses were for sale for £65m and £38m, offering Italian marble, lifts with leather padding, and luxury panic rooms.

But on the land of the empty houses, stone fountains are falling apart and lawns have turned into muddy bogs. Inside one house, water drips through a large crystal chandelier onto a thick carpet that is rotting under parts of a collapsed ceiling. Moss grows through broken bricks. Broken mirror tiles are scattered in a bathroom. A swimming pool has about a foot of dirty, salty water and flowers growing through the tiles. Wooden pieces on the sauna are pushing outward.

The most dramatic ruin is The Towers. It is a grand house among many trees, including hornbeams, oaks, and limes. Pigeons fill its large halls. Water pours down through three floors, and the walls have turned bright green with algae. The water drops into a huge, empty basement swimming pool. Wooden crates marked “bullet-proof glass” show the security fears of the past owners.

Today, very few people live on The Bishops Avenue all the time. A security guard outside one house said the owners were not there. Another guard, outside Royal Mansion, would not say if anyone was home. A staff member at another house warned the Guardian about the guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare mogul with personal wealth of £45m, said he is one of the few full-time residents.

He said, “It’s not a neighbourly place, where you can chat over the fence. To be honest, I have never seen what my neighbours look like. Next door, a Saudi princess spent £35m on a new house and I’ve never seen her. There are about three houses that are lived in 24/7 and half of the properties are occupied three to six months a year. The other half, who knows if they come or not?”

These broken, multi-million-pound houses show a property culture where the world’s richest people treat British homes as investments. One Hyde Park, an apartment block in Knightsbridge, is another example. More than half of its flats are registered with the council as empty or second homes.

Even so, people on the avenue talk about building £5m apartments instead of £50m mansions to bring residents back.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, plans to rebuild one valuable site on the avenue into 20 apartments. They would have a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

He said, “If you build a big house and try and sell for £30m to £40m, it won’t sell. Locals won’t buy and so you have to bring in overseas buyers.”

But using the empty homes to help solve the housing crisis still seems far away. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some of the run-down homes could become affordable housing. He said the land price would be too high.

He said, “Very wealthy people own property there. Sometimes they live in them and sometimes they don’t.”
