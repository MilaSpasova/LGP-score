More and more people are looking for dark places to see the night sky. On Black Fell in Northumberland, near the Kielder Water reservoir, people wait in a car park beside a strange wooden building. This is Kielder Observatory. It is part of Britain’s new “astrotourism” industry, where people travel to look at stars. Only a small number of people can come each night, so many people who applied did not get in.

Inside the observatory, the lights are low and there is a woodburner. The founder and lead astronomer, Gary Fildes, talks to staff and volunteers. They hope to see the northern lights, but Fildes does not think they will. Instead, they plan to use strong telescopes to look at Jupiter and Venus. Later they will look for stars like Capella and Betelgeuse. They also hope to see the International Space Station.

Fildes tells his team that the night should be interactive, entertaining, and inspiring. He plays music by Pink Floyd, the Jam, and the Pogues. He says that by 9.30 the sky will look amazing.

Fildes, 49, is an important person in the UK’s growing astrotourism industry. A key moment for Northumberland came in 2013. The national park that includes Hadrian’s Wall, plus Kielder Water and Forest Park, about 1,500 sq km, was given Dark Sky Park status. It was the only one in England. Dark Sky Parks are rare. A 2013 Star Count showed that only 5% of people in the UK can see more than 31 stars on a good night.

The International Dark-Sky Association (IDA), based in Tucson, Arizona, gives this status only to places that work hard to reduce light pollution. They must also show that their skies are dark enough. In Northumberland Dark Sky Park, it is so dark that Venus can cast a shadow on the Earth.

Duncan Wise, a visitor development officer for the Northumberland National Park Authority, helped lead the work to get dark-sky status. The Council for the Protection of Rural England said it was one of Britain’s most peaceful places. Wise said people often look only at the land up to the horizon, but they should also think about the sky above it. Wise and others spent years preparing their application to the IDA. They took many light readings and worked with local councils, park groups, and community groups. They created an outdoor lighting plan that affects how new buildings are made in the area.

Their work has paid off. Many of the 1.5 million people who visit Northumberland each year now know about its Dark Sky status. A man at a car-hire firm in Newcastle says many people now come to see the sky, especially in autumn and winter when nights are darkest. This helps B&Bs because they can get business all year. Some hotels give guests night-vision torches and put deckchairs outside at night. Hotels can earn a badge to show they are “Dark Sky Friendly.”

Wise says Northumberland should do more to use this special resource. He thinks the area needs a couple more observatories so visitors are more likely to see what they came for. A £14m national landscape discovery centre, which he calls the north’s answer to the Eden Project, will include an observatory when it is finished in a couple of years.

Fildes has big plans too. He wants to build Britain’s first “astrovillage.” It would include the largest public observatory in the world, a 100-seat auditorium, a 100-seat planetarium, a one-metre aperture telescope, and radiomagnetic and solar telescopes. The project would also have a hotel. It would bring in 100,000 people each year, four times the number who can use the observatory now. Fildes does not say much about who is paying for it, but he believes it will be real by 2018.

Northumberland also has competition. Galloway Forest Park in Scotland has Dark Sky Park status too. Exmoor became Europe’s first International Dark Sky Reserve in 2011, and local businesses there now offer stargazing breaks and safaris. The UK also competes with northern Chile, which has more than a dozen tourist observatories and some of the clearest skies in the world. Teide National Park in Tenerife is also becoming a major place for astrotourism.

Why do more people want to look up at the sky? The media has helped. TV presenters like Brian Cox have brought in a new group of stargazers. Wise says Brian Cox has made astronomy easier for everyone, not just professors with old brass telescopes. Technology has helped too. Apps like Stellarium can turn smartphones into small planetariums. In the end, Fildes thinks people are starting to value what is above them. He says that if you had to build a visitor attraction from nothing, nothing could be better than the universe.
