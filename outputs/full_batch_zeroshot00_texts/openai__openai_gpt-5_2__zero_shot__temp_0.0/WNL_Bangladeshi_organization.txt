The small room is made to look like a classroom. The walls are corrugated iron. There are learning posters on the walls, with pictures of alphabet letters and a map of Bangladesh.

But it is hard to study there. There is always loud hammering. There are also chemicals in the air that make your throat hurt and your eyes sting. Even so, the children in this three-square-metre room are lucky. They do not have to work in the factories across the street.

For 14 years, SOHAY, a grassroots nongovernmental organization (NGO), has worked in slums in Dhaka to help child workers go to school. It is funded by the Global Fund for Children and Comic Relief. SOHAY helps children who work in dangerous places, like aluminium and plastic factories, and tanneries.

This classroom is one of 23 urban development centres that SOHAY has set up in the city. The centres teach children and help them get ready for primary school. They help children catch up on lessons they missed. After the children start primary school, they can come to the centres to do homework and get help from other children.

Alamin is ten. He used to work in a plastic factory, but now he goes to one of the centres. His father sells things on the street. His mother works part time in other people’s homes. His family is happy he is in school now and not doing dangerous work. His friend Rabi says he wants to forget his factory past. “I like school,” he says.

SOHAY’s programme manager, Mohammed Abdullah al-Mamun, says the centres try to make the community support education and change how people think about children. SOHAY also holds sessions for parents and employers to discourage child labour. It also offers skills training to help families earn more money.

Mamun says it is very hard to move working children into formal school. Their bodies and minds are not like other children’s, because of the work they did. After they stop working, they may find it hard to make friends and get used to school. It is also hard to make sure they stay in school, because many drop out. Mamun says schools need to be more sensitive and caring toward these children.

Zhorna Akter Sumayya is seven. She has two older brothers who work. One works at a restaurant, and one works at a local club. After learning at a SOHAY centre, she now goes to a state primary school. Her family lives in a slum. Her parents need the money her sons bring home to survive. Her father works in a rickshaw garage, and her mother is a domestic worker. But they really wanted their daughter to go to school.

In 2015, SOHAY helped 1,540 children leave dangerous work and helped 2,125 vulnerable children go to school. These vulnerable children were at risk of starting work. About 780 more children were preparing to start school in 2017. SOHAY also helped 635 children working in dangerous conditions learn about their rights under Bangladesh’s 2010 child labour elimination code of conduct. That policy wanted to end all child labour by 2015, but the goal was not reached.

Bangladesh’s Labour Law of 2006 bans children under 14 from working. But UNICEF says 4.7 million children under 14 work in the informal sector. UNICEF also says 1.3 million children aged five to 17 work in hazardous industries.

Sadia Nasrin runs Sonjag, another grassroots NGO in Dhaka. She says it was hard to get children into school because families did not get money to replace the children’s work time. To solve this, Sonjag worked closely with slum communities where the children live.

Sonjag talked with community members about why school is important. It chose community volunteers who wanted to change children’s lives. It formed groups with social workers, community leaders, mothers, young volunteers, and local government.

Nasrin says these groups are very important. They encourage employers to let children leave work for two or three hours each day to go to school. They also work to make the workplace safer for children. She says the community people are the real change-makers.

Nasrin also says people in slums face eviction, police raids, and being forced to move. She says the national plan of action for children does not recognize the needs of street children. She also says the laws are limited.

When children miss starting school at age five, there is little time to help them. If they get too old, it becomes very hard for schools to accept them. Mamun says many children then pass their time without education and wait until they start dangerous work. He says SOHAY is working to stop the flow of children into child labour.
