Virunga National Park is in DR Congo. It is home to rare mountain gorillas. A WWF report says the park could bring DR Congo about $400m a year from tourism, hydropower, and carbon credits.

But WWF warns that if oil is taken from the park, it could cause serious pollution and long-lasting conflict in a region that is already unstable. Virunga is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and lies on the equator.

SOCO International, a British company, is the only company trying to explore for oil inside Virunga. SOCO says its work would stay in one area called Block V and would not harm the gorillas.

SOCO Chairman Rui de Sousa said SOCO understands how important the park is for nature. He said oil companies still play a central role in the world’s energy supply. He also said a successful oil project could improve the economy and social well-being of a whole country. He added that the park has been getting worse for many years and has fallen below the standards for a World Heritage Site, and that development could help reverse this.

Raymond Lumbuenamo, WWF’s country director in DR Congo, said security in and around the park would get worse if SOCO continues. He said the security situation is already bad. The UN is involved with fighting units, and the M23 rebel force is inside the park. He said oil would increase conflict and attract sabotage. He warned the park could become like the Niger Delta.

Lumbuenamo also said the population there is very dense, with over 350 people per square kilometre. He said if land is taken for oil, it puts more pressure on the rest of the land. He said oil would not create many jobs, but many people would still move in looking for work.

WWF also fears the area has earthquakes, and a volcano in the park could erupt again. This could damage oil equipment and cause oil spills in the lakes. Lumbuenamo said Virunga’s natural resources should benefit the Congolese people, not foreign oil prospectors. He said the country’s future depends on sustainable development. He said, "Once you have started drilling for oil, there’s no turning back."

Lumbuenamo said the gorillas are safe right now, but he also said it is unlikely the park can earn the full $400m a year at present. He said Virunga used to be peaceful and could be again, but security is bad now and the UN is involved with fighting units.

The WWF report says the park’s ecosystems could support hydropower, fishing, and ecotourism. It also says the park helps provide safe water, regulate climate, and prevent soil erosion.

Virunga is Africa’s oldest and most diverse park. It has over 3,000 kinds of animals. But many very poor people now live there, including people who fled after the Rwanda massacre in 1994.

The report says the park could support about 45,000 permanent jobs. It also says people around the world gain value from knowing the park is well managed and safe for future generations. The report says plans to explore for oil put Virunga’s value at risk.

Lasse Gustavsson, executive director of WWF International, said WWF will not stop until Virunga is safe from what he called a possible environmental disaster. He said, "Virunga has snow fields and lava fields, but it should not have oil fields."

The UNESCO World Heritage Committee called for all Virunga oil permits to be cancelled. It asked Total SA and SOCO International plc not to explore in World Heritage Sites. Total has agreed to respect Virunga’s current boundary. This leaves UK-based SOCO as the only oil company planning to explore inside the park.
