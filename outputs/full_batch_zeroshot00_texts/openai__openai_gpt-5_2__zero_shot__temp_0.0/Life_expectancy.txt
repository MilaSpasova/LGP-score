A girl born today in the UK will live to almost 82 years old on average. Her brother will live to about 78. In Andorra, they would live longer (85 and 79). In the US, they would live a little less (81 and 76). In the Central African Republic, they would live much less (49 and 44).

In most places in the world, people are living longer. A few countries, such as Lesotho, are different because HIV and violence have hurt people’s lives. The best news is that young children are much less likely to die than they were 40 years ago. Deaths of children under five dropped by almost 60%, from 16.4 million in 1970 to 6.8 million in 2010.

This is why a large project led by the Institute of Health Metrics and Evaluation (IHME) in Seattle is important. Over five years, nearly 500 researchers worked to measure the world’s “burden of disease.” If we know how many children die and why, the world can better use money and effort to keep them alive. The database can help world groups and governments make better health plans, like focusing more on diet, dealing with alcohol problems, and continuing work against HIV in Africa.

The Lancet published seven papers from this project. The work is big and some people disagree with it. IHME used bold methods. In places without death records or medical records, they used “verbal autopsies,” which means they interview the family to decide the cause of death.

One surprising result was about malaria. In 2012, IHME said 1.2 million people die from malaria each year. This was about twice as many as people thought before. The biggest increase was in adult deaths. Many people have believed malaria mostly kills children under five.

Dr Christopher Murray, the IHME Director, said doctors are usually taught that adults in malaria areas become partly immune. He said they first agreed with that view, but later changed as they used more real-world evidence. African doctors often write in hospital records that many adults die of malaria. But Murray said a fever could be caused by something else. The results have led to more studies.

Margaret Chan, the Director General of the World Health Organization (WHO), officially welcomed the IHME study. But some WHO staff were careful. Colin Mathers, a senior scientist, said they must be very careful about whether the numbers are correct and should wait for strong evidence. Dr Ties Boerma said the WHO cannot quickly accept every new academic paper that gives different numbers. Mathers also said that IHME’s new kinds of analysis are stimulating. Murray said one main message was that the top causes of death are changing very fast, faster than expected.

Because people are having fewer children and living longer, the average age of the world’s population rose in one decade, from 26 to almost 30. This change has been very strong in Latin America. Murray said that in places like Brazil, the change is so fast that many institutions are not ready for it.

Another theme is that, outside Africa, many countries are moving away from infectious diseases and common causes of mother and baby deaths. More people now die from “lifestyle” diseases like heart disease, stroke, diabetes, and cancer. Some of these diseases can be linked to genes. Murray said this change is clear in Latin America, the Middle East, and south-east and south Asia.

A third big finding surprised the researchers. Many people are living longer but not living healthier, and disability is very common. Murray said the main causes of disability are not the same as the main causes of death. Common causes of disability include anxiety and depression, arthritis and lower back pain, anaemia, sight and hearing loss, and skin disease. There is also substance abuse. Murray said these problems are not decreasing over time, and there has been no progress in reducing them.
