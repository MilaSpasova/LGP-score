In Iceland, a girl can have many names, like Aagot, Arney, Ásfríður, Baldey, Bebba, Brá, Dögg, Dimmblá, Etna, Eybjört, Frigg, Glódís, Hörn, Ingunn, Jórlaug, Obba, Sigurfljóð, Úranía, or Vagna. But a girl in Iceland cannot be called Harriet.

Tristan Cardew said the situation is silly. He and his Icelandic wife, Kristin, are fighting a decision by the National Registry in Reykjavik. The office will not renew their 10-year-old daughter Harriet’s passport because it does not accept her first name.

The office also does not accept the name of Harriet’s 12-year-old brother, Duncan. Until now, the children traveled with passports that called them Stúlka and Drengur Cardew. These words mean Girl and Boy Cardew. Cardew said the authorities are now following the law closely. The law says no official document will be given to people who do not have an approved Icelandic name.

Because of this problem, the family from Kópavogur almost missed their holiday in France. They asked the British embassy for an emergency UK passport. This should now let them leave.

Names are very important in Iceland. The country has about 320,000 people. The phone book lists people by first name. This is because most Icelandic last names show who your father (or mother) is. For example, Jón Einarsson’s children might be Ólafur Jónsson and Sigríður Jónsdóttir.

The law says the names of children born in Iceland must be sent to the National Registry within six months. This is required unless both parents are foreign. If a name is not on a list of 1,853 female names and 1,712 male names, the parents must ask the Icelandic Naming Committee to approve it.

About 5,000 children are born in Iceland each year. The committee gets about 100 requests and rejects about half. A 1996 law tries to protect the Icelandic language. The rules say names must work with Icelandic grammar endings, must not hurt the language structure, and must follow normal Icelandic spelling rules.

In practice, this means some names are not allowed. Names with letters not in Iceland’s 32-letter alphabet, like “c”, are not accepted. Also, names must be able to change form for Icelandic grammar cases. Cardew said this was the problem with Harriet.

Many people have criticized these naming laws. In 2013, a person named Blær (“Light Breeze”) Bjarkardóttir Rúnarsdottir won the right to use that name officially instead of “Girl.” A court said it was against the Icelandic constitution to deny her name. The former mayor of Reykjavik, Jón Gnarr, also said the naming law is unfair, stupid, and against creativity.

The Cardew family could fix Harriet’s problem by giving her an Icelandic middle name. But Cardew said it is too late and too silly. He asked, “Are they saying they don’t want us here?”
