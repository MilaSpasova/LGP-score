Kenton Cool usually likes to talk, but now he can hardly speak. Climbing high mountains has made his voice rough. He is in Kathmandu, the capital city of Nepal. He flew down from Everest base camp that morning.

Cool is thinking about three big climbs he finished last weekend. Early on Saturday, he reached the top of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of three main peaks in the Everest “horseshoe.” This horseshoe is around an icy valley called the Western Cwm.

On the same day, he climbed to the top of Everest. He reached the summit in total darkness early on Sunday. Then he and his climbing partner continued. On Monday morning, they reached the top of Lhotse, the third peak.

Cool says it was a rare chance. For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. He says this does not remove the hard physical work he did. But he thinks the next person will set a higher goal and do it without ropes or bottled oxygen.

Sixty years after Everest was first climbed, many stories look back to Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. They talk about an earlier time, before today’s business tourism and damage to the environment. The writer asks Cool to look ahead and imagine what top climbers might do 60 years from now.

Cool says he does not like to think about it, but he talks about the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. Steck left the mountain in April after what Cool calls “an altercation” with a group of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck planned a very hard climb: he wanted to climb Everest’s west ridge, first climbed in 1963, then go down to the South Col, and then climb Lhotse right away on a new route. He planned to do this without fixed ropes. Cool says Steck trained extremely hard. He says Steck was special, with great skills and very strong fitness. Cool says it would have been amazing to see what Steck could do.

Tourism is another issue. One sign of change is a helicopter rescue by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro’s angry language helped cause the conflict at Camp 2. On Tuesday, Moro flew a powerful helicopter back to Everest. He rescued a very sick climber at 7,800 metres.

This was the highest rescue ever done on Everest. It also shows that helicopter flights have increased very fast in recent years. By 2073, the mountain could even have a helipad on the South Col. Tourists could arrive while using bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing how people can rescue climbers and also many trekkers who go as far as base camp.

Mountain geographer and environmentalist Alton Byers says it is not clear if the Everest region can handle fast-growing tourism. He says climate change and tourism together are causing new problems for the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers are shrinking, and some are disappearing. He says this is already having a big effect. He says people everywhere talk about having less water. There is less water for farming and less water for the new lodges being built.

Byers says that in the Sherpa town of Namche Bazaar, workers are building a new five-mile pipeline to bring in water. This is to meet the growing tourist demand for showers and flush toilets. The local stream has been polluted by human waste. It also does not have enough water during the busy season, when the town is packed. Byers says every village digs a pit just outside the houses for garbage. He says Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world. At base camp, human waste is now handled well and taken away in plastic barrels. But Byers says the barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley, and it could leak into the region’s streams and rivers.

Byers says these problems can be fixed, but people must take them seriously. He says one climber can spend $85,000 to climb Everest, and that is fine. But he says people must also focus on other needs. He says for half a million dollars a year, most of these problems could be solved.

Byers says climate change is harder. He works with local conservation groups to plan for climate change. They often look for new water sources or collect rainwater. He says fast-growing glacial lakes are always dangerous. Some lakes might burst and flood the Sherpa homeland. He says there will be a time when people will have to move away.

Changing weather also affects tourism. More clouds during times that are usually clear are closing Lukla Airport more often. Lukla Airport is the main entrance to the Everest region. A new road for 4x4 vehicles is being built to Lukla to keep tourists and money coming. But Byers worries that Nepal is building roads cheaply and too fast. He says this can cause serious soil erosion and landslides.

Byers says everyone knows Everest. He calls it “the canary in the coalmine” that everyone understands. He says it is the best place to study how to deal with problems like climate change and tourism.
