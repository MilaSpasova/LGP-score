In 2010, the old city in Srinagar was very dangerous. Police only went there wearing body armour. Violent separatists wanted an independent Kashmir. Uprisings happened there, and more than 100 people died.

Now things look very different. Tourists walk in the same streets and line up for food like mutton tikka and rogan josh. The Nowhatta mosque, where young people once met after Friday prayers to throw stones at security forces, will be part of an official walking tour about history, crafts, and markets. Near Dal Lake, houseboats are booked months ahead. In the gardens by the lake, visitors take photos. Until the snow melted, nearby ski resorts were also full of rich Russians.

In 2002, only a little over 27,000 tourists visited the Kashmir Valley. Many were scared because of an anti-Indian insurgency that has killed up to 70,000 people. In 2012 so far, almost one million holidaymakers have visited, including more than 23,000 from outside India. But fewer than 150 Britons came. One reason is that the UK’s Foreign Office still gives strong warnings about travel there, and it lists recent security incidents.

Omar Abdullah, the Chief Minister of Jammu and Kashmir, was born in the UK. He asked the British High Commission in Delhi to relax the travel advice, but it did not change. Abdullah said this makes people’s insurance invalid when they visit. The last well-known case of foreign tourists being murdered in J&K was in 1995. Then, six westerners, including two Britons, were kidnapped by Al-Faran, a Kashmiri militant Islamist group. Only one person escaped alive.

Abdullah said British citizens have been killed in other places too, but people still travel there. He mentioned 9/11 in New York, and also Spain and Bali. He also said Indians have died in London, but Indians still travel to London. He said there is still a chance al-Qaida could do something, but that is not a reason to single out Jammu and Kashmir, or Srinagar, as unsafe.

Germany changed its travel advice in 2011. It said foreigners are usually not direct targets in clashes. Some people thought this was brave, because it happened less than a year after the 2010 troubles ended. On 15 August, India celebrated 65 years of independence. In the past, this day could be dangerous in a state where many people do not feel part of India. This time, the celebrations had no trouble.

Abdullah says tourists are safe if they take normal precautions. He says they should not go trekking near the border that separates the Indian- and Pakistani-controlled parts of J&K. Syed Ali Shah Geelani, a leader who supports Kashmiri independence, disagrees with Abdullah about many things. But they agree on tourism. At the start of summer, Geelani wrote to tourists and pilgrims. He said that no matter their faith, language, or region, they are connected by humanity. He said visitors are honoured guests, and protecting guests is a duty.

Some visitors may feel unsure about enjoying a place where many people have high anxiety and mental health problems. But locals in Srinagar said they support tourism. Amjid Gulzar, 26, said Abdullah should look for truth and reconciliation and also bring foreign visitors. He said without tourism, the economy would be in chaos. He welcomed the million tourists this year, but said Kashmir should do more to attract big-spending visitors, especially foreigners.

Gulzar said Kashmir needs better infrastructure, better roads, and reliable electricity. He said tourists also need more to do in the evening. He said there is not even one cinema in the city, and not enough to do after dark.

Some tourists might wonder if they will feel welcome. In June, a local Islamic group suggested a “dress code” for foreign tourists. Abdullah said tourists are not expected to cover their faces. He said the issue was probably short shorts and sleeveless vests. He said the main idea was to respect the local culture and dress appropriately.

Abdullah said he is pushing tourism because he needs to help the economy. Jammu and Kashmir’s finances are in very bad shape after more than 20 years of trouble. The state gets only £72m a year in taxes, but it pays £155m in salaries to 500,000 public employees. He needs more money quickly. For now, he is careful but happy to see tourists return. He said one million tourists does not mean everything is normal, but he feels satisfied that people can come, enjoy themselves, and go home.
