Cathal Redmond was swimming near the coast of Greece. He took photos with his first underwater camera. He thought they would be great. But when he looked at them on land, the pictures were brown and unclear. He took the photos while holding his breath. He thought the problem was that he did not have enough time to set up the shots. He wanted a little more time underwater to film fish in their natural home.

He decided to make that extra time possible. He invented the Express Dive. It is a refillable device that stores air and is held in the mouth. It lets a person swim underwater for two minutes. It is meant to sit between snorkelling and scuba diving. Snorkelling is simple but you cannot go deep or stay long. Scuba diving lets you breathe underwater, but the equipment is heavy and expensive. The prototype looks like a mix of a scuba mouthpiece and a water bottle. It is still in early testing, with separate parts being tested.

Redmond is 27. He says he wanted people to do more than hold their breath for about 30 seconds. In 2006, he took a scuba-diving course. He loved breathing underwater and watching fish. But he did not enjoy the fins, weight belt, wetsuit, air tank, mask, and other gear.

He says he felt like he had about 50kg strapped to him. Getting into the water felt strange, because he was used to staying on the surface. He says scuba diving can be limiting. Even though you can stay underwater longer, you must plan your whole day around it. You may have 20kg to 50kg of gear, so you cannot just walk on the beach and decide to go in. It takes planning and relearning.

Redmond made the Express Dive during his final-year project for his product design degree at the University of Limerick. The device has two main parts. Above the surface, a fan pulls in air through a vent in the mouthpiece. The air goes around a motor-driven turbine. Then it is pushed through valves and stored in a tank. A light flashes green when the tank is full. When air stops going in, the vent closes. When the user dives, air goes back to the mouth through the mouthpiece.

The light is in front of the user’s eyes. It works like a health bar. It changes from green to red when the air is low. The device holds enough air for two minutes underwater. It takes about the same time to refill. Redmond says the hard part was making it small enough to hold with the teeth, with the compressor, batteries, electronics, and parts for delivering air.

The electrical parts are protected from water inside the casing. The device is recharged using inductive power transfer. This uses an electromagnetic field, like pads that can charge mobile phones without wires, so there are no exposed wires.

Redmond says the mouthpiece feels like a snorkel. He says it is like an extra set of lungs. The user takes a deep breath, then uses that stored air underwater. The prototype is made from high-density foam, aluminium, and silicone. Parts of it have been tested. Redmond says the motor can compress two minutes of air, and the device can be held in the mouth. He has not yet tested it on a diver who is fully underwater for two minutes. But he believes that with enough testing, he can make a working device that will not put swimmers in danger.

Redmond recently came runner-up for the International James Dyson Award. This will give him £5,000 to develop the project further. He says early signs suggest the device might cost £280. It may weigh from 1kg to 3kg, depending on the safety features needed.

Some people may think two minutes is not much more than snorkelling. Redmond says it could matter a lot underwater. He says a typical swimmer can hold their breath for about 40 seconds underwater. He says two minutes is not a lot, but it is much longer than 40 seconds.
