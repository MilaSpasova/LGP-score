Passing clouds
One pleasure of flying is seeing clouds close up. Clouds look light, but they hold a lot of water. A small cumulus cloud can hold about 500 tonnes of water. Water is heavier than air. So why don’t clouds fall like rain? They do fall, but the tiny drops fall very slowly. A normal cloud would take about a year to fall one metre.

On cloud nine
Many people just say clouds are “fluffy” or “black”. But weather scientists (meteorologists) name more than 50 cloud types by their shape and height. They group them into types numbered one to nine. Cloud nine is the huge, tall cumulonimbus cloud. So “on cloud nine” means feeling on top of the world.

Around the rainbow
A plane is a great place to see a rainbow. Rainbows happen when sunlight hits raindrops. From the ground we see a bow because the Earth blocks part of it. From a plane, a rainbow can be a full circle. When a plane passes over clouds, the plane’s shadow can appear in the middle.

Mr blue sky
Sunlight is white and has all colours in it. When it goes through air, some light is scattered by gas molecules. Blue light scatters more than the other colours, so the sky looks blue.

There’s life out there
From a plane window, we expect to see clouds and other planes. But the air is also full of bacteria. Over cities, scientists have found as many as 1,800 different types. They can reach twice the normal cruising height of a plane.

Turbulence terror
Turbulence can make even experienced flyers feel sick. It can be light bumps or sudden drops. The good news is that no modern airliner has ever crashed because of turbulence. People can be hurt, and sometimes killed, if they are not strapped in or if loose luggage hits them. But turbulence will not knock a plane out of the sky.

In-flight radiation
When body scanners arrived at airports, people worried about radiation. But the scanner level is the same as what passengers get in one minute of flight. The Earth is always hit by cosmic rays, which are natural radiation from space. This radiation is stronger at high altitude.

You can’t cure jet lag
The world has time zones. Long flights can make local time different from your body clock, and this causes jet lag. You can reduce it by eating bland food for 24 hours before travel, drinking lots of fluids, and living on your destination time as soon as you get on the aircraft.

Supersonic 747s
Many people have travelled faster than sound. High in the sky there are fast winds called jet streams. On trips from the US to Europe, a temperature inversion can make a band of air move at up to 250mph. If a plane flies at 550mph and enters this wind, its speed over the ground can be 800mph. That is faster than the speed of sound, which is 740mph.

Flying through time
Time zones feel like travelling through time, but special relativity says flying also causes real time travel. The effect is tiny. If you crossed the Atlantic every week for 40 years, you would only move 1/1,000th of a second into the future.

Terrible tea
If your tea tastes bad, it is not the cabin attendant’s fault. Tea needs water just under 100°C. But on a plane the water cannot get hotter than 90°C during flight. So it may be better to choose coffee.

I can’t hear my food
Airline food is known for being bland. Some of the problem may not be the cooking. A plane is very noisy, and there is evidence that loud noise makes food taste less savoury.

Needle in a haystack
With today’s technology, it seems strange that Malaysian flight MH370 could disappear. But finding a missing aircraft is very hard. The plane knows where it is using GPS and inertial tracking, but it does not send this information elsewhere in real time. It could do so. Ships have had tracking since the 1980s. The problem is not technology, but missing laws that require it.

Volcanic fallout
Flights can be cancelled because of volcanoes. Ash has tiny glass-like pieces. They can melt in the hot engine and then harden on the rotors. Avoiding ash clouds can be annoying, but flying through ash is dangerous.

The wing myth
For many years, people taught the wrong reason for why wings lift planes. In fact, almost all lift comes from Newton’s Third Law of Motion. The wing shape pushes air downward. When air is pushed down, the wing gets an equal push upward, and the plane rises.

Forget electric planes
Small, light electric planes exist, so it is easy to think big electric airliners will come soon. But not any time soon. Plane fuel holds a huge amount of energy. Batteries hold much less. To match the energy in one tonne of fuel, you would need 100 tonnes of batteries. A 747 uses 150 to 200 tonnes of fuel. Unless batteries become much better, electric airliners will not be able to fly.

Beware the vortex
Pilots often wait before take-off. This is to let the air calm down after another plane leaves. A plane’s wingtips make spinning air called vortices. These can take two or three minutes to disappear. If the next plane takes off right away, the fast spinning air can make it hard to control. Waiting lets the air recover from the small whirlwinds.

The doors aren’t locked
Plane doors do not need to be locked. When a plane door opens, it moves in a special way. It must first move inward, then be moved out of the way. After take-off, the air pressure inside the plane becomes much higher
