Dame Sally Davies, the Chief Medical Officer for England, says antibiotic resistance is as dangerous as international terrorism. This may sound too strong, but the danger may actually be even bigger. Every year, many more people die because of bacteria that resist antibiotics than die in terrorist attacks.

It is hard to measure the worldwide harm across all kinds of bacteria. But the World Health Organization says that for tuberculosis alone, multi-drug resistance causes more than 150,000 deaths each year. Antibiotic resistance is not just a future worry. It is happening now.

In the last 100 years, life and health have changed a lot. At the start of the 1900s, life expectancy in the UK was about 47 years for a man and 50 for a woman. This was strongly affected by many babies dying. About one third of all deaths were in children under five, mostly because of infectious disease.

Today, a child born in Britain has a better than one in four chance of reaching 100 years old. We can thank public health systems, vaccination, and antibiotics. Preventing and treating illnesses caused by microorganisms is how we mainly win the fight against disease.

We also keep very sick people alive in intensive care. This is the area where I trained. It is also where antibiotic resistant organisms are most common. In intensive care, doctors often use strong antibiotics to treat life-threatening illness. These drugs kill normal bacteria, but they can leave behind tougher bacteria that learn ways to avoid antibiotics.

When I was a new doctor in the late 1990s, I learned about Methicillin Resistant Staphylococcus Aureus (MRSA). This bacterium resists methicillin and all other penicillins. To fight it, we used a few drugs like vancomycin and teicoplanin. These were meant to be our last lines of defence. But resistant bacteria became more common, and new kinds of resistance appeared. Drugs we hardly knew before became normal. New last-line drugs replaced the old ones. We got used to this rising "arms race" between people and bacteria.

But the situation has been changing. In hospitals and GP surgeries, we have used antibiotics too often and without enough care. Some of the worst misuse has happened outside healthcare too. Antibiotics have entered the food chain through agriculture, such as adding anti-bacterial drugs to livestock feed. We acted as if antibiotics would work forever. We also trusted that the pharmaceutical industry would always stay ahead.

Now that is not happening. New, more resistant bacteria have been found. Vancomycin, which we used to treat MRSA, stopped working in some cases. Vancomycin Resistant Staphylococcus Aureus (VRSA) appeared in hospitals. Other bacteria learned similar tricks. Enterobacteria, usually found in the gut, also became resistant to vancomycin. Today, infections with very resistant organisms are common, and the pharmaceutical industry is not keeping up. Fewer new anti-microbial drugs are being made. It is getting harder to develop drugs that work against resistant strains.

When drug companies invent a new way to attack, bacteria quickly evolve a defence. The simple ideas have already been used up. Antibiotics are expensive to develop, used only for short courses, and can stop working as resistance grows. Because of this, drug companies have less reason to make new antibiotics.

Soon after antibiotics became widely used in the 1940s, the first signs of resistance appeared. At first, this seemed like a small and unusual issue. The numbers were too small to seem important. But today, resistance is a normal part of medicine. Less than a century after penicillin was discovered, we are starting to lose the fight.

After the first MRSA deaths in otherwise healthy children in the US in 1998, yearly deaths from MRSA infection in the US rose to tens of thousands. This became more than the number of deaths caused by AIDS. Resistance in hospitals is everywhere. This fight is like no other.

We need a big change in how we prescribe antibiotics. We also need to use antibiotics more carefully in farming and agriculture. And drug companies must somehow be encouraged to develop these drugs, even though they make less profit.

During my working life, antibiotic resistance in healthcare has changed from rare to a very large problem. If we want to avoid going back to the time before antibiotics, when many more people died, we must act boldly. Losing the advantage we have only recently gained against microorganisms would be unthinkable.
