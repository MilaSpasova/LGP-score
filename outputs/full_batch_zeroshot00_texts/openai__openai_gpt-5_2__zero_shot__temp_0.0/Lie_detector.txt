For almost 100 years, police and intelligence agencies around the world have used lie detectors to help catch criminals and find spies.

Many movies show a polygraph test, with the guilty person sweating while connected to the machine.

But the polygraph may soon be replaced. Researchers in Britain and the Netherlands have made a new method. In tests, it worked more than 70% of the time. It could be used in police stations around the world within 10 years. Instead of looking for face twitches, talking too much, or waving arms, this method watches full-body movement. It looks for signs of guilty feelings.

The polygraph is used a lot in the US in criminal cases and for security checks for the FBI and CIA. It is less popular in Europe. Many scientists and lawyers doubt how reliable it is. The new method has done well in experiments.

The idea is simple: people who lie move around more. The researchers used an all-body motion suit, like the kind used in movies to make computer characters. The suit has 17 sensors. They measure movement 120 times each second in three directions, for 23 joints.

Ross Anderson, a professor at Cambridge University, said that after many years of research, interviewers can tell truth from lies only a little better than guessing: about 55 out of 100.

He said the polygraph has existed since the 1920s. It measures stress from anxiety, and this can raise success to about 60 out of 100. But it can be misused to scare people, and many people feel anxious during a polygraph because their job or freedom may depend on it.

Anderson said the new method reached over 70% reliability, and he believes it can get even better. In some tests, the team has already reached more than 80%.

He said the main message is that guilty people fidget more, and this can be measured in a strong way.

Anderson also said this research matters now because of the US Senate report about torture by the CIA. He said torture is wrong, and it is also a very unreliable way to get true information. He said, “We have known for a long time that torture does not work.” The new method offers a practical, science-based choice for interviews.

The research paper was written by Dr Sophie van der Zee of Cambridge University, Professor Ronald Poppe of Utrecht University, Professor Paul Taylor of Lancaster University, and Anderson.

The polygraph was created in 1921 by policeman John Larson, based on research by psychologist William Marston. It records changes in pulse, blood pressure, sweating, and breathing to decide if a person is lying.

Movies often make the polygraph seem almost perfect, but it is not. In 1998, the US Supreme Court said there was no agreement that the polygraph was reliable. The US National Academy of Scientists supported this in 2003.

Anderson and his team did an experiment with 180 students and employees at Lancaster University. Half were told to tell the truth and half to lie. Each person was paid £7.50 for a 70-minute experiment with two tests.

In one test, some people were interviewed about a computer game called Never End, which they played for seven minutes. Others lied about playing it and had only seen notes about it.

In the second test, there was a lost wallet with £5 inside. Some people took the wallet to a lost-and-found box. Others hid it and lied about it.

The report said: “Overall, we correctly classified 82.2% (truths: 88.9%; lies: 75.6%) of the interviewees as either being truthful or deceptive,” using the combined movement of their limbs.

Anderson said their first try looked at which body parts showed lying. They found that liars wave their arms more, but this was only about 60%, like a normal polygraph.

He said the big success came when they looked at total body motion. That could tell truth from lies more than 70% of the time. He said it might work even better when combined with the best ways of asking questions.

Anderson said total body motion is less affected by culture, anxiety, and cognitive load (how much you are thinking). These things can confuse other lie-detection methods.

All-body suits are expensive, costing about £30,000, and they can be uncomfortable. So the team is looking for cheaper options. One idea is to use motion-sensing technology from video games, such as Kinect devices made by Microsoft for the Xbox.

Anderson said agencies like the CIA could teach agents to fight the method by keeping their bodies very still, but he said that could also be a clear sign.
