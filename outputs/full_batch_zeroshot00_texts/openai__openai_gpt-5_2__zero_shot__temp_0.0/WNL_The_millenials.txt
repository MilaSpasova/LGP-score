It is not a secret that many millennials use a lot of technology. More than 8 out of 10 say they sleep with a mobile phone next to their bed. Almost 2 out of 3 say they text while driving. About 1 out of 5 has posted a video of themselves online. And 3 out of 4 have made a profile on a social networking site.

A 2010 report says millennials use social media more than other generations. About 75% of millennials have at least one social media account. Only 50% of Generation X, 30% of baby boomers, and 6% of people age 65 and older use social media. But some millennials do not use social media at all. Here are a few of them.

Celan Beausoleil, 31, Oakland, California
Beausoleil is a social worker. She has used Facebook on and off, but mostly off. She last turned off her account in December 2015. She did this because she felt it was too hard to see so much personal information from other people, on top of her work.

She said her job is listening to people’s lives all day. Then going online and seeing every detail of many people’s lives felt overwhelming, even for people she did not know well. She said it felt like “too much closeness.” She also said she already carries a lot from her work, and it felt too heavy to carry that online too.

But she liked how social media can connect people around the world. She said one reason she stayed on Facebook was that she could click from one friend to another and end up on someone’s page on the other side of the world. She said she used to do that a lot.

Jason Mathias, 26, Baltimore, Maryland
Mathias said he one day noticed he was spending a lot of time on social media. He thought that small seconds add up. He wondered what life would be like if he used those seconds for other things.

Mathias works for the Baltimore City government. He had Facebook and Twitter for years, but he deleted both in November 2012. After that, he quickly forgot about Facebook.

He still understands some good things about social media. For example, when meeting his girlfriend’s friends, it can help to remember people by looking at photos and learning where they are from and what they do. It is also easy to plan big events online. Mathias now depends on friends to invite him to parties, and he thinks sometimes he misses things.

But he enjoys having more free time. In elevator rides and short breaks at work, he reads news and books instead of scrolling online. And because he cannot follow friends online, he has to call them on the phone, and he says he really enjoys that.

Lauren Raskauskas, 22, Naples, Florida
Raskauskas says she is a private person. Social media does not feel good to her because other people can watch and judge what you share. She worries about giving out her data. She is currently looking for a job.

She deleted her Twitter account and turned off her Facebook account two years ago. She did this after she realized she did not like everyone knowing what she was doing. She joined Facebook later than many people because her parents were very strict about technology.

She can still see good parts of social media. One time, a friend she had lost touch with moved to Naples for a month. Raskauskas did not know her friend was there until after she left, and she said that was a bummer.

In the end, Raskauskas cared more about privacy than the benefits of social media. She also saw a good side after a break-up. The last time she broke up with someone while using Facebook, she worried about things like changing her profile photo and status. This time, she did not have to think about any of that.

Hariharan Rajagopalan, 18, Boston, Massachusetts
Rajagopalan is a student at Boston College. He does not think there are bad results from not using social media. He says he has not seen any effect so far. Even when classmates post about parties and events on Facebook, they also send him a text message.

He said that since he was young, he was usually a step behind on those things, so it did not matter to him.

The only time he used social media was when he had to. Before his first year of college, he joined Facebook so he could contact his new roommate and talk about plans for the year.

Months later, he still has the account, but he says he does not use it or check it. The only activity is when his two sisters tag him in family photos. He has not made other social media accounts. He says Twitter is tempting because sports news often breaks there, but he did not make an account because he does not need one to read tweets.
