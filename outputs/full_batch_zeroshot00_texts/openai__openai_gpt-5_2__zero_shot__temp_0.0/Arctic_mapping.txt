Google has mapped many places, like the world’s highest mountains, the ocean floor, and the Amazon rainforest. It even showed some of North Korea. But Google has mostly avoided mapping the cold Arctic.

Now Google is starting a big new mapping project in the Arctic. It wants to help millions of people understand what life is like on frozen ground called permafrost. Google flew a small team to Iqaluit, the largest town in Nunavut, Canada. They brought very warm winter clothes, laptop computers, and an 18kg backpack with a camera on it.

With help from an Inuit mapping expert, and watched by curious local people, the team walked for four days. They collected photos and information. This will help the community on Baffin Island get the same kind of map many city people already have.

Iqaluit has about 7,000 people. It will appear in Google Street View in July 2013.

Aaron Brindle, the project leader, said he lives in Toronto. He said he is used to having everything clearly shown on a map, but people in the north have not had that for a long time.

In most places, Google uses a camera on top of a car. But in Iqaluit, the team had to walk on snowy roads and on trails that are not well known. Some trails are made of ice and disappear during the short summer.

The team also went along part of a 15km dead-end road called the Road to Nowhere. They did this even though there were warnings about polar bears and other animals.

Mayor John Graham said the mappers were followed by excited and curious local people, sometimes called Google stalkers.

Graham said the excitement made sense. He said Street View continues a history of exploration in the area. In 1576, the English explorer Martin Frobisher sailed into the bay where Iqaluit is now, while looking for the Northwest Passage. In 1941, Captain Elliott Roosevelt, a reconnaissance officer and son of the US President, flew over the area. This helped the site get chosen for a military airbase. That exploration led to the founding of the modern town of Iqaluit. Today, it is the seat of government and also a transport and communications hub for Nunavut.

One early problem for Google was getting the basic information to finish its map. The satellite map was mostly correct. But because the town has been growing quickly, helped by a mining boom, Google missed one road built in the past year. This was said by Arif Sayani, the town’s Director of Planning.

Another problem was locating many businesses and homes. Many people get mail at the post office, not at their home address. If Google used only PO box locations, many places like companies, banks, and schools would all look like they were at the Canada Post building in the middle of town.

About 30 Inuit elders, business owners, and high-school students came one night to help fix these problems. They used laptop computers and learned how to make sure their homes, stores, and meeting places appeared in the correct locations.

The project is not only for fun or charity. Sayani, 32, said Iqaluit can use the maps to attract people who want to visit or move there. It may also help the town make faster planning decisions about how Iqaluit will grow.

Google tested this kind of mapping last summer in Cambridge Bay. Cambridge Bay is another Nunavut town with about 1,500 people. It is 1,700km west of Iqaluit and in a different time zone.

But the online pictures of gravel roads and muddy puddles do not show what life is usually like there, because the land is often covered in snow. That is one reason Google chose March, a harsher month, to visit Iqaluit.

Brindle said he hopes the work will continue in other northern towns. But shipping costs and airfares to move people and equipment around the huge Arctic area may be limiting Google’s plans.

Google has not chosen the next northern place yet. When it does, Brindle said the company might send one high-tech backpack and let volunteers do the work to put their town on the map.

Brindle said he hopes that in three, four, or five years, the map of Canada’s north will look very different.
