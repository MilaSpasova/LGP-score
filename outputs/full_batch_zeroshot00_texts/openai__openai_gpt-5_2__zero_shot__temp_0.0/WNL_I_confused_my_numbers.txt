My Spanish degree helped me get my first job as a journalist. I worked for an international news agency in Mexico City. But I still made big mistakes as a new reporter.

I arrived in Mexico City after a long Greyhound bus trip from New York. The job interview tested my Spanish. In the daytime, I went out to cover political rallies. At night, I stayed alone in the office and helped organize news from El Salvador, Nicaragua, Guatemala, and other parts of Central America. I also had to report on disasters like fires, floods, and explosions at firework factories.

Soon I learned something about myself: I was bad at understanding numbers in Spanish. I also was not great at math in English. When I tried to call the police, I sometimes dialed the wrong number. I woke up Mexican grandmothers at 2 a.m. because I mixed up a digit.

Even worse, I got numbers wrong in my news stories. A report of almost 83 people dead in a fire at 6 p.m. became only 38 by 7 p.m. A report of 12 people hurt in a bus crash soon became two. This kept happening. Finally, an editor from the main office in Washington called me and shouted, “I don’t know what training you have had, but has no one ever told you a death toll can’t go down?!”

Why are numbers so hard in another language? One reason may be that languages use numbers in different ways. For example, in German, 2:30 p.m. is halb drei (“half of three”), and 21 is einundzwanzig (“one and twenty”). These differences can confuse people. Some indigenous languages have very rare number systems, and some are close to dying out.

Some experts think there may be a link between dyscalculia (trouble understanding arithmetic) and trouble learning foreign languages, especially when students learn by repeating things from memory. This is because it uses step-by-step processes that people with dyscalculia may find hard. But some students who do badly with a grammar textbook may do well in a real foreign-language setting, where learning feels more natural. For me, languages were usually easy, except for numbers.

Another reason may be that people often hear numbers alone, without other clues. If a listener has stopped paying attention to the foreign language, it can be hard to suddenly focus again. I asked some friends who speak many languages. Many said they can speak French or Italian well when ordering food, but they freeze when they must say numbers, especially on the phone. Numbers feel difficult, but no one could explain exactly why.

My trouble with numbers in other languages followed me from Mexico to other countries, and from Spanish to German and Portuguese. Still, in that first journalism job, getting numbers wrong did not always mean I failed.

One night, a Mexican co-worker found out that the American consul in the port city of Veracruz was being held hostage at gunpoint in his office. No senior reporter who spoke English was there, so I had to call the consulate. I got the number wrong, and the call went to another extension in the building. The voice was clear: I had reached the gunman. I talked with him for 15 minutes. I did not get him to put away his gun, but my reputation as a new reporter rose overnight.
