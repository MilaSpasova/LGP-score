Most exercise classes do not stop for tea in the middle. But Margaret Allen’s class does. After a gentle warm-up and some faster exercises, the 93-year-old great-grandmother lets the group drink tea and sit down for a short rest.

There are eight people in the class. Some look like they need the break more than others. Allen wears a thick turquoise shirt, a navy knitted waistcoat, black slacks, and sensible shoes. She is not sweating. She has a very painful trapped nerve in one leg and a knee that needs to be replaced, but she still looks like she could keep going for a long time.

Usually it is not a good idea to eat right before sport, or during it. But when I visit her class in a church hall in Saltburn-by-the-Sea near Middlesbrough, people are passing around slices of fruitcake during the break. The cake was baked for Allen’s recent birthday by her sister Joan, who is 89. People in the town call Joan the “scone queen of Saltburn”.

Soon after the last crumbs are eaten, Allen stands up again. She leads the group in a lively Scottish dance with toe pointing and leg kicking. Forty-five minutes later, the class ends.

Allen used to volunteer with the Red Cross. She has led classes in this seaside town for 45 years. She was not very sporty at school. During the second world war, she began playing the piano for a keep-fit class, mainly to have something to do during the blackouts. Later, when she was in her 40s, she took over the class when the old instructor retired.

At its busiest time, the class had more than 18 regular members, and each person paid £1 each time. Now the group is getting smaller fast. During the tea break, the women talk about a funeral they mostly went to that week. It was for one of the younger members, who died at 68 from motor neurone disease.

Allen is the oldest member, and her sister is next. The youngest person is Jean Cunion, who is 60. She says the group helped her through a hard time when her mother died. She feels a bit shy to say she may be the least fit. She remembers the first time she came, Allen said, “Who’s that huffing and panting?” and she had to say it was her. Ruth Steere, who is 76, is surprised that Allen always knows what is happening, even when her back is to the class. She says Allen shouts at them if they do it wrong, and she is very good at knowing what they are doing.

Allen loves dancing. She has never had formal training as a fitness instructor. She makes up her own moves using five tapes from the BBC’s first fitness guru, Eileen Fowler. Fowler died in 2000 at age 93, which is Allen’s age now. Allen thinks her good health mostly comes from staying busy, especially after her husband Joe died in 1997. When she was 80, she started writing poetry.

She says she writes poems about everything and cannot stop. A few days after the interview, she phones me to read a short poem she wrote about the joy of exercise. Winnie Robertson, an 84-year-old former teacher in the class, thinks the secret to staying fit is not letting yourself go. She says, “Use it or lose it.”

Allen still plays the piano and gives speeches. She is president of the Women’s Fellowship at the local methodist church. She is also one of three people over 90 at the scrabble club of the University of the Third Age. She did a computer course when she was 88 and tried to get online, but it did not work out.

Allen says getting older is not fun. She reads me a few lines from her poem called “That Beast Called Age”. She also remembers a doctor who saw her for the first time a few years ago and said she could not be older than 78. She told him, “Thank you, doctor. You can go now.”

She is also very direct about weight gain. She says people should not eat too much. When someone says, “Oh, I can’t lose weight,” she says, “Sellotape.” She acts like she is taping her mouth shut. She says she said this the other day to a very fat man. Her motto is: everything in moderation.

Earlier in 2013, Allen watched the news and saw a woman getting the British Empire Medal. She seems to mean Margaret Chartwood from Horley, who got the honour in January at age 77. Allen says the woman said, “I’m 80 and I’m the oldest fitness instructor in the country!” Allen thought, “No, you’re not.” But she says she will not write to Buckingham Palace.
