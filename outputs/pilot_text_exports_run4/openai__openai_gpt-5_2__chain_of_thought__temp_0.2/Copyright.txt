Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is theft. He says it is as wrong as reaching into someone’s pocket and stealing their wallet. In an article for Index on Censorship, Pullman, who is president of the Society of Authors, strongly supports copyright laws. He criticizes internet users who think it is OK to download music or books without paying.

Pullman writes that the technology is so impressive that some people do not see how wrong it is. He says it is shocking that someone can take an artist’s work and not be punished. He repeats that it is theft, just like stealing a wallet.

His article was written after leaders in the music industry met British Prime Minister David Cameron in Downing Street. They discussed web piracy.

Pullman, who wrote the His Dark Materials trilogy, says authors and musicians often work in poverty and are not well known for years. They work hard to make something people enjoy. But when they finally might make a living from it, that chance is taken away by illegal downloading. He says the rule is simple: if we want to enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

In the next issue of the group’s magazine, Pullman is in a dialogue with Cathy Casserly. She is the chief executive of Creative Commons. Creative Commons offers open content licences that let creators manage copyright in different ways. Casserly says there are many problems with copyright, which was created in an “analogue age”. She says copyright often blocks many ways people can share, build on, and remix each other’s work.

Casserly says artists should think of new ways to share and make money from their work. She quotes science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees that there is a problem. The editor, Rachael Jolley, said existing copyright laws do not work well in the digital age. She said they could turn consumers into criminals. She said new models are needed so artists, writers, and musicians can earn a living from their work.

The debate is active, and illegal downloading is very common. Ofcom, the independent regulator and competition authority for the UK communications industries, collected data. It suggests that in the UK, between November 2012 and January 2013, 280 million music tracks were pirated. Also, 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files were pirated.

Ofcom said 18% of internet users aged over 12 admit they have recently pirated content. Also, 9% say they fear getting caught. Pullman writes that it is still amazing how easily and quickly people can get music as MP3 downloads, especially for people who have been building their iTunes list for some time.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron appointed Conservative MP Mike Weatherley as his adviser on this issue. A spokesman for the BPI, the record industry trade body, said Mike Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creative producers it protects. The spokesman said they hope Weatherley’s influence, and the prime minister’s support of copyright, will affect the approach of the UK’s intellectual property office.
