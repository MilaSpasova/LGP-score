This invention could be as useful as Trevor Baylis’s wind-up radio in the pre-internet 1990s. It is a cheap light that makes free power from gravity. It could help stop people using dangerous kerosene lamps in Africa and India.

British designer Patrick Hunt tried the usual way to get money for his invention, like banks or venture capital. But he had a problem. He said it was slow, complicated, unproven, and people did not want to take a risk.

So he tried crowdfunding on a US website called Indiegogo, which had recently opened in the UK. In five days, he reached his target and raised £36,200. His campaign was very popular at the end of 2012, and in 40 days he raised £400,000.

The LED light uses a dynamo. The dynamo is turned by a 10kg bag of rocks as it goes down. The bag is attached to the light and lifted to about 2m high. When it slowly falls to the ground, it makes enough power for 30 minutes of light.

Hunt is getting ready to make the light in China. He will test the market again by sending 1,000 lights to Africa before full mass production. He hopes mass production will be millions of units.

Hunt is part of a new group of business creators using the fast-growing crowdfunding industry to get money. Another new site is InvestingZone, which matches rich people with start-up entrepreneurs.

Indiegogo does not give investors shares. Instead, people who donate can get “perks” for different donation amounts. People who helped fund Hunt’s light could feel good about helping poorer people, and they also got their own light.

Danae Ringelmann, a co-founder of Indiegogo, said the “gravity light” shows how crowdfunding can be fair and how it can test a business idea. She said it is fast and efficient, and it is not about knowing the right people at a bank or venture capital firm. It is about showing your value to customers and fans, getting them to support your idea, and getting them to fund it.

She also said that even ideas that do not get funding are still worth testing. That way, you can learn sooner that it was not a good idea and learn faster.

Ringelmann lives in the US and began her career as a Wall Street analyst. In 2008, she quit and used her skills to help friends in the arts raise money. The site first focused on films and launched at the Sundance Film Festival that year.

Five years later, Indiegogo was raising about $2m a week for new businesses that are starting or growing. In December 2012, it added euro and sterling services to build a base in Europe. It says Britain is its third biggest market. International activity is up 41% since December.

There are many competitors, like Kickstarter, Seedrs, and Funding Circle. But Ringelmann says Indiegogo is different because anyone can start a campaign, and no project is seen as too strange.

Indiegogo charges a 4% fee for successful campaigns. If a campaign does not reach its target, users can either return all the money to the contributors for free, or keep the money but pay a 9% fee.

A British woman, Lauren Pears, raised £100,000 on the site to open a “cat café” in London. It is called Lady Dinah’s Cat Emporium. It has not opened yet, but it is described as a place where people can “come in from the cold to a comfortable wingback chair, a hot cup of tea, a book and a cat”.

Ringelmann said venture capitalists sometimes reject ideas because they are not sure the market really wants them. She said entrepreneurs can run an Indiegogo campaign, even before they launch the project, and the campaign can be proof that customers want it. She said it lets you test the market, pricing, and features, find new ways to make money, and get important feedback.

With her Wall Street experience and work helping 100,000 businesses and services raise money, Ringelmann gives advice to new entrepreneurs. She said ideas are common, and what matters is doing the work well. She said if you are scared someone will steal your idea and do it better and faster, then you are not the right person to carry it out. She said you need confidence to move fast and learn.

Ringelmann said expanding into Europe and making a deal with a web transaction provider are part of a dream to make finance open to more people. This will let people pay with local card services like Maestro in the UK and Carte Bleue in France, as well as PayPal. More than 7,000 campaigns are live on the site.

Crowdfunding has grown as another choice besides banks, but it does not attract many big investors. Many big investors want at least a stake in a business. This could start to change in the UK with the launch of InvestingZone.
