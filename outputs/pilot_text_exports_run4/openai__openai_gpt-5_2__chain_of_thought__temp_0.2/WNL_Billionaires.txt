Thick bird droppings cover the gold railing of one of Britain’s most expensive homes. Pigeon bones lie near broken mirrors, and water runs through broken parts of the ceiling. This house is called The Towers. It is a £30m palace on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London. Its bad condition has been kept secret until now.

The Towers is one of ten mansions in the middle of The Bishops Avenue. This road is linked to London’s rising house prices. These homes have been almost empty since they were bought about 25 years ago. It is believed they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. The Greek-style columns are breaking. Swimming pools with mosaic tiles are full of rubble. Nature is taking over, and owls have moved in.

This same sad scene is seen along the road. Lloyds Bank has said The Bishops Avenue is the second most expensive street in Britain. At the same time, more people are trying to buy homes in London, as prices rise by 11.2% a year. But 16 mansions on the most expensive part of The Bishops Avenue are empty. Many are behind locked gates. Their windows are covered with steel bars, and the grounds are overgrown. Guard dogs patrol the area.

Across the street is another damaged mansion worth £18m, with broken windows and walls covered in anti-climb paint. Another house has metal bars on its windows and was sold for £20m.

Even so, prices still rise. Dryades, a mansion that was owned until recently by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007. It is believed to be worth about £30m today. Heath Lodge, where fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou was murdered in 1984 with a silver bullet, is worth £13m today. It was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

For people who struggle to afford housing in one of the world’s most expensive cities, these empty, falling-apart mansions can be painful to see. One security guard said it was frustrating to watch so much space—enough to house dozens of people—being ruined.

Rich royals from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia were among the first to come to this curved road near Hampstead Heath. Iranians came here after the fall of the shah. Now Chinese buyers are coming after Russians and Kazakhs. Estate agents tell them the address is as famous as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions have been for sale for £65m and £38m. They promised lots of Italian marble, lifts with leather padding, and luxury panic rooms.

But on the empty properties, stone fountains are breaking and lawns have turned to mud. Inside one mansion, water drips through a huge crystal chandelier onto a thick carpet that is rotting under parts of a collapsed ceiling. Moss grows through broken bricks. Mirror tiles are scattered across a bathroom. The swimming pool has about a foot of dirty water and flowers growing through its tiles. Wooden slats push out from the sauna.

The most dramatic ruin is The Towers. It is a large mansion set among acres of hornbeams, oaks, and limes. Pigeons fill its big, high rooms. Water pours through three floors, and the walls have turned bright green with algae. The water drops into a huge, empty basement swimming pool. Wooden crates marked “bullet-proof glass” were never opened, showing the old owners’ security fears.

Today, very few people live on The Bishops Avenue all the time. A security guard outside one mansion said the owners were not there. Another guard, outside Royal Mansion, would not say if anyone was home. Staff at another mansion warned the Guardian about the guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare mogul with personal wealth of £45m, said he was one of the few people who live there permanently.

“It’s not a neighbourly place, where you can chat over the fence,” he said. “To be honest, I have never seen what my neighbours look like. Next door, a Saudi princess spent £35m on a new house and I’ve never seen her. There are about three houses that are lived in 24/7 and half of the properties are occupied three to six months a year. The other half, who knows if they come or not?”

These ruined, multi-million-pound homes show a property culture where the world’s richest people treat British homes as investments. One Hyde Park, an apartment building in Knightsbridge, is another example. More than half its flats are registered with the council as empty or second homes.

Even so, people on the avenue talk about building £5m apartments instead of £50m mansions, to bring people back.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, plans to rebuild one of the most valuable sites on the avenue into 20 apartments. They would have a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

“If you build a big house and try and sell for £30m to £40m, it won’t sell,” he said. “Locals won’t buy and so you have to bring in overseas buyers.”

But using these empty homes to help solve the housing crisis still seems far away. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some of the derelict homes could become affordable housing. He said the land price would be too high.

“Very wealthy people own property there,” he said. “Sometimes they live in them and sometimes they don’t.”
