Kenton Cool usually likes to talk a lot. But now he can hardly speak. Climbing high on the mountain has made his voice rough. He is now in Kathmandu, the capital of Nepal. That morning, he flew down from Everest base camp.

Cool is thinking about an amazing set of climbs he finished the weekend before. Early on Saturday morning, he reached the top of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of three main summits in the Everest “horseshoe.” This horseshoe surrounds the icy valley called the Western Cwm.

Later that same day, he climbed to the top of Everest. He reached the summit in complete darkness early on Sunday. Then he and his climbing partner kept going. On Monday morning, they reached the summit of Lhotse. This was the third peak in the three-peaks challenge.

“It was a snatched opportunity,” he says. “For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. That does not take away the physical achievement of what I did. I have set the bar at a certain level. But whoever comes next will move the bar further and do it without ropes or bottled oxygen.”

Sixty years after Everest was first climbed, many stories look back to Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. They talk about a more innocent time, before modern commercialism and damage to the environment. I asked Cool to look ahead and imagine what top climbers might do 60 years from now.

“I hate to think,” he says. But he talks about the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. In April, Steck left the mountain after what Cool calls “an altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck planned to climb Everest’s west ridge, first climbed in 1963. Then Steck wanted to go down to the South Col and immediately climb Lhotse by a new route, all without fixed ropes. “Ueli had been training like a machine,” Cool says. “He’s a climber in a class all his own. He’s technically brilliant, and he had also taken his physical condition to an astronomic level. It would have been amazing to see what he could have done.”

Tourism is another question. One clue is a helicopter rescue done by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro’s angry language helped cause the fight at Camp 2. On Tuesday, Moro flew back to Everest in a high-powered helicopter. He rescued a sick climber at 7,800 metres.

It was the highest rescue yet on Everest. It also shows how fast helicopter flights have increased in recent years. By 2073, the mountain could even have a helipad on the South Col. Tourists could be brought there while breathing bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing how rescues can happen for both climbers and the many trekkers who go as far as base camp.

Mountain geographer and environmentalist Alton Byers says it is not clear if the Everest region can keep handling fast-growing tourism. He says climate change and tourism together are creating new problems for the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers are shrinking, and some are disappearing. This is already having a big effect. “Everywhere you go, people are talking about how there’s less water,” he says. “There’s less water for agriculture and less water for all the new lodges that are getting built.”

Byers says that in the Sherpa town of Namche Bazaar, a new five-mile pipeline is being built to bring water. It is meant to meet the rising tourist demand for showers and flush toilets. The local stream is polluted with human waste. It also does not have enough water for a town that, in high season, is very crowded. “Every village is digging a pit just beyond the houses for garbage. Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world,” he says. Human waste at base camp is now handled well and taken away in plastic barrels. But Byers says the barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley. That pit could leak into the region’s rivers and streams.

“These problems can be solved, but we need to get serious about it,” he says. “One climber can spend $85,000 climbing Everest. And that’s fine. But at some point we’re going to have to address these other priorities. For half a million dollars a year, you could solve most of them.”

Climate change is harder to deal with. Byers works with local conservation groups to find and plan for climate change effects. They often look for new water sources or set up rainwater harvesting. Another danger is the fast growth of glacial lakes. These lakes could burst and flood the Sherpa homeland. “There’s going to come a time when people are going to have to get out of their way,” he says.

Changing weather is also affecting tourism. More clouds during times that are usually clear are closing Lukla Airport more often. Lukla Airport is the main way into the Everest region. A new road for 4x4s is being built to Lukla to keep tourists and their money coming. But Byers worries the fast growth of roads in Nepal is being done cheaply. He says this can lead to serious soil erosion and landslides.

“Everest is the icon everyone knows,” he says. “It’s the canary in the coalmine that everyone understands. It’s the perfect laboratory for figuring out how to address some of these problems, like the impacts of climate change and tourism.”
