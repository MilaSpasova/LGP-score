The lottery balls have dropped, and all six numbers match. Many winners then buy an Audi, book a holiday in the US, and call an estate agent. An analysis of how jackpot winners spend and invest says this is common.

The UK National Lottery started in 1994. Since then, it has made 3,000 millionaires. Together they have won more than £8.5bn, with an average of £2.8m each. Oxford Economics, the authors of the study, say the money also helped create 3,780 more millionaires among winners’ children, family, and friends.

Most winners (59%) stop working right away. But 19% keep doing their normal job, and 31% do unpaid volunteer work. The study says 98% of winners’ spending stayed in the UK. It also estimates that each winner’s spending on homes, vehicles, and holidays keeps six people in full-time work for one year.

Winners have added almost £750m to gross domestic product (GDP). They also created more than £500m in tax money for the Exchequer. Most money was spent on property. Winners spent £2.72bn on their main homes, and £170m on paying off debt and mortgages.

Keeping a steady income was important, so £2.125bn was spent on investments. Winners gave £1.17bn as gifts to family and friends. They spent £680m on cars and holidays.

Camelot, the operator of the UK National Lottery, asked for the study to mark the milestone of 3,000 winners. The study used research from 100 winners who won £1m or more. It found that the 3,000 winners bought 7,958 houses or flats in the UK, which is 2.7 each on average, and spent £3.3bn. Most winners (82%) changed their main home and spent an average of £900,000.

Many new homes had extra features. Almost a third of winners (29%) bought a hot tub. A walk-in wardrobe was important for 28%. Almost a quarter (24%) chose a home behind electric gates. Also, 22% had a games room, and 7% put in a snooker table.

Bigger homes need more care. About 30% of winners hired a cleaner, and 24% hired a gardener. A small group (5%) hired a beautician.

Audis were the favourite car for 16% of winners. Range Rovers and BMWs were also popular (11% each), as well as Mercedes (10%) and Land Rovers (5%). Winners spent £463m on 17,190 cars. The average price of their favourite car was £46,116.

Holidays were also important. Most winners (68%) chose five-star hotels overseas. The US was the top destination for 27%, followed by the Caribbean for 9%. Closer to home, UK caravan sales also gained. Over the past 18 years, 10% of millionaires bought a caravan, creating sales of about £7.4m.

Some winners (15%) started their own business. Another 9% helped other people start businesses, and 6% invested in or bought other people’s businesses. The study says businesses started or supported by lottery winners employ 3,195 people.

Andy Logan, the report author, said the effects of a win spread further than expected. He said a win can change the lives of friends and family, and it also has a measurable effect on the UK economy because so much is spent in the UK. He said each win creates a ripple effect in this generation and often the next.
