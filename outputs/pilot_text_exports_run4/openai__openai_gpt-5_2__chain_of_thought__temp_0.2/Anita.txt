Anitta is Brazil’s newest funk star. She has won millions of fans by bringing the favela sound into mainstream music. But now there is a debate about skin colour.

Anti-discrimination groups and social writers say that this fast-rising star had to give up part of her black identity to sell music to the mostly white, middle-class market.

The debate grew after people shared “then and now” photos of Anitta. The photos show her skin looking much lighter after she signed a deal with Warner.

In the first photo, from when she was not well known, she looked darker. In the second photo, a marketing picture after she became famous, she looks paler. People argue about why this happened. They mention skin-whitening products, cosmetic surgery, or—more likely—Photoshop changes. The big difference in the photos has brought back an old question in Brazil: do you need to be pale to succeed?

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote a post about this. She said the media and marketing hide discrimination. She wrote that many people do not accept that they are racist, and they think Brazil is a multiracial democracy, but statistics show it is not. She said the “whitening” shows a society that does not support diversity. She wrote that people often connect white skin with being rich, nice, successful, and good, and connect black skin with the opposite.

Anitta was born Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

Now she is very famous. She released an album and had a huge hit song, Show das Poderosas. It reached number one on the charts and got 52 million views on YouTube.

Many people love her as a pop idol with a strong message and catchy songs. The people supporting her present her as a bridge between the mostly black and mixed-race shanty towns on Rio’s hillsides and the richer and whiter areas below.

She has also changed her style. She reduced the suggestive dancing, gangsta talk, and explicit lyrics of baile funk. But now some people ask if she—or her marketing team—changed her image too much to attract a richer audience. Arraes said that if pop stars have curly hair, they may feel pushed to straighten it. If they have a big nose, they may feel pushed to get rhinoplasty. She said this can create a harmful cycle for self-esteem.

Race is a sensitive subject in Brazil, a country with many mixed-race people. Brazil was one of the last big countries to ban slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. But in Brazil, colour often matters more than race and ancestry. Even though the country aims to be a multiracial democracy, there is a clear link between skin tone and inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Up to 2011, black or mixed-race students also spent, on average, two fewer years in school.

The government says the gap is shrinking because of quota systems for university places and other types of affirmative action. But the difference is still easy to see. Most business and government leaders are white, while many low-level jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In Ipanema, Gávea, and other rich areas, you are much more likely to see black nannies pushing strollers with white toddlers than a white nanny pushing a black child.

Colour categories are not simple. In the 2010 census, people who called themselves white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million said they were black, two million said they were Asian, and 0.5% said they were indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology teacher at the Federal University of Pernambuco, said Anitta won over the middle class by taking a rebellious sound and making it calmer and easier for everyone to accept.

He said this was not done by racial whitening. He said what changed was the social space where Anitta made her art: from the periphery to the centre.

Other people say the colour issue is too big. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who worked with Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot, but not her colour.

He said he did not think it was whitening. He said it was more about how they present her with makeup, hairstylists, and clothes. He said he did not think it happened because people pressured her. He said she always liked to show off, sing, and dance, and that this was natural for her. He said he believes this naturalness is why she is successful today.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador in the north-east, said the prejudice was not in the public, but in music producers and media leaders. He said the public showed they care about music itself by choosing Ellen Oléria—a black lesbian—as the recent winner of the talent show The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for comment. But in a recent interview, Anitta said identity should be defined by the person.

She said an “all-powerful” woman does not need to be beautiful. She said a woman can be marvellous and powerful because of her attitude. She said she tries to show everyone through her work that we can be who we want.
