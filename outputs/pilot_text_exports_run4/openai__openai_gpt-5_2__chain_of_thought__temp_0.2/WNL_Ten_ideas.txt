From glow-in-the-dark trees to underground bike storage and solar-powered bins, there are some unusual ideas to make cities better places to live.

Living in a city has many good points, but living in a way that is good for the environment is getting harder. People in cities often face pollution, traffic, and fewer green spaces.

So inventors have stepped in. Here are some of the wackier ideas to make cities more livable.

Pop-up parks
Some cities look like they are built more for cars than for people. A pop-up park is a simple idea. You take an empty car park, a little money, and one or two plants, and you make a small park.

The PARK(ing) project started as an art experiment in San Francisco. Since then, it has spread around the world. People have also created temporary city farms and ecology demonstrations. The movement has a day of action every September.

Subterranean storage
Not everyone in cities uses a car. Bikes are becoming more popular. But people ask: where can you keep a bike safe?

An engineering company in Tokyo called Giken designed an “eco-cycle, anti-seismic underground bicycle park.” It is a round storage system that is only seven metres wide. It goes deep into the ground and can hold 204 bikes. Owners can get their bike back by pressing a button. The automatic system brings the bike back above ground in about 13 seconds.

Glow-in-the-dark trees
When many people think of glowing trees, they think of Christmas decorations. But Dutch designer-artist Daan Roosegaarde has a different idea.

He created an experimental “bioluminescent” plant. The technology mixes DNA from glowing sea bacteria with a plant’s chloroplast genome, so the plant can glow like a jellyfish. Tests are happening to make a large, industrial version. Roosegaarde hopes it could one day replace normal street lights.

Footfall harvesting
Every day, many commuters and shoppers in West Ham in east London walk across a raised walkway near the underground station. Most people do not notice that the ground feels a bit springy. Even fewer people know that the rubber surface can bend about five millimetres and help power the streetlights above.

The walkway has smart tiles. They capture the energy from people’s footsteps and turn it into electricity. The UK company Pavegen made this system. It has also put a similar system in London’s Heathrow Airport and in other places around the world.

Supertrees
People have made man-made trees. Singapore’s Gardens by the Bay has a small group of them.

These steel-framed “supertrees” can be up to 50 metres tall. Flowers and ferns grow on them. Their metal tops help take in and spread out heat. They also collect rainwater and help move air for two “climate-controlled biomes” (large conservatories) below. Eleven of the 18 trees also have solar panels on their “branches.”
