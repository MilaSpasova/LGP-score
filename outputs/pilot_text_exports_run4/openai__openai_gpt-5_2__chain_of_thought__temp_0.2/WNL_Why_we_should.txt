James Hamblin is a senior editor at the American magazine The Atlantic. In his series “If Our Bodies Could Talk,” he tried a challenge of not showering much. He took fewer showers, and when he did shower, he did not use shampoo or soap. He wanted to see what happens when we clean our bodies too much.

He learned something many other people have learned: when we use a lot of soap, body wash, and scrubbing, our skin works harder to get back to normal balance. Showering can wash away the skin’s natural oil and bacteria. This is often the reason people shower, but it can sometimes work too well, especially with hot water and cleaning products.

Have you ever felt tight and dry after a shower? That can happen because much of your skin’s natural moisture gets washed away. Also, your skin has millions of helpful bacteria, like your gut does. Frequent rubbing and scrubbing can destroy these bacteria. When the bacteria grow back after being washed off with soap, they may include more microbes that cause bad smell. So showering too often may actually make you smell more. If you stop showering and stop using soap, your skin may have an early adjustment period that can feel gross. After that, the skin often returns to balance, makes less oil, and healthy bacteria grow again.

After this, Hamblin decided that the human body, working on its own, can be quite lovely. It is not only about smell or looks. Dermatologists suggest that using less soap can improve acne and eczema.

Showering less can also help the environment. The average shower lasts seven minutes and uses 65 litres of water. That is 65 litres of clean, drinkable water that we mix with soap and send down the drain every day, sometimes more than once.

Clean water is becoming harder to ignore as California enters another summer of drought. It is clear that clean water is one of the world’s most valuable things, and it may soon be in short supply. Also, think about the environmental effect of all those body wash bottles. These are strong reasons to let your body be more natural.

If this idea makes you feel worried because you remember being close to people who do not shower much and do not smell good yet, relax. Many people who use less soap still use deodorant if they need it. Everyone’s natural smell is different, and it can change because of diet, hydration, and exercise. Also, washing your hands with soap is still recommended as an important way to reduce the spread of infectious diseases.

You do not have to stop all at once like Hamblin did. But there is space between cleaning many times a day and never showering. The simple advice is: reduce. Skip a few showers, use less soap, and let the helpful bacteria grow a little.
