It was a beautiful summer evening, and I decided to go for a swim near Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland. I moved there in 2012. In the water, a woman was swimming with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty often swims with a group of people.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Since then, hundreds of people have swum with her, so she can seem very tame. She was even in an Irish tourist-board ad where a girl says she wants to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty’s belly. It looked friendly. About 20 tourists and local people stood on the pier and watched.

Soon after I got into the water, Dusty left the woman and suddenly went wild. Later I learned that Dusty protects her space when she is with someone. Her tail was flapping hard. At first I thought she was just showing off. Then I realized she might be angry.

I tried to swim back to the pier to get out. But almost at once, Dusty crashed into me with her nose. It was very strong and very painful, and she was very fast. I was thrown forward.

People on the pier stared down at me in shock. Dusty stayed next to me, still flapping her tail. That scared me even more. I thought, if she hits me with her tail, I could go under the water.

I reached the pier, but I could not get out because I was hurt. I felt terrified. I shouted for help. A man reached in, grabbed my arm, and pulled me onto the steps.

Then another man came. He said he was an orthopaedic surgeon who specialized in marine injuries. He was driving into Doolin and saw what was happening. I was very cold and very worried. My biggest fear was bleeding inside my body. He checked me and tried to calm me. He said he could not feel signs of internal bleeding, but he thought I might have broken bones.

Later I learned my injuries were serious: six fractures in my spine, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in the hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for several weeks. I could not work for five months. I had limited movement, stiffness, and pain.

Later I was diagnosed with post-traumatic stress. The near-death experience made me anxious and made me react too strongly. I felt like people were looking at me the wrong way. Loud noises became hard for me. I also had memory loss.

Three months before the accident, I had opened a health-food shop. But I had to give it up because I could not work anymore. That year was the hardest, but now it is behind me. I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building up my own osteopathy practice. I understand patients better because I have been one.

I am grateful that I am healthy, and I want to stop other people from getting hurt. Many people think dolphins are always safe. Who would expect a dolphin to attack a person? If a dangerous animal comes at you showing its teeth, you know to be scared. But dolphins have a wide smile, so people do not expect danger.

I am not angry at Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her space, and she is a wild and unpredictable animal. People need to understand that. Many people come to swim with her and do not know how dangerous it can be. My injuries were reported as one of several injuries that summer.

After the man pulled me out of the water, Dusty swam away. Then she came back and floated upright next to me, looking at me. We looked into each other’s eyes, and I felt she was sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin, and the anger was gone. People on the pier watched in amazement. After that moment, my terror ended. I felt at peace with her.
