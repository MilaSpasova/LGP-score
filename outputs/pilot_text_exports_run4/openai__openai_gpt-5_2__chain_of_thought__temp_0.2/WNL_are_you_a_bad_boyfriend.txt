Dan Sullivan was late to breakfast with the Guardian. He was late because police pulled him over while he was riding his motorcycle.

Sullivan says he works too much. He misses dinner dates. He forgets to give presents.

So, like many people in Silicon Valley, the 27-year-old venture capitalist made a start-up to help. It is called BetterBoyfriend.me. It sends girlfriends and wives a present every month for about $70.

Sullivan works in finance. He has been quietly testing the program. He has signed up about 350 boyfriends. They bring in about $17,000 in revenue each month. He says most members are his Harvard and investor friends. They include venture capitalists, founders, and employees at companies like Apple, Google, and Facebook. Their girlfriends have been getting presents from Sullivan for the last year.

Each month, members pick from a list of seven possible gifts, like chocolate, tea sets, and manicures. The package is sent to the boyfriend with no brand name and no label.

Sullivan tests ideas by buying Facebook ads to see what kind of results they get. BetterBoyfriend.me grew fast. Sullivan said, "BetterBoyfriend hit something. It’s a pain point everyone has."

BetterBoyfriend.me was not Sullivan’s first idea. For a couple of days, he thought about a dog-sharing idea. He said he saw rich guys looking miserable while walking dogs. He said, "a dog is only valuable 5% of the time." He wondered if one person could babysit the dogs and other people could rent them.

Another start-up, called InvisibleBoyfriend, lets users "design and build a believable social partner who is everything you want and is always available to talk, whenever you want". The result is a "boyfriend" who can text with you all day.

Founder Kyle Tabor launched it to give users a fake boyfriend to stop unwanted advances. But he found that people became attached to their algorithm-based partners. He said, "Many more users are seeking companionship through conversation rather than external ‘proof’ of a relationship to get people off their back."

For Sullivan and Tabor, a surprise was the real relationships that formed with customers. Sullivan says he has started to see himself as a kind of relationship helper for the boyfriends.

Sullivan says he has made mistakes. Early on, the packages had receipts with his name on them, Dan Sullivan. "One of the boyfriends wrote to me and said, ‘Listen, dude, she’s not mad but Cynthia found out’."

About 50% of the women who get BetterBoyfriend.me packages know about Sullivan’s role in their relationships. Sullivan says it connects to age. He thinks that after people have been married a while, they do not keep many secrets.

Over the year, Sullivan says he has gotten to know the boyfriends very well. They even made a delivery to a hospital delivery room. He said the key is to remember that his relationship is with the boyfriend.

When he first started the company, he put tags that said BetterBoyfriend.me on flower bouquets. Then he went to Dolores Park, a popular place in the tech- and youth-heavy Mission District.

He said, "I looked for couples and gave the bouquet to the girl but that didn’t go down well with the boyfriend. Like, not at all. It made sense, though. It was cool." He said he then changed his plan and started giving the bouquets to the boyfriends.
