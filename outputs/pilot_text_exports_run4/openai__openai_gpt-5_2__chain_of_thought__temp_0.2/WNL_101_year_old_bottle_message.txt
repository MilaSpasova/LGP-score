Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8 April 2014, she told about an amazing moment. She received a message in a bottle, 101 years after her grandfather threw it into the Baltic Sea. People think it may be the world’s oldest message in a bottle. A museum in Germany, which is showing it now, gave it to Erdmann.

Erdmann, 62, said it was very surprising. She said a man came to her door and told her he had mail from her grandfather. He said a bottle with a message had been found, and the name on the card was her grandfather’s. The visitor was a family-history researcher. He found Erdmann in Berlin after the letter was given to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg, a port city in northern Germany.

The message was in a brown beer bottle. It had been in the water for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it in his catch while fishing in the Baltic Sea near the northern city of Kiel.

Holger von Neuhoff, a curator at the museum for ocean and science, said it was the oldest bottled message he had seen. He said the museum has older documents that were found without a bottle. But with both the bottle and the document together, this was the oldest at the moment. He also said it was in very good condition.

Researchers believe Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913. He did it while hiking with a nature group. He was 20 years old then.

Most of the postcard could not be read. But the Berlin address on the front could be read. The card also had a polite request asking the finder to send the note to his home address.

Erdmann said her grandfather also put two stamps from that time in the bottle, so the finder would not have to pay. But he did not think it would take 101 years.

Erdmann said the message made her feel very moved. She had not known her grandfather because he died at age 54, six years before she was born.

She said she knew very little about him. But she learned he was a writer who was open-minded. He believed in freedom and in people respecting each other. She said he did a lot for young people and later traveled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful because she could see where her family roots came from.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and traveling around the world, like her grandfather. She said she is open-minded too. She said what he taught his two daughters, her mother taught her, and she then passed it on to her sons.

Even though she was happy to get the message, she said she hopes others will not throw bottles with messages into the sea. She said the sea is now full of bottles and trash, and people should not throw more in.

The message and the bottle will be on display at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts will try to read the rest of the text. It is not clear what will happen to the bottle after that, but Erdmann hopes it will stay at the museum.

Erdmann said they want to provide a few photos to show with the bottle. This way, visitors can see the young man who threw the bottle into the water.
