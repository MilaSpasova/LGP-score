Opposition to Western Australia’s shark cull has grown. Thousands of people went to beaches across Australia to ask the state’s premier to stop the policy. RSPCA Australia and Richard Branson, who owns Virgin Atlantic, also spoke against it.

The policy involves catching and killing sharks longer than three metres. It started after what the state government called an “unprecedented” number of shark attacks on Western Australia’s coast. In November 2013, a 35-year-old surfer was killed. He was the sixth person to die from a shark attack in two years.

However, the Shark Attack File says that Australia has averaged one shark-related death each year for the last 50 years.

Kate Faehrmann, a board member of the Sea Shepherd Conservation Society, spoke at a protest in Perth. She said the policy will not work. She said drumlines are used to catch sharks, and they can kill many animals. She said they can kill sharks of any size, and also dolphins, turtles, and other animals. She said that is why the community does not want the policy.

Thousands of people protested at Perth’s Cottesloe Beach and Sydney’s Manly Beach. Hundreds protested at Glenelg in south-west Adelaide, and at beaches in Victoria and Queensland. Faehrmann said the protests showed many Australians want sharks protected. She said that even though people fear sharks, many people still respect them and do not want them killed.

Anthony Joyce, a surfer who once had his foot caught in a shark’s mouth, said killing sharks would make no real difference.

The state government has refused to give a running total of how many sharks have been killed. There have been reports that sharks smaller than three metres were released after being caught. Drumlines are floating drums anchored to the sea bed, with bait on hooks under them.

Conservationists say there is no evidence the cull will reduce shark attacks on humans. They say no earlier cull used only drumlines. Researchers at the University of Western Australia say the recent increase in shark attacks may be linked to Western Australia having the fastest-growing population in Australia, not to more sharks.

Richard Peirce, chairman of the UK conservation charity the Shark Trust, said the cull would not work and could even bring more predators closer to the coast. He said the government ignored good advice and chose an approach that is ineffective and makes the problem worse. He said that even if drumlines are checked during the day, leaving them out overnight could attract other predators because hooked animals may be injured.

Worldwide in 2012, there were 80 unprovoked shark attacks, and seven were fatal. This is compared to nearly 100m sharks killed by humans each year. RSPCA Australia said it believes the cull is not justified. It said there is no evidence that more attacks are caused by more sharks. It said the rise fits with changes in population and human behaviour, because more people are in the water.

Richard Branson told Fairfax Radio the policy could have the opposite effect from what was intended. He said Premier Colin Barnett may have thought it would help tourism, but he thinks it will do the reverse. He said it makes people think there is a big problem when there is not, and it could stop people from wanting to visit Perth and the surrounding countryside. He said it will only make people worry for no good reason.
