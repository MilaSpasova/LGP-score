This invention could be very useful, like Trevor Baylis’s wind-up radio in the 1990s. It is a cheap light that uses gravity to make power. It could stop people in Africa and India from using dangerous kerosene lamps.

British designer Patrick Hunt tried to get money from banks or venture capital companies to start selling his invention. But he had trouble. He said getting money was slow and hard, and the idea was unproven, so people did not want to take a risk.

So he tried crowdfunding on a US website called Indiegogo, which had just opened in the UK. In five days, he reached his goal and raised £36,200. By the end of 2012, the campaign became very popular. In 40 days, he raised £400,000.

The LED light uses a dynamo to make electricity. The dynamo is powered by a 10kg bag of rocks that slowly falls. The bag is attached to the light and lifted to about 2m high. As it falls, it makes enough power for 30 minutes of light.

Hunt is getting ready to make the product in China. He will test the market again by sending 1,000 lights to Africa before making many more. He hopes to make millions of units.

Hunt is part of a new group of business owners using crowdfunding to get money. Another new site is InvestingZone, which connects rich people with start-up entrepreneurs.

Indiegogo does not give shares in projects. Instead, it lets people offer “perks” for different donation amounts. People who donated to Hunt’s light could feel good about helping poorer people, and they also got their own light.

Danae Ringelmann, a co-founder of Indiegogo, says the “gravity light” shows how crowdfunding can work fairly and can test an idea. She says finance can be fast and efficient because it is not about knowing the right people in a bank or venture capital firm. It is about proving the idea to customers and fans so they support and fund it.

She also says even ideas that do not get funding are worth testing. Then you save time and learn faster if the idea is not good.

Ringelmann is based in the US. She first worked as a Wall Street analyst. In 2008, she quit and used her skills to help friends in the arts raise money.

At first, the site focused on films and launched at the Sundance Film Festival that year.

Five years later, the site was raising about $2m each week for new businesses that are starting or growing. In December 2012, it started services in euros and pounds to grow in Europe. It says Britain is its third biggest market. International activity is up 41% since December.

Indiegogo has many competitors, like Kickstarter, Seedrs, and Funding Circle. But Ringelmann says Indiegogo is different because anyone can start a campaign, and no project is seen as too strange.

Indiegogo charges a 4% fee for campaigns that reach their goal. If a campaign does not reach its goal, users can either give all money back to donors for free, or keep the money raised and pay a 9% fee.

A British woman named Lauren Pears raised £100,000 on the site to open a “cat café” in London. It is called Lady Dinah’s Cat Emporium. It has not opened yet, but it is described as a place where people can sit in a comfortable wingback chair with a hot cup of tea, a book, and a cat.

Ringelmann says some campaigns are rejected by venture capitalists. The venture capitalists may say, “Great idea, but we don’t know if people really want it.”

She says entrepreneurs can run an Indiegogo campaign even before launching the product. The campaign can show proof that there is a market. Then venture capitalists may decide there is demand.

Ringelmann says crowdfunding lets you test your market, your price, and your product features. It can also help you find new ways to earn money and get important feedback.

She says ideas are common, and what matters is doing the work well. She says if you are scared someone will steal your idea and do it faster, then you may not be the right person to carry it out. She says it is important to be confident, move fast, and learn.

Ringelmann says expanding into Europe and making a deal with a web payment provider are part of a dream to make finance more open to everyone. This will allow payments through local card services like Maestro in the UK and Carte Bleue in France, as well as PayPal. More than 7,000 finance campaigns are live on the site.

Crowdfunding has grown as another choice instead of banks. But it is not as attractive to big investors, who usually want a share of a good business. That may start to change in the UK with the launch of InvestingZone.
