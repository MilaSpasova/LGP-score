Anitta is a new star in Brazil. She sings funk music from the favelas (poor neighborhoods). She has gained millions of fans. But there is now a debate about her skin colour.

Some people who fight discrimination say Anitta had to change how she looks to become popular with the mostly white middle class. The debate grew after “before and after” photos were published. In older photos, when she was not famous, she looked darker. In a newer marketing photo, after she became famous, she looks paler. People argue about why this happened. It could be from makeup, products, surgery, or photo editing like Photoshop. The big difference brought back a question in Brazil: do you need to be pale to succeed?

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote that this shows hidden discrimination in media and marketing. She said many people do not admit racism and think Brazil is a multiracial democracy, but statistics show it is not. She said society often connects “white” with rich, good, and successful, and sees “black” as the opposite.

Anitta’s real name is Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

She became very famous with an album and a hit song, Show das Poderosas. The song reached the top of the charts and got 52 million YouTube views.

Many people love her as a pop star. Her supporters also present her as a bridge between the mostly black and mixed-race shanty towns on Rio’s hillsides and the richer and whiter areas below.

She changed her style to fit the mainstream. She reduced the suggestive dancing, gang references, and explicit lyrics of baile funk. But some people now ask if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract a richer audience. Arraes said this can pressure stars to change things like curly hair or their nose, and it can hurt self-esteem.

Race and colour are sensitive issues in Brazil. Brazil was one of the last large countries to ban slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. In Brazil, colour often matters more than race or family history. There is also a clear link between skin tone and inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Up to 2011, black or mixed-race students also spent about two fewer years in school on average.

The government says the gap is getting smaller because of university quota systems and other affirmative action. But big differences remain. Most business and government leaders are white. Many low-paid jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In wealthy areas like Ipanema and Gávea, it is common to see black nannies pushing strollers with white toddlers.

Colour is also hard to define. In the 2010 census, people who called themselves white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million black, two million Asian, and 0.5% indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology lecturer at the Federal University of Pernambuco, said Anitta became popular with the middle class by taking a rebellious sound and making it tamer. He said this was not “racial whitening.” He said it was a change in social space: from the periphery to the centre.

Other people also say the colour issue is too big. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who worked with Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot, but not her colour. He said the change was more about makeup, hair styling, and clothing. He also said he did not think she was being pressured, and that she always liked to sing, dance, and show off.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador, said the prejudice was more in music producers and media executives, not in the public. He said people showed they cared about music itself by choosing Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, as a recent winner of the TV talent show The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for comment. But in a recent interview, she said identity should be defined by the person. She said an “all-powerful” woman does not need to be beautiful. She said she tries to show that we can be who we want.
