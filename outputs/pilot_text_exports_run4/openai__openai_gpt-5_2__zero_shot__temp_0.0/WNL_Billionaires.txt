A thick layer of bird droppings covers a shiny railing at one of Britain’s most expensive homes. Dead pigeons lie near broken mirrors, and water runs through damaged parts of the ceiling. This house is called The Towers. It is a £30m palace on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London. Its terrible condition has been kept secret until now.

The Towers is one of ten mansions on The Bishops Avenue. This road is in the middle of London’s rising property market. These houses have been almost empty since they were bought about 25 years ago. People believe they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. The houses are falling apart. Their Greek-style columns are breaking. Their swimming pools, covered with mosaic tiles, are full of rubble. Nature has taken over so much that owls now live there.

This sad sight happens again and again on this famous street. Lloyds Bank says it is the second most expensive street in Britain. At the same time, more people are struggling to buy a home in London because prices rise 11.2% each year. But 16 mansions on the most expensive part of The Bishops Avenue are empty. Many are behind locked gates. Their windows are covered with steel bars, and their gardens are overgrown. Guard dogs walk around the grounds.

Across the street is another empty mansion worth £18m. Its windows are smashed, and its walls have anti-climb paint. Another house has metal bars on the windows and was sold for £20m.

Even so, prices keep rising. Dryades, a mansion that was owned until recently by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007 and is believed to be worth about £30m today. Heath Lodge is worth £13m today. It was the place where fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou was murdered in 1984 with a silver bullet. Heath Lodge was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

Seeing these empty, broken houses can be very painful for people who struggle to afford housing in one of the world’s most expensive cities. A security guard on the street said it was upsetting to watch so much space—enough room for dozens of people—falling apart.

In the past, royals with oil money from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia came to this curved road near Hampstead Heath. Iranians came after the fall of the shah. Now, Chinese buyers are following Russians and Kazakhs. They spend millions to get an address that estate agents say is as famous as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions were for sale for £65m and £38m. They promised Italian marble, lifts with leather padding, and luxury panic rooms.

But on the grounds of the empty houses, stone fountains are breaking, and lawns have turned into muddy bogs. Inside one house, water drips through a huge crystal chandelier onto a thick carpet that is rotting under parts of a collapsed ceiling. Moss grows through broken bricks, and pieces of mirror tile are spread across a bathroom. The swimming pool has about a foot of dirty, salty water, and flowers grow through the tiles. Wooden boards in the sauna are bending out.

The worst damage is at The Towers. It is a large mansion surrounded by many trees, including hornbeams, oaks, and limes. Its big halls with high ceilings are full of pigeons. Water pours through three floors, and the walls have turned bright green with algae. The water falls into a huge, empty basement swimming pool. There are unopened wooden crates marked “bullet-proof glass,” showing the old owners had security worries.

Today, very few people live on The Bishops Avenue all the time. A security guard outside one mansion said the owners were not there. Another guard outside Royal Mansion would not say if anyone was home. A worker at another mansion warned the Guardian about guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare businessman with personal wealth of £45m, said he is one of the few people who lives there permanently.

He said, “It’s not a friendly place where you can chat over the fence. To be honest, I have never seen what my neighbours look like. Next door, a Saudi princess spent £35m on a new house and I’ve never seen her. There are about three houses that are lived in 24/7 and half of the properties are occupied three to six months a year. The other half, who knows if they come or not?”

These broken, very expensive homes show a culture where the richest people in the world buy British property as investments. One Hyde Park, an apartment building in Knightsbridge, is another example. More than half the flats there are registered with the council as empty or second homes.

Still, people on the avenue talk about building £5m apartments instead of £50m mansions, to try to bring people back.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, plans to rebuild one valuable site on the avenue into 20 apartments. The building would have a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

He said, “If you build a big house and try and sell for £30m to £40m, it won’t sell. Locals won’t buy and so you have to bring in overseas buyers.”

But using these empty homes to help solve the housing crisis still seems far away. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some of the broken houses could become affordable homes. He said the land price would be too high.

He said, “Very wealthy people own property there. Sometimes they live in them and sometimes they don’t.”
