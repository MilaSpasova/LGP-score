Opposition to Western Australia’s shark cull has grown. Thousands of people went to beaches around Australia to ask the state premier to stop the policy. RSPCA Australia and Virgin Atlantic owner Richard Branson also spoke against it.

The policy catches and kills sharks longer than three metres. It began after what the state government called an “unprecedented” number of shark attacks on Western Australia’s coast. A 35-year-old surfer was killed in November 2013. He was the sixth person to die from a shark attack in two years.

However, the Shark Attack File says Australia as a whole has averaged one shark-related death a year for the last 50 years.

Kate Faehrmann, a board member of the Sea Shepherd Conservation Society, spoke at a protest in Perth. She said the policy will not work. She said drumlines are not selective. She said they can kill sharks of any size, and also dolphins, turtles, and other animals. She said this is why the community does not want the policy.

Thousands of people protested at Perth’s Cottesloe Beach and Sydney’s Manly Beach. Hundreds also protested at Glenelg in south-west Adelaide, and at beaches in Victoria and Queensland. Faehrmann said the protests showed many Australians want sharks protected. She said people are coming out across the country to say the ocean belongs to sharks too and they do not want them killed.

Anthony Joyce, a surfer who once had his foot caught in a shark’s mouth, said the number of sharks killed would make no difference.

The state government has refused to give an ongoing count of sharks killed. Reports say sharks smaller than three metres have been released after being caught on drumlines. Drumlines are floating drums anchored to the sea bed, with bait on hooks under them.

Conservationists say there is no evidence the cull will reduce shark attacks on humans. They say no earlier cull used only drumlines. Researchers at the University of Western Australia say the recent increase in attacks may be linked to the state having the fastest-growing population in Australia, not to more sharks.

Richard Peirce, chairman of the UK charity the Shark Trust, said the cull would not work and could bring more predators near the coast. He said it is sad the government did not follow advice and did not try other options instead of killing animals. He also said that leaving drumlines in overnight could attract other predators, because sharks and other animals may be hooked and injured.

Around the world in 2012, there were 80 unprovoked shark attacks, and seven were fatal. In the same period, humans kill nearly 100 million sharks each year. RSPCA Australia said the cull is not justified. It said there is no evidence attacks are increasing because shark numbers are increasing. It said the increase fits with more people being in the water because of changes in population and human behaviour.

Richard Branson told Fairfax Radio the policy was backfiring. He said Premier Colin Barnett may have thought it would help tourism, but he believes it will do the opposite. He said it makes the problem seem bigger than it is and may stop people from coming to Perth and nearby countryside. He said it will only worry people for no good reason.
