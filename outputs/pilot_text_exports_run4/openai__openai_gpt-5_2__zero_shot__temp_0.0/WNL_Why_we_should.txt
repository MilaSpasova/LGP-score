James Hamblin is a senior editor at The Atlantic magazine. In his series “If Our Bodies Could Talk,” he tried a challenge: he stopped showering for a while. He also took fewer showers and did not use shampoo or soap when he did shower. He did this to see what happens when we clean our bodies too much.

He learned what many others have found. When we use lots of soap, body wash, and scrubbers, our skin works harder to get back to normal. Showering can wash away the skin’s natural oils and helpful bacteria. Hot water and cleaning products can make this worse.

After a shower, you might feel like your skin is too tight. That can happen because your skin’s natural moisture gets washed away. Our skin also has millions of helpful bacteria, like the gut does. Frequent scrubbing can destroy many of these bacteria. When the bacteria come back, the new mix may include more microbes that cause smell. So showering too often may make you smell more. If you stop showering and stop using soap, your skin may go through an early adjustment time that can seem gross. After that, the skin often returns to balance, makes less oil, and healthy bacteria grow again.

After his experiment, Hamblin felt the human body can be nice when it works on its own. It is not only about smell or looks. Dermatologists say using less soap can help acne and eczema.

Taking fewer showers can also help the environment. The average shower lasts seven minutes and uses 65 litres of water. That is 65 litres of clean, drinkable water mixed with soap and sent down the drain every day, sometimes more than once.

Clean water is becoming harder to ignore, especially as California faces another summer of drought. It is clear that clean water is one of the world’s most valuable resources, and it may soon be in short supply. Also, body wash bottles affect the environment. These are strong reasons to let your body be more natural.

If this idea makes you feel worried, relax. Many people who cut back on washing still use deodorant if they need it. Everyone’s natural smell is different, and it can change with diet, how much water you drink, and exercise. Also, washing your hands with soap is still important to help stop infectious diseases from spreading.

You do not have to stop all at once like Hamblin did. Between cleaning many times a day and never showering, there is a middle choice. Try to reduce. Skip a few showers, use less soap, and let your skin’s helpful bacteria grow.
