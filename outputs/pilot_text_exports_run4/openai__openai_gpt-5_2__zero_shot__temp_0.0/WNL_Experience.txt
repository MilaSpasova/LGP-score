One beautiful summer evening, I decided to swim near Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland. I moved there in 2012. In the water, a woman was swimming with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty often swims with a group of people.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Since then, hundreds of people have swum with her, so she can seem very tame. She was even in an Irish tourist-board ad, where a girl says she wants to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty’s tummy. It looked friendly and safe. About 20 tourists and local people were watching from the pier. Soon after I got into the water, Dusty left the woman and suddenly went wild. Later I learned she is very territorial when she is with someone. Her tail was hitting the water hard. At first I thought she was just showing off, but then I realized she might be angry.

I knew I needed to get out, so I swam toward the pier. But in a split second, Dusty crashed into me with her nose. It was very strong and very painful. I was thrown forward.

People on the pier stared at me in shock. Dusty stayed next to me, still hitting the water with her tail. That scared me the most. I thought, if she hits me with her tail, I could sink.

I reached the pier, but I could not climb out because I was hurt. I felt terrified. I shouted for help. A man reached in and pulled me out onto the steps. Then another man came and said he was an orthopaedic surgeon who specialized in marine trauma. He had been driving into Doolin and saw what was happening. I was very cold and very worried. My biggest fear was bleeding inside my body. He checked me and said he could not feel signs of that, but he thought I probably had broken bones. Later I learned I had six fractures in my spine, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in the hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for several weeks. I could not work for five months, and I had trouble moving, plus stiffness and pain. Then doctors said I had post-traumatic stress. After the near-death experience, I felt anxious about everything and reacted in ways I never had before. I felt like people were looking at me the wrong way. Loud noises became hard for me. I also had memory loss.

Three months before the accident, I had opened a health-food shop. But I had to give it up because I could not work anymore. That year was very hard, but now it is behind me. I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building my own osteopathy practice. I understand patients more because I have been one.

I am thankful that I am healthy, and I want to stop other people from getting hurt. Many people think dolphins are always safe and gentle. Who would think a dolphin could attack a person? If you see a fierce animal coming with its teeth showing, it is clearly scary. But dolphins look like they have a friendly, wide smile.

I am not angry at Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her space, and she is a wild animal that can be unpredictable. People need to know this. Many people come to swim with her and do not understand how dangerous it can be. People said my injuries were one of several injuries that summer.

After I was pulled out, Dusty swam away. Then she came back and floated upright next to me, looking at me. We looked into each other’s eyes. I felt she was sorry. She seemed different, and the anger was gone. The people on the pier were amazed. After that moment, my fear ended. I felt at peace with her.
