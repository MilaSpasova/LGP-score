Dan Sullivan was late to breakfast with the Guardian. He was late because police stopped him while he was riding his motorcycle.

Sullivan says he works too much. He misses dinner dates. He forgets to give presents.

So, like many people in Silicon Valley, the 27-year-old venture capitalist made a start-up to help: BetterBoyfriend.me. This service sends girlfriends and wives a present every month for about $70.

Sullivan works in finance. He has been testing the program quietly. He has signed up about 350 boyfriends. This brings in about $17,000 each month. He says most members are his Harvard and investor friends. They include venture capitalists, founders, and employees at companies like Apple, Google, and Facebook. Their girlfriends have been getting presents from Sullivan for the last year.

Each month, members choose from a list of seven gifts, like chocolate, tea sets, and manicures. The package is sent to the boyfriend with no brand name and no label.

Sullivan tests ideas by buying Facebook ads to see what results he gets. BetterBoyfriend.me became popular. Sullivan said, “BetterBoyfriend hit something. It’s a pain point everyone has.”

BetterBoyfriend.me was not his first idea. For a couple of days, he thought about sharing dogs. He said he saw rich guys looking miserable walking dogs. He said a dog is only valuable 5% of the time. He wondered if someone could babysit the dogs and other people could rent them.

Another start-up is called InvisibleBoyfriend. It lets users design a believable social partner who is everything you want and is always available to talk. The result is a “boyfriend” who can text with you all day.

The founder, Kyle Tabor, launched it to help users stop unwanted advances by using a fake boyfriend. But he found that people became attached to their computer-made partners. He said many more users want companionship through conversation, instead of needing outside “proof” of a relationship.

For Sullivan and Tabor, a surprise was the real relationships they formed with customers. Sullivan says he has started to see himself as a kind of relationship consultant for the boyfriends.

Sullivan admits he made mistakes. At first, the packages included receipts with his name, Dan Sullivan. One boyfriend wrote to him and said, “Listen, dude, she’s not mad but Cynthia found out.”

About 50% of the women who get packages know about Sullivan’s role. He says it connects to age. He thinks that after you have been married a while, you do not keep many secrets.

Over the year, Sullivan says he got to know the boyfriends very well. They even did a delivery to a hospital delivery room. He said the key is to remember that his relationship is with the boyfriend.

When he first started the company, he put BetterBoyfriend.me tags on flower bouquets. Then he went to Dolores Park, a popular place in the Mission District with many tech workers and young people.

He looked for couples and gave the flowers to the girl, but the boyfriends did not like it. He said it made sense. Then he changed his plan and started giving the flowers to the boyfriends.
