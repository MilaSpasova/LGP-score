When the lottery balls fall and all six numbers match, many winners quickly start spending. An analysis of lottery jackpot winners says many people buy a new car, book a holiday in the US, and contact an estate agent to buy a home.

Since it started in 1994, the lottery has made 3,000 millionaires. Together they have won more than £8.5bn in total, which is an average of £2.8m each. The study authors, Oxford Economics, say this has also helped create 3,780 more millionaires among winners’ children, family, and friends.

Most winners (59%) stop working right away. But 19% keep doing their paid job, and 31% do unpaid voluntary work. The study says 98% of winners’ spending stayed in the UK. It estimates that each winner’s spending on property, vehicles, and holidays keeps six people in full-time jobs for a year.

The study says winners added almost £750m to gross domestic product (GDP) and created more than £500m in tax money for the Exchequer. Most money was spent on property. Winners spent £2.72bn on their main homes, and £170m was used to pay off old debt and mortgages.

Keeping a steady income was important too, so £2.125bn was spent on investments. Gifts to family and friends totalled £1.17bn. Cars and holidays cost £680m.

Camelot, the company that runs the UK National Lottery, paid for the study to mark 3,000 winners. It used research from 100 winners who won £1m or more. It found that the 3,000 winners bought 7,958 houses or flats in the UK (about 2.7 each), spending £3.3bn. Most winners (82%) moved to a different main home and spent an average of £900,000.

Many winners added special things to their homes. Almost a third (29%) bought a hot tub. A walk-in wardrobe was important for 28%. Almost a quarter (24%) chose a home behind electric gates. Also, 22% had a games room, and 7% installed a snooker table.

Big homes need help to look after them. About 30% hired a cleaner, and 24% hired a gardener. A small number (5%) hired a beautician.

Audi was the top car choice for 16% of winners. Range Rovers and BMWs were also popular (11% each), as well as Mercedes (10%) and Land Rovers (5%). Winners spent £463m on 17,190 cars. The average price of the favourite car was £46,116.

Holidays mattered too. Most winners (68%) chose five-star hotels overseas. The US was the most popular place to go (27%), followed by the Caribbean (9%). In the UK, caravan sales also increased. In the past 18 years, 10% of millionaires bought a caravan, creating about £7.4m in sales.

Some winners used money for business. About 15% started their own business, 9% helped other people start one, and 6% invested in or bought other people’s businesses. The study says these businesses employ 3,195 people.

Andy Logan, who wrote the report, said the results were bigger than expected. He said a win can change the lives of friends and family, and it also affects the UK economy because so much money is spent in the UK. He said each win creates a ripple effect for this generation and often the next.
