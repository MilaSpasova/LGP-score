Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is theft. He says it is like putting your hand in someone’s pocket and stealing their wallet. Pullman wrote this in an article for Index on Censorship. He is president of the Society of Authors, and he strongly supports copyright laws. He is very unhappy with internet users who download music or books without paying.

Pullman writes that the technology is so impressive that people do not see the wrong thing they are doing. He says it is terrible that people can steal an artist’s work and not get punished.

His article came after leaders from the music industry met British Prime Minister David Cameron at Downing Street. They talked about web piracy.

Pullman, who wrote the His Dark Materials trilogy, says authors and musicians often work in poverty and are not well known for many years. He says they work hard to make something people enjoy, and then they may lose the chance to earn a living from it. He says the idea is simple: if we want to enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

Pullman is writing in the next issue of the group’s magazine in a conversation with Cathy Casserly. She is the chief executive of Creative Commons. Creative Commons offers open content licences that let creators control copyright in a different way. Casserly says copyright has many problems because it was made in an “analogue age.” She says copyright can stop many ways people can share, build on, and remix each other’s work.

Casserly says artists need to think of new ways to share and make money from their work. She quotes science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees with her. The editor, Rachael Jolley, says today’s copyright laws do not work well in the digital age and may treat consumers like criminals. She says we need new ways for artists, writers, and musicians to earn money.

The debate is strong, and illegal downloading is very large. Ofcom, the UK communications regulator, collected data. It suggests that in the UK, between November 2012 and January 2013, people illegally copied 280 million music tracks, 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files.

Ofcom says 18% of internet users over age 12 admit they recently pirated content, and 9% say they worry about getting caught. Pullman says it is still surprising how fast people can get music as MP3 downloads, even to people who have been building up their iTunes list for some time.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron chose Conservative MP Mike Weatherley to be his adviser on this topic. A spokesman for the BPI, a record industry trade body, said Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creative producers it protects. The spokesman said they hope Weatherley’s influence and the prime minister’s support will affect how the UK’s intellectual property office acts.
