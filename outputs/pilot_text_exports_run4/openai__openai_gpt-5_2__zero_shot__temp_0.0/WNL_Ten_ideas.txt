From glow-in-the-dark trees to underground bike storage and solar-powered bins, people are trying unusual ideas to make cities nicer places to live.

Living in a city has good points, but it can be hard to live in a clean, earth-friendly way. People often face pollution, heavy traffic, and fewer green spaces. Inventors have come up with wacky ideas to help.

Pop-up parks
Some cities seem built more for cars than for people. A pop-up park is a simple idea: use an empty parking space, a little money, and a few plants to make a small park. The PARK(ing) project started as an art experiment in San Francisco and then spread around the world. People have also made temporary city farms and nature demonstrations. The movement has a special day of action every September.

Underground bike storage
Not everyone in cities uses a car. Bikes are becoming more popular, but people ask: where can you keep a bike safe? An engineering company in Tokyo called Giken created an “eco-cycle, anti-seismic underground bicycle park.” It is a round storage space about seven metres wide, built deep underground, and it can hold 204 bikes. Owners can get their bike back by pressing a button. The system brings the bike up in about 13 seconds.

Glow-in-the-dark trees
Many people think of glowing tree lights at Christmas. But Dutch designer-artist Daan Roosegaarde created a “bioluminescent” plant. This experimental technology mixes DNA from glowing marine bacteria with part of a plant’s cells to make a glow like a jellyfish. Tests are happening to make a version that could be produced on a large scale. Roosegaarde hopes it could one day replace normal street lights.

Footstep power
In West Ham in east London, many commuters and shoppers cross a raised walkway near the underground station every day. Most do not notice that the ground feels slightly springy. The rubber surface bends about five millimetres, and this movement helps power the streetlights above. The floor has smart tiles that catch energy from people’s footsteps and turn it into electricity. The UK company Pavegen made this system and has installed a similar one at London’s Heathrow Airport and in other places.

Supertrees
Singapore’s Gardens by the Bay has man-made trees called “supertrees.” They can be up to 50 metres tall and have a steel frame. Flowers and ferns grow on them. Their canopies help take in and spread out heat. They can collect rainwater and help provide air ventilation for two “climate-controlled biomes” (large conservatories) below. Eleven of the 18 trees also have solar panels on their “branches.”
