Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8th April, 2014, she told the story of an amazing event. She received a message in a bottle. Her grandfather had thrown it into the Baltic Sea 101 years earlier. People think it may be the world’s oldest message in a bottle. A museum in Germany gave it to her, and the museum is now showing it.

Erdmann, 62, said it was a big surprise. She said a man came to her door and told her he had mail from her grandfather. He explained that a message in a bottle had been found, and the name on the card was her grandfather’s. The man was a genealogy researcher. He found her in Berlin after the letter was given to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg.

The bottle was a brown beer bottle. It had been in the water for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it in his catch while fishing in the Baltic Sea near the city of Kiel. Holger von Neuhoff, a curator at the museum, said it was the oldest bottled message he had seen. He said the museum has older documents that were found without a bottle, but this bottle and its document are the oldest set like this. He said it was in very good condition.

Researchers believe Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913. He was hiking with a nature group at the time. He was 20 years old.

Most of the postcard was hard to read. But the Berlin address on the front could be read. The card also politely asked the person who found it to send it to his home address.

Erdmann said her grandfather also put two stamps from that time in the bottle, so the finder would not have to pay. But he did not think it would take 101 years.

Erdmann said she felt very touched by the message. She did not know her grandfather because he died at age 54, before she was born.

She said she knew little about him, but she learned that he was a writer. She said he was open-minded, believed in freedom, and believed people should respect each other. She said he helped young people and later travelled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful because it helped her see where her family came from.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and travelling around the world, like her grandfather. She said she is open-minded too. She said what he taught his two daughters, her mother taught her, and she has passed it on to her sons.

Even though she was happy to get the message, she said she hopes people today will not throw bottles with messages into the sea. She said the sea is already full of bottles and rubbish.

The message and the bottle will be on display at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts will try to read the rest of the text. It is not clear what will happen to the bottle after that, but Erdmann hopes it will stay at the museum.

She said they want to add a few photos with the bottle, so visitors can see the young man who threw it into the water.
