Kenton Cool usually likes to talk, but now he can barely speak. Climbing high mountains has made his voice rough. He is in Kathmandu, the capital of Nepal. He flew down from Everest base camp that morning.

Cool is thinking about what he climbed the weekend before. Early on Saturday, he reached the top of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of three main peaks in the Everest “horseshoe.” These peaks surround a valley with ice called the Western Cwm.

That same day, he climbed to the top of Everest. He reached the summit in complete darkness early on Sunday. Then he and his climbing partner went on to the top of Lhotse on Monday morning.

“It was a snatched opportunity,” he says. “For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. That doesn’t take away the physical achievement of what I did. I’ve set the bar at a certain level. But whoever comes along next will move the bar further and do it without ropes or bottled oxygen.”

Everest was first climbed 60 years ago. Many stories look back to Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. People talk about their earlier time, before today’s commercialism and harm to the environment. I ask Cool to think forward and imagine what top climbers might do 60 years from now.

“I hate to think,” he says. He talks about the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. Steck left the mountain in April after what Cool calls “an altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck planned a very hard climb. He wanted to climb Everest’s west ridge, first done in 1963. Then he wanted to go down to the South Col and immediately climb Lhotse by a new route, all without fixed ropes. “Ueli had been training like a machine,” Cool says. “He’s a climber in a class all his own. He’s technically brilliant but he had also taken his physical condition to an astronomic level. It would have been amazing to see what he could have done.”

Tourism is a different issue. One clue is a helicopter rescue by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro’s harsh language helped start the confrontation at Camp 2. On Tuesday, Moro flew back to Everest in a powerful helicopter. He rescued a sick climber at 7,800 metres.

It was the highest rescue ever done on Everest. It also shows how helicopter flights have risen very fast in recent years. By 2073, the mountain might even have a helipad on the South Col. Tourists could arrive there while using bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing what is possible for rescuing climbers and the many trekkers who go as far as base camp.

Mountain geographer and environmentalist Alton Byers says it is not clear if the Everest region can handle fast-growing tourism. He says climate change and tourism together are putting new pressure on the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers are shrinking, and some are disappearing. This is already causing big problems. “Everywhere you go, people are talking about how there’s less water. There’s less water for agriculture and less water for all the new lodges that are getting built.”

Byers says that in Namche Bazaar, a Sherpa town, workers are building a new five-mile pipeline. It will bring water for the growing tourist demand for showers and flush toilets. The local stream is polluted with human waste. It also does not have enough water when the town is very crowded in high season. “Every village is digging a pit just beyond the houses for garbage. Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world,” he says. Human waste at base camp is now handled well and removed in plastic barrels. But Byers says the barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley. That pit could leak into the region’s water.

“These problems can be solved, but we need to get serious about it,” he says. “One climber can spend $85,000 climbing Everest. And that’s fine. But at some point we’re going to have to address these other priorities. For half a million dollars a year, you could solve most of them.”

Climate change is harder. Byers works with local conservation groups to plan for climate change. They often look for new water sources or collect rainwater. Fast growth of glacial lakes is a constant danger because the lakes could burst and flood the Sherpa homeland. “There’s going to come a time when people are going to have to get out of their way.”

Changing weather also affects tourism. More clouds during times that are usually clear are closing Lukla Airport more often. Lukla is the gateway to the Everest region. A new road for 4x4s is being built to Lukla to keep tourists and money coming. But Byers worries the road building in Nepal is being done cheaply. He says this can cause serious soil erosion and landslides.

“Everest is the icon everyone knows,” he says. “It’s the canary in the coalmine that everyone understands. It’s the perfect laboratory for figuring out how to address some of these problems, like the impacts of climate change and tourism.”
