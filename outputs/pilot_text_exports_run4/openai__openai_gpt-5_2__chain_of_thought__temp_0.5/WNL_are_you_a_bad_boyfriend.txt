Dan Sullivan was late to breakfast with the Guardian. He was late because police stopped him while he was riding his motorcycle.

Sullivan says he works too much. He misses dinner dates. He forgets to buy presents.

So, like many people in Silicon Valley, the 27-year-old venture capitalist made a start-up to help. It is called BetterBoyfriend.me. The service sends girlfriends and wives a present every month for about $70.

Sullivan works in finance. He has been testing the program quietly. About 350 boyfriends have signed up. They bring in about $17,000 in revenue each month. He says most members are his Harvard and investor friends. They include venture capitalists, founders, and workers at companies like Apple, Google, and Facebook. Their girlfriends have been getting presents from Sullivan for the last year.

Each month, members pick from a list of seven gifts, such as chocolate, tea sets, and manicures. The package is sent to the boyfriend with no brand name and no label.

Sullivan tests ideas by buying Facebook ads to see what happens. BetterBoyfriend.me grew fast. Sullivan said, “BetterBoyfriend hit something. It’s a pain point everyone has.”

BetterBoyfriend.me was not his first idea. For a couple of days, he thought about sharing dogs. He said he saw rich guys looking miserable walking dogs. He said a dog is only valuable 5% of the time. He wondered if someone could babysit the dogs and other people could rent them.

Another start-up, called InvisibleBoyfriend, lets users create a fake social partner who is always available to talk. The result is a “boyfriend” who can text with you all day.

Founder Kyle Tabor started it to help users stop unwanted advances. But he found people became attached to their computer-made partners. He said many users want companionship through conversation, not just “proof” of a relationship to make people leave them alone.

For Sullivan and Tabor, a surprise was the real connections they formed with customers. Sullivan says he now sees himself like a relationship consultant for the boyfriends.

Sullivan says he made mistakes. Early on, the packages had receipts with his name, Dan Sullivan. “One of the boyfriends wrote to me and said, ‘Listen, dude, she’s not mad but Cynthia found out’.”

About 50% of the women who get BetterBoyfriend.me packages know he is involved. Sullivan says it relates to age. He thinks after you have been married a while, you do not keep many secrets.

Over the year, Sullivan says he has gotten to know the boyfriends well. They even sent a delivery to a hospital delivery room. He said the key is to remember that his relationship is with the boyfriend.

When he first started the company, he put tags that said BetterBoyfriend.me on flower bouquets. He went to Dolores Park, a popular relaxing place in the Mission District, where many tech workers and young people spend time.

He said he looked for couples and gave the bouquet to the girl, but the boyfriend did not like it. He said it made sense. So he changed his plan and started giving the flowers to the boyfriends.
