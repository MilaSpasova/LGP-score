Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is theft. He says it is like putting your hand in someone’s pocket and stealing their wallet. In an article for Index on Censorship, Pullman (who is president of the Society of Authors) strongly defends copyright laws. He criticizes internet users who think it is OK to download music or books without paying.

Pullman writes that new technology is so impressive that some people do not notice how wrong their actions are. He says it is wrong that someone can steal an artist’s work and not be punished.

His article came after leaders from the music industry met British Prime Minister David Cameron at Downing Street. They talked about web piracy.

Pullman, who wrote the His Dark Materials trilogy, says authors and musicians often work for years with little money and little attention. He says that when their work finally brings joy to audiences, their chance to earn a living from it can be taken away by illegal downloading. He ends by saying the idea is simple: if we want to enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

Pullman’s article appears in the next issue of the group’s magazine as part of a dialogue with Cathy Casserly. Casserly is the chief executive of Creative Commons. Creative Commons offers open content licences that let creators control how their copyright is used. Casserly says many things are wrong with copyright because it was made in an “analogue age.” She says copyright often stops people from sharing, building on, and remixing each other’s work.

Casserly says artists need to think in new ways about how they share and make money from their work. She quotes science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees. The magazine’s editor, Rachael Jolley, said current copyright laws do not work well in the digital age and could make ordinary consumers into criminals. She said new ways are needed for artists, writers, and musicians to earn a living.

The debate is active, and illegal downloading is very large. Data collected by Ofcom (the independent regulator and competition authority for the UK communications industries) suggests that in the UK, between November 2012 and January 2013, 280 million music tracks were pirated. Also pirated were 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files.

Ofcom has said 18% of internet users over age 12 admit they recently pirated content, and 9% say they fear getting caught. Pullman also writes that getting music quickly as MP3 downloads is still surprising to people who have been building up their iTunes list for some time.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron appointed the Conservative MP Mike Weatherley as his adviser on this issue. A spokesman for the BPI (the record industry trade body) said Mike Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creative producers it protects. The spokesman said they hope Weatherley’s influence, and the prime minister’s support for copyright, will shape the approach of the UK’s intellectual property office.
