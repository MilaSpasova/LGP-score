From glow-in-the-dark trees to underground bike sheds and solar-powered bins, here are some unusual ideas to make cities easier and nicer to live in.

City life has many good points, but living in a sustainable way is becoming harder. People in cities deal with pollution, traffic, and fewer green spaces.

Here are ten of the wackier ideas to help make cities more livable.

Pop-up parks
Some cities look like they are built more for cars than for people. A pop-up park is a simple idea. You take an empty car park, a little money, and one or two pot plants, and you make a small park for a short time. The PARK(ing) project started as an art experiment in San Francisco and then spread around the world. The movement has led to ideas like temporary urban farms and ecology demonstrations. It also has a day of action every September.

Subterranean storage
Not everyone in a city uses a car. Bikes are becoming more popular. But where can you keep a bike safe? A Tokyo-based engineering firm called Giken made a plan called an “eco-cycle, anti-seismic underground bicycle park”. It is a cylinder that is only seven metres wide, and it goes deep into the ground. It can store 204 bikes. Owners can get their bike back by pressing a button. The system brings the bike back above ground in about 13 seconds.

Glow-in-the-dark trees
When many people think about trees that glow in the dark, they think of Christmas decorations. But Daan Roosegaarde does not. This Dutch designer-artist created an experimental “bioluminescent” plant. The technology mixes DNA from glowing marine bacteria with the chloroplast genome of a plant to make a jellyfish-like glow. Trials are happening to make an industrial-scale version of this biomimicry-inspired technology. Roosegaarde hopes it could one day replace normal street lighting.

Footfall harvesting
Every day, hundreds of commuters and shoppers in West Ham, in east London, cross an elevated walkway near the underground station. Most people probably do not notice that the ground feels a little springy. Even fewer people know that the rubber surface bends by five millimetres and helps power the streetlights above. The floor has smart tiles that capture the kinetic energy from footsteps and turn it into electricity. Pavegen, the UK firm behind this idea, has put a similar system in London’s Heathrow Airport and in other places around the world.

Supertrees
At some point, people were going to make man-made trees. Singapore’s Gardens by the Bay has a small group of them. These steel-framed “supertrees” can be up to 50 metres tall. Flowers and ferns grow on them, and their metal tops help absorb and spread out heat. They can also collect rainwater and help move air for two “climate-controlled biomes” (large conservatories) below. Eleven of the 18 trees also have solar panels on their “branches”.
