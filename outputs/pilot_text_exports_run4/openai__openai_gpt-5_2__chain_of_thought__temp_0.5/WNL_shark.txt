Opposition to Western Australia’s shark cull has grown. Thousands of people went to beaches across Australia to ask the state’s premier to stop the policy. RSPCA Australia and Richard Branson, who owns Virgin Atlantic, also spoke against it.

The policy involves catching and killing sharks longer than three metres. It began after what the state government called an “unprecedented” number of shark attacks on Western Australia’s coast. A 35-year-old surfer was killed in November 2013. He was the sixth person to die from a shark attack in two years.

However, the Shark Attack File says Australia as a whole has averaged one shark-related death each year for the last 50 years.

Kate Faehrmann, a board member of the Sea Shepherd Conservation Society, spoke at a protest in Perth. She said they have said from the start that the policy will not work. She said drumlines, which are used to catch sharks, can kill many animals. She said they can kill sharks of any size, and also dolphins, turtles, and other animals. She said this is why the community does not want the policy.

Thousands of people protested at Perth’s Cottesloe Beach and Sydney’s Manly Beach. Hundreds also protested at Glenelg in south-west Adelaide, and at beaches in Victoria and Queensland. Faehrmann said the protests showed many Australians want sharks protected. She said people are coming out in large numbers to say the ocean belongs to sharks too, and they do not want them killed.

Anthony Joyce, a surfer who once had his foot caught in a shark’s mouth, said killing sharks will not make a difference.

The state government has refused to give a running total of how many sharks have been killed. There have been reports that sharks smaller than three metres were released after being caught. Drumlines are floating drums anchored to the sea bed, with bait hanging on hooks below them.

Conservationists say there is no evidence the cull will reduce shark attacks on humans. They say no earlier cull used only drumlines. Researchers at the University of Western Australia say the recent rise in shark attacks in the state may be linked to Western Australia having the fastest-growing population in Australia, rather than more sharks.

Richard Peirce, chairman of the UK conservation charity the Shark Trust, said the cull will not work and could even bring more predators close to the coast. He said the drumlines do not choose what they catch. He said even if they are checked during the day, leaving them overnight could attract other predators to the area, because sharks and other animals may be hooked and injured.

Around the world in 2012, there were 80 unprovoked shark attacks, and seven were fatal. This is compared with nearly 100m sharks killed by humans each year. RSPCA Australia said it believes the cull is not justified. It said there is no evidence the increase in attacks is because there are more sharks. It said it matches changes in population and human behaviour, meaning more people are in the water.

Richard Branson told Fairfax Radio the policy is backfiring. He said he thinks Premier Colin Barnett may have done it to encourage tourism. Branson said it will do the opposite. He said it makes people think there is a big problem when there is not, and it may stop people from coming to Perth and the countryside around it. He said it will only make people worried for no reason.
