James Hamblin is a senior editor at The Atlantic magazine. In his series, “If Our Bodies Could Talk,” he tried a challenge: he stopped showering as much to see what happens when we clean our bodies too much. He took fewer showers, and when he did shower, he did not use shampoo or soap.

He learned something many other people have learned. When we try very hard to scrub our bodies with soap, body wash, and scrubbers, our skin works harder to get back to normal. Showering removes the skin’s natural oil and bacteria. This can happen even more when we use hot water and cleaning products.

Have you ever felt very dry and tight after a shower? That happens because much of your skin’s natural moisture gets washed away. Also, your skin has millions of helpful bacteria, like your gut does. Frequent rubbing and scrubbing can wipe out these bacteria. When the bacteria grow back, they can sometimes include more germs that cause bad smells. So, showering too often may make you smell more. If you stop using soap and shower less, your skin may go through an early adjustment period that can seem unpleasant. After that, the skin often returns to balance, makes less oil, and the helpful bacteria grow again.

After his experiment, Hamblin agreed with other people who avoid soap and frequent showers: the human body can work well on its own. It is not only about smell or looks. Dermatologists say using less soap can help acne and eczema. Showering less can also help the environment. The average shower lasts seven minutes and uses 65 litres of water. That is 65 litres of clean, drinkable water mixed with soap and sent down the drain every day—sometimes more than once.

Clean water is becoming harder to ignore as California goes into another summer of drought. It is clear that clean water is one of the world’s most valuable resources, and it may soon be in short supply. When you also think about the waste from body wash bottles, there are strong reasons to let your body be more natural.

If this idea makes you feel uncomfortable because you remember being close to people who seem not to shower enough—and do not smell good yet—relax. Many people who shower less still use deodorant if they need it. Everyone’s natural smell is different and can change with diet, how much water you drink, and exercise. Also, washing your hands with soap is still recommended to help stop the spread of infectious diseases.

You do not have to stop all at once like Hamblin did. But between cleaning many times a day and never showering, there is a middle choice. Shower less often, use less soap, and let the helpful bacteria on your skin grow.
