A thick layer of bird droppings covers a gold-covered railing at one of Britain’s most expensive homes. Pigeon bones lie among broken mirrors, and water runs through broken parts of the ceiling. This is The Towers, a £30m palace on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London. Its terrible condition has been kept secret until now.

The Towers is one of ten mansions in the middle of The Bishops Avenue. This road is a key part of London’s fast-rising property market. These homes have been almost completely empty since they were bought about 25 years ago. It is believed they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. Their Greek-style columns are cracking. Their mosaic-tiled swimming pools are filled with broken stone. Nature has moved in so much that owls now live there.

This empty, broken-down scene can be seen all along the avenue. Lloyds Bank has calculated that it is the second most expensive street in Britain. At the same time, more and more people struggle to buy a home in London as house prices rise by 11.2% a year. Yet 16 mansions on the most expensive stretch of The Bishops Avenue are sitting empty. Many are behind locked gates. Their windows are covered with steel bars, their grounds are overgrown, and guard dogs patrol the area.

Across the street is another run-down mansion worth £18m, with smashed windows and walls covered in anti-climb paint. Metal bars cover the windows of another mansion that has been sold for £20m.

But prices still rise. Dryades, a mansion that was owned until recently by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007 and is believed to be worth about £30m today. Heath Lodge, where fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou was murdered in 1984 by a silver bullet, is worth £13m today. It was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

These empty mansions can feel painful to people who are trying to keep a roof over their heads in one of the world’s most expensive cities. One security guard on the avenue said it was frustrating to see so much space—tens of thousands of square feet, enough to house dozens of people—falling apart.

Royals with oil wealth from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia were among the first to come to this curved road near Hampstead Heath. Iranians came here after the fall of the shah. Now Chinese buyers are following Russians and Kazakhs, who have spent millions to get an address that estate agents say is as famous as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions have been for sale at £65m and £38m. They promised lots of Italian marble, lifts with leather padding, and luxury panic rooms.

However, on the grounds of the empty mansions, stone fountains are breaking and lawns have turned into muddy wet areas. Inside one mansion, water drips through a huge crystal chandelier onto a thick carpet that is rotting under parts of a fallen ceiling. Moss grows through broken bricks. Mirror tiles are scattered across a bathroom. The swimming pool holds about a foot of dirty, slightly salty water, and flowers grow through its tiles. Wooden boards push outward from the sauna.

The worst damage is at The Towers. It is a large mansion set among acres of hornbeams, oaks, and limes. Its huge high-ceiling halls are filled with pigeons. Water pours through three storeys, turning the walls bright green with algae, and drips into a large, empty basement swimming pool. Unopened wooden crates marked “bullet-proof glass” show the security worries of the previous owners.

Today, very few people live on The Bishops Avenue all the time. A security guard outside one mansion said the owners were not there. Another guard outside Royal Mansion would not say if anyone was home. A staff member at another mansion warned the Guardian about the guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare businessman with personal wealth of £45m, said he is one of the few permanent residents.

“It’s not a friendly place where you can talk over the fence,” he said. “To be honest, I have never seen what my neighbours look like. Next door, a Saudi princess spent £35m on a new house and I’ve never seen her. There are about three houses that are lived in 24/7 and half of the properties are occupied three to six months a year. The other half, who knows if they come or not?”

These multi-million-pound wrecks show a housing culture where the richest people in the world treat British homes as investments. One Hyde Park, a block of flats in Knightsbridge, is another example. More than half the flats there are registered with the council as empty or second homes.

Even so, people on the avenue are talking about building £5m apartments instead of £50m mansions, to try to bring people back.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, has decided to rebuild one of the most valuable sites on the avenue as 20 apartments. They will have a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

“If you build a big house and try and sell for £30m to £40m, it won’t sell,” he said. “Locals won’t buy and so you have to bring in overseas buyers.”

But the idea that these empty homes could help solve the housing crisis still seems far away. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some of the run-down homes could become affordable housing. He said the land price would be too high.

“Very wealthy people own property there,” he said. “Sometimes they live in them and sometimes they don’t.”
