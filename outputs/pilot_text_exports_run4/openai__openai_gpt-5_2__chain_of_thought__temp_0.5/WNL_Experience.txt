It was a beautiful summer evening. I decided to go for a swim near Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland. I moved there in 2012. In the water, there was a woman swimming with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty often swims with a group of people, and she seems close to them.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Since then, hundreds of people have swum with her. This can make people think she is completely tame. She was even in an Irish tourist-board advertisement, where a girl says she wants to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty’s tummy. It looked safe and tempting. About 20 tourists and local people were watching from the pier. Soon after I got into the water, Dusty left the woman and suddenly became very wild. Later I learned that she is very protective of her space when she is with someone. Her tail was flapping hard. At first I thought she was showing off, but then I realized she might be angry.

I knew I had to get out. I swam toward the pier, but very quickly Dusty slammed into me with her nose. It was very strong and painful, and she was moving very fast. I was thrown forward.

People on the pier stared down at me with their mouths open. Dusty stayed beside me, still flapping her tail. That scared me the most. I thought that if she hit me with her tail, I could go under the water and die.

I reached the pier, but I could not get out because I was hurt. I felt terror. I shouted for help. A man reached in and pulled me onto the steps. Then another man came and said he was an orthopaedic surgeon who specialized in marine trauma. He had been driving into Doolin when he saw what was happening.

I was very cold and very worried. I did not know how bad my injuries were, and my biggest fear was bleeding inside my body. The surgeon checked me and tried to calm me. He said he could not feel signs of internal bleeding, but he thought I probably had broken bones. Later I learned I had six fractures in my spine, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in the hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for several weeks. I could not work for five months, and I had limited movement, stiffness, and pain. Later I was diagnosed with post-traumatic stress. The near-death experience made me anxious and made me overreact in ways I never had before. I felt like people looked at me the wrong way. Loud noises became hard for me. I also had memory loss.

Three months before the accident, I opened a health-food shop. But I had to give it up because I could not work anymore. It was the hardest year, but now it is behind me. I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building my own osteopathy practice. I understand patients better now because I have been one.

I am thankful that I am healthy, and I really want to stop other people from getting hurt. People often have a sweet idea about dolphins and trust them. Who would think a dolphin would attack a person? If a fierce animal runs at you with its teeth showing, it is scary. But dolphins have a wide smile that looks friendly.

I am not angry at Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her space, and she is a wild animal that can act in sudden ways. People need to know that. Many people come to swim with her, and they do not understand how dangerous it can be. People said my injuries were among several injuries that summer.

After the man pulled me out, Dusty swam away. Then she came back and floated upright next to me, looking at me. We looked into each other’s eyes, and I felt she was sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin, and her anger was gone. The people on the pier were amazed. After that moment, I no longer felt terror. I made peace with her.
