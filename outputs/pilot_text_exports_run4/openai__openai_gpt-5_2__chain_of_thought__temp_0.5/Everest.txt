Kenton Cool usually likes to talk, but now he can hardly speak. After working hard high in the mountains, his voice is rough. He is in Kathmandu, the capital of Nepal. That morning, he flew down from Everest base camp.

Cool is thinking about an amazing set of climbs he finished the weekend before. Early on Saturday morning, he reached the top of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of three main peaks in the Everest “horseshoe.” This horseshoe surrounds an icy valley called the Western Cwm.

Later that same day, he climbed to the top of Everest. He reached the summit in complete darkness early on Sunday. Then he and his climbing partner kept going. They climbed to the top of Lhotse on Monday morning. Lhotse was the third peak in this three-peaks challenge.

“It was a snatched opportunity,” he says. “For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. That does not remove the physical achievement of what I did. I have set the bar at a certain level. But the next person will raise the bar more and do it without ropes or bottled oxygen.”

Sixty years after Everest was first climbed, many stories look back to Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. They talk about a time before today’s commercial climbing and environmental damage. I asked Cool to look ahead and imagine what top climbers might do 60 years from now.

“I hate to think,” he says. But he talks about the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. In April, Steck left the mountain after what Cool calls “an altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck planned to climb Everest’s west ridge, first climbed in 1963. Then Steck planned to go down to the South Col and immediately climb Lhotse by a new route, all without fixed ropes. “Ueli had been training like a machine,” Cool says. “He is a climber in a class all his own. He is technically brilliant, and he had also raised his fitness to an astronomic level. It would have been amazing to see what he could have done.”

What tourism will look like is a different question. One clue is a helicopter rescue done by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro’s angry words helped start the conflict at Camp 2. On Tuesday, Moro flew a high-powered helicopter back to Everest to rescue a badly hurt climber at 7,800 metres.

This was the highest rescue ever done on Everest. It also shows how fast helicopter flights have increased in recent years. By 2073, the mountain could even have a helipad on the South Col. Tourists could be brought there while breathing bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing what is possible for rescuing both climbers and the many trekkers who go as far as base camp.

Mountain geographer and environmentalist Alton Byers says it is not clear if the Everest region can keep up with fast-growing tourism. He says climate change and tourism together are creating new pressure on the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers in the Everest region are shrinking, and some are disappearing. This is already having a big effect. “Everywhere you go, people are talking about how there’s less water,” he says. “There’s less water for farming and less water for all the new lodges being built.”

Byers says that in the Sherpa town of Namche Bazaar, workers are laying a new five-mile pipeline to bring water. This is to meet tourists’ growing demand for showers and flush toilets. The local stream is polluted by human waste. It also does not have enough water for a town that is extremely crowded in high season. “Every village is digging a pit just beyond the houses for garbage. Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world,” he says.

Human waste at base camp is now handled well and taken away in plastic barrels. But Byers says the barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley. That pit could leak into the region’s rivers and streams.

“These problems can be solved, but we need to get serious about it,” he says. “One climber can spend $85,000 climbing Everest. And that’s fine. But at some point we will have to deal with these other needs. For half a million dollars a year, you could solve most of them.”

Climate change is harder. Byers works with local conservation groups to find and plan for climate change effects. This often means finding new water sources or collecting rainwater. Another danger is that glacial lakes are growing quickly. These lakes could burst and flood the Sherpa homeland. “There’s going to come a time when people are going to have to get out of their way,” he says.

Changing weather is also affecting tourism. More clouds during times that are usually clear are closing Lukla Airport more often. Lukla Airport is the main entry point to the Everest region. A new road for 4x4 vehicles is being built to Lukla to keep tourists and money coming. But Byers worries the road network in Nepal is being built cheaply and quickly. He says this can lead to serious soil erosion and landslides.

“Everest is the icon everyone knows,” he says. “It’s the canary in the coalmine that everyone understands. It is the perfect place to learn how to deal with problems like climate change and tourism.”
