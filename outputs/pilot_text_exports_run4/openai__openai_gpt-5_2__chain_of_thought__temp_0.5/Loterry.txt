The lottery balls drop down the chute and all six numbers match. Now it is time to buy an Audi, book a holiday in the US, and phone the estate agent. An analysis says many lottery millionaires do these things.

Since it started in 1994, the lottery has made 3,000 millionaires. Together they have won more than £8.5bn, with an average of £2.8m each. A study by Oxford Economics says their money also helped create 3,780 more millionaires among their children, family, and friends.

Most winners (59%) stop working right away. But 19% keep doing their job, and 31% do unpaid volunteer work. The study says 98% of winners’ spending stayed in the UK. It estimates that each winner’s spending on homes, vehicles, and holidays keeps six people in full-time jobs for one year.

The winners added almost £750m to gross domestic product (GDP). They also created more than £500m in tax money for the Exchequer. Most of their money went on property. Winners spent £2.72bn on their main homes, and £170m to pay off existing debt and mortgages.

Keeping a steady income mattered to winners, so £2.125bn went into investments. Gifts to family and friends were £1.17bn. Cars and holidays cost £680m.

Camelot, the company that runs the UK National Lottery, asked for the study to mark the 3,000-winners milestone. The study used research from 100 winners who each won £1m or more. It found that all 3,000 winners bought 7,958 houses or flats in the UK. That is 2.7 homes each, costing £3.3bn in total. Most winners (82%) changed their main home, spending an average of £900,000.

Many winners added extra features to their homes. Almost a third (29%) bought a hot tub. A walk-in wardrobe was important for 28%. Nearly a quarter (24%) chose a home behind electric gates. Some (22%) had a games room, and 7% put in a snooker table.

Bigger homes need care. So 30% of winners hired a cleaner, and 24% hired a gardener. A small number (5%) hired a beautician.

Audis were the favourite car for 16% of winners. Range Rovers and BMWs were also popular (11% each), as well as Mercedes (10%) and Land Rovers (5%). Winners spent £463m on 17,190 cars. The average price of their favourite car was £46,116.

Holidays were important too. Most winners (68%) chose five-star hotels overseas. The US was the top place to visit for 27%, followed by the Caribbean (9%). Some money was spent closer to home. Over the past 18 years, 10% of millionaire winners bought a caravan in the UK, creating about £7.4m in sales.

Some winners (15%) started their own business. Also, 9% helped other people start a business, and 6% invested in or bought other people’s businesses. The study says businesses started or supported by lottery winners employ 3,195 people.

Andy Logan, who wrote the report, said the effect of a win spreads further than expected. He said it changes the lives of friends and family, and each win has a clear effect on the UK economy because so much money is spent in the UK. He said each win creates a ripple effect in this generation and often the next.
