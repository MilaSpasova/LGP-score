Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8th April, 2014, she told about an unusual moment. She received a message in a bottle, 101 years after her grandfather threw it into the Baltic Sea. People think it may be the world’s oldest message in a bottle. A museum in Germany gave it to her and is now showing it.

Erdmann, 62, said it was a big surprise. She said a man came to her door and told her he had mail from her grandfather. He said a bottle with a message had been found, and the name on the card was her grandfather’s name. The visitor was a family history researcher. He found Erdmann in Berlin after the letter was given to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg, a port city in northern Germany.

The bottle was a brown beer bottle. It had been in the water for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it in his catch while he was in the Baltic Sea near the northern city of Kiel. Holger von Neuhoff, a museum worker in charge of ocean and science, said it was the oldest bottled message he had seen. He said the museum has older documents that were found without a bottle. But with both the bottle and the document together, this one is the oldest for now. He said it is in very good condition.

Researchers believe Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913. He was hiking with a group that enjoyed nature. He was 20 years old then.

Much of the postcard was too hard to read. But people could read the Berlin address on the front. They could also read his polite request asking the finder to send the note to his home address.

Erdmann said he also put two stamps from that time inside the bottle, so the finder would not have to pay. She said he did not think it would take 101 years.

Erdmann said she felt deeply touched by the message. She did not know her grandfather because he died at age 54, six years before she was born.

She said she knew very little about him. But she learned he was a writer who was open-minded. He believed in freedom and that everyone should respect each other. She said he did a lot for young people, and later he traveled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful because she could see where her family roots came from.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and traveling around the world. She said she is open-minded too. She said what her grandfather taught his two daughters, her mother taught her, and she then taught her sons.

Even though she was happy to receive the message, she said she hopes others will not throw bottles with messages into the sea today. She said the sea is already full of bottles and rubbish, so people should not add more.

The message and bottle will be on display at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts will try to read the rest of the text. It is not clear what will happen to the bottle later, but Erdmann hopes it will stay at the museum.

She said, “We want to make a few photos available to put with the bottle and give it a face, so visitors can see the young man who threw the bottle into the water.”
