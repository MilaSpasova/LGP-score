This invention could be as useful as Trevor Baylis’s wind-up radio in the pre-internet 1990s. It is a cheap light that gets free power from gravity. It could stop people in Africa and India from using dangerous kerosene lamps.

The British designer Patrick Hunt tried to get money from banks or venture capital investors so he could start selling the light. But he had a problem. He said getting funding was slow and complicated, the idea was unproven, and nobody wanted to take the risk.

So he tried crowdfunding (raising money from many people) on a US website called Indiegogo, which had recently opened in the UK. In five days he reached his goal and raised £36,200. By the end of 2012, after 40 days, he had raised £400,000.

The LED light is powered by a dynamo. The dynamo is turned by a 10kg bag of rocks as it goes down. The bag is attached to the light and lifted to about 2m high. As the bag slowly falls to the ground, it makes enough power for half an hour of light.

Hunt is getting ready to make the light in China. Before full mass production, he will send 1,000 lights to Africa to test the market. He hopes to later make millions of units.

Hunt is part of a new group of entrepreneurs who use the fast-growing crowdfunding industry to get money. Another new site is InvestingZone, which matches rich individuals with start-up entrepreneurs.

Indiegogo does not give investors shares. Instead, it lets people offer “perks” for different donation amounts. People who helped fund Hunt’s light not only felt good about helping poorer people, but also got their own light.

Danae Ringelmann, a co-founder of Indiegogo, said the “gravity light” shows how crowdfunding can reward good ideas and test an entrepreneur’s plan. She said fundraising can be fast and fair, because it is not about knowing bank decision makers or people in a venture capital firm. It is about proving your value to customers and fans, getting them to support your idea, and funding it.

She also said even ideas that do not get funded are worth testing, because you can save time by learning sooner that it is not a good idea.

Ringelmann is based in the US. She began her career as a Wall Street analyst. In 2008, she quit and decided to use her skills to help friends in the arts raise money.

At first, the site focused on films. It launched at the Sundance Film Festival that year.

Five years later, Indiegogo was raising about $2m a week for new businesses that were starting or growing. In December 2012, it launched services in euros and in pounds sterling to grow in Europe. It says Britain is its third biggest market. It also says international activity is up 41% since December.

Indiegogo has many competitors, including Kickstarter, Seedrs, and Funding Circle. But Ringelmann says Indiegogo is different because anyone can start a campaign. No project is seen as too strange.

The site charges a 4% fee for campaigns that reach their goal. If a campaign does not reach its goal, users can choose to return all the money to donors for free, or keep the money raised but pay a 9% fee.

A British woman, Lauren Pears, raised £100,000 on the site to open a “cat café” in London. It is called Lady Dinah’s Cat Emporium. It has not opened yet, but it is described as a place where people can sit in a comfortable wingback chair with a hot cup of tea, a book, and a cat.

Ringelmann said some campaigns are rejected by venture capital investors who say, “Great idea, but we don’t know if the market really wants it; it could be a gadget no one cares about.” She said entrepreneurs can run an Indiegogo campaign, even before launching the product, and the campaign can show venture capitalists that a market exists.

She said crowdfunding lets you test your market, your prices, and your features. It can also help you find new ways to make money and get important feedback.

Ringelmann said that many people have ideas, and what matters is doing the work to make the idea happen. She said that if you are scared someone will steal your idea and do it better and faster, then you are not the right person to carry it out. She said it is about confidence, moving fast, and learning.

Ringelmann said expanding into Europe and making a deal with a web payments company are part of a plan to make finance more open to everyone. The payment system will allow local card payments like Maestro in the UK and Carte Bleue in France, as well as PayPal. More than 7,000 funding campaigns are live on the site.

Crowdfunding has grown as an alternative to banks, but it is not very attractive to very rich investors. They often want a stake in a promising business. In the UK, that may start to change with the launch of InvestingZone.
