Anitta is a new funk star in Brazil. She has won millions of fans by bringing favela music into the mainstream. But she is now in the middle of a debate about skin colour.

Some anti-discrimination groups and writers say the music industry’s fastest rising star had to give up looking black to sell music to a mostly white, middle-class market.

The debate started after people shared “before and after” photos of Anitta. They show her skin looking much lighter after she signed a deal with Warner.

In the first photo, when she was not famous, she looks darker. In the second photo, a marketing picture after she became famous, she looks paler. People argue about why. It could be whitening products, cosmetic surgery, or (more likely) Photoshop. The big difference made people talk again about whether you must be pale to succeed in Brazil.

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote that the photos show hidden discrimination in media and marketing. She said many people do not want to admit racism. She said they talk about a “multiracial democracy,” but statistics show that is not true. She said society often treats white as the image of rich, nice, successful, and good, and treats black as the opposite.

Anitta was born Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

She became very famous with an album and a big hit single, Show das Poderosas. It reached number one on the charts and got 52 million views on YouTube.

Many people love her as a pop idol with catchy songs and a strong message. Her supporters also show her as a bridge between the mostly black and mixed-race shanty towns on Rio’s hillsides and the richer and whiter areas below.

She has made baile funk “softer” for the mainstream. She reduced suggestive dancing, gangsta talk, and explicit lyrics. Now some people ask if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract a richer group of customers. Arraes said pop stars may feel forced to change things like curly hair or a big nose, and that this can hurt self-esteem.

This topic is sensitive in Brazil, a country with many mixed-race people. Brazil was one of the last large countries to ban slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. In Brazil, people often focus more on skin colour than on race and ancestry. And even though the country aims to be a multiracial democracy, skin tone is clearly linked to inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Until 2011, black or mixed-race students also spent, on average, two fewer years in school.

The government says the gap is shrinking because of quotas for university places and other affirmative action. But big differences remain. Most business and government leaders are white. Many low-paid jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In places like Ipanema and Gávea, you are more likely to see black nannies pushing strollers with white toddlers than a white nanny pushing a black child.

Colour labels in Brazil can be complicated. In the 2010 census, people who called themselves white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million black, two million Asian, and 0.5% indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology lecturer at the Federal University of Pernambuco, said Anitta won middle-class fans by taking a rebellious sound and making it tamer and easier for everyone to accept. He said this was not “racial whitening.” He said it was a change in social space: Anitta’s art moved from the periphery to the centre.

Other people say the colour issue is being made too big. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who worked with Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot, but not her colour. He said it was about makeup, hair styling, and clothing. He said he did not think she was being pressured. He said she always liked to show off, sing, and dance, and that this natural side helped her succeed.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador, said the prejudice was not mainly in the public, but in music producers and media bosses. He said the public showed they care about music itself by choosing Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, as a recent winner of The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for comment. But in a recent interview, she said identity should be defined by the person. She said an “all-powerful” woman does not need to be beautiful. She said a woman can be powerful because of her attitude. She said her work tries to show that we can be who we want.
