Dan Sullivan was late to breakfast with the Guardian because police stopped him while he was riding his motorcycle.

Sullivan says he works too much. He misses dinner dates. He forgets to buy presents.

So the 27-year-old venture capitalist in Silicon Valley made a start-up to help: BetterBoyfriend.me. It sends girlfriends and wives a present every month for about $70.

Sullivan works in finance. He has been testing the program quietly. He has signed up about 350 boyfriends. That brings in about $17,000 each month. He says most members are his Harvard and investor friends, like venture capitalists, founders, and workers at companies like Apple, Google, and Facebook. Their girlfriends have been getting presents from him for the last year.

Each month, members choose from seven gifts, like chocolate, tea sets, and manicures. The gift is sent to the boyfriend without a brand name or label.

Sullivan tests ideas by buying Facebook ads to see if people are interested. BetterBoyfriend.me became popular fast. He said it fixed a problem many people have.

BetterBoyfriend.me was not his first idea. For a few days, he thought about a dog-sharing idea. He said he saw rich men who looked unhappy walking dogs. He thought a dog is only valuable 5% of the time. He wondered if one person could watch the dogs and other people could rent them.

Another start-up, called InvisibleBoyfriend, lets users design a make-believe social partner who is always available to talk. The result is a “boyfriend” who can text with you all day.

Founder Kyle Tabor started it to help users stop unwanted attention. But he found that people became attached to their computer-made partners. He said many users want companionship through conversation, not just “proof” of a relationship.

For Sullivan and Tabor, a surprise was that real relationships formed with customers. Sullivan says he has started to act like a relationship consultant for the boyfriends.

Sullivan says he made mistakes. At first, the packages had receipts with his name, Dan Sullivan. One boyfriend wrote to him and said, “Listen, dude, she’s not mad but Cynthia found out.”

Sullivan says about 50% of the women who get BetterBoyfriend.me packages know he is involved. He says this connects to age. He thinks that after people have been married for a while, they do not keep many secrets.

Over the year, Sullivan says he has gotten to know the boyfriends well. They even sent a package to a hospital delivery room. He says the important thing is to remember that his relationship is with the boyfriend.

When he first started the company, he put BetterBoyfriend.me tags on flower bouquets and went to Dolores Park in the Mission District. He looked for couples and gave the bouquet to the girl, but the boyfriend did not like it. So he changed his plan and started giving the bouquets to the boyfriends.
