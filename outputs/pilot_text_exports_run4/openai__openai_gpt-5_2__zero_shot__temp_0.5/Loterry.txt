The lottery balls fall down the chute. If all six numbers match, some people think it is time to buy an Audi, book a holiday in the US, and call an estate agent. A study says many lottery millionaires spend money in these ways.

Since it started in 1994, the lottery has made 3,000 millionaires. Together they have won more than £8.5bn, with an average of £2.8m each. The study authors, Oxford Economics, say this also helped create 3,780 more millionaires among winners’ children, family, and friends.

Most winners (59%) stop working right away. But 19% keep doing their job, and 31% do unpaid voluntary work. The study says 98% of winners’ spending stayed in the UK. By spending on homes, cars, and holidays, each winner is estimated to keep six people in full-time work for one year.

Winners have added almost £750m to GDP and created more than £500m in tax for the Exchequer. Most money was spent on property. Winners spent £2.72bn on their main homes, and £170m paying off debt and mortgages.

Winners also wanted to keep an income, so they spent £2.125bn on investments. They gave £1.17bn as gifts to family and friends. They spent £680m on cars and holidays.

Camelot, the company that runs the UK National Lottery, paid for the study to mark 3,000 winners. The study used research from 100 winners who won £1m or more. It found that the 3,000 winners bought 7,958 houses or flats in the UK, about 2.7 each, spending £3.3bn. Most winners (82%) moved to a new main home, spending an average of £900,000.

Many winners added extra things to their homes. Almost a third (29%) bought a hot tub. A walk-in wardrobe was chosen by 28%. Nearly a quarter (24%) picked a home behind electric gates. A games room was chosen by 22%, and 7% put in a snooker table.

Bigger homes need care. 30% of winners hired a cleaner and 24% hired a gardener. A small group (5%) hired a beautician.

Audis were the favourite car for 16% of winners. Range Rovers and BMWs were also popular (11% each). Mercedes were 10%, and Land Rovers were 5%. Winners spent £463m on 17,190 cars. The average price of their favourite car was £46,116.

Holidays were important too. Most winners (68%) chose five-star hotels overseas. The US was the top place to go for 27%, and the Caribbean was next at 9%. Some winners also helped UK caravan sales. Over the past 18 years, 10% of millionaires bought a caravan, creating about £7.4m in sales.

Some winners (15%) started their own business. Another 9% helped other people start a business, and 6% invested in or bought other people’s businesses. The study says these businesses employ 3,195 people.

Andy Logan, who wrote the report, said the effect of winning spreads further than they expected. He said it changes the lives of friends and family, and each win also affects the UK economy because so much money is spent in the UK. He said each win creates a ripple effect for this generation and often the next.
