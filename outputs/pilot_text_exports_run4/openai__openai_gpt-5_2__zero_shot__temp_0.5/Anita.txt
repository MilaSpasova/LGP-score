Anitta is a new Brazilian star in funk music. She has millions of fans. She helped bring the favela sound into popular music. But there is a debate about her skin colour.

Some anti-discrimination groups and writers say Anitta had to give up looking black to sell music to a mostly white, middle-class audience.

The debate grew after people shared “then-and-now” photos. They show Anitta’s skin looking much lighter after she signed a deal with Warner. In the first photo, from when she was not famous, she looked darker. In the second photo, a marketing picture after she became famous, she looks paler. People argued about why this happened. Some mentioned whitening products or cosmetic surgery, but others said it was more likely Photoshop. The photos started the discussion again about whether people need pale skin to succeed in Brazil.

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote a post about this. She said the media and marketing show hidden discrimination. She wrote that many people say they are not racist and believe Brazil is a multiracial democracy, but statistics show otherwise. She said whitening suggests a society that does not support diversity. She said people often connect white skin with being rich, nice, successful, and good, and connect black skin with the opposite.

Anitta’s real name is Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

She became very famous with an album and a big hit song, Show das Poderosas. The song reached number one and got 52 million views on YouTube.

Many fans love her as a pop idol with a strong message and catchy songs. The people who support her also describe her as a bridge between the mostly black and mixed-race shanty towns on Rio’s hillsides and the richer and whiter neighborhoods below.

She has made baile funk less explicit. She reduced suggestive dancing, gangsta references, and explicit lyrics. Now some people ask if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract a richer audience. Arraes said that if a pop star has curly hair, they may feel forced to straighten it. If they have a big nose, they may feel forced to get rhinoplasty. She said this can harm self-esteem.

Race is a sensitive topic in Brazil, a country with many mixed-race people. Brazil was one of the last large countries to ban slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. But in Brazil, colour often matters more than race or ancestry. Even though the country aims to be a multiracial democracy, skin tone is clearly linked to inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Until 2011, black or mixed-race students also spent about two fewer years in school on average.

The government says the gap is getting smaller because of quota systems for university places and other affirmative action policies. But inequality is still easy to see. Most business and government leaders are white, while many low-paid jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In Ipanema, Gávea, and other rich areas, you are more likely to see black nannies pushing strollers with white toddlers than a white nanny pushing a black child.

Colour categories in Brazil can be complicated. In the 2010 census, people who called themselves white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million said black, two million said Asian, and 0.5% said indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology lecturer at the Federal University of Pernambuco, said Anitta won over the middle class by taking a rebellious style and making it calmer and easier for many people to accept. He said this was not because of racial whitening. He said it was a change in where she made her art: from the periphery to the centre.

Other people also said the colour issue is exaggerated. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who worked with Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot but not her colour. He said it was more about makeup, hairstylists, and clothing. He said he did not think she was being pressured. He said she always liked to show off, sing, and dance, and that this naturalness helped her succeed.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador, said the prejudice was not in the public, but in music producers and media bosses. He said the public showed they care about music itself by choosing Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, as the recent winner of the talent show The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for comment. But in a recent interview, she said identity should be defined by the person. She said an “all-powerful” woman does not need to be beautiful. She said a woman can be powerful because of her attitude. She said she tries to show people that we can be who we want.
