From glow-in-the-dark trees to bike sheds under the ground and bins powered by the sun, there are many unusual ideas to make cities nicer places to live.

Living in a city can be great, but it is often hard to live in a way that is good for the planet. People deal with pollution, heavy traffic, and fewer green spaces.

Here are some of the wackier ideas to help make cities more livable.

Pop-up parks
Some cities seem built more for cars than for people. A pop-up park is a simple idea: use an empty car park, a little money, and a few plants to make a small park for a short time. The PARK(ing) project started as an art experiment in San Francisco and later spread around the world. People have also made temporary city farms and ecology demonstrations. The movement has a day of action every September.

Subterranean storage
Not everyone in cities uses a car. Bikes are becoming more popular, but people need a safe place to store them. A Tokyo engineering company called Giken designed an “eco-cycle, anti-seismic underground bicycle park.” It is a cylinder that is only seven metres wide, and it goes deep enough to store 204 bikes. Bike owners can get their bike back by pressing a button, and the system brings it above ground in about 13 seconds.

Glow-in-the-dark trees
Some people think of glowing trees as Christmas lights. Dutch designer-artist Daan Roosegaarde imagined something different. He created an experimental “bioluminescent” plant. The technology mixes DNA from glowing marine bacteria with part of a plant to make a glow like a jellyfish. Tests are happening to make this on a large scale. Roosegaarde hopes it could one day replace normal street lights.

Footfall harvesting
In West Ham in east London, many commuters and shoppers walk across a raised walkway near the underground station. Most do not notice that the ground feels a bit springy. The rubber surface moves about five millimetres. Under the walkway are smart tiles that capture the energy from people’s footsteps and turn it into electricity to power the streetlights above. The UK company Pavegen made this system and has also installed it at London’s Heathrow Airport and other places.

Supertrees
Singapore’s Gardens by the Bay has man-made “supertrees.” They are steel-framed and can be up to 50 metres tall. Flowers and ferns grow on them. Their metal tops help take in and spread out heat. They can collect rainwater and help ventilate two “climate-controlled biomes” (large conservatories) below. Eleven of the 18 trees also have solar panels on their “branches.”
