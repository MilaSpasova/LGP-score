One beautiful summer evening, I decided to go swimming near Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland. I moved there in 2012. In the water, there was a woman swimming with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty often swims with a group of people she knows.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Since then, hundreds of people have swum with her, so she can seem very tame. She was even in an Irish tourist ad where a girl says she wants to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty’s tummy, and it looked very friendly. About 20 tourists and local people were watching from the pier. Soon after I got into the water, Dusty left the woman and suddenly became wild. Later I learned that she is very protective of her space when she is with someone. Her tail was flapping hard. At first I thought it was just a show, but then I realized she might be angry.

I knew I had to get out, so I swam toward the pier. But in a split second, Dusty rammed into me with her nose. It was extremely strong and painful, and she was very fast. I was thrown forward.

People on the pier stared down at me with their mouths open. Dusty stayed beside me, still beating her tail. That scared me most. I thought if she hit me with her tail, I could be pushed underwater.

I reached the pier, but I could not climb out because I was hurt. I felt pure terror. I shouted for help. A man reached in and pulled me onto the steps. Then another man came and said he was an orthopaedic surgeon who specialized in marine injuries. He had been driving into Doolin when he saw what was happening. I was very cold and very worried. I did not know how badly I was injured, and I feared internal bleeding. He checked me and told me he could not find signs of internal bleeding, but he thought I had broken bones. Later I learned I had six spinal fractures, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for several weeks. I could not work for five months, and I had trouble moving, plus stiffness and pain. Later I was diagnosed with post-traumatic stress. The near-death experience made me anxious, and I overreacted in ways I never had before. I felt like people were looking at me the wrong way. Loud noises were hard to handle, and I had memory loss.

Three months before the accident, I had opened a health-food shop, but I had to give it up because I could no longer work. That year was the hardest, but now it is in the past. I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building my own osteopathy practice. I understand patients better because I have been one.

I am thankful I am healthy, and I want to stop other people from getting hurt. People often think dolphins are always safe and friendly. Who would imagine a dolphin could attack a person? If you see a dangerous animal coming with its teeth showing, it is scary. But dolphins have a wide smile, so they do not look threatening.

I am not angry with Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her space, and she is a wild animal that can act in unexpected ways. People need to know that. Many people come to swim with her, and they do not understand how dangerous it can be. My injuries were said to be among several injuries that summer.

After the man pulled me out, Dusty swam away. Then she came back and floated upright near me, looking at me. We looked into each other’s eyes, and I felt she was sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin, and the anger was gone. The people on the pier watched in amazement. After that moment with her, I no longer felt terror. I felt at peace with her.
