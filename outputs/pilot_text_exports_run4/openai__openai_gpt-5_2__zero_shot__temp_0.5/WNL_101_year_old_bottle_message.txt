Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8th April, 2014, she told people about an unusual event. She received a message in a bottle. Her grandfather had thrown the bottle into the Baltic Sea 101 years earlier. People think it may be the world’s oldest message in a bottle. A museum in Germany showed it to Erdmann and is now displaying it.

Erdmann, 62, said it was a big surprise. She said a man came to her door and said he had mail from her grandfather. He explained that a message in a bottle had been found, and the name on the card was her grandfather’s name. The visitor was a family history researcher. He found Erdmann in Berlin after the letter was given to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg.

The bottle was a brown beer bottle. It had been in the water for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it in his catch while fishing in the Baltic Sea near the northern city of Kiel. Holger von Neuhoff, a curator at the museum, said it was the oldest bottled message he had seen. He said the museum has older documents, but not with the bottle. He said the bottle and the paper were in very good condition.

Researchers believe Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913. He did it while hiking with a nature group. He was 20 years old.

Most of the postcard could not be read. But people could read the Berlin address on the front. They could also read a polite request asking the finder to send the note to his home address.

Erdmann said her grandfather also put two stamps from that time in the bottle, so the finder would not have to pay. She said he did not think it would take 101 years.

Erdmann said the message made her feel emotional. She did not know her grandfather because he died at 54, six years before she was born.

She said she knew very little about him. But she learned he was a writer who was open-minded. She said he believed in freedom and that everyone should respect each other. She said he did a lot for young people, and later traveled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful to learn about her roots.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and traveling around the world, like her grandfather. She said she is open-minded too. She said what he taught his two daughters, her mother taught her, and she taught it to her sons.

Even though she was happy to get the message, she said she hoped others would not copy her grandfather and throw bottles into the sea. She said the sea is now full of bottles and rubbish, and people should not add more.

The message and the bottle were planned to be on display at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts would try to read the rest of the text. It was not clear what would happen to the bottle later, but Erdmann hoped it would stay at the museum.

She said they wanted to add some photos with the bottle, so visitors could see the young man who threw it into the water.
