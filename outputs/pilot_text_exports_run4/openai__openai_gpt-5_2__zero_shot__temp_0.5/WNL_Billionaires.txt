A thick layer of bird droppings covers a shiny railing at one of Britain’s most expensive homes. Dead pigeons are inside, mirrors are broken, and water runs through broken ceiling edges. This house is called The Towers. It is a £30m mansion on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London. People have kept its ruin secret until now.

The Towers is one of 10 big houses in the middle of The Bishops Avenue. This street is part of London’s fast-rising housing market. These homes have been almost empty for about 25 years. People believe they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. The houses are falling apart. Their columns are cracking. Their swimming pools, covered in small tiles, are filled with rubble. Nature has taken over so much that owls now live there.

This sad sight is seen up and down this street, which Lloyds Bank says is the second most expensive street in Britain. At the same time, more and more people find it hard to buy a home in London. House prices rise by 11.2% a year. But 16 mansions on the most expensive part of The Bishops Avenue are empty. Many are behind locked gates. Their windows are covered with metal bars, and the gardens are overgrown. Guard dogs patrol the land.

Across the street is another empty mansion worth £18m, with broken windows and walls covered in anti-climb paint. Another house has metal bars on its windows and was sold for £20m.

Even so, prices keep going up. Dryades, a mansion that was owned until recently by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007 and is believed to be worth about £30m today. Heath Lodge, where fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou was murdered in 1984 by a silver bullet, is worth £13m today. It was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

Seeing these empty, broken homes can feel painful for people who are trying just to keep a place to live in one of the world’s most expensive cities. A security guard said it was upsetting to watch so much space—enough to house dozens of people—fall apart.

Rich royals with oil money from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia were among the first to come to this curved road near Hampstead Heath. Iranians came after the fall of the shah. Now, Chinese buyers are coming after Russians and Kazakhs who have spent millions. Estate agents say the address is as famous as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions were for sale for £65m and £38m. They promised Italian marble, lifts with leather padding, and luxury panic rooms.

But on the land around the empty homes, stone fountains crumble and lawns turn into wet bogs. Inside one house, water drips through a huge crystal chandelier onto a thick carpet. Parts of the ceiling have collapsed, and the carpet is rotting. Moss grows through broken bricks. Mirror tiles are scattered in a bathroom. The swimming pool has about a foot of dirty water, and flowers grow through the tiles. Wooden pieces on the sauna push outward.

The most dramatic ruin is The Towers. It is a large house set among many trees, like hornbeams, oaks, and limes. Pigeons fill its tall halls. Water pours through three floors. The walls have turned bright green with algae. Water drops into a huge, empty basement swimming pool. Unopened wooden boxes marked “bullet-proof glass” show the past owners feared for safety.

Today, very few people live on The Bishops Avenue all the time. A guard outside one house said the owners were not there. Another guard outside Royal Mansion would not say if anyone was home. A staff member at another house warned about guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare mogul with personal wealth of £45m, said he is one of the few people who live there permanently.

He said, “It’s not a neighbourly place, where you can chat over the fence. To be honest, I have never seen what my neighbours look like. Next door, a Saudi princess spent £35m on a new house and I’ve never seen her. There are about three houses that are lived in 24/7 and half of the properties are occupied three to six months a year. The other half, who knows if they come or not?”

These broken, very expensive homes show a property culture where some of the world’s richest people buy British homes as investments. One Hyde Park, an apartment building in Knightsbridge, is another example. More than half of its flats are registered as empty or as second homes.

Still, people on the avenue talk about building £5m apartments instead of £50m mansions, to try to bring people back.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, plans to rebuild one valuable site into 20 apartments. They would include a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

He said, “If you build a big house and try and sell for £30m to £40m, it won’t sell. Locals won’t buy and so you have to bring in overseas buyers.”

But using these empty homes to help with the housing crisis still seems far away. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some of the run-down homes could become affordable housing. He said the land price would be too high.

He said, “Very wealthy people own property there. Sometimes they live in them and sometimes they don’t.”
