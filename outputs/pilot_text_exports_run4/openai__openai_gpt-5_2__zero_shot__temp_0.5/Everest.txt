Kenton Cool usually likes to talk, but now he can hardly speak. Climbing high mountains made his voice rough. He is in Kathmandu, the capital of Nepal. He flew down from Everest base camp that morning.

Cool is thinking about what he climbed the weekend before. Early on Saturday, he reached the top of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of three main summits in the Everest “horseshoe.” This horseshoe surrounds a valley of ice called the Western Cwm.

Later that same day, Cool climbed to the top of Everest. He reached the summit in total darkness early on Sunday. Then he and his climbing partner kept going. They climbed to the summit of Lhotse on Monday morning. Lhotse is the third peak in this three-peaks challenge.

Cool says it was a lucky chance. For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. He says this does not remove the hard physical work he did. But he also says the next person may go further and do it without ropes or bottled oxygen.

It has been 60 years since Everest was first climbed. Many stories look back to Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. People talk about a more innocent time, before today’s commercialism and environmental damage. The writer asks Cool to look ahead and imagine what top climbers might do 60 years from now.

Cool says he does not like to think about it. But he talks about the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. In April, Steck left the mountain after what Cool calls “an altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck planned a very hard climb: up Everest’s west ridge, first climbed in 1963, then down to the South Col, then straight up Lhotse by a new route. Steck wanted to do this without fixed ropes. Cool says Steck trained extremely hard. He says Steck was very skilled and very fit, and it would have been amazing to see what he could have done.

What tourism will be like is another question. One sign is a helicopter rescue done by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro’s angry language helped cause the fight at Camp 2. On Tuesday, Moro flew a powerful helicopter back to Everest. He rescued a sick or hurt climber at 7,800 metres.

This was the highest rescue ever done on Everest. It also shows that helicopter flights have increased very fast in recent years. By 2073, there could be more building on the mountain, like a helipad on the South Col. Tourists could be brought there while breathing bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing rescue for climbers and also for the many trekkers who go as far as base camp.

Mountain geographer and environmentalist Alton Byers says it is not clear if the Everest region can keep handling fast-growing tourism. He says climate change and tourism together are putting new pressure on the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers in the Everest region are shrinking, and some are disappearing. Byers says this already has a big effect. People say there is less water. There is less water for farming, and less water for the new lodges being built.

Byers says that in the Sherpa town of Namche Bazaar, workers are building a new five-mile pipeline to bring water. This is to meet tourist demand for showers and flush toilets. The local stream is dirty with human waste. It also does not have enough water when it is high season and the town is very crowded. Byers says every village is digging a pit near the houses for garbage. He says Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world. At base camp, human waste is now handled well and taken away in plastic barrels. But Byers says the barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley, and it could leak into the region’s rivers and streams.

Byers says these problems can be solved, but people must take them seriously. He says one climber can spend $85,000 climbing Everest, and that is fine. But he says people will need to focus on other needs too. He says for half a million dollars a year, most of the problems could be fixed.

Climate change is harder. Byers works with local conservation committees to plan for climate change. They often look for new water sources or collect rainwater. He says glacial lakes are growing fast, and they could break and flood the Sherpa homeland. He says there may come a time when people will have to get out of the way.

Changing weather also affects tourism. More clouds during times that are usually clear are closing Lukla Airport more often. Lukla Airport is the main entry to the Everest region. A new road for 4x4 vehicles is being built to Lukla to keep tourists and money coming. But Byers worries the road building in Nepal is being done cheaply. He says this could cause serious soil erosion and landslides.

Byers says everyone knows Everest. He says it is like a warning sign that everyone understands. He says it is a perfect place to learn how to deal with problems like climate change and tourism.
