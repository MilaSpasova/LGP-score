Opposition to Western Australia’s shark cull has grown. Thousands of people went to beaches around Australia to ask the state’s premier to stop the policy. RSPCA Australia and Virgin Atlantic owner Richard Branson also spoke against it.

The policy catches and kills sharks longer than three metres. It began after what the state government called an “unprecedented” number of shark attacks on Western Australia’s coast. A 35-year-old surfer was killed in November 2013. He was the sixth person to die from a shark attack in two years.

But the Shark Attack File says Australia as a whole has averaged one shark-related death a year for the last 50 years.

Kate Faehrmann, from the Sea Shepherd Conservation Society, spoke at a protest in Perth. She said the policy will not work. She said drumlines, which are used to catch sharks, can kill many animals. She said they can catch sharks of any size, and also dolphins, turtles, and other animals. She said this is why many people do not want the policy.

Thousands of people protested at Perth’s Cottesloe Beach and Sydney’s Manly Beach. Hundreds protested at Glenelg in south-west Adelaide, and at beaches in Victoria and Queensland. Faehrmann said the protests showed many Australians want sharks protected. She said people are saying it is the sharks’ ocean and they do not want them killed.

Anthony Joyce, a surfer who once had his foot caught in a shark’s mouth, said the number of sharks killed will not make a real difference.

The state government has refused to give a running total of sharks killed. There have been reports of sharks smaller than three metres being released after getting caught. Drumlines are floating drums anchored to the sea bed, with baited hooks below them.

Conservationists say there is no evidence the cull will reduce shark attacks on humans. They say no earlier cull used only drumlines. Researchers at the University of Western Australia say the recent increase in attacks may be linked to the state’s fast-growing population, not to more sharks.

Richard Peirce, chairman of the UK charity the Shark Trust, said the cull will not work and could bring more predators closer to the coast. He said it is sad that the government did not try other ideas instead of killing animals. He also said that leaving drumlines in overnight could attract other predators to the area because sharks and other animals may be hooked and injured.

Around the world in 2012, there were 80 unprovoked shark attacks, and seven were fatal. In comparison, humans kill nearly 100 million sharks each year.

RSPCA Australia said it believes the cull is not justified. It said there is no evidence that more attacks are caused by more sharks. Instead, it said the increase fits with changes in population and human behaviour, because more people are in the water.

Richard Branson told Fairfax Radio the policy is backfiring. He said the premier, Colin Barnett, may have thought it would help tourism, but it may do the opposite. He said it makes the problem seem bigger than it is, and could stop people from coming to Perth and the countryside around it. He said it may only make people worry for no good reason.
