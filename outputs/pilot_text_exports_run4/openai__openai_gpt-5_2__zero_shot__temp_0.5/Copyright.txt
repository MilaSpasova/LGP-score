Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is theft. He says it is like putting your hand in someone’s pocket and stealing a wallet. He wrote this in an article for Index on Censorship. Pullman is president of the Society of Authors, and he strongly supports copyright laws. He criticizes people who think it is OK to download music or books without paying.

Pullman writes that the technology is very impressive, but people do not see the wrong thing they are doing. He says it is shocking that people can steal an artist’s work and not be punished.

His article came after music industry leaders met British Prime Minister David Cameron at Downing Street to talk about web piracy.

Pullman, who wrote the His Dark Materials trilogy, says authors and musicians often work for years in poverty and without much attention. He says that when their work finally becomes good enough to make audiences happy, their chance to earn money can be taken away by illegal downloading. He says the rule is simple: if we want to enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

In the next issue of the magazine, Pullman is in a dialogue with Cathy Casserly. She is chief executive of Creative Commons, which offers open content licences that let creators control copyright in different ways. Casserly says many things are wrong with copyright, because it was created in an “analogue age.” She says copyright can stop people from sharing, building on, and remixing other people’s work.

Casserly says artists should think of creative ways to share and make money from their work. She quotes science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees that there is a problem. The editor, Rachael Jolley, says today’s copyright laws do not work well in the digital age and could make ordinary consumers into criminals. She says new ways are needed so artists, writers, and musicians can earn a living.

Illegal downloading is very common. Ofcom, the UK communications regulator, collected data that suggests that in the UK, between November 2012 and January 2013, people illegally copied 280 million music tracks, 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files.

Ofcom also said 18% of internet users over age 12 admit they recently pirated content, and 9% say they worry about getting caught. Pullman says it is still amazing how fast people can get music as MP3 downloads, especially to people who have been building an iTunes list for a long time.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron chose Conservative MP Mike Weatherley to advise him on this issue. A spokesman for the BPI, the record industry trade body, said Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creators it protects. The spokesman said they hope Weatherley and the prime minister will support copyright in the work of the UK’s intellectual property office.
