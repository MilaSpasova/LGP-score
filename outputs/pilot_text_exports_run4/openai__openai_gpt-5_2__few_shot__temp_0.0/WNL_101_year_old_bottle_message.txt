Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8 April 2014, she talked about a very unusual moment. She received a message in a bottle, 101 years after her grandfather threw it into the Baltic Sea. People think it is the world’s oldest message in a bottle. A museum in Germany gave it to her and is showing it now.

Erdmann is 62. She said it was very surprising. A man came to her door and said he had post from her grandfather. He said a message in a bottle had been found, and the name on the card was her grandfather’s. The man was a family history researcher. He found her in Berlin after the letter was given to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg.

The bottle was a brown beer bottle. It was in the water for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it in his catch in the Baltic Sea near the northern city of Kiel. Holger von Neuhoff, a curator at the museum, said it was the oldest bottled message he had seen. He said the museum has older documents, but not with the bottle. He said the bottle and the document were in very good condition.

Researchers think Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913. He was hiking with a nature group. He was 20 years old then.

People could not read much of the postcard. But they could read the Berlin address on the front. They could also read that the writer politely asked the finder to send the note to his home address.

Erdmann said her grandfather also put two stamps in the bottle, so the finder would not have to pay. But he did not think it would take 101 years.

Erdmann said she felt very moved. She did not know her grandfather, because he died at age 54.

She said she knew very little about him. She learned he was a writer. She said he was open-minded and believed in freedom and respect for everyone. She said he did a lot for young people. Later he travelled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful to learn where her roots came from.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and travelling around the world. She said she is open-minded too. She said what her grandfather taught his two daughters, her mother taught her, and she then taught her sons.

Even though she was happy to get the message, she said she hoped people would not throw more bottles with messages into the sea. She said the sea is already full of bottles and rubbish.

The bottle and the message will be on display at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts will try to read the rest of the text. It is not clear what will happen to the bottle later, but Erdmann hopes it will stay at the museum.

She said they want to add some photos with the bottle, so visitors can see the young man who threw it into the water.
