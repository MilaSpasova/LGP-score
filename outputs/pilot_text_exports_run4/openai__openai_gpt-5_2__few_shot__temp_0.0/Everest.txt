Kenton Cool usually likes to talk, but now he can hardly speak. Climbing high mountains has made his voice rough. He is now in Kathmandu, the capital of Nepal. He flew down from Everest base camp that morning.

Cool is thinking about three big climbs he finished over the previous weekend. Early on Saturday morning, he reached the top of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of three main summits in the Everest “horseshoe” around the valley called the Western Cwm.

That same day, he climbed to the top of Everest. He reached the summit in complete darkness early on Sunday. Then he and his climbing partner went on to the top of Lhotse on Monday morning.

Cool says it was a quick chance. For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. He says this does not remove the physical achievement. He says he has set the bar at a certain level, but the next person may do more, without ropes or bottled oxygen.

Sixty years after Everest was first climbed, many stories look back to Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. They compare that time with today, with commercialism and environmental damage. The writer asks Cool to look forward and imagine what top climbers might do 60 years from now.

Cool says he hates to think about it, but he talks about the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. Steck left the mountain in April after what Cool calls “an altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck planned to climb Everest’s west ridge (first done in 1963), go down to the South Col, and then climb Lhotse by a new route, all without fixed ropes. Cool says Steck had been training very hard. He says Steck was technically brilliant and in amazing physical condition.

Tourism is another question. One example is a helicopter rescue by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro flew back to Everest on Tuesday in a high-powered helicopter to rescue a sick or injured climber at 7,800 metres.

This was the highest rescue yet on Everest. It shows the fast rise in helicopter flights in recent years. By 2073, the mountain could even have a helipad on the South Col, bringing tourists who breathe bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing how rescuers can help both climbers and the many trekkers who go as far as base camp.

Mountain geographer and environmentalist Alton Byers says it is not clear if the Everest region can keep coping with fast-growing tourism. He says climate change and tourism together are creating new pressure on the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers are retreating, and some are disappearing. He says this is already having a big effect. He says people talk about having less water for farming and less water for the new lodges being built.

Byers says that in Namche Bazaar, a new five-mile pipeline is being built to bring more water. This is to meet tourist demand for showers and flush toilets. The local stream is polluted by human waste and does not provide enough water. He says every village is digging a pit near the houses for garbage. He says Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world.

Byers says human waste at base camp is now managed well and taken away in plastic barrels. But he says the barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley, and it could leak into the region’s watercourses.

Byers says these problems can be solved, but people need to take them seriously. He says one climber can spend $85,000 to climb Everest, and that is fine. But he says people also need to deal with other needs. He says for half a million dollars a year, most of these problems could be solved.

Climate change is harder. Byers works with local conservation committees to plan for climate change. This often means finding new water sources or collecting rainwater. He says glacial lakes are growing quickly, and they may burst and flood the Sherpa homeland. He says there may come a time when people will have to move away.

Changing weather is also affecting tourism. More clouds during times that are usually clear are closing Lukla Airport more often. Lukla Airport is the gateway to the Everest region. A new road for 4x4s is being built to Lukla to keep tourists and money coming. But Byers worries the road network in Nepal is being built cheaply, which can cause soil erosion and landslides.

Byers says Everest is the famous mountain everyone knows. He says it is like a warning sign that everyone can understand. He says it is a perfect place to study how to deal with problems like climate change and tourism.
