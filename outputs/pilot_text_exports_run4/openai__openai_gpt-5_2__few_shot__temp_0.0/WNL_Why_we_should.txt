James Hamblin is a senior editor at the American magazine The Atlantic. In his series “If Our Bodies Could Talk,” he tried a challenge: he stopped showering for a while to see what happens if we clean our bodies too much. He took fewer showers. When he did shower, he did not use shampoo or soap.

He found something many other people have also found. When we use a lot of soap and body wash, the skin tries harder to fix its natural balance. Showering can remove the skin’s natural oil and bacteria. Hot water and cleaning products can remove too much.

After a shower, some people feel their skin is tight and dry. This is because the skin’s natural moisture has been washed away. Our skin also has millions of helpful bacteria. Frequent rubbing and scrubbing can destroy these bacteria. Later, new bacteria grow back, and they may include microbes that make bad smells. So, showering too often may make you smell more. When you stop using soap and shower less, the skin may have a first adjustment period. After that, the skin often returns to balance, makes less oil, and healthy bacteria grow again.

In the end, Hamblin thought the human body can be nice when it works on its own. It is not only about smell or looks. Dermatologists say using less soap can help acne and eczema.

Showering less can also help the environment. The average shower is seven minutes and uses 65 litres of water. This is clean, drinkable water, and we mix it with soap and send it down the drain every day, sometimes more than once.

Clean water is very important, and it is becoming harder to ignore, as California has another summer of drought. Clean water is one of the world’s most valuable resources, and it may soon be in short supply. There is also waste from many body wash bottles. These are good reasons to be more natural.

If this idea makes you feel uncomfortable, relax. Many people who reduce washing still use deodorant if they need it. Everyone’s natural smell is different and can change with diet, hydration, and exercise. Also, hand-washing with soap is still recommended to reduce the spread of infectious diseases.

You do not have to stop showering completely, like Hamblin did. But you can reduce. Skip a few showers, use less soap, and let the skin’s bacteria grow a little.
