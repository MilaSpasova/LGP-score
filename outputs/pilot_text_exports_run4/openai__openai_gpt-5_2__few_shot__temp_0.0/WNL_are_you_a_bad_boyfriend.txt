Dan Sullivan was late to breakfast with the Guardian. He was stopped by police while riding his motorcycle.

Sullivan says he works too much. He misses dinner dates. He forgets to give presents.

Like many people in Silicon Valley, the 27-year-old venture capitalist made a start-up to help. It is called BetterBoyfriend.me. The service sends a present to girlfriends and wives every month for about $70.

Sullivan works in finance. He has been testing the program quietly. About 350 boyfriends have signed up. This brings about $17,000 in revenue each month. He says most members are his Harvard friends and investor friends. They include venture capitalists, founders, and employees at companies like Apple, Google, and Facebook. Their girlfriends have been getting presents from Sullivan for the last year.

Each month, members choose one gift from a list of seven. The gifts include chocolate, tea sets, and manicures. The package is sent to the boyfriend without a brand name or label.

Sullivan tests ideas by buying Facebook ads to see what happens. BetterBoyfriend.me became popular fast. Sullivan said it solved a problem many people have.

BetterBoyfriend.me was not his first idea. For a few days, he thought about a dog-sharing idea. He said he saw rich guys looking unhappy while walking dogs. He said a dog is only valuable 5% of the time. He thought about having someone babysit dogs so other people could rent them.

Another start-up is called InvisibleBoyfriend. It lets users design a social partner who is always available to talk. The result is a “boyfriend” who can text with you all day.

Founder Kyle Tabor launched it to give users a fake boyfriend to stop unwanted advances. But he found that some people became attached to their algorithmic partners. He said more users want companionship through conversation, not just “proof” of a relationship.

For Sullivan and Tabor, a surprise was the real relationships they formed with customers. Sullivan says he now sees himself as a kind of relationship consultant for the boyfriends.

Sullivan says he made mistakes. At first, the packages had receipts with his name on them: Dan Sullivan. One boyfriend wrote to him and said, “Listen, dude, she’s not mad but Cynthia found out.”

Sullivan says about 50% of the women who receive BetterBoyfriend.me packages know he is involved. He says it is connected to age. He thinks that after you have been married a while, you do not keep many secrets.

Over the year, Sullivan says he has gotten to know the boyfriends well. They even made a delivery to a hospital delivery room. He says the key is to remember that his relationship is with the boyfriend.

When he first started the company, he put BetterBoyfriend.me tags on flower bouquets. He went to Dolores Park, a popular place in the Mission District, where many young people and tech workers spend time.

He looked for couples and gave flowers to the girl. The boyfriend did not like that. So he changed his plan and started giving the flowers to the boyfriends.
