From glow-in-the-dark trees to underground bike storage and solar-powered bins, this text looks at unusual ideas to make cities better to live in.

Living in a city has good points, but it is harder to live in a sustainable way. People often face pollution, traffic, and less green space. Inventors are trying new ideas. This text looks at ten of the stranger solutions to make cities more livable.

Pop-up parks
Some cities seem built more for cars than for people. A pop-up park is a simple idea. You use an empty parking space, a little money, and one or two plants, and you make a small park. The PARK(ing) project began as an art experiment in San Francisco and later spread around the world. People have also made temporary city farms and ecology demonstrations. The movement has a day of action every September.

Underground bike storage
Not everyone uses a car. More people are riding bikes. But they need a safe place to keep them. A Tokyo engineering company called Giken made an idea for an “eco-cycle, anti-seismic underground bicycle park.” It is a round storage system that is only seven metres wide, and it goes deep into the ground. It can hold 204 bikes. Owners can get their bike by pressing a button. The system brings the bike back above ground in about 13 seconds.

Glow-in-the-dark trees
Some people think of Christmas lights when they think of glowing trees. Dutch designer and artist Daan Roosegaarde has a different idea. He created an experimental “bioluminescent” plant. The technology mixes DNA from glowing marine bacteria with the chloroplast genome of a plant, so the plant glows like a jellyfish. Trials are happening to make a large industrial version. Roosegaarde hopes this could one day replace normal street lighting.

Footstep energy
In West Ham in east London, many commuters and shoppers cross a raised walkway near the underground station every day. Most people do not notice that the ground feels a little springy. The rubber surface bends about five millimetres. This movement helps power the streetlights above. The floor uses smart tiles that catch energy from footsteps and turn it into electricity. The UK company Pavegen made this system. It also installed a similar system at London’s Heathrow Airport and in other places.

Supertrees
Singapore’s Gardens by the Bay has man-made trees called “supertrees.” There are 18 of them. They are steel-framed and can be up to 50 metres high. Flowers and ferns grow on them. Their metal tops help take in and spread out heat. They can also collect rainwater and help move air for two climate-controlled biomes (large conservatories) below. Eleven of the 18 trees also have solar panels on their branches.
