The lottery balls fall down the chute. All six numbers match. Then it may be time to buy an Audi, book a holiday in the US, and call an estate agent. An analysis says many lottery millionaires do this.

The UK National Lottery started in 1994. Since then, it has made 3,000 millionaires. Together they have won more than £8.5bn, about £2.8m each on average. The study authors, Oxford Economics, say this money also helped create 3,780 more millionaires among winners’ children, family, and friends.

Many winners (59%) stop working right away. But 19% keep their jobs, and 31% do unpaid volunteer work. The study says 98% of winners’ spending stayed in the UK. It estimates that each winner’s spending on homes, cars, and holidays keeps six people in full-time work for one year.

Winners added almost £750m to GDP and created more than £500m in taxes for the Exchequer. Most money was spent on property. Winners spent £2.72bn on their main homes, and £170m on paying off debts and mortgages.

Winners also wanted steady income, so they spent £2.125bn on investments. They gave £1.17bn as gifts to family and friends. They spent £680m on cars and holidays.

Camelot, the company that runs the UK National Lottery, paid for the study to mark 3,000 winners. The study used research from 100 winners who won £1m or more. It found the 3,000 winners bought 7,958 houses or flats in the UK, about 2.7 each, and spent £3.3bn. Most winners (82%) changed their main home, spending about £900,000 on average.

Many winners added things to their new homes. Almost a third (29%) wanted a hot tub. 28% wanted a walk-in wardrobe. 24% chose a home behind electric gates. 22% had a games room, and 7% installed a snooker table.

Big homes need care. 30% hired a cleaner and 24% hired a gardener. 5% hired a beautician.

Audis were the top choice for 16% of winners. Range Rovers and BMWs were also popular (11% each), plus Mercedes (10%) and Land Rovers (5%). Winners spent £463m on 17,190 cars. The average price of their favourite car was £46,116.

Holidays were important too. Most winners (68%) chose five-star hotels overseas. The US was the top place to go (27%), then the Caribbean (9%). In the UK, caravan sales also grew. In the past 18 years, 10% of millionaires bought a caravan, creating about £7.4m in sales.

Some winners started businesses. 15% started their own business, 9% helped other people start one, and 6% invested in or bought other people’s businesses. These businesses employ 3,195 people.

Andy Logan, who wrote the report, said a win spreads further than expected. It changes the lives of friends and family, and it also affects the UK economy. He said each win creates a ripple effect for this generation and often the next.
