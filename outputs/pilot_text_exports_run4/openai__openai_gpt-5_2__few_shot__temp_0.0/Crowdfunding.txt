This new light could be as useful as Trevor Baylis’s wind-up radio in the 1990s, before the internet. It is a cheap light that uses gravity to make free power. It could stop people using dangerous kerosene lamps in Africa and India.

A British designer, Patrick Hunt, first tried to get money from banks or venture capital. But he had a problem. He said it was slow and complicated, the idea was not proven, and nobody wanted to take a risk.

So he tried crowdfunding on a US website called Indiegogo, which had just opened in the UK. In five days, he reached his goal and raised £36,200. By the end of 2012, after 40 days, he had raised £400,000.

The LED light uses a dynamo. A 10kg bag of rocks goes down and drives the dynamo. The bag is tied to the light and lifted about 2m. When it slowly falls to the ground, it makes enough power for 30 minutes of light.

Hunt is getting ready to make the light in China. He will test the market again by sending 1,000 lights to Africa before full mass production. He hopes to make millions of units.

He is part of a new group of entrepreneurs who use crowdfunding to get money. Another new site is InvestingZone, which connects rich people with start-up entrepreneurs.

Indiegogo does not give people shares. Instead, it lets projects offer “perks” for different donation amounts. People who donated to Hunt’s light felt good about helping poorer people, and they also got their own light.

Danae Ringelmann, a co-founder of Indiegogo, says the “gravity light” shows how crowdfunding can work. She says it is fast and fair because it is not about knowing people at a bank or a venture capital company. It is about proving your idea to customers and fans, and getting them to support it.

She also says it is good to test ideas even if they may not get funding. If it does not work, you save time and learn faster.

Ringelmann lives in the US. She started her career as a Wall Street analyst. In 2008, she quit and tried to help friends in the arts raise money.

At first, the site focused on films. It launched at the Sundance Film Festival that year.

Five years later, it was raising about $2m a week for new businesses that were starting or growing. In December 2012, it started euro and sterling services to grow in Europe. It says Britain is its third biggest market. International activity is up 41% since December.

Indiegogo has many competitors, like Kickstarter, Seedrs, and Funding Circle. But Ringelmann says Indiegogo is different because anyone can start a campaign. No idea is too strange.

Indiegogo takes a 4% fee from successful campaigns. If a campaign does not reach its target, users can choose to return all the money to donors for free, or keep the money but pay a 9% fee.

A British woman, Lauren Pears, raised £100,000 on the site to open a “cat café” in London. It is called Lady Dinah’s Cat Emporium. It has not opened yet, but it is described as a place where people can sit in a comfortable chair, have hot tea, read a book, and be with a cat.

Ringelmann says some campaigns are rejected by venture capitalists. They say the idea might be good, but they do not know if people want it.

She says entrepreneurs can run an Indiegogo campaign, even before launching the product. The campaign can show there is a market, and then venture capitalists may believe it.

She says crowdfunding helps people test the market, price, and features. It can also help them find new ways to earn money and get important feedback.

Because of her Wall Street experience, and helping 100,000 businesses and services raise money, she has advice for new entrepreneurs.

She says ideas are common. What matters is doing the work well. She says if you fear someone will steal your idea and do it better, then you are not the right person to do it. She says you need confidence to move fast and learn.

Ringelmann says expanding into Europe and making a deal with a web payment company are part of a dream to make finance more open to everyone. Payments will work with local card services like Maestro in the UK and Carte Bleue in France, and also PayPal. More than 7,000 funding campaigns are live on the site.

Crowdfunding has grown as another choice instead of banks. But it is not very attractive to very rich investors, who usually want a stake in a business. That may start to change in the UK with the launch of InvestingZone.
