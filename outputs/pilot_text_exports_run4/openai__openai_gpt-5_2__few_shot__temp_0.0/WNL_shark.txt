Opposition to Western Australia’s shark cull has grown. Thousands of people went to beaches across Australia to ask the state premier to stop the policy. RSPCA Australia and Richard Branson, the owner of Virgin Atlantic, also spoke against it.

The policy is the catching and killing of sharks longer than three metres. It began after what the state government called an “unprecedented” number of shark attacks on Western Australia’s coast. A 35-year-old surfer was killed in November 2013. He was the sixth person to die from a shark attack in two years.

However, the Shark Attack File says Australia as a whole has averaged one shark-related death a year for the last 50 years.

Kate Faehrmann, a board member of the Sea Shepherd Conservation Society, spoke at a protest in Perth. She said the policy will not work. She said drumlines are “indiscriminate killers”. They can kill sharks of any size, and also dolphins, turtles, and other animals. She said this is why the community does not want it.

Thousands protested at Cottesloe Beach in Perth and at Manly Beach in Sydney. Hundreds also protested at Glenelg in south-west Adelaide, and at beaches in Victoria and Queensland. Faehrmann said the protests showed many Australians want sharks protected. She said that even with fear from films like Jaws, people are coming out to say the ocean is the sharks’ home and they do not want them killed.

Anthony Joyce, a surfer who once had his foot caught in a shark’s mouth, said the number of sharks killed will not make a real difference.

The state government has refused to give a running total of how many sharks have been killed. There have been reports of sharks smaller than three metres being released after being caught on drumlines. Drumlines are floating drums fixed to the sea bed, with bait on hooks under them.

Conservationists say there is no evidence the cull will reduce shark attacks on humans. They say no earlier cull used only drumlines. Researchers at the University of Western Australia say the recent group of attacks may be linked to Western Australia having the fastest-growing population in Australia, not to more sharks.

Richard Peirce, chairman of the UK conservation charity the Shark Trust, said the cull will not work. He said it could also pull more predators toward the coast. He said the government ignored good advice and chose an approach that is ineffective and counterproductive. He also said that even if drumlines are checked during the day, leaving them in overnight could attract other predators, because sharks and other animals can be hooked and injured.

Around the world in 2012, there were 80 unprovoked shark attacks, and seven were fatal. In the same time, humans kill nearly 100m sharks each year. RSPCA Australia said it believes the cull is not justified. It said there is no evidence that more attacks are caused by more sharks. Instead, it said, there are more people in the water because of changes in population and human behaviour.

Richard Branson told Fairfax Radio the policy was backfiring. He said Premier Colin Barnett may have thought it would help tourism, but it could do the opposite. He said it makes the problem seem bigger than it is, and could stop people from coming to Perth and the countryside around it. He said it will only worry people for no good reason.
