Anitta is a new star of Brazilian funk music. She has millions of fans. She brought the sound of the favela into popular music. But there is a debate about her skin colour.

Some anti-discrimination groups and writers say she had to give up part of her black identity to succeed in a mostly white, middle-class market.

People started talking because old and new photos of Anitta were published. In the older photo, before she was famous, she looked darker. In the newer marketing photo, after she became famous, she looks paler. People ask if this change came from skin-whitening products, cosmetic surgery, or Photoshop. The photos restarted a bigger question in Brazil: do you need to be lighter-skinned to succeed?

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote that the change shows hidden discrimination in media and marketing. She said many people do not accept that they are racist. They think Brazil is a multiracial democracy, but statistics show it is not. She said whitening shows a society that does not support diversity. She also said people often connect white skin with being rich and successful, and black skin with the opposite.

Anitta was born Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

Now she is very famous. She released an album and a hit song, “Show das Poderosas.” The song reached number one and got 52 million views on YouTube.

Many people love her as a pop idol with catchy songs and a strong message. Her supporters present her as a bridge between Rio’s mostly black and mixed-race shanty towns on the hills and the richer, whiter areas below.

She also changed her style. She reduced the suggestive dancing, gang-style references, and explicit lyrics of baile funk. But some people now ask if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract a richer audience. Arraes said pop stars may feel forced to change their hair or their noses, and this can hurt self-esteem.

Race and colour are sensitive topics in Brazil. Brazil was one of the last big countries to ban slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. But in Brazil, skin colour can matter more than ancestry. Even though the country aims to be a multiracial democracy, skin tone is linked to inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Until 2011, black or mixed-race students also spent about two fewer years in school on average.

The government says this gap is getting smaller because of university quotas and other affirmative action. But big differences remain. Most business and government leaders are white. Many low-paid jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In rich areas like Ipanema and Gávea, you are more likely to see black nannies pushing strollers with white toddlers than the opposite.

Colour is hard to define. In the 2010 census, people who called themselves white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million said black, two million said Asian, and 0.5% said indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology teacher at the Federal University of Pernambuco, said Anitta won over the middle class by taking a rebellious sound and making it calmer and easier for everyone to accept. He said this was not done by racial whitening. He said it was a change in where she made her art: from the outskirts to the centre.

Others say people talk too much about colour. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who worked with Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot, but not her colour. He said it was more about makeup, hair styling, and clothes. He did not think she was being pressured. He said she always liked to sing, dance, and show her talent, and this natural way helped her succeed.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador, said the problem is not society, but music producers and media leaders. He said the public showed they care about music itself when they chose Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, as a recent winner of the talent show The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for comment. But in a recent interview, she said identity should be defined by the person. She said a powerful woman does not need to be beautiful. She said she tries to show that we can be who we want.
