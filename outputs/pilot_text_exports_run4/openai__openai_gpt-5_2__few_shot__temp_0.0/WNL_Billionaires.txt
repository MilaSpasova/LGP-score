A thick layer of bird droppings covers a gold-colored railing at one of Britain’s most expensive homes. Pigeon skeletons are among broken mirrors, and water runs through broken ceilings. This house is called The Towers. It is a £30m mansion on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London. People did not know how ruined it was until now.

The Towers is one of ten mansions on The Bishops Avenue. This street is a very expensive part of London. These houses have been almost empty for about 25 years. It is believed they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. The columns are breaking, and swimming pools with mosaic tiles are full of broken pieces. Nature has taken over, and owls now live there.

This empty and ruined scene can be seen along the street. Lloyds Bank says The Bishops Avenue is the second most expensive street in Britain. At the same time, more people in London cannot afford a home because prices rise 11.2% each year. On the most expensive part of The Bishops Avenue, 16 mansions are empty. Many are behind locked gates, with metal covers on windows, overgrown gardens, and guard dogs.

Across the street is another empty mansion worth £18m, with broken windows and special paint to stop people climbing. Another mansion has metal bars on the windows and was sold for £20m.

Even so, prices keep rising. Dryades, a mansion that was owned until recently by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007 and is believed to be worth about £30m today. Heath Lodge, where fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou was murdered in 1984 with a silver bullet, is worth £13m today. It was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

This is painful for people who cannot afford housing in one of the world’s most expensive cities. A security guard said it was upsetting to see so much space—enough for dozens of people—falling apart.

Rich royal families from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia were some of the first to come to this road near Hampstead Heath. Iranians came here after the fall of the shah. Now, Chinese buyers are following Russians and Kazakhs. Estate agents say the address is as famous as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions were for sale for £65m and £38m. They promised Italian marble, lifts with leather inside, and panic rooms.

But outside these empty homes, stone fountains are breaking and lawns have turned into wet bogs. Inside one house, water drips through a big crystal chandelier onto a thick carpet. Parts of the ceiling have fallen down, and the carpet is rotting. Moss grows through broken bricks, and broken mirror tiles cover a bathroom. The swimming pool has about one foot of dirty salty water, and flowers grow through the tiles. Wooden slats in the sauna bend outward.

The most dramatic ruin is The Towers. It is a large mansion among many trees. Pigeons fill its tall rooms. Water pours through three floors, and algae has turned the walls bright green. The water falls into a huge, empty basement swimming pool. Unopened wooden boxes marked “bullet-proof glass” show the old owners were worried about security.

Today, very few people live on The Bishops Avenue all the time. One security guard said the owners of a mansion were not there. Another guard, outside Royal Mansion, would not say if anyone was home. A worker at another mansion warned the Guardian about the guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare mogul worth £45m, said he is one of the few people who live there permanently.

He said it is not a friendly place where you talk to neighbours. He said he has never seen what his neighbours look like. He said a Saudi princess next door spent £35m on a new house, but he has never seen her. He said about three houses are lived in all day and night. About half the properties are used only three to six months a year. He said for the other half, nobody knows if the owners come or not.

These ruined mansions show a culture where very rich people buy British homes as investments. One Hyde Park, a group of apartments in Knightsbridge, is another example. More than half the flats there are registered with the council as empty or second homes.

Still, people on the avenue talk about building £5m apartments instead of £50m mansions, to bring people back.

Anil Varma, a local developer, plans to rebuild one valuable site as 20 apartments. They would have a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

He said if you build a big house and try to sell it for £30m to £40m, it will not sell. He said local people will not buy, so you must find buyers from overseas.

But using these empty homes to help solve the housing crisis still seems far away. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some of the ruined homes could become affordable housing. He said the land price would be too high.

He said very wealthy people own property there. Sometimes they live in the homes, and sometimes they do not.
