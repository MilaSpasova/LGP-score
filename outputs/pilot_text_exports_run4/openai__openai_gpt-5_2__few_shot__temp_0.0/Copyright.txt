Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is theft. He says it is like putting your hand in someone’s pocket and taking their wallet. In an article for Index on Censorship, Pullman, who is president of the Society of Authors, strongly supports copyright laws. He criticizes internet users who think it is OK to download music or books without paying.

Pullman writes that the technology looks so impressive that people do not see the bad thing they are doing. He says it is wrong that people can steal an artist’s work and not be punished.

His article came after leaders in the music industry met British Prime Minister David Cameron in Downing Street. They talked about web piracy.

Pullman, the writer of the His Dark Materials trilogy, says authors and musicians often work for years in poverty and without attention. They work until their work gives joy to audiences. But when they finally might earn a living from it, that chance is taken away. He says the rule is simple: if we want to enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

Pullman is writing in the next issue of the group’s magazine in a dialogue with Cathy Casserly. She is the chief executive of Creative Commons, which offers open content licences that let creators control their copyright. Casserly says copyright has many problems because it was made in an “analogue age”. She says copyright often stops people from sharing, building on, and remixing each other’s work.

Casserly says artists need to think in new ways about how they share and make money from their work. She quotes science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees. The magazine’s editor, Rachael Jolley, says today’s copyright laws do not work well in the digital age and may turn consumers into criminals. She says we need new ways for artists, writers, and musicians to earn a living from their work.

The debate is strong, and illegal downloading is very large. Ofcom, the UK communications regulator, collected data. It suggests that in the UK, between November 2012 and January 2013, 280 million music tracks were pirated. Also pirated were 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files.

Ofcom said 18% of internet users over age 12 say they have recently pirated content. Also, 9% say they fear getting caught. Pullman writes that it is still surprising how easily and quickly people can get music as MP3 downloads.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron appointed Conservative MP Mike Weatherley as his adviser on the issue. A spokesman for the BPI, the record industry trade body, said Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creators it protects. The spokesman said they hope Weatherley’s influence and the prime minister’s support will affect the approach of the UK intellectual property office.
