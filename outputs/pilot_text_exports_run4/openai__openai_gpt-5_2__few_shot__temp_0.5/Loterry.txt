The lottery balls fall down the chute and all six numbers match. Many people then buy an Audi, book a holiday in the US, and call an estate agent. This is what most lottery millionaires do, based on a study of how jackpot winners spend and invest their money.

The lottery started in 1994. Since then, it has made 3,000 millionaires. Together they have won more than £8.5bn, about £2.8m each on average. The study says their money also helped create 3,780 more millionaires among their children, family, and friends. The study was done by Oxford Economics.

Most winners (59%) stop working straight away. But 19% keep doing their normal job, and 31% do unpaid volunteer work. For the economy, 98% of winners’ spending stayed in the UK. The study estimates that each winner’s spending keeps six people in full-time work for one year.

Winners added almost £750m to GDP and created more than £500m in tax for the Exchequer. Most money went to property. Winners spent £2.72bn on their main homes, and £170m to pay off debts and mortgages.

Winners also wanted to keep getting income. They spent £2.125bn on investments. They gave £1.17bn as gifts to family and friends. They spent £680m on cars and holidays.

Camelot, which runs the UK National Lottery, paid for the study to mark 3,000 winners. The study used research from 100 winners who won £1m or more. It found that all 3,000 winners bought 7,958 houses or flats in the UK, about 2.7 each, and spent £3.3bn. Most winners (82%) changed their main home, spending about £900,000 on average.

Many winners added extra things to their new home. Almost a third (29%) wanted a hot tub. A walk-in wardrobe was important for 28%. Almost a quarter (24%) chose a home behind electric gates. And 22% had a games room, with 7% putting in a snooker table.

Bigger homes need more help. Some winners hired a cleaner (30%) or a gardener (24%). A few (5%) hired a beautician.

Audi was the favourite car for 16% of winners. Range Rover and BMW were also popular (11% each). Mercedes was 10%, and Land Rover was 5%. Winners spent £463m on 17,190 cars. The average price of the favourite car was £46,116.

Holidays were also important. Most winners (68%) chose five-star hotels in other countries. The US was the top place (27%), then the Caribbean (9%). In the UK, caravan sales also grew. Over 18 years, 10% of millionaires bought a caravan, creating sales of about £7.4m.

Some winners started a business (15%). Some helped other people start one (9%). Others invested in or bought other people’s businesses (6%). These businesses employ 3,195 people, the study says.

Andy Logan, who wrote the report, said a win affects more people than expected. It changes the lives of friends and family. It also has a clear effect on the UK economy, because so much of the money is spent in the UK. He said each win creates a ripple effect in this generation and often the next.
