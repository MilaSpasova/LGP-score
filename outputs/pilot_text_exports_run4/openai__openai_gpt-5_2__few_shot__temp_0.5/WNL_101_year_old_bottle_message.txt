Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8th April 2014, she talked about a special moment. She got a message in a bottle. Her grandfather had thrown it into the Baltic Sea 101 years earlier. People think it is the world’s oldest message in a bottle. A museum in Germany gave it to her and is showing it.

Erdmann is 62. She said it was very surprising. A man came to her door and said he had mail from her grandfather. He said a bottle had been found, and the card inside had her grandfather’s name. The visitor was a genealogy researcher. He found her in Berlin after the letter was given to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg.

The bottle was a brown beer bottle. It had been in the water for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it in his catch in the Baltic Sea, near the city of Kiel. Holger von Neuhoff, a curator at the museum, said it was the oldest bottle message he had seen. He said the museum has older documents without bottles, but this one has both the bottle and the document. He said it is in very good condition.

Researchers think Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913. He was on a hike with a nature group. He was 20 years old.

Most of the postcard could not be read. But the Berlin address on the front was clear. The card also asked the person who found it to send it to his home address.

Erdmann said her grandfather put two stamps in the bottle, so the finder would not have to pay. But he did not think it would take 101 years.

She said the message made her emotional. She did not know her grandfather because he died at age 54, before she was born.

She said she learned a little about him. She found out he was a writer. She said he was open-minded, believed in freedom, and thought people should respect each other. She said he helped young people. Later he travelled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful because she could see where her family came from.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and travelling. She said she is open-minded too. She said what her grandfather taught his daughters, her mother taught her, and she then taught her sons.

She was happy to get the message, but she hopes people today will not throw message bottles into the sea. She said the sea now has too many bottles and too much rubbish.

The message and bottle will be on display at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts will try to read the rest of the text. It is not clear what will happen to the bottle later, but Erdmann hopes it will stay at the museum.

She said they want to add some photos with the bottle. Then visitors can see the young man who threw it into the water.
