From glow-in-the-dark trees to underground bike storage and solar-powered bins, we look at some unusual ideas to make cities nicer to live in.

Living in a city has many good points, but it is getting harder to live in a sustainable way. People often face pollution, traffic, and fewer green spaces.

Inventors are trying to help. Here are some of the ten stranger ideas for making cities more livable.

Pop-up parks
Some cities look like they are built more for cars than for people. A pop-up park is a simple idea. You use an empty car park, a little money, and one or two plants, and you make a small park.

The PARK(ing) project started as an art experiment in San Francisco. It has now spread around the world. People have also made temporary urban farms and ecology demonstrations. The movement has a day of action every September.

Subterranean storage
Not everyone in cities uses a car. More people are riding bikes. A key question is where to keep a bike safe.

A Tokyo-based engineering firm, Giken, made a plan for an “eco-cycle, anti-seismic underground bicycle park”. It is a cylinder that is seven metres wide. It goes deep underground and can hold 204 bikes. Owners can get their bike back by pressing a button. The automated system brings the bike above ground in about 13 seconds.

Glow-in-the-dark trees
Many people think of Christmas decorations when they think of something that glows. But Dutch designer-artist Daan Roosegaarde has created a “bioluminescent” plant.

This experimental technology combines DNA from glowing marine bacteria with part of a plant’s genome (the chloroplast genome). This makes a glow like a jellyfish. Trials are happening to make an industrial-scale version. Roosegaarde hopes it could one day replace normal street lighting.

Footfall harvesting
Every day, many commuters and shoppers in West Ham, in east London, cross an elevated walkway near the underground station. Most people do not notice that the ground feels slightly springy.

The rubber surface can flex by five millimetres. This movement helps power the streetlights above. The walkway has smart tiles that capture energy from people’s footsteps and turn it into electricity. The UK firm Pavegen made this system. It has also installed a similar system at London’s Heathrow Airport and in other places.

Supertrees
Some trees are man-made. Singapore’s Gardens by the Bay has a small group of them.

These steel-framed “supertrees” can be up to 50 metres high. They have flowers and ferns growing on them. Their metal canopies help absorb and spread heat. They can collect rainwater. They also help provide air ventilation for two “climate-controlled biomes” (large conservatories) below.

Eleven of the 18 trees also have solar panels on their “branches”.
