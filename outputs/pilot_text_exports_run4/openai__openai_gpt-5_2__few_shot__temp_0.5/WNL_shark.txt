Opposition to Western Australia’s shark cull has grown. Thousands of people went to beaches across Australia to ask the state’s premier to stop the policy. RSPCA Australia and Virgin Atlantic owner Richard Branson also spoke against it.

The policy catches and kills sharks longer than three metres. It began after what the state government called an “unprecedented” number of shark attacks on Western Australia’s coast. A 35-year-old surfer was killed in November 2013. He was the sixth person to die from a shark attack in two years.

However, the Shark Attack File says that Australia as a whole has averaged one shark-related death a year for the last 50 years.

Kate Faehrmann, a board member at the Sea Shepherd Conservation Society, spoke at a protest in Perth. She said, “We’ve been saying all along that this policy won’t work. Drumlines, used to catch the sharks, kill without choosing. They will kill sharks whether they’re one, two, three metres or more. They can also kill dolphins, turtles, and other animals. That’s why the community doesn’t want it.”

Thousands of people protested at Perth’s Cottesloe Beach and Sydney’s Manly Beach. Hundreds protested at Glenelg in south-west Adelaide, and at beaches in Victoria and Queensland.

Faehrmann said the protests showed many Australians want sharks protected. She said that even with fear from films like Jaws, people are coming out to say, “That’s their ocean. We respect them, we love them and we don’t want them killed.”

Anthony Joyce, a surfer who once had his foot caught in a shark’s mouth, said, “The number of sharks they are going to kill is going to make no difference in the scheme of things.”

The state government has refused to give a running total of sharks killed. There have been reports of sharks smaller than three metres being released after being caught on drumlines. Drumlines are floating drums anchored to the sea bed, with bait hanging on hooks below.

Conservationists say there is no evidence the cull will reduce shark attacks on humans. They say no earlier cull used only drumlines. Researchers at the University of Western Australia say the recent rise in shark attacks in the state may be linked to Western Australia having the fastest-growing population in Australia, not to more sharks.

Richard Peirce, chairman of the UK-based conservation charity the Shark Trust, said the cull would not work and could bring more predators closer to the coast. He said the government ignored good advice and chose an approach that is “ineffective and counterproductive.” He also said that even if drumlines are checked during the day, leaving them in overnight could attract other predators. This could happen because sharks and other animals may be hooked and injured.

Worldwide in 2012, there were 80 unprovoked shark attacks, and seven were fatal. This was compared to nearly 100m sharks killed by humans each year.

RSPCA Australia said it believes the cull is not justified. It said there is no evidence that more attacks are caused by more sharks. Instead, it said the change fits with changes in population and human behaviour, meaning more people are in the water.

Richard Branson told Fairfax Radio the policy was backfiring. He said Western Australia Premier Colin Barnett may have hoped it would encourage tourism, but Branson thought it would do the opposite. He said it advertises a problem that does not exist “in a major way,” and it may stop people from wanting to come to Perth and the countryside around it. He said it will only make people worry for no good reason.
