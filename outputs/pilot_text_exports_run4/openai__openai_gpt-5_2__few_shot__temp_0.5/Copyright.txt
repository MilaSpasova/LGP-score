Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is theft. He says it is like putting your hand in someone’s pocket and stealing their wallet. In an article for Index on Censorship, Pullman defends copyright laws. He criticizes internet users who think it is OK to download music or books without paying.

Pullman writes that the technology looks impressive, but people do not see that what they are doing is wrong. He says it is shocking that someone can steal an artist’s work and not be punished.

His article came after music industry leaders met British Prime Minister David Cameron in Downing Street. They talked about web piracy.

Pullman, who wrote the His Dark Materials trilogy, says authors and musicians often work in poverty and without attention for years. They work hard to make work that audiences enjoy. But when they finally might earn a living, illegal downloading takes that chance away. He says the rule is simple: if we want to enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

Pullman is writing in the next issue of the campaign group’s magazine in a dialogue with Cathy Casserly. Casserly is chief executive of Creative Commons, which offers open content licences. She says copyright has many problems and was made in an “analogue age”. She says copyright often stops people from sharing, building on, and remixing each other’s work in ways that were not imagined when the laws were made.

Casserly says artists need to think of new ways to share and make money from their work. She quotes science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees. The magazine’s editor, Rachael Jolley, says current copyright laws do not work well in the digital age and may make consumers into criminals. She says we need new models so artists, writers, and musicians can earn a living.

The debate is active, and illegal downloading is very large. Ofcom data suggests that in the UK, between November 2012 and January 2013, 280 million music tracks were pirated. Also pirated were 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files.

Ofcom says 18% of internet users aged over 12 admit they recently pirated content, and 9% say they fear getting caught. Pullman writes that it is still amazing how quickly music can be gotten as MP3 downloads.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron appointed Conservative MP Mike Weatherley as his adviser on this subject. A spokesman for the BPI said Mike Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creative producers it protects. The BPI hopes his influence, and the prime minister’s support for copyright, will affect the approach of the UK’s intellectual property office.
