A thick layer of bird droppings covers a gold-colored railing at one of Britain’s most expensive homes. Pigeon bones lie near broken mirrors, and water runs through broken parts of the ceiling. This house is called The Towers. It is a £30m mansion on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London. People did not know how badly damaged it was until now.

The Towers is one of ten mansions in the middle of The Bishops Avenue. This street is at the center of London’s rising property market. These mansions have been almost empty for about 25 years. It is believed they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. The columns are breaking, and the swimming pools are full of broken material. Nature has taken over, and owls now live there.

This scene can be seen along the avenue. Lloyds Bank says The Bishops Avenue is the second most expensive street in Britain. At the same time, more people in London cannot afford a home because prices rise by 11.2% each year. On the most expensive part of the avenue, 16 mansions are empty. Many are behind locked gates, with steel bars on the windows and overgrown gardens. Guard dogs patrol the grounds.

Across the street, there is another empty mansion worth £18m, with broken windows and walls covered in anti-climb paint. Another mansion has metal bars on its windows. It was sold for £20m.

Even so, prices keep rising. Dryades, a mansion that was owned until recently by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007 and is believed to be worth about £30m today. Heath Lodge, where fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou was murdered in 1984 by a silver bullet, is worth £13m today. It was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

For people who struggle to pay for housing, it is painful to see these homes falling apart. One security guard said it was very frustrating. The houses are huge and could fit dozens of people, but they are being left to ruin.

In the past, rich royals from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia came to this road near Hampstead Heath. Iranians moved here after the fall of the shah. Now, Chinese buyers are following Russians and Kazakhs, who have paid millions to live on a street that estate agents say is as famous as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions were for sale for £65m and £38m. They promised Italian marble, lifts with leather padding, and luxury panic rooms.

But on the land around the empty mansions, stone fountains break down and the grass has turned into wet bogs. Inside one house, water drips through a large crystal chandelier onto a thick carpet that is rotting under parts of a fallen ceiling. Moss grows through broken bricks. Broken mirror tiles lie across a bathroom. The swimming pool has a foot of dirty water, and flowers grow through the tiles. Wooden slats stick out from the sauna.

The most dramatic ruin is The Towers. It is a large house set among many trees. Pigeons live in its big halls. Water runs through three floors, and the walls have turned bright green with algae. The water drops into a large, empty basement swimming pool. Unopened wooden boxes marked “bullet-proof glass” show that the previous owners were worried about security.

Today, very few people live full time on The Bishops Avenue. One security guard said the owners of a mansion were not there. Another guard, outside Royal Mansion, would not say if anyone was home. At another mansion, a staff member warned the Guardian about the guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare mogul with personal wealth of £45m, said he is one of the few permanent residents.

He said, “It’s not a friendly place where you can talk over the fence. To be honest, I have never seen what my neighbours look like. Next door, a Saudi princess spent £35m on a new house and I’ve never seen her. There are about three houses that are lived in 24/7 and half of the properties are occupied three to six months a year. The other half, who knows if they come or not?”

These ruined mansions show a property culture where the world’s richest people treat British homes as investments. One Hyde Park, an apartment building in Knightsbridge, is another example. There, more than half the flats are registered with the council as empty or second homes.

Even so, people on the avenue are talking about building £5m apartments instead of £50m mansions, to bring people back.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, plans to rebuild one valuable site on the avenue as 20 apartments. They will have a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

He said, “If you build a big house and try and sell for £30m to £40m, it won’t sell. Locals won’t buy and so you have to bring in overseas buyers.”

But using these empty homes to help solve the housing crisis still seems far away. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some of the empty homes could become affordable homes. He said the land would be too expensive.

He said, “Very wealthy people own property there. Sometimes they live in them and sometimes they don’t.”
