Kenton Cool usually likes to talk, but now he can hardly speak. Climbing high mountains has made his voice rough. He is now in Kathmandu, the capital of Nepal. He flew down from Everest base camp that morning.

Cool is thinking about an amazing weekend of climbing. Early on Saturday morning, he reached the top of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of the three main summits in the Everest “horseshoe.” This horseshoe surrounds an icy valley called the Western Cwm.

Later the same day, he climbed to the top of Everest. He reached the summit in complete darkness early on Sunday. Then he and his climbing partner continued to Lhotse. They reached the summit of Lhotse on Monday morning.

Cool says it was a lucky chance. For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. He says this does not remove the physical achievement. But he believes the next person will set a higher level and do it without ropes or bottled oxygen.

Sixty years after Everest was first climbed, many stories look back to Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. These stories compare the past with today’s time of business on the mountain and damage to the environment. The writer asks Cool to look forward and imagine what top climbers might do 60 years from now.

Cool says he does not like to think about it. But he talks about the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. In April, Steck left the mountain after what Cool calls “an altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck planned a very hard climb. He wanted to climb Everest’s west ridge, first done in 1963, then go down to the South Col, and then immediately climb Lhotse by a new route. He planned to do this without fixed ropes. Cool says Steck trained extremely hard. He says Steck was technically brilliant and in very strong physical condition. Cool says it would have been amazing to see what Steck could do.

Tourism is another question. One sign is a helicopter rescue by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro’s angry language helped start the confrontation at Camp 2. On Tuesday, Moro flew a high-powered helicopter back to Everest. He rescued a very sick or injured climber at 7,800 metres.

This was the highest rescue ever done on Everest. It shows that helicopter flights have increased very fast in recent years. By 2073, the mountain might even have a helipad on the South Col. Tourists could arrive there while breathing bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing what is possible for rescuing climbers and also many trekkers who go only as far as base camp.

Alton Byers is a mountain geographer and environmentalist. He says it is not clear if the Everest region can keep handling fast-growing tourism. He says climate change and tourism together are creating new pressures on the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers are shrinking, and some are disappearing. This is already having a big effect. Byers says people everywhere talk about having less water. There is less water for farming and less water for the new lodges that are being built.

Byers says that in the Sherpa town of Namche Bazaar, workers are building a new five-mile pipeline to bring more water. It is needed because tourists want showers and flush toilets. The local stream is polluted with human waste. It also does not give enough water during the busy season, when the town is extremely crowded. Byers says every village is digging a pit just beyond the houses for garbage. He says Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world.

Byers says human waste at base camp is now managed well and taken away in plastic barrels. But he says the barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley. This pit could leak into the region’s water streams and rivers.

Byers says these problems can be solved, but people must take them seriously. He says one climber can spend $85,000 to climb Everest. He says that is fine, but at some point other needs must be addressed. He says that with half a million dollars a year, most of the problems could be solved.

Climate change is harder. Byers works with local conservation committees to find and plan for climate change effects. They often look for new water sources or collect rainwater. Byers says glacial lakes are growing quickly. Some could break open and flood the Sherpa homeland. He says there may come a time when people will have to move out of the way.

Weather changes also affect tourism. More clouds during times that are usually clear are closing Lukla Airport more often. Lukla Airport is the main entry to the Everest region. A new road for 4x4 vehicles is being built to Lukla to keep tourists and money coming. But Byers worries the road network in Nepal is spreading too fast and too cheaply. He says this can cause serious soil erosion and landslides.

Byers says everyone knows Everest. He says it is like a warning sign that people understand. He says it is a perfect place to study how to deal with problems like climate change and tourism.
