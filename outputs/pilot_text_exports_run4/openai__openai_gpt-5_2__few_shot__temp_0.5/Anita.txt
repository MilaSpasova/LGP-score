Anitta is a new star of Brazilian funk music. She became very popular by bringing favela music to more people. But now people are debating her skin colour.

Some anti-discrimination activists and writers say Anitta had to look less black to be accepted by a mostly white, middle-class audience.

The debate grew after “before and after” photos were shared. In the older photo, before she was famous, she looks darker. In a newer marketing photo, after she became famous, she looks paler. People do not know if this is from skin-lightening products, cosmetic surgery, or, more likely, Photoshop. These photos made people ask again if you need to be pale to succeed in Brazil.

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote that the media and marketing still have hidden racism. She said many people deny they are racist and say Brazil is a multiracial democracy, but statistics show it is not. She said the “whitening” image shows a society that does not support diversity. She said people often connect white skin with being rich and successful, and see black skin as the opposite.

Anitta’s real name is Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

Now she is very famous. She released an album and had a big hit song, “Show das Poderosas.” It reached number one and got 52 million views on YouTube.

Many people love her as a pop idol with catchy songs and a strong message. Her supporters also present her as a bridge between the mostly black and mixed-race shanty towns on Rio’s hills and the richer, whiter areas below.

She has also changed her style. She reduced the suggestive dancing, gang references, and very explicit lyrics of baile funk. But some people now ask if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract a richer audience. Arraes said pop stars may feel forced to change things like curly hair or a big nose, and this can hurt self-esteem.

Race and colour are sensitive topics in Brazil. Brazil was one of the last big countries to ban slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. In Brazil, colour can matter more than race or ancestry. Even though the country says it wants to be a multiracial democracy, there is a clear link between skin tone and inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Until 2011, black or mixed-race students also spent about two fewer years in school on average.

The government says the gap is getting smaller because of university quotas and other affirmative action. But big differences remain. Most business and government leaders are white, while most low-level jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In rich areas like Ipanema and Gávea, it is more common to see black nannies pushing strollers with white toddlers than the reverse.

Colour is also hard to define. In the 2010 census, people who called themselves white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million said black, two million said Asian, and 0.5% said indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology teacher at the Federal University of Pernambuco, said Anitta won middle-class fans by taking a rebellious sound and making it calmer and easier for everyone to accept. He said this was not done by racial whitening. He said it was mainly a change in where she made her art: from the periphery to the centre.

Others say the colour issue is exaggerated. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who worked with Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot, but not her colour. He said the change came from makeup, hairstyling, and clothing. He also said he does not think she was pressured. He said she always liked to sing, dance, and show off, and that this natural style helped her succeed.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador, said the problem was not the public, but music producers and media leaders. He said the public showed they cared about the music itself when they chose Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, as the recent winner of the TV show The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for comment. But in a recent interview, she said identity should be defined by the person. She said a powerful woman does not need to be beautiful, but can be powerful because of her attitude. She said she tries to show people that we can be who we want.
