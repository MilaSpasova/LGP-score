It was a beautiful summer evening. I decided to go for a swim near Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland. I moved there in 2012.

A woman was in the water with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty often swims with a group of people, and she seems close to them.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Since then, hundreds of people have swum with her. Many people think she is completely tame. She was even in an Irish tourist-board advertisement, where a girl says she wants to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty’s belly. It looked very friendly. About 20 tourists and local people were watching from the pier.

Soon after I got into the water, Dusty left the woman. She suddenly became wild and aggressive. Later I learned that she is very territorial when she is with someone. Her tail was moving fast. At first I thought she was only showing off, but then I realized she might be angry.

I tried to swim back to the pier to get out. But very quickly, Dusty hit me hard with her nose. It was very strong and very painful. I was pushed forward.

People on the pier stared at me in shock. Dusty stayed next to me, still hitting the water with her tail. That scared me the most. I thought, if she hits me with her tail, I could go under the water and die.

I reached the pier, but I could not climb out because I was hurt. I felt terrible fear. I shouted for help. A man reached in and pulled me onto the steps.

Then another man came. He said he was an orthopaedic surgeon who specialized in marine trauma. He had been driving into Doolin and saw what was happening. I was very cold and very worried. I did not know how bad my injuries were. My biggest fear was internal bleeding.

He examined me and tried to calm me. He said he could not find signs of internal bleeding, but he thought I probably had broken bones. Later I found out I had six fractures in my spine, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for several weeks. I could not work for five months. I had little movement, and I felt stiff and in pain.

Later doctors said I had post-traumatic stress. After the near-death experience, I felt anxious about everything. I reacted strongly in ways I never did before. I felt people were looking at me in a wrong way. Loud noises became hard for me. I also had memory loss.

Three months before the accident, I opened a health-food shop. But I had to give it up because I could not work.

It was the hardest year of my life, but now it is in the past. I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building my own osteopathy practice. I understand patients more because I have been a patient.

I am thankful that I am healthy. I also want to stop other people from getting hurt. People often think dolphins are always kind and safe. Many people trust them. Who would think a dolphin could attack a person? If a dangerous animal comes at you with its teeth showing, it looks scary. But dolphins have a wide smile, so they do not look frightening.

I am not angry with Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her territory, and she is a wild animal that can act in unexpected ways. People need to know this. Many people come to swim with her, and they do not understand how dangerous it can be. People said my injuries were among several injuries that summer.

After the man pulled me out, Dusty swam away. Then she came back. She floated upright next to me and looked at me. We looked into each other’s eyes. I felt she was sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin. The anger was gone. The people on the pier were amazed. After that moment with her, the terror ended. I made peace with her.
