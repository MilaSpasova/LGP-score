This new light could be as useful as Trevor Baylis’s wind-up radio was in the 1990s, before the internet. It is a cheap light that gets free power from gravity. It could help stop the use of dangerous kerosene lamps in Africa and India.

British designer Patrick Hunt first tried to get money from banks or venture capital to start selling his invention. But he had trouble. He said getting funding was slow and complicated. The idea was new, and people did not want to take the risk.

So he tried crowdfunding on a US website called Indiegogo, which had just opened in the UK. In five days, he reached his goal and raised £36,200. His campaign was very popular at the end of 2012. In 40 days, he raised £400,000.

The LED light uses a dynamo. The dynamo is turned by a 10kg bag of rocks that slowly falls. The bag is attached to the light and lifted to about 2m high. As it falls to the ground, it makes enough power for 30 minutes of light.

Hunt is getting ready to produce the light in China. He will test the market again by sending 1,000 lights to Africa before full mass production. He hopes to make millions of units.

Hunt is part of a new group of entrepreneurs using the fast-growing crowdfunding industry to get money. Another new site is InvestingZone. It connects wealthy people with start-up entrepreneurs.

Indiegogo does not give shares. Instead, it lets people offer “perks” for different donation levels. People who donated to Hunt’s light could feel good about helping poorer people, and they also got their own light.

Danae Ringelmann, a co-founder of Indiegogo, says the “gravity light” shows how crowdfunding can work fairly and can test an idea. She says money can be fast and efficient because it is not about knowing bank decision makers or venture capital people. It is about showing customers and fans that the idea is good, and getting them to support it with money.

Ringelmann also says that even if an idea does not get funding, it is still worth testing. You can save time by learning early that it is not a good idea, and you can learn faster.

Ringelmann is based in the US. She started her career as a Wall Street analyst. In 2008, she quit and used her skills to help friends in the arts raise money. The site first focused on films and launched at the Sundance Film Festival that year.

Five years later, Indiegogo was raising about $2m a week for new businesses in start-up and growth stages. In December 2012, it launched euro and sterling services to grow in Europe. It says Britain is its third biggest market. International activity is up 41% since December.

Indiegogo has many competitors, including Kickstarter, Seedrs, and Funding Circle. But Ringelmann says Indiegogo is different because anyone can start a campaign. No project is seen as too strange.

Indiegogo charges a 4% fee for successful campaigns. If a campaign does not reach its target, users can choose to refund all the money to donors for free, or keep the money raised but pay a 9% fee.

A British woman, Lauren Pears, raised £100,000 on the site to open a “cat café” in London. It is called Lady Dinah’s Cat Emporium. It has not opened yet, but it is described as a place where people can sit in a comfortable wingback chair with hot tea, a book, and a cat.

Ringelmann says some campaigns are rejected by venture capitalists. The investors may say the idea is good, but they do not know if the market wants it. They may worry it is a gadget that nobody cares about.

She says entrepreneurs can run an Indiegogo campaign even before launching the project. The campaign can be enough proof for venture capitalists to see there is a market.

Ringelmann says crowdfunding lets you test your market, your price, and your features. It can also help you find new ways to make money and get important feedback.

With her Wall Street experience, and after helping 100,000 businesses and services raise money, Ringelmann has developed advice for new entrepreneurs. She says many people have ideas, but success depends on doing the work well. She also says that if you are afraid someone else will steal your idea and do it faster and better, then you are not the right person to do it. She says you need confidence to move fast and learn.

Ringelmann says expanding into Europe and making a deal with a web transaction provider are part of a dream to make finance more open to everyone. This deal will allow payments through local card services like Maestro in the UK and Carte Bleue in France, as well as PayPal. More than 7,000 finance campaigns are live on the site.

Crowdfunding as an alternative to banks has grown, but it is not very attractive to rich investors. They usually want a stake in a strong business. This could start to change in the UK with the launch of InvestingZone.
