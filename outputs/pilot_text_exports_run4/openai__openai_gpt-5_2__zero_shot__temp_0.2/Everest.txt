Kenton Cool usually likes to talk, but now he can hardly speak. Climbing high mountains has made his voice rough. He is in Kathmandu, the capital of Nepal. He flew there from Everest base camp that morning.

Cool is thinking about a surprising set of climbs he finished the weekend before. Early Saturday, he reached the top of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of three main peaks in the Everest “horseshoe.” This horseshoe surrounds a icy valley called the Western Cwm.

That same day, he climbed to the top of Everest. He reached the summit early on Sunday, in complete darkness. Then he and his climbing partner went on to the top of Lhotse on Monday morning. Lhotse is the third peak in this three-peak challenge.

Cool says it was a quick chance to do it. For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. He says this does not remove the hard physical work he did. But he thinks the next person will set a higher goal, and do it without ropes or bottled oxygen.

Sixty years after Everest was first climbed, many stories look back at Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. People talk about an earlier, simpler time, before today’s commercial business and damage to the environment. The writer asks Cool to look ahead and imagine what top climbers might do 60 years from now.

Cool says he does not like to think about it. But he talks about the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. In April, Steck left the mountain after what Cool calls “an altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck planned to climb Everest’s west ridge, first climbed in 1963. Then Steck planned to go down to the South Col and immediately climb Lhotse by a new route. He planned to do this without fixed ropes. Cool says Steck had been training extremely hard. Cool says Steck was a special climber, very skilled and very fit. Cool says it would have been amazing to see what Steck could have done.

Tourism is another question. One sign is a helicopter rescue done by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro’s angry words helped cause the conflict at Camp 2. On Tuesday, Moro flew a powerful helicopter back to Everest to rescue a sick climber at 7,800 metres.

This was the highest rescue ever done on Everest. It shows that helicopter flights have been growing very fast in recent years. By 2073, there could even be a helipad on the South Col. Tourists could be brought there while breathing bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing rescue possibilities for both climbers and for the many trekkers who go as far as base camp.

Mountain geographer and environmentalist Alton Byers says it is not clear if the Everest region can handle the fast-growing tourism business. He says climate change and tourism together are putting new pressure on the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers in the Everest region are shrinking, and some are disappearing. This is already causing big problems. Byers says people everywhere are talking about having less water. There is less water for farming, and less water for the new lodges being built.

Byers says that in the Sherpa town of Namche Bazaar, a new five-mile pipeline is being built to bring water. It will help meet the growing tourist demand for showers and flush toilets. The local stream has human waste in it, and it does not provide enough water. In the busy season, the town is very crowded. Byers says every village is digging a garbage pit just beyond the houses. He says Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world. At base camp, human waste is now handled well and taken away in plastic barrels. But Byers says the barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley, and it could leak into the region’s water.

Byers says these problems can be fixed, but people need to take them seriously. He says one climber can spend $85,000 to climb Everest. He says that is fine, but at some point other needs must be addressed too. He says for half a million dollars a year, most of the problems could be solved.

Byers says climate change is harder. He works with local conservation groups to find and plan for climate change effects. This often means finding new water sources or collecting rainwater. Another danger is that glacial lakes are growing quickly. They could burst and flood the Sherpa homeland. Byers says there may come a time when people will have to move out of the way.

Changing weather is also affecting tourism. More clouds during times that are usually clear are closing Lukla Airport more often. Lukla is the main entry point to the Everest region. A new road for 4x4 vehicles is being built to Lukla to make sure tourists and their money can still arrive. But Byers worries that Nepal is building roads too cheaply and too fast. He says this can cause serious soil erosion and landslides.

Byers says everyone knows Everest. He says it is like a warning sign that people understand. He says it is the perfect place to learn how to deal with problems like climate change and tourism.
