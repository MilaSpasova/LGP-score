This new light could be a great invention, like Trevor Baylis’s wind-up radio in the 1990s before the internet. It is a cheap light that uses gravity for free power. It could help stop people using dangerous kerosene lamps in Africa and India.

A British designer named Patrick Hunt wanted money to start selling his invention. He tried banks and venture capital, but it was hard. He said funding was slow and complicated, and the idea was unproven, so people did not want to take the risk.

So he tried crowdfunding on a US website called Indiegogo, which had just opened in the UK. In five days, he reached his goal and raised £36,200. By the end of 2012, his campaign was very popular. In 40 days, he raised £400,000.

The LED light uses a dynamo. The dynamo is powered when a 10kg bag of rocks slowly falls. The bag is attached to the light and lifted to about 2m high. As it slowly falls to the ground, it makes enough power for half an hour of light.

Hunt is getting ready to make the light in China. He will test the market again by sending 1,000 lights to Africa before making many more. He hopes to make millions of units.

Hunt is part of a new group of business people using crowdfunding to get money. Another new website is InvestingZone. It matches rich people with start-up entrepreneurs.

Indiegogo does not give shares. Instead, people can offer “perks” for different donation amounts. People who helped fund Hunt’s light felt good about helping poor people, and they also got their own light.

Danae Ringelmann, a co-founder of Indiegogo, said the “gravity light” shows how crowdfunding can work. She said it is fast and fair because it is not about knowing the right people at a bank or a venture capital company. It is about showing customers and fans that your idea is good, so they support it and pay for it.

She also said it can help you test ideas that might not get funding. If the idea does not work, you learn sooner and do not waste so much time.

Ringelmann lives in the US. She started her career as a Wall Street analyst. In 2008, she quit and used her skills to help friends in the arts raise money.

Indiegogo first focused on films and launched at the Sundance Film Festival that year.

Five years later, Indiegogo was raising about $2m a week for new businesses that are starting or growing. In December 2012, it started services in euros and sterling to grow in Europe. It says Britain is its third biggest market. International activity is up 41% since December.

Indiegogo has many competitors, such as Kickstarter, Seedrs, and Funding Circle. But Ringelmann says Indiegogo is different because anyone can start a campaign. No project is too strange.

Indiegogo charges a 4% fee if a campaign succeeds. If a campaign does not reach its goal, users can choose to refund all the money to contributors with no charge, or keep the money raised but pay a 9% fee.

A British woman named Lauren Pears raised £100,000 on the site to open a “cat café” in London. It is called Lady Dinah’s Cat Emporium. It has not opened yet, but it is described as a place where people can sit in a comfortable wingback chair, have hot tea, read a book, and spend time with a cat.

Ringelmann said some campaigns are rejected by venture capitalists. They may say it is a great idea but they do not know if people really want it.

She said entrepreneurs can run an Indiegogo campaign. Even if they do not launch the product, the campaign can show proof that there is a market, and then venture capitalists may believe in it.

She said crowdfunding lets you test your market, pricing, and features, find new ways to earn money, and get important feedback.

Ringelmann said ideas are common. What matters is doing the work well. She said if you are afraid someone will steal your idea and do it better and faster, then you may not be the right person to do it. She said you need confidence, speed, and a willingness to learn.

Ringelmann said expanding into Europe and making a deal with a web transaction provider are part of a plan to make finance more open to everyone. This will allow payments through local card services like Maestro in the UK and Carte Bleue in France, as well as PayPal. More than 7,000 finance campaigns are live on the site.

Crowdfunding has grown as an option instead of banks, but it does not interest some very rich investors. They often want a stake in a business. That could start to change in the UK with the launch of InvestingZone.
