It was a warm summer evening. I decided to go for a swim near Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland. I moved there in 2012. In the water, a woman was swimming with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty often swims with a group of people.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Since then, hundreds of people have swum with her. This made it seem like she was very tame. She was even in an Irish tourist-board advertisement, where a girl said she wanted to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty’s tummy. It looked friendly. About 20 tourists and local people stood on the pier and watched. Soon after I got into the water, Dusty left the woman and suddenly became very wild. I later learned that she is very territorial when she is with someone. Her tail hit the water hard. At first I thought she was just showing off, but then I realized she might be angry.

I tried to swim back to the pier to get out. But very quickly, Dusty crashed into me with her snout. It was very strong and very painful. I was thrown forward.

People on the pier stared at me with their mouths open. Dusty stayed in the water next to me, still hitting the water with her tail. That scared me the most. I thought if she hit me with her tail, I could go under the water.

I reached the pier, but I could not climb out because I was hurt. I felt terrible fear. I shouted for help. A man reached in and pulled me onto the steps. Then another man came. He said he was an orthopaedic surgeon who specialized in marine trauma. He had been driving into Doolin and saw what was happening.

I was very cold and very worried. I did not know how badly I was hurt. I feared internal bleeding. The surgeon checked me and said he could not feel signs of internal bleeding, but he thought I probably had broken bones. Later I found out I had six fractures in my spine, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in the hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for several weeks. I could not work for five months. I had limited movement, stiffness, and pain. Then I was diagnosed with post-traumatic stress. After the near-death experience, I felt anxious and I overreacted in ways I never had before. I felt like people were looking at me the wrong way. Loud noises were hard for me. I also had memory loss.

Three months before the accident, I had opened a health-food shop. But I had to give it up because I could not work anymore. It was the hardest year. Now it is behind me. I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building my own osteopathy practice. I understand patients better because I have been a patient.

I am thankful that I am healthy. I also want to stop other people from getting hurt. People often think dolphins are always safe and friendly. Who would think a dolphin would attack a person? If you see a fierce animal coming with its teeth showing, it is scary. But dolphins have a wide smile, so they can seem harmless.

I am not angry at Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her territory, and she is a wild animal that can act in unexpected ways. People need to understand this. Many people come to swim with her, and they do not know how dangerous it can be. My injuries were said to be one of several injuries that summer.

After the man pulled me out, Dusty swam away. Then she came back. She floated upright near me and looked at me. We looked into each other’s eyes. I felt she was sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin, and the anger was gone. The people on the pier were amazed. After that moment, my terror ended. I made peace with her.
