Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8th April, 2014, she told people about a surprising event. She received a message in a bottle. Her grandfather had thrown the bottle into the Baltic Sea 101 years earlier. People think it may be the world’s oldest message in a bottle. A museum in Germany gave it to her and is now showing it.

Erdmann, 62, said it was a big surprise. A man came to her door and said he had mail from her grandfather. He explained that a message in a bottle had been found, and the name on the card was her grandfather’s. The visitor was a family history researcher. He found Erdmann in Berlin after the letter was given to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg.

The bottle was a brown beer bottle. It had been in the water for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it in his catch while fishing in the Baltic Sea near the city of Kiel. Holger von Neuhoff, a curator at the museum, said it was the oldest bottled message he had seen. He said the museum has older documents, but not with the bottle. With both the bottle and the document, he said this is the oldest right now. He also said it is in very good condition.

Researchers think Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913 while hiking with a nature group. He was 20 years old.

Much of the postcard could not be read. But the Berlin address on the front could be read. The card also asked the person who found it to send the note to his home address.

Erdmann said he also put two stamps in the bottle, so the finder would not have to pay. She said he did not think it would take 101 years.

She said the message made her feel very emotional. She did not know her grandfather because he died at age 54, six years before she was born.

She said she knew very little about him, but learned he was a writer. She said he was open-minded and believed in freedom and respect for others. She said he did a lot for young people and later travelled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful to learn more about her family roots.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and travelling, like her grandfather. She said she is open-minded too. She said what he taught his two daughters, her mother taught her, and she has taught the same things to her sons.

Even though she was happy to get the message, she said she hopes people today will not throw bottles into the sea. She said the sea is already full of bottles and rubbish.

The message and bottle will be shown at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts will try to read the rest of the text. It is not clear what will happen to the bottle later, but Erdmann hopes it will stay in the museum.

She said they want to share some photos to show with the bottle, so visitors can see the young man who threw it into the water.
