All six lottery numbers match, so some people think it is time to buy an Audi, book a holiday in the US, and call an estate agent. A study says many lottery millionaires spend money like this.

Since it started in 1994, the lottery has made 3,000 millionaires. Together, they have won more than £8.5bn in total. On average, each winner got £2.8m. The study by Oxford Economics says their money also helped create another 3,780 millionaires among their children, family, and friends.

Most winners (59%) stop working right away. But 19% keep doing their usual job, and 31% do unpaid volunteer work. The study says 98% of winners’ spending stayed in the UK. It also says that by spending on homes, cars, and holidays, each winner supports six full-time jobs for one year.

The winners added almost £750m to GDP and brought in more than £500m in taxes for the Exchequer. Most money went on property. Winners spent £2.72bn on their main homes, and £170m on paying off debt and mortgages.

Many winners wanted to keep an income, so they spent £2.125bn on investments. They gave £1.17bn as gifts to family and friends. They spent £680m on cars and holidays.

Camelot, which runs the UK National Lottery, paid for the study to mark 3,000 winners. The study used research from 100 winners who won £1m or more. It says the 3,000 winners bought 7,958 houses or flats in the UK, which is 2.7 each, and spent £3.3bn. Most winners (82%) changed their main home and spent about £900,000 on average.

Some new homes had extra things. Almost a third (29%) bought a hot tub. A walk-in wardrobe was important for 28%. Almost a quarter (24%) chose a home behind electric gates. About 22% had a games room, and 7% added a snooker table.

Big homes need help. About 30% hired a cleaner and 24% hired a gardener. A small number (5%) hired a beautician.

Audis were the favourite car for 16% of winners. Range Rovers and BMWs were also popular (11% each), plus Mercedes (10%) and Land Rovers (5%). Winners spent £463m on 17,190 cars. The average price of their favourite car was £46,116.

Holidays were also important. Most winners (68%) chose five-star hotels overseas. The US was the top place to go (27%), followed by the Caribbean (9%). In the UK, caravan sales also grew. Over the past 18 years, 10% of millionaires bought a caravan, making sales of about £7.4m.

Some winners (15%) started their own business. About 9% helped other people start businesses, and 6% invested in or bought other people’s businesses. The study says these businesses employ 3,195 people.

Andy Logan, who wrote the report, said a win affects more people than expected. It can change the lives of friends and family, and it also affects the UK economy because so much money is spent in the UK. He said each win creates a ripple effect for this generation and often the next one too.
