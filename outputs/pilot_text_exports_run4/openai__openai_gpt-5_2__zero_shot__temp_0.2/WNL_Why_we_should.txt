James Hamblin is a senior editor at the American magazine The Atlantic. In his series “If Our Bodies Could Talk,” he tried a challenge: he stopped showering for a while to see what happens when we clean our bodies too much. He took fewer showers, and when he did shower, he did not use shampoo or soap.

He found something many other people have noticed. When we use a lot of soap, body wash, and scrubbing tools, our skin tries harder to get back to normal. Showering can wash away the skin’s natural oils and helpful bacteria. This can happen even more when you use hot water and cleaning products.

After a shower, you might feel dry and tight, like your skin is too small. That is because a lot of your skin’s natural moisture has been washed away. Also, our skin, like our gut, has millions of helpful bacteria living on it. Frequent scrubbing can remove these bacteria. When the bacteria grow back, the new mix can include more microbes that make body odour. So showering too often may actually make you smell more. When you stop showering and stop using soap, your skin may go through an early adjustment period that can seem gross. After that, the skin often returns to balance, makes less oil, and healthy bacteria can grow again.

After his experiment, Hamblin agreed with other people who avoid soap and showers: the human body can do well on its own. This is not only about smell or looks. Dermatologists suggest that using soap less may help acne and eczema.

Showering less can also help the environment. The average shower lasts seven minutes and uses 65 litres of water. That is 65 litres of clean, drinkable water mixed with soap and sent down the drain each day, sometimes more than once.

Clean water is becoming more important, especially as California enters another summer of drought. It is becoming clear that clean water is one of the world’s most valuable resources, and it may soon be in short supply. Also, body wash bottles create more waste. These are strong reasons to use fewer products and let your body be more natural.

If this idea makes you feel uncomfortable because you remember being near people who do not shower much and still smell, try not to worry. Many people who cut back on washing still use deodorant if they need it. Everyone’s natural smell is different, and it can change with diet, how much water you drink, and exercise. Also, washing your hands with soap is still recommended to help stop infectious diseases from spreading.

You do not have to stop all at once like Hamblin did. But between cleaning many times a day and never showering, there is a simple middle choice. Reduce. Skip a few showers, use less soap, and let your helpful skin bacteria grow.
