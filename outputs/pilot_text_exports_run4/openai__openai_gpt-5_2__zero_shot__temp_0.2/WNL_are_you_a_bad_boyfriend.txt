Dan Sullivan was late to breakfast with the Guardian. He was stopped by the police while riding his motorcycle.

Sullivan says he works too much. He misses dinner dates. He forgets to buy presents.

So the 27-year-old venture capitalist made a start-up called BetterBoyfriend.me. For about $70 a month, it sends a present every month to a girlfriend or wife.

Sullivan works in finance. He has been testing the service quietly. About 350 boyfriends have signed up. The service makes about $17,000 each month. Most members, he says, are his Harvard and investor friends. They include venture capitalists, founders, and workers at companies like Apple, Google, and Facebook. Their girlfriends have been getting presents from Sullivan for the last year.

Each month, members pick from a list of seven gifts, like chocolate, tea sets, or manicures. The package is sent to the boyfriend with no brand name and no label.

Sullivan tests ideas by buying Facebook ads to see how well they do. BetterBoyfriend.me became popular. He said it solved a problem many people have.

BetterBoyfriend.me was not his first idea. For a few days, he thought about a dog-sharing idea. He said he saw rich guys looking unhappy while walking dogs. He said a dog is only valuable 5% of the time. He thought about paying someone to watch dogs so other people could rent them.

Another start-up is called InvisibleBoyfriend. It lets users create a social partner who is always available to talk. The result is a “boyfriend” who can text with you all day.

The founder, Kyle Tabor, started it to help users stop unwanted attention. But he found that some people became attached to their computer-made partners. He said many users want companionship through conversation, not just “proof” of a relationship.

For Sullivan and Tabor, a surprise was the real relationships they formed with customers. Sullivan says he has started to act like a relationship helper for the boyfriends.

Sullivan says he made mistakes. Early packages had receipts with his name, Dan Sullivan. One boyfriend wrote to him and said Cynthia found out.

Sullivan says about 50% of the women who get the packages know he is involved. He says this is linked to age. He thinks that after people have been married a while, they keep fewer secrets.

Over the year, Sullivan says he got to know the boyfriends well. One time, they even delivered to a hospital delivery room. He said the important thing is that his business relationship is with the boyfriend.

When he first started the company, he put BetterBoyfriend.me tags on flower bouquets. He went to Dolores Park in the Mission District, a place where many young people and tech workers relax.

He looked for couples and gave the flowers to the girl. The boyfriend did not like it. So he changed his plan and started giving the flowers to the boyfriends instead.
