From glow-in-the-dark trees to bike storage under the ground and solar-powered bins, there are many unusual ideas to make cities better places to live.

Living in a city can be great, but it is often not easy to live in a way that is good for the planet. People deal with pollution, traffic, and fewer green spaces. Inventors have new ideas to help. Here are some of the wackier solutions.

Pop-up parks
Some cities seem built more for cars than for people. A pop-up park is a simple idea. You take an empty car park and add a few things, like some coins and a plant, and you make a small park. The PARK(ing) project started as an art experiment in San Francisco and then spread around the world. People have also made temporary city farms and ecology demos. The movement has a day of action every September.

Subterranean storage
Not everyone in a city wants to drive a car. Bicycles are more popular now. But people need a safe place to keep them. A Tokyo engineering company called Giken made an idea called an “eco-cycle, anti-seismic underground bicycle park”. It is a round storage space that is only seven metres wide, and it goes deep underground. It can hold 204 bikes. Owners press a button, and the system brings the bike back above ground in about 13 seconds.

Glow-in-the-dark trees
When many people think of trees that glow in the dark, they think of Christmas decorations. But Dutch designer-artist Daan Roosegaarde has a different idea. He has made a “bioluminescent” plant. The experimental technology mixes DNA from glowing sea bacteria with parts of a plant so it can glow like a jellyfish. Trials are happening to make a large-scale version. Roosegaarde hopes this could one day replace normal street lighting.

Footfall harvesting
In West Ham in east London, many commuters and shoppers walk over a raised walkway near the underground station every day. Most do not notice that the ground feels a little springy. The rubber surface moves about five millimetres when people step on it. The floor has smart tiles that capture energy from footsteps and turn it into electricity. This helps power the streetlights above. Pavegen, the UK company behind this idea, has also installed a similar system at Heathrow Airport in London and other places.

Supertrees
Singapore’s Gardens by the Bay has man-made trees called “supertrees”. They are steel-framed and can be up to 50 metres high. Flowers and ferns grow on them. Their metal tops help absorb and spread out heat. They can collect rainwater and also help move air for two “climate-controlled biomes” (large conservatories) below. Eleven of the 18 trees also have solar panels on their “branches”.
