Anitta is a new Brazilian funk star. She has won millions of fans by bringing favela music into the mainstream. But she is now in a debate about skin colour.

Anti-discrimination groups and writers say the music industry’s fastest rising star had to give up part of her black identity to succeed with the mostly white middle-class market.

The debate grew after people shared “then-and-now” photos of Anitta. They show her skin looking much lighter after she signed a deal with Warner.

In the first photo, from when she was not famous, she looked darker. In the second photo, a marketing picture after she became famous, she looks paler. People asked if this happened because of skin-whitening products, cosmetic surgery, or (more likely) Photoshop. The big change made people talk again about whether you need to be pale to get ahead in Brazil.

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote a post about hidden discrimination in media and marketing. She said many people say they are not racist and think Brazil is a “multiracial democracy,” but statistics show that is not true. She said whitening shows a society that does not support diversity. She said people often connect “white” with rich, successful, and good, and “black” with the opposite.

Anitta’s real name is Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

She became very famous with an album and with a hit single, Show das Poderosas. The song reached number 1 and got 52 million YouTube views.

Many fans love her as a pop idol with strong messages and catchy songs. Her supporters also present her as a bridge between the mostly black and mixed-race shanty towns on Rio’s hillsides and the richer and whiter areas below.

She has reduced the sexual dancing, gangsta talk, and explicit words of baile funk. But now people ask if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract a richer audience. Arraes said pop stars may feel forced to change their looks, like straightening curly hair or getting rhinoplasty for a big nose. She said this can harm self-esteem and create a bad cycle.

Race is a sensitive topic in Brazil, a mostly mixed-race country. Brazil was one of the last large countries to ban slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. But in Brazil, colour often matters more than race and ancestry. Even though Brazil wants to be a multiracial democracy, skin tone is clearly linked to inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Up to 2011, black and mixed-race students also spent about two fewer years in school on average.

The government says the gap is getting smaller because of quota systems for university places and other affirmative action. But big differences remain. Most business and government executives are white, while many menial jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In areas like Ipanema and Gávea, it is more common to see black nannies pushing strollers with white toddlers than the other way around.

Colour is hard to define. In the 2010 census, people who called themselves white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million black, two million Asian, and 0.5% indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology lecturer at the Federal University of Pernambuco, said Anitta won middle-class fans by taking a rebellious sound and making it calmer and easier for everyone to accept. He said this was not done by racial whitening. He said it was a change in the social space where she made her art: from the periphery to the centre.

Others say the focus on colour is too strong. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who worked with Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot but her colour did not. He said it was more about production choices, like makeup, hairstylists, and clothes. He said he did not think it came from pressure. He said she always liked to sing, dance, and show herself, and that this natural side helped her succeed.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador, said the prejudice was not from the public but from music producers and media executives. He said the public showed they cared about music itself when they chose Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, as the recent winner of The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for comment. But in a recent interview, she said identity should be defined by the person. She said an “all-powerful” woman does not need to be beautiful, but can have so much attitude that she is marvellous and powerful. She said her work tries to show that we can be who we want.
