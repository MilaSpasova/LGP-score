Opposition to Western Australia’s shark cull has grown. Thousands of people went to beaches across Australia and asked the state’s premier to stop the policy. RSPCA Australia and Virgin Atlantic owner Richard Branson also spoke against it.

The policy involves catching and killing sharks longer than three metres. It started after what the state government called an “unprecedented” number of shark attacks on Western Australia’s coast. In November 2013, a 35-year-old surfer was killed. He was the sixth person to die from a shark attack in two years.

However, the Shark Attack File says that Australia has averaged one shark-related death a year for the last 50 years.

Kate Faehrmann, a board member at the Sea Shepherd Conservation Society, spoke at a protest in Perth. She said the policy will not work. She said drumlines, used to catch sharks, can kill many animals. She said they can kill sharks of any size, and also dolphins, turtles, and other animals. She said this is why the community does not want the policy.

Thousands protested on Perth’s Cottesloe Beach and Sydney’s Manly Beach. Hundreds also protested at Glenelg in south-west Adelaide, and at beaches in Victoria and Queensland. Faehrmann said the protests showed many Australians want sharks protected. She said people are saying, “That’s their ocean. We respect them, we love them and we don’t want them killed.”

Anthony Joyce, a surfer who once had his foot caught in a shark’s mouth, said killing the sharks will make no difference overall.

The state government has refused to give a running total of sharks killed. There have been reports of sharks smaller than three metres being released after being caught on drumlines. Drumlines are floating drums anchored to the sea bed, with bait hanging on hooks under them.

Conservationists say there is no evidence the cull will reduce shark attacks on humans. They say no earlier cull used only drumlines. Researchers at the University of Western Australia say the recent rise in attacks may be linked to the state having the fastest-growing population in Australia, not to more sharks.

Richard Peirce, chairman of the UK-based conservation charity the Shark Trust, said the cull will not work and could even bring more predators closer to the coast. He said it is sad that the government ignored advice and chose a plan that is ineffective and harmful. He also said that even if drumlines are checked during the day, leaving them overnight could attract other predators to the area because injured animals are on the hooks.

Around the world in 2012, there were 80 unprovoked shark attacks, and seven were fatal. In comparison, humans kill nearly 100m sharks each year.

RSPCA Australia said it believes the cull is not justified. It said there is no evidence that more attacks are caused by more sharks. Instead, it said the pattern fits changes in population and human behaviour, meaning more people are in the water.

Richard Branson told Fairfax Radio the policy was backfiring. He said Premier Colin Barnett may have thought it would help tourism, but Branson said it will do the opposite. He said it makes the problem seem bigger than it is and could stop people from visiting Perth and the countryside. He said it will only make people worry for no good reason.
