Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is theft. He says it is like reaching into someone’s pocket and stealing a wallet. Pullman wrote this in an article for Index on Censorship. He is president of the Society of Authors. He strongly supports copyright laws. He criticizes internet users who download music or books without paying.

Pullman writes that the technology is very impressive, but people do not see that what they are doing is wrong. He says it is outrageous that people can steal an artist’s work and not be punished.

His article was written after music industry leaders met British Prime Minister David Cameron at Downing Street. They talked about web piracy.

Pullman, who wrote the His Dark Materials trilogy, says authors and musicians often work for years in poverty and without attention. He says that when their work finally becomes popular, the chance to earn a living can be taken away by illegal downloading. He ends with a simple idea: if we want to enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

In the next issue of the magazine, Pullman is in a dialogue with Cathy Casserly. She is the chief executive of Creative Commons. Creative Commons offers open content licences that let creators choose how to use copyright. Casserly says many things are wrong with copyright because it was made in an “analogue age.” She says copyright often stops people from sharing, building on, and remixing each other’s work.

Casserly says artists need to think in new ways about how they share and make money from their work. She quotes science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees that the current system has problems. The editor, Rachael Jolley, said existing copyright laws do not work well in the digital age and could make consumers into criminals. She said new models are needed so artists, writers, and musicians can earn a living.

Illegal downloading is very large. Ofcom, the UK communications regulator, collected data showing that in the UK, between November 2012 and January 2013, 280 million music tracks were pirated. Also pirated were 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files.

Ofcom said 18% of internet users over age 12 admit they recently pirated content. Another 9% say they fear getting caught. Pullman also writes that it is still surprising how easy and fast it is to get music as MP3 downloads, especially for people who have been building their iTunes list for some time.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron appointed Conservative MP Mike Weatherley as his adviser on this topic. A spokesman for the BPI, the record industry trade body, said Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creative producers it protects. The spokesman said they hope Weatherley and the prime minister will influence the UK’s intellectual property office.
