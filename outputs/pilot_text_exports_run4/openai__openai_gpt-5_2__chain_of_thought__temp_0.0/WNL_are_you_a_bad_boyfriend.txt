Dan Sullivan was late to breakfast with the Guardian. He was late because police pulled him over while he was riding his motorcycle.

Sullivan says he works too much. He misses dinner dates. He forgets to buy presents.

So, like many people in Silicon Valley, the 27-year-old venture capitalist made a start-up to help: BetterBoyfriend.me. This service sends girlfriends and wives a present every month for about $70.

Sullivan works in finance. He has been testing the program quietly. He has signed up about 350 boyfriends. This brings in about $17,000 each month. He says most members are his Harvard and investor friends. They include venture capitalists, founders, and employees at companies like Apple, Google, and Facebook. Their girlfriends have been getting presents from Sullivan for the last year.

Each month, members pick from a list of seven possible gifts, like chocolate, tea sets, and manicures. The package is sent to the boyfriend without any brand name or label.

Sullivan tests ideas by buying Facebook ads to see how much interest they get. BetterBoyfriend.me quickly became popular. He said it solved a problem many people have.

BetterBoyfriend.me was not his first idea. For a few days, he thought about a dog-sharing idea. He said he saw rich guys looking unhappy while walking dogs. He thought a dog is only valuable 5% of the time. His idea was to have someone babysit the dogs, and other people could rent them.

Another start-up, called InvisibleBoyfriend, lets users create a believable social partner who is always available to talk. The result is a “boyfriend” who can text with you all day.

Founder Kyle Tabor started it to give users a fake boyfriend to stop unwanted attention. But he found that people started to care about their algorithm partners. He said many users want companionship through conversation, not just “proof” of a relationship.

For Sullivan and Tabor, a surprise was how real the relationships with customers became. Sullivan says he has started to feel like a relationship adviser for the boyfriends.

Sullivan says he made mistakes. At first, the packages included receipts with his name, Dan Sullivan. One boyfriend wrote to him and said, “Listen, dude, she’s not mad but Cynthia found out.”

About 50% of the women who get BetterBoyfriend.me packages know Sullivan is involved. He says this is connected to age. He thinks that after people have been married for a while, they keep fewer secrets.

Over the year, Sullivan says he has gotten to know the boyfriends well. They even made a delivery to a hospital delivery room. He said the key is to remember that his business relationship is with the boyfriend.

When he first launched the company, he put tags that said BetterBoyfriend.me on flower bouquets. He went to Dolores Park, a popular place in the Mission District, which has many tech workers and young people.

He looked for couples and gave the bouquet to the girl. But the boyfriend did not like that. Sullivan said it made sense, so he changed his plan and started giving the bouquets to the boyfriends instead.
