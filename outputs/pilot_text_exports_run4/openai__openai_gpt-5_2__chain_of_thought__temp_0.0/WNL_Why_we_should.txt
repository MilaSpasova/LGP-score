James Hamblin is a senior editor at The Atlantic magazine. He recently tried living with much less washing. In his series, "If Our Bodies Could Talk," he did a no-showering challenge to see what happens when we clean our bodies too much. He took fewer showers. When he did shower, he did not use shampoo or soap.

He found something many other people have also found. When we try very hard to clean with soaps, body washes, and scrubbers, our skin works harder to get back to normal. Showering washes away the skin’s natural oil and bacteria. This can work too well, especially with hot water and cleaning products.

After a shower, you may feel like your skin is too tight. That happens because much of your skin’s natural moisture gets washed away. Our skin also has millions of helpful bacteria, like our gut does. Frequent rubbing and scrubbing can destroy these bacteria.

When bacteria grow back after being washed off with soap, the new bacteria may make more smell. So showering too often may actually make you smell more. If you stop showering and using soap, your skin may go through an early adjustment time that can seem gross. After that, the skin usually returns to balance, makes less oil, and healthy bacteria can grow again.

In the end, Hamblin learned what other no-soap and no-shower followers have known for years: the human body can work well on its own. It is not only about smell or looks. Dermatologists suggest that using soap less can help acne and eczema.

Showering less can also help the environment. The average shower lasts seven minutes and uses 65 litres of water. That is 65 litres of clean, drinkable water mixed with soap and sent down the drain every day, sometimes more than once.

Clean water is getting harder to ignore, as California enters another summer of drought. It is becoming clear that clean water is one of the world’s most valuable resources, and it may soon be in short supply. Also think about the waste from all those body wash bottles. These are strong reasons to let your body be more natural.

If this idea makes you feel uncomfortable because you remember being close to people who do not shower much and still smell, relax. Many people who wash less still use deodorant if they need it. Everyone’s natural smell is different, and it can change with diet, hydration, and exercise. Also, washing your hands with soap is still recommended to help stop the spread of infectious diseases.

You do not have to stop all at once like Hamblin did. But there is a middle choice between cleaning many times a day and never showering. Reduce. Skip a few showers, use less soap, and let the helpful bacteria grow.
