The lottery balls have fallen down the chute, and all six numbers match. Now it is time to buy an Audi, book a holiday in the US, and call a real estate agent. An analysis says many lottery millionaires do these things.

Since the lottery started in 1994, it has made 3,000 millionaires. Together they have won more than £8.5bn in total. The average win was £2.8m each. The study by Oxford Economics says this money also helped create 3,780 more millionaires among winners’ children, family, and friends.

Most winners (59%) stop working right away. But 19% keep doing their usual job, and 31% do unpaid volunteer work. For the economy, 98% of winners’ spending stayed in the UK. The study estimates that each winner’s spending on homes, vehicles, and holidays keeps six people in full-time jobs for one year.

Winners have added almost £750m to gross domestic product (GDP). They also created more than £500m in tax money for the UK government. Most money was spent on property. Winners spent £2.72bn on their main homes, and £170m paying off old debts and mortgages.

Keeping an income was important too, so £2.125bn was spent on investments. Gifts to family and friends totaled £1.17bn. Cars and holidays cost £680m.

Camelot, the company that runs the UK National Lottery, asked for the study to mark 3,000 winners. It used research from 100 winners who had won £1m or more. It found that the 3,000 winners bought 7,958 houses or flats in the UK, which is 2.7 each on average, spending £3.3bn. Most winners (82%) changed their main home and spent an average of £900,000.

Many new homes had extra features. Almost a third (29%) bought a hot tub. A walk-in wardrobe was a must for 28%. Almost a quarter (24%) chose a home behind electric gates. Also, 22% had a games room, and 7% put in a snooker table.

Big homes need looking after. So 30% of winners hired a cleaner, and 24% hired a gardener. A small number (5%) hired a beautician.

Audi was the favourite car for 16% of winners. Range Rovers and BMWs were also popular (11% each), along with Mercedes (10%) and Land Rovers (5%). Winners spent £463m on 17,190 cars. The average price of their favourite car was £46,116.

Holidays were important too. Most winners (68%) chose five-star hotels overseas. The US was the top destination for 27%, followed by the Caribbean (9%). In the UK, caravan sales also gained. Over the past 18 years, 10% of millionaires bought a caravan, creating sales worth about £7.4m.

Some winners (15%) started their own businesses. Another 9% helped other people start businesses. Also, 6% invested in or bought other people’s businesses. The study says businesses started or supported by lottery winners employ 3,195 people.

Andy Logan, the author of the report, said the effect of a win spreads further than expected. He said it changes the lives of friends and family, and each win has a clear effect on the UK economy, especially because so much is spent in the UK. He said each win creates a ripple effect in this generation and often the next.
