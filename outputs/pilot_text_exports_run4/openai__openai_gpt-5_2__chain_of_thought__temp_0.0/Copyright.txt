Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is “moral squalor” and theft. He says it is as much theft as reaching into someone’s pocket and stealing their wallet. In an article for Index on Censorship, Pullman (who is president of the Society of Authors) strongly defends copyright laws. He is very critical of internet users who think it is OK to download music or books without paying.

Pullman writes that the technology is so impressive that people do not see the wrong thing they are doing. He says it is outrageous that someone can steal an artist’s work and get away with it. He repeats that it is theft, like stealing a wallet.

His article comes after music industry leaders met British Prime Minister David Cameron at Downing Street. They discussed web piracy.

Pullman, the writer of the His Dark Materials trilogy, says authors and musicians often work in poverty and without attention for years. They work to make something that gives delight to their audiences. But when they finally reach that level, he says their chance to make a living from it is taken away. He concludes that the rule is simple: if we want to enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it. He says this does not change because of technology, science, or magic.

Pullman is writing in the next issue of the group’s magazine in a dialogue with Cathy Casserly. Casserly is chief executive of Creative Commons, which offers open content licences that “lets creators take copyright into their own hands”. Casserly argues that much is wrong with copyright, which she says was created “in an analogue age”. She writes that copyright, by default, closes the door on many ways people can share, build on, and remix each other’s work. She says these possibilities were unimaginable when the laws were made.

Casserly says artists need to think in new ways about how they distribute and monetize their work. She quotes the science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees. The magazine’s editor, Rachael Jolley, said existing copyright laws do not work in the digital age and could criminalize consumers. She says new models are needed for how artists, writers, and musicians earn a living from their work.

The debate is lively, and illegal downloading is very large. Data collected by Ofcom (the independent regulator and competition authority for the UK communications industries) suggests that in the UK, between November 2012 and January 2013, 280 million music tracks were digitally pirated. Also pirated were 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files.

Ofcom has said 18% of internet users aged over 12 admit they have recently pirated content. Also, 9% say they fear getting caught. Pullman writes that the ease and speed of getting music as MP3 downloads is still astonishing to people like him who have been building up an iTunes list for some time.

One result of the Downing Street meeting was that Cameron appointed Conservative MP Mike Weatherley as his adviser on this subject. A spokesman for the BPI, the record industry trade body, said Mike Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creative producers it protects. The spokesman said they hope Weatherley’s influence, and the prime minister’s endorsement of copyright, will affect the approach of the UK’s intellectual property office.
