This invention could be as important as Trevor Baylis’s wind-up radio in the 1990s, before the internet. It is a cheap light that uses free power from gravity. It could help stop people in Africa and India from using dangerous kerosene lamps.

A British designer named Patrick Hunt tried to get money from banks or venture capital firms to start selling his invention. But he had a problem. He said getting funding was slow and complicated, and the idea was unproven, so nobody wanted to take the risk.

So Hunt tried crowdfunding on a US website called Indiegogo. The site had recently opened in the UK. In five days, he reached his goal and raised £36,200. His campaign was very popular at the end of 2012, and in 40 days he raised £400,000.

The LED light uses a dynamo to make power. The dynamo is turned by a 10kg bag of rocks that slowly drops down. The bag is attached to the light and lifted about 2m high. As it slowly falls to the ground, it makes enough power for 30 minutes of light.

Hunt is getting ready to make the light in China. Before making many more, he will test the market again by sending 1,000 lights to Africa. After that, he hopes to make millions of units.

Hunt is part of a new group of entrepreneurs who use the fast-growing crowdfunding industry to get money. Another new site is InvestingZone, which connects wealthy people with start-up entrepreneurs.

Indiegogo does not give people shares. Instead, it lets campaign owners offer “perks” for different donation amounts. People who helped fund Hunt’s light could feel good about helping poorer people, and they also got their own light.

Danae Ringelmann, a co-founder of Indiegogo, says the “gravity light” shows how crowdfunding can reward good ideas and test them. She says money can be fast and fair because it is not about knowing the right people at a bank or a venture capital firm. It is about proving your idea to customers and fans, so they support it and pay for it.

She also says it is useful to test ideas even if they do not get funding. Then you learn sooner that the idea was not good, and you save time.

Ringelmann lives in the US. She began her career as a Wall Street analyst. In 2008, she quit and used her skills to help friends in the arts raise money.

Indiegogo first focused on films. It launched at the Sundance Film Festival that year.

Five years later, the site raises about $2m a week for new businesses that are starting or growing. In December 2012, it added euro and sterling services to grow in Europe. The company says Britain is its third biggest market. International activity has risen 41% since December.

Indiegogo has many competitors, like Kickstarter, Seedrs, and Funding Circle. But Ringelmann says Indiegogo is different because anyone can start a campaign, and no project is too strange.

Indiegogo charges a 4% fee for campaigns that reach their goal. If a campaign does not reach its goal, users can choose to refund all the money to donors for free, or keep the money raised but pay a 9% fee.

A British woman named Lauren Pears raised £100,000 on the site to open a “cat café” in London. It is called Lady Dinah’s Cat Emporium. It has not opened yet, but it is advertised as a place where people can sit in a comfortable wingback chair, have a hot cup of tea, read a book, and be with a cat.

Ringelmann says some campaigns are rejected by venture capitalists because they do not know if people really want the product. She says entrepreneurs can run an Indiegogo campaign, and even if they do not launch the project, the campaign can show there is a real market. Then venture capitalists may believe customers want it.

She says crowdfunding helps you test your market, your price, and your features. It can also help you find new ways to make money and get important feedback.

Ringelmann says she has helped 100,000 businesses and services raise money, and she has improved her advice for new entrepreneurs. She says ideas are very common, and what matters is doing the work well. She says if you are scared someone will steal your idea and do it better and faster, then you are not the right person to do that idea. She says you need confidence to move fast and learn.

Ringelmann says expanding into Europe and making a deal with a web payments company are part of a dream to make finance open to more people. The deal will let people pay using local card services like Maestro in the UK and Carte Bleue in France, as well as PayPal. More than 7,000 fundraising campaigns are live on Indiegogo.

Crowdfunding has grown as a choice besides banks. But it does not interest some very wealthy investors, who want at least a stake in a good business. That might start to change in the UK with the launch of InvestingZone.
