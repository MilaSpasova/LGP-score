From glow-in-the-dark trees to underground bike sheds and solar-powered bins, we look at some unusual ideas that could make city life better.

Living in a city has many good points, but it is getting harder to live in a green, sustainable way. People in cities deal with pollution, heavy traffic, and fewer green spaces.

Now inventors are stepping in. We look at ten of the stranger ideas for making our cities more livable.

Pop-up parks
Some cities seem built more for cars than for people. A pop-up park is a simple idea. You take an empty parking space, a little money, and one or two potted plants, and you make a small park for a short time. The PARK(ing) project started as an art experiment in San Francisco. Since then, it has spread around the world. People have also tried temporary city farms and ecology demonstrations. The movement has a day of action every September.

Subterranean storage
Not everyone in cities uses a car. Bikes are becoming more popular. But where can you keep a bike safely? A Tokyo-based engineering firm called Giken created an idea called an “eco-cycle, anti-seismic underground bicycle park”. The storage space is a cylinder that is only seven metres wide. It goes deep into the ground and can hold 204 bikes. Owners can get their bike back by pressing a button. The system brings the bike up in about 13 seconds.

Glow-in-the-dark trees
Many people think of glowing Christmas decorations when they think of things that shine in the dark. But designer-artist Daan Roosegaarde thinks about glowing plants. The Dutch designer-artist has created an experimental “bioluminescent” plant. The technology mixes DNA from glowing marine bacteria with the chloroplast genome of a plant. This creates a glow like a jellyfish. Trials are happening to make a large industrial version of this biomimicry-inspired technology. Roosegaarde hopes it could one day replace normal street lights.

Footfall harvesting
Every day, many commuters and shoppers in West Ham, in east London, cross an elevated walkway near the underground station. Most people do not notice that the ground feels slightly springy. Even fewer people know that the five-millimetre flex in the rubber surface helps power the streetlights above. The walkway uses smart tiles that capture the kinetic energy from footsteps and turn it into electricity. Pavegen, the UK firm behind this idea, has also installed a similar system at London’s Heathrow Airport and in other places around the world.

Supertrees
Man-made trees were bound to appear one day. Singapore’s Gardens by the Bay has a small group of them. These steel-framed “supertrees” are up to 50 metres tall. Flowers and ferns grow on them. Their metal tops also help take in and spread out heat. They can collect rainwater too. They also help provide air ventilation for two “climate-controlled biomes” (large conservatories) below. Eleven of the 18 trees also have solar panels along their “branches”.
