Opposition to Western Australia’s shark cull has grown. Thousands of people went to beaches around Australia to ask the state’s premier to stop the policy. RSPCA Australia and Virgin Atlantic owner Richard Branson also spoke against it.

The policy involves catching and killing sharks longer than three metres. It started after what the state government called an “unprecedented” number of shark attacks on Western Australia’s coast. In November 2013, a 35-year-old surfer was killed. He was the sixth person to die from a shark attack in two years.

However, the Shark Attack File says Australia as a whole has averaged one shark-related death each year for the last 50 years.

Kate Faehrmann, a board member of the Sea Shepherd Conservation Society, spoke at a protest in Perth. She said the policy will not work. She said drumlines, which are used to catch sharks, kill many kinds of animals. She said they can kill sharks of any size, and also dolphins, turtles, and other animals. She said this is why the community does not want the policy.

Thousands of people protested at Perth’s Cottesloe Beach and Sydney’s Manly Beach. Hundreds protested at Glenelg in south-west Adelaide, and at beaches in Victoria and Queensland. Faehrmann said the protests showed many Australians want sharks protected. She said that even with fear from the movie Jaws, people were coming out to say the ocean belongs to sharks too, and they do not want them killed.

Anthony Joyce, a surfer who once had his foot caught in a shark’s mouth, said the number of sharks they plan to kill will make no difference.

The state government has refused to give an ongoing count of how many sharks have been killed. There have been reports of sharks smaller than three metres being released after being caught on drumlines. Drumlines are floating drums tied to the sea bed, with bait hanging on hooks under them.

Conservationists say there is no evidence the cull will reduce shark attacks on humans. They say no earlier cull used only drumlines. Researchers at the University of Western Australia say the recent group of shark attacks may be linked to the state having the fastest-growing population in Australia, not to more sharks.

Richard Peirce, chairman of the UK-based conservation charity the Shark Trust, said the cull would not work and could even bring more predators closer to the coast. He said leaving drumlines in overnight could attract other predators. He said they could be drawn in by sharks and other animals that are hooked and hurt.

Worldwide in 2012, there were 80 unprovoked shark attacks, and seven were fatal. This is compared to nearly 100 million sharks killed by humans each year.

RSPCA Australia said it believes the cull is not justified. It said there is no evidence the rise in attacks is because there are more sharks. Instead, it said the rise matches changes in population and human behaviour, meaning more people are in the water.

Richard Branson told Fairfax Radio the policy could do the opposite of what was intended. He said he thought the Western Australia Premier, Colin Barnett, may have wanted to encourage tourism. Branson said it could instead stop people from wanting to visit Perth and the surrounding countryside. He said it may worry people for no good reason.
