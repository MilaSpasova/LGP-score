Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8 April 2014, she talked about a special moment. She received a message in a bottle. Her grandfather had thrown it into the Baltic Sea 101 years earlier. People think it may be the world’s oldest message in a bottle. A museum in Germany, which is showing it now, gave it to her.

Erdmann, who is 62, said it was very surprising. She said a man came to her door and told her he had mail from her grandfather. He said a message in a bottle had been found, and the name on the card was her grandfather’s name. The visitor was a family history researcher. He found Erdmann in Berlin after the letter was given to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg.

The bottle was a brown beer bottle. It had been in the water for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it in his catch while fishing in the Baltic Sea near the northern city of Kiel. Holger von Neuhoff, a museum curator for ocean and science, said it was the oldest bottled message he had seen. He said the museum has older documents found without a bottle, but with both the bottle and the document, this one is the oldest at the moment. He said it is in very good condition.

Researchers think Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913. He was hiking with a nature group. He was 20 years old then.

Most of the postcard cannot be read. But the address in Berlin on the front can be read. The card also asked the person who found it to send the note to his home address.

Erdmann said her grandfather also put two stamps from that time in the bottle, so the finder would not have to pay. She said he did not think it would take 101 years.

Erdmann said the message made her feel emotional. She did not know her grandfather because he died at age 54, six years before she was born.

She said she knew very little about him. But she learned that he was a writer. She said he was open-minded, believed in freedom, and believed people should respect each other. She said he did a lot for young people and later traveled with his wife and two daughters. She said it was wonderful because she could see where her family roots came from.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and traveling around the world. She said she is open-minded too. She said what her grandfather taught his two daughters, her mother taught her, and she has passed it on to her sons.

Even though she was happy to get the message, she said she hoped others would not do the same thing and throw bottles with messages into the sea. She said the sea today is already full of bottles and trash, so people should not throw more in.

The message and the bottle will be shown at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts will try to read the rest of the text. It is not clear what will happen to the bottle after that, but Erdmann hopes it will stay in the museum.

She said they want to add some photos with the bottle, so visitors can see the young man who threw the bottle into the water.
