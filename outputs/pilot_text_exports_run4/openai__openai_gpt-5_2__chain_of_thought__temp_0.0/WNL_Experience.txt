It was a beautiful summer evening, and I decided to go for a swim at Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland, where I moved in 2012. A woman was in the water with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty often swims with a group of people, and she seems to know them well.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Since then, hundreds of people have swum with her, so she can seem completely tame. She was even in an Irish tourist-board ad where a girl says she wants to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty’s tummy. It looked very tempting. About 20 tourists and local people were on the pier, watching.

Right after I got into the water, Dusty left the woman and suddenly went crazy. I learned later that she is very territorial when she is with someone. Her tail was flapping hard. At first, I thought she was just showing off, but then I realized she might be angry.

I knew I had to get out, so I swam toward the pier. But in a tiny moment, Dusty crashed into me with her nose. It was very strong and very painful, and she was unbelievably fast. I was thrown forward.

Everyone on the pier stared down at me with their mouths open. Dusty was still next to me, and her tail kept flapping wildly. That scared me the most. I thought, if she hits me with her tail, I could go under the water and die.

I reached the pier, but I could not get out because I was hurt. I felt terrified. I shouted for help, and a man reached in and pulled me onto the steps. Then another man came and said he was an orthopaedic surgeon who specialized in marine trauma. He had been driving into Doolin when he saw what was happening.

I was very cold and very worried. I did not know how bad my injuries were, and my biggest fear was bleeding inside my body. He checked me and said he could not feel signs of internal bleeding, but he thought I had broken bones. Later I found out I had six spinal fractures, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in the hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for several weeks. I could not work for five months, and I had trouble moving, plus stiffness and pain. Then doctors said I had post-traumatic stress. After coming close to death, I felt anxious about everything and reacted in ways I never had before. I felt like people were looking at me the wrong way. Loud noises became hard for me, and I had memory loss.

Three months before the accident, I had opened a health-food shop, but I had to give it up because I could not work anymore. It was the hardest year ever, but now it is behind me. I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building my own osteopathy practice. I understand patients better because I have been a patient.

I am thankful that I am healthy, and I really want to stop other people from getting hurt. Many of us think dolphins are always safe. Who would believe a dolphin could attack a person? If a fierce animal comes at you with its teeth showing, it is scary. But dolphins seem friendly because they look like they are smiling.

I am not angry at Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her territory, and she is a wild animal that can be unpredictable. People need to know that. Many people come to swim with her and do not understand how dangerous it can be. People said my injuries were among several injuries that summer.

After the man pulled me out of the water, Dusty swam away. Then she came back and stayed upright in the water next to me, looking at me. We looked into each other’s eyes, and I felt she was sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin; the anger was gone. The people on the pier were amazed. After that moment, I did not feel the terror anymore. I made peace with her.
