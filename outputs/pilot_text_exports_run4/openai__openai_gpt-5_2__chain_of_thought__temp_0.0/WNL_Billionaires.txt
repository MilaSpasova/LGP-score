A thick layer of bird droppings covers a gold-painted railing at one of Britain’s most expensive homes. Pigeon skeletons are on the floor near broken mirrors, and water runs through broken ceiling edges. This home is called The Towers. It is a £30m palace on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London. People have kept its ruin secret until now.

The Towers is one of ten big houses in the middle of The Bishops Avenue. This road is at the centre of London’s fast-rising property market. These houses have been almost empty since they were bought about 25 years ago. It is believed they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. Their Greek-style columns are breaking apart, and swimming pools with mosaic tiles are full of rubble. Nature has taken over so much that owls now live there.

This empty and broken look can be seen all along this famous road. Lloyds Bank has said The Bishops Avenue is the second most expensive street in Britain. At the same time, more and more people cannot afford to buy a home in London, as house prices rise by 11.2% a year. On the most expensive part of The Bishops Avenue, 16 mansions are sitting empty. Many are behind locked gates. Their windows are covered with steel bars, their gardens are overgrown, and guard dogs patrol the grounds.

Across the street, there is another broken-down mansion worth £18m. Its windows are smashed, and its walls are covered in anti-climb paint. Another house has metal bars on its windows and has been sold for £20m.

Even so, prices still go up. Dryades, a mansion that was until recently owned by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007 and is believed to be worth about £30m today. Heath Lodge, where fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou was murdered in 1984 by a silver bullet, is worth £13m today. It was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

For people who struggle just to keep a home in one of the world’s most expensive cities, this can be painful to see. One security guard on the road said it was upsetting to watch so many tens of thousands of square feet of space—enough to house dozens of people—falling apart.

Royals with oil money from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia were among the first to move to this curving road near Hampstead Heath. Iranians came here after the fall of the shah. Now Chinese buyers are following Russians and Kazakhs. They have spent millions to get an address that estate agents say is as famous as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions have been for sale for £65m and £38m, offering Italian marble, lifts with leather padding, and luxury panic rooms.

But on the land around these empty homes, stone fountains are breaking and lawns have turned into muddy swamps. Inside one house, water drips through a huge crystal chandelier onto a thick carpet that is rotting under parts of a collapsed ceiling. Moss grows through broken bricks. Broken mirror tiles lie across a bathroom floor. The swimming pool has about a foot of dirty, salty water, and flowers grow through its tiles. Wooden boards push out from the sauna.

The most dramatic ruin is The Towers. It is a grand house set among acres of hornbeams, oaks, and limes. Pigeons fill its large, tall rooms. Its walls have turned bright green with algae. Water pours through three storeys and drops into a huge, empty basement swimming pool. Unopened wooden boxes marked “bullet-proof glass” show that the old owners worried about security.

Today, very few people live on The Bishops Avenue all the time. A security guard outside one mansion said the owners were not there. Another guard outside Royal Mansion would not say if anyone was home. Staff at another mansion warned the Guardian about the guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare mogul with personal wealth of £45m, said he is one of the few who live there full time.

He said it is not friendly, and people do not talk over fences. He said he has never seen what his neighbours look like. He said a Saudi princess next door spent £35m on a new house, and he has never seen her. He said about three houses are lived in 24/7. He said half the properties are lived in for three to six months a year. He said about the other half, he does not know if the owners come or not.

These broken, multi-million-pound homes show a property culture where the richest people in the world treat British homes as investments. One Hyde Park, an apartment block in Knightsbridge, is another example. More than half of its flats are registered with the council as empty or as second homes.

People on the road now talk about building £5m apartments instead of £50m mansions, to bring residents back.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, plans to rebuild one of the most valuable sites on the road into 20 apartments. They will have a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

Varma said that if you build a big house and try to sell it for £30m to £40m, it will not sell. He said locals will not buy, so you must bring in overseas buyers.

But using these empty homes to help solve the housing crisis still seems far away. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if some of the broken homes could become affordable homes. He said the land price would be too high. He said very wealthy people own property there, and sometimes they live in the homes and sometimes they do not.
