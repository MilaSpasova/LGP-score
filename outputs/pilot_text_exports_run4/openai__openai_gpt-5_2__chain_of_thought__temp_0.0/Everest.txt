Kenton Cool usually likes to talk, but now he can hardly speak. After working hard high up in the mountains, his voice is rough and low. He is now in Kathmandu, the capital of Nepal. He flew down from Everest base camp that morning.

Cool is thinking about an amazing set of climbs he finished over the last weekend. Early on Saturday morning, he reached the top of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of the three main peaks in the Everest “horseshoe.” This horseshoe surrounds an icy valley called the Western Cwm.

That same day, he climbed up to the top of Everest. He reached the summit in complete darkness early on Sunday. Then he and his climbing partner kept going. On Monday morning, they reached the summit of Lhotse. This was the third peak in this big three-peaks challenge.

“It was a snatched opportunity,” he says. “For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains.” (Fixed ropes are ropes that are already put in place.) He says this does not take away what he achieved. “I’ve set the bar at a certain level. But whoever comes along next will move the bar further and do it without ropes or bottled oxygen.”

Sixty years after Everest was first climbed, many news stories look back to Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. They talk about an earlier time, before today’s commercialism and environmental damage. I ask Cool to look forward and imagine what top climbers might be doing 60 years from now.

“I hate to think,” he says. But he talks about the Swiss climber Ueli Steck. In April, Steck left the mountain after what Cool calls “an altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck was planning to climb Everest’s west ridge, first climbed in 1963. Then he would go down to the South Col and immediately climb Lhotse by a new route, all without fixed ropes. “Ueli had been training like a machine,” Cool says. “He’s a climber in a class all his own. He’s technically brilliant but he had also taken his physical condition to an astronomic level. It would have been amazing to see what he could have done.”

What tourism will look like is another question. One clue is a helicopter rescue by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro’s angry language helped start the fight at Camp 2. Moro flew back to Everest on Tuesday in a high-powered helicopter. He rescued a sick or injured climber at 7,800 metres.

This was the highest rescue yet on Everest. It also shows how quickly helicopter flights have increased in recent years. By 2073, the mountain could even have a helipad on the South Col. Tourists could be flown there while breathing bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing what is possible for rescuing climbers and also the many trekkers who go only as far as base camp.

Mountain geographer and environmentalist Alton Byers says it is not clear if the Everest region can keep handling fast-growing tourism. He says climate change plus tourism is creating new pressure on the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers are pulling back, and in some places disappearing, and this is already causing big effects. “Everywhere you go, people are talking about how there’s less water. There’s less water for agriculture and less water for all the new lodges that are getting built.”

Byers says that in the Sherpa town of Namche Bazaar, workers are building a new five-mile pipeline to bring in water. This is to meet the growing tourist demand for showers and flush toilets. The local stream is now polluted by human waste. It also does not give enough water for a town that, in high season, is extremely crowded. “Every village is digging a pit just beyond the houses for garbage. Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world,” he says.

Human waste at base camp is now handled well and taken away in plastic barrels. But Byers says the barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley. That pit could leak into the region’s streams and rivers.

“These problems can be solved, but we need to get serious about it,” he says. “One climber can spend $85,000 climbing Everest. And that’s fine. But at some point we’re going to have to address these other priorities. For half a million dollars a year, you could solve most of them.”

Climate change, he says, is harder. Byers works with local conservation committees to find and plan for climate change impacts. This often means finding new water sources or collecting rainwater. Another constant danger is the fast growth of glacial lakes. These lakes could burst and flood the Sherpa homeland. “There’s going to come a time when people are going to have to get out of their way.”

Changing weather is also affecting tourism. More clouds during times that are usually clear are closing Lukla Airport more often. Lukla Airport is the gateway to the Everest region. A new road for 4x4 vehicles is being built to Lukla. This is meant to make sure tourists and their money can still arrive. But Byers worries that roads in Nepal are spreading fast and being built cheaply. He says this can cause serious soil erosion and landslides.

“Everest is the icon everyone knows,” he says. “It’s the canary in the coalmine that everyone understands. It’s the perfect laboratory for figuring out how to address some of these problems, like the impacts of climate change and tourism.”
