Anitta is a new star of Brazilian funk music. She has gained millions of fans by bringing favela music into popular music. But she is now part of a debate about skin colour.

Some anti-discrimination campaigners and writers say Anitta had to give up part of her “blackness” to succeed in the mostly white, middle-class market.

The debate grew after “before and after” photos were published. They show Anitta’s skin looking much lighter after she signed a deal with Warner. In an older photo, when she was not famous, she looks darker. In a newer marketing photo, after she became famous, she looks paler. People argue about why. It could be from skin-lightening products and cosmetic surgery, or more likely from Photoshop. The photos started the old question again: do you need to be pale to succeed in Brazil?

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote a post about discrimination in media and marketing. She said many people deny racism and think Brazil is a “multiracial democracy,” but statistics show big unfairness. She said the “whitening” idea shows a society that does not support diversity. She wrote that many people see white as rich, nice, successful, and good, and see black as the opposite.

Anitta was born Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

She later became very famous with an album and a big hit song, Show das Poderosas. The song reached No. 1 and got 52 million views on YouTube.

Many people love her as a pop idol with catchy songs and a strong message. Her supporters also present her as a bridge between mostly black and mixed-race shanty towns on Rio’s hills and the richer, whiter areas below.

She has reduced some parts of baile funk, like very suggestive dancing, gang references, and explicit lyrics. Now some people ask if she, or her marketing team, has changed her image too much to attract a richer audience. Arraes said that if pop stars have curly hair, they may feel forced to straighten it, and if they have a big nose, they may feel forced to get rhinoplasty. She said this can harm self-esteem.

Race and colour are sensitive topics in Brazil, a country with many mixed-race people. Brazil was one of the last large countries to end slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. In Brazil, colour often matters more than race or ancestry. Even though Brazil aims to be a multiracial democracy, skin tone is strongly linked to inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Until 2011, black or mixed-race students also spent about two fewer years in school on average.

The government says the gap is shrinking because of university quota systems and other affirmative action. But the difference is still very clear. Most business and government leaders are white, while many low-paying jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In rich areas like Ipanema and Gávea, you are more likely to see black nannies pushing strollers with white toddlers than the other way around.

Defining colour is not simple. In the 2010 census, people who said they were white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million said black, two million said Asian, and 0.5% said indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology lecturer at the Federal University of Pernambuco, said Anitta won over the middle class by taking a rebellious style and making it calmer and easier for more people to accept. He said it was not “racial whitening.” He said what changed was the social place where she made her art: from the periphery to the centre.

Other people say the colour issue is too big. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who worked with Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot but not her colour. He said it was more about makeup, hairstyling, and clothing. He also said he did not think she was pressured, because she always liked to perform, sing, and dance.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador, said the prejudice was not mainly in the public, but in music producers and media leaders. He said the public showed they care about music itself by choosing Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, as a recent winner of The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for comment. But in a recent interview, she said identity should be defined by the person. She said an “all-powerful” woman does not need to be beautiful. She said she tries to share the message that we can be who we want.
