Opposition to Western Australia’s shark cull has grown. Thousands of people went to beaches around Australia to ask the state’s premier to stop the policy. RSPCA Australia and Richard Branson, the owner of Virgin Atlantic, also spoke against it.

The policy catches and kills sharks longer than three metres. It began after what the state government called an “unprecedented” number of shark attacks on Western Australia’s coast. A 35-year-old surfer was killed in November 2013. He was the sixth person to die from a shark attack in two years.

But the Shark Attack File says Australia as a whole has averaged one shark-related death a year for the last 50 years.

Kate Faehrmann, a board member of the Sea Shepherd Conservation Society, spoke at a protest in Perth. She said the policy will not work. She said drumlines, used to catch sharks, can kill many animals. They can catch sharks of any size, and also dolphins, turtles, and other animals.

Thousands protested at Cottesloe Beach in Perth and Manly Beach in Sydney. Hundreds also protested at Glenelg in south-west Adelaide, and at beaches in Victoria and Queensland. Faehrmann said the protests showed many Australians want sharks protected, even though people fear sharks.

Anthony Joyce, a surfer who once had his foot caught in a shark’s mouth, said killing the sharks will make no real difference.

The state government has refused to give an ongoing count of sharks killed. Reports say some sharks smaller than three metres were released after being caught on drumlines. Drumlines are floating drums fixed to the sea bed, with bait on hooks below.

Conservationists say there is no evidence the cull will reduce shark attacks on humans. They say no earlier cull used only drumlines. Researchers at the University of Western Australia say the recent rise in attacks may be linked to the state’s fast-growing population, not to more sharks.

Richard Peirce, chairman of the UK charity the Shark Trust, said the cull will not work and could bring more predators closer to the coast. He said leaving drumlines in the water overnight could attract other predators, because sharks and other animals may be hooked and injured.

Around the world in 2012, there were 80 unprovoked shark attacks, and seven were fatal. This is compared to nearly 100m sharks killed by humans each year.

RSPCA Australia said it believes the cull is not justified. It said there is no evidence the increase in attacks is because there are more sharks. It said the increase fits with a changing population and human behaviour, meaning more people are in the water.

Richard Branson told Fairfax Radio the policy is backfiring. He said Western Australia Premier Colin Barnett may have thought it would help tourism, but it could do the opposite. Branson said it makes the problem look bigger than it is, and may stop people from coming to Perth and the countryside around it. He said it will only make people worry for no need.
