This new light could be a great invention, like Trevor Baylis’s wind-up radio in the 1990s before the internet. It is a cheap light that uses free power from gravity. It could stop people in Africa and India from using dangerous kerosene lamps.

British designer Patrick Hunt tried to get money from banks or venture capital to start selling the light. But he had trouble. He said getting funding was slow and difficult, and people did not want to take a risk.

So he tried crowdfunding on a US website called Indiegogo, which had just opened in the UK. In five days he reached his goal and raised £36,200. By the end of 2012, his campaign was very popular. In 40 days he raised £400,000.

The LED light uses a dynamo. The dynamo is powered when a 10kg bag of rocks slowly drops. The bag is attached to the light and lifted about 2m high. When it falls slowly to the ground, it makes enough power for 30 minutes of light.

Hunt is getting ready to make the lights in China. He will test the market again by sending 1,000 lights to Africa before full mass production. He hopes to make millions of units.

Hunt is part of a new group of entrepreneurs using crowdfunding to get money. Another new website is InvestingZone. It connects wealthy people with start-up entrepreneurs.

Indiegogo does not give shares. Instead, people can get “perks” for giving different amounts of money. People who helped fund Hunt’s light could feel good about helping poorer people, and they could also get their own light.

Danae Ringelmann, a co-founder of Indiegogo, says the “gravity light” shows how crowdfunding can work well and can test an idea. She says finance can be fast and fair because it is not about knowing the right people at a bank or a venture capital firm. It is about showing customers and fans that the idea is good, and getting them to support it with money.

She also says it is useful to test ideas even if they do not get enough funding. Then you learn faster and do not waste time on a bad idea.

Ringelmann lives in the US. She first worked as a Wall Street analyst. In 2008 she quit and used her skills to help friends in the arts raise money. The site first focused on films and launched at the Sundance Film Festival that year.

Five years later, Indiegogo was raising about $2m a week for new businesses in start-up and growth stages. In December 2012 it started euro and sterling services to grow on this side of the Atlantic. It says Britain is its third biggest market. Activity from other countries is up 41% since December.

There are many competitors, like Kickstarter, Seedrs, and Funding Circle. But Ringelmann says Indiegogo is different because anyone can start a campaign. No project is seen as too strange.

Indiegogo takes a 4% fee from successful campaigns. If a campaign does not reach its goal, users can choose to refund all the money for free, or keep the money raised and pay a 9% fee.

A British woman, Lauren Pears, raised £100,000 on the site to open a “cat café” in London. It is called Lady Dinah’s Cat Emporium. It has not opened yet, but it is described as a place where people can sit in a comfortable chair, have hot tea, read a book, and spend time with a cat.

Ringelmann says some people are rejected by venture capitalists. The venture capitalists may say the idea is good, but they do not know if the market wants it. She says entrepreneurs can run an Indiegogo campaign to show market proof, even before they launch the project. Then venture capitalists can see there is a market.

She says crowdfunding lets you test the market, the price, and the features. It can also help you find new ways to earn money and get important feedback.

With her Wall Street experience and her work helping 100,000 businesses and services raise money, Ringelmann gives advice to new entrepreneurs. She says many people have ideas, but success depends on doing the work. She says if you are afraid someone will steal your idea and do it better and faster, then you are not the right person to do it. She says you need confidence, speed, and learning.

Ringelmann says growth in Europe and a deal with a web transaction provider are part of a dream to make finance open to more people. This deal will let people pay with local card services like Maestro in the UK and Carte Bleue in France, as well as PayPal. More than 7,000 campaigns are live on the site.

Crowdfunding has grown as another choice besides banks. But it is not very attractive to very rich investors, because they usually want a stake in a promising business. This could start to change in the UK with the launch of InvestingZone.
