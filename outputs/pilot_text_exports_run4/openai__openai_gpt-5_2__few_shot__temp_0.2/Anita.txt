Anitta is a new star of Brazilian funk music. She has millions of fans. She helped bring the sound of the favela into mainstream pop music. But people are arguing about her skin colour.

Some anti-discrimination activists and writers say Anitta had to give up looking black to become popular with a mostly white, middle-class market.

The argument started after people shared “before and after” photos. In an older photo, before she was famous, she looks darker. In a newer marketing photo, after she signed a deal with Warner, she looks paler. People ask if this happened because of whitening products, cosmetic surgery, or more likely Photoshop. This has started the old question in Brazil: do you need to be pale to succeed?

Jarid Arraes, a psychology student and blogger, wrote a post about it. She said there is hidden discrimination in media and marketing. She said many people say they are not racist and think Brazil is a multiracial democracy, but the statistics show this is not true. She said “white” is often seen as rich, successful, and good, and “black” is seen as the opposite. She also said this can hurt people’s self-esteem. She gave examples: people with curly hair may feel forced to straighten it, and people with a big nose may feel forced to get rhinoplasty.

Anitta’s real name is Larissa de Macedo Machado. As a child, she sang in a church choir. As a teenager, she became known in Rio de Janeiro’s baile funk scene as a dancer and singer.

Now she is very famous. She released an album and had a big hit song, “Show das Poderosas.” It reached number one and got 52 million views on YouTube.

Many people love her as a pop idol with a strong message and catchy songs. The people who support her also present her as a bridge between the mostly black and mixed-race shanty towns on the hills in Rio and the richer, whiter areas below.

She changed her style to fit the mainstream. She reduced the suggestive dancing, gangsta references, and explicit lyrics of baile funk. But now some people ask if she, or her marketing team, changed her image too much to attract a more profitable audience.

Race and colour are sensitive topics in Brazil. Brazil was one of the last big countries to ban slavery. It has the largest population of African descent outside Africa. But in Brazil, colour often matters more than race and ancestry. There is also a clear link between skin tone and inequality.

In Brazilian cities, white workers earn about twice as much as workers of African descent. Until 2011, black or mixed-race students also spent two fewer years in school on average.

The government says the gap is getting smaller because of university quotas and other affirmative action. But the difference is still clear. Most business and government executives are white. Many low-paid jobs are done by black and mixed-race workers. In rich areas like Ipanema and Gávea, you are more likely to see black nannies pushing strollers with white toddlers than the opposite.

Colour categories can be complicated. In the 2010 census, people who called themselves white were a minority for the first time. Out of 197 million people, 82 million said they were “pardu” (mixed race), 15 million black, two million Asian, and 0.5% indigenous.

Sylvio Ferreira, a psychology lecturer at the Federal University of Pernambuco, said Anitta became popular with the middle class because she made a rebellious sound calmer and easier for everyone to accept. He said this was not done by racial whitening. He said it happened because Anitta changed where she made her art: from the periphery to the centre.

Others say people are making the colour issue too big. Maycon de Mattos Batista, a financial analyst who worked with Anitta when she was an intern, said her image changed a lot, but not her colour. He said it was more about makeup, hairstyling, and clothes, and not because of pressure. He said she always liked to sing, dance, and show off, and that this natural style helped her succeed.

Leandro Silva de Souza, a racial equality activist in Salvador, said the prejudice is not mainly in the public, but in music producers and media executives. He said the public showed they care about music itself when they chose Ellen Oléria, a black lesbian, as a recent winner of the talent show The Voice Brazil.

The Guardian could not reach Anitta for comment. But in a recent interview, Anitta said identity should be defined by the person. She said an “all-powerful” woman does not need to be beautiful, but can be powerful because of her attitude. She said she tries to show that we can be who we want.
