Kenton Cool usually likes to talk, but now he can hardly speak. Climbing at high altitude has made his voice rough. He is in Kathmandu, the capital of Nepal. He flew down from Everest base camp that morning.

Cool is thinking about three big climbs he finished in one weekend. Early on Saturday, he reached the top of Nuptse. Nuptse is the first and lowest of three main peaks in the Everest “horseshoe”. This horseshoe surrounds a icy valley called the Western Cwm.

That same day, he climbed to the top of Everest. He reached the summit in complete darkness early on Sunday. Then he and his climbing partner went on to the top of Lhotse on Monday morning.

Cool says it was a rare chance. For the first time since the late 1990s, there were fixed ropes on all three mountains. He says this does not remove the hard physical work he did. But he thinks the next person will do even more, maybe without ropes or bottled oxygen.

It has been 60 years since people first climbed Everest. Many news stories look back to Edmund Hillary and Tenzing Norgay. They compare that time with today, when there is more business tourism and more damage to the environment. I asked Cool to think about what top climbers might do 60 years from now.

Cool says he does not like to think about it. But he talks about Swiss climber Ueli Steck. In April, Steck left the mountain after what Cool calls “an altercation” with a crowd of Sherpas at Camp 2. Cool says Steck planned to climb Everest’s west ridge, first climbed in 1963. Then Steck wanted to go down to the South Col and climb Lhotse right away by a new route, without fixed ropes. Cool says Steck trained extremely hard. He says Steck was technically brilliant and also in amazing physical shape.

Tourism is another question. One sign is a helicopter rescue by Simone Moro, Steck’s climbing partner. Moro flew a powerful helicopter back to Everest on Tuesday to rescue a very sick or injured climber at 7,800 metres.

This was the highest rescue ever done on Everest. It also shows that helicopter flights have increased a lot in recent years. By 2073, there could be more structures on the mountain, like a helipad on the South Col, bringing tourists who use bottled oxygen. For now, helicopters are changing what is possible for rescuing climbers and also many trekkers who go only as far as base camp.

Mountain geographer and environmentalist Alton Byers says it is not clear if the Everest region can keep handling fast-growing tourism. He says climate change and tourism together are creating new pressure on the Sherpa homeland. Glaciers in the Everest region are shrinking, and some are disappearing. He says this is already causing big problems. People say there is less water. There is less water for farming and less water for new lodges.

Byers says that in the Sherpa town of Namche Bazaar, workers are building a new five-mile pipeline to bring water for tourists who want showers and flush toilets. The local stream has human waste in it and does not have enough water in the busy season. He says villages dig pits near houses for garbage. He says Khumbu has the highest landfill sites in the world.

Byers says human waste at base camp is now handled well and taken away in plastic barrels. But he says the barrels are emptied into a huge pit a few hours down the valley. He says this pit could leak into the region’s rivers and streams.

Byers says these problems can be fixed, but people must take them seriously. He says one climber can spend $85,000 to climb Everest. He says that is fine, but other needs must also be addressed. He says for half a million dollars a year, most of these problems could be solved.

Climate change is harder to solve. Byers works with local conservation groups to plan for climate change. This often means finding new water sources or collecting rainwater. He says glacial lakes are growing quickly, and they could burst and flood the Sherpa homeland. He says there may be a time when people will need to move away.

Changing weather also affects tourism. More clouds during times that are usually clear are closing Lukla Airport more often. Lukla Airport is the main entry to the Everest region. A new road for 4x4 vehicles is being built to Lukla so tourists and money can still arrive. But Byers worries that roads in Nepal are being built cheaply. He says this can cause serious soil erosion and landslides.

Byers says everyone knows Everest. He says it is a clear warning sign that people understand. He says it is a perfect place to learn how to deal with problems like climate change and tourism.
