Dan Sullivan was late to breakfast with the Guardian. He was stopped by the police while riding his motorcycle.

Sullivan says he works too much. He misses dinner dates. He forgets to buy presents.

So the 27-year-old venture capitalist started a new service called BetterBoyfriend.me. For about $70 a month, it sends a present each month to a girlfriend or wife.

Sullivan works in finance. He has been testing the service quietly. About 350 boyfriends have signed up. The service makes about $17,000 each month. He says many customers are his Harvard and investor friends: venture capitalists, founders, and workers at companies like Apple, Google, and Facebook. Their girlfriends have been getting gifts from Sullivan for the last year.

Each month, members choose one gift from a list of seven options, like chocolate, tea sets, or manicures. The gift is sent to the boyfriend with no brand name and no label.

Sullivan tests ideas by buying Facebook ads to see if people are interested. BetterBoyfriend.me became popular fast. Sullivan said it solved a problem many people have.

This was not Sullivan’s first idea. For a few days, he thought about a dog-sharing business. He said he saw rich men looking unhappy while walking dogs. He said a dog is only valuable 5% of the time. He thought maybe one person could watch the dogs, and other people could rent them.

Another start-up is called InvisibleBoyfriend. It lets users create a fake social partner who is always available to talk. The result is a “boyfriend” who can text with you all day.

Founder Kyle Tabor started it to help users stop unwanted attention from other people. But he found that some users became attached to their computer-made partners. He said many users want companionship through conversation, not just “proof” of a relationship.

For Sullivan and Tabor, a surprise was how real the relationships became with customers. Sullivan says he now feels like a relationship adviser for the boyfriends.

Sullivan says he made mistakes. At first, the packages had receipts with his name, Dan Sullivan. One boyfriend wrote to him and said, “Listen, dude, she’s not mad but Cynthia found out.”

Sullivan says about 50% of the women who get the packages know he is involved. He says this is connected to age. He thinks that after people have been married for a while, they keep fewer secrets.

Over the year, Sullivan says he got to know the boyfriends very well. They even delivered a package to a hospital delivery room. He said the key is to remember that his relationship is with the boyfriend.

When he first started, he put BetterBoyfriend.me tags on flower bouquets and went to Dolores Park in the Mission District. He looked for couples and gave flowers to the woman, but the boyfriend did not like it. So he changed his plan and started giving the flowers to the boyfriends.
