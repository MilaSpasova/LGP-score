A thick layer of bird droppings covers a gold-painted railing at one of Britain’s most expensive homes. Pigeon bones lie near broken mirrors, and water runs through damaged decoration on the ceiling. This home is The Towers. It is a £30m mansion on “Billionaires’ Row” in north London. People did not know how badly ruined it was until now.

The Towers is one of ten big homes in the middle of The Bishops Avenue. This street is a key part of London’s rising property market. These homes have been almost empty since they were bought about 25 years ago. It is believed they were bought for members of the Saudi Arabian royal family. Their Greek-style columns are breaking. Swimming pools with mosaic tiles are full of rubble. Nature has taken over, and owls now live there.

This ruined scene appears along the street. Lloyds Bank says The Bishops Avenue is the second most expensive street in Britain. At the same time, many people in London cannot afford a home, and house prices rise by 11.2% a year. On the most expensive part of the street, 16 mansions are empty. Many are behind locked gates. Their windows have steel covers, and the gardens are overgrown. Guard dogs patrol the grounds.

Across the street is another ruined mansion worth £18m, with broken windows and walls covered in anti-climb paint. Another mansion has metal bars on its windows and was sold for £20m.

Even so, prices keep rising. Dryades, a mansion that was owned until recently by a Pakistani politician, sold for £12m in 2007 and is believed to be worth about £30m today. Heath Lodge, where fashion tycoon Aristos Constantinou was murdered in 1984 by a silver bullet, is worth £13m today. It was sold in the late 1970s for £400,000.

For people who struggle to pay for housing, these empty, falling buildings can be painful to see. A security guard said it was very frustrating. He said there were tens of thousands of square feet of space, enough to house dozens of people, but it was being left to fall apart.

Rich royals from Nigeria and Saudi Arabia were among the first to come to this curving road near Hampstead Heath. Iranians came here after the fall of the shah. Now Chinese buyers are coming after Russians and Kazakhs. They pay millions for an address that estate agents say is as famous as the Champs Elysées and Rodeo Drive. Recently, two mansions were for sale for £65m and £38m. They promised Italian marble, lifts with leather padding, and luxury panic rooms.

But on the grounds of the empty homes, stone fountains break down and lawns turn into wet mud. Inside one mansion, water drips through a large crystal chandelier. A thick carpet is rotting under parts of a collapsed ceiling. Moss grows through broken bricks. Mirrored tiles are scattered across a bathroom. The swimming pool has about a foot of dirty water, and flowers grow through the tiles. Wooden boards on the sauna bend outward.

The worst damage is at The Towers. It is a grand mansion among many trees. Pigeons fill its large halls. Algae has turned the walls bright green. Water pours through three storeys and drops into a huge, empty basement swimming pool. Unopened wooden boxes marked “bullet-proof glass” show that the previous owners worried about security.

Today, very few people live on The Bishops Avenue all the time. A security guard outside one mansion said the owners were not there. Another guard outside Royal Mansion would not say if anyone was home. A staff member at another mansion warned the Guardian about the guard dogs. Magdy Adib Ishak-Hannah, an Egyptian-born private healthcare mogul with personal wealth of £45m, said he is one of the few people who lives there permanently.

He said it is not a friendly place where people talk over the fence. He said he has never seen his neighbours. Next door, he said, a Saudi princess paid £35m for a new house, and he has never seen her. He said about three houses have people living in them all day and night. About half the properties are used only three to six months a year. He said no one knows about the other half.

These broken mansions show a property culture where the world’s richest people buy British homes as investments. One Hyde Park, a group of apartments in Knightsbridge, is another example. More than half the flats there are registered with the council as empty homes or second homes.

Even so, people on the street now talk about building £5m apartments instead of £50m mansions, to bring more residents back.

Anil Varma, a local property developer, plans to rebuild one valuable site on the street into 20 apartments. They would have a concierge, maid service, a 25-metre pool, a spa, and a cinema.

He said if you build a big house and try to sell it for £30m to £40m, it will not sell. He said local people will not buy, so you must find buyers from overseas.

But it still seems unlikely that the empty homes will help fix the housing crisis. Andrew Harper, a local Conservative councillor, laughed when asked if the ruined homes could become affordable housing. He said the land price would be too high.

He said very wealthy people own property there. Sometimes they live in the homes, and sometimes they do not.
