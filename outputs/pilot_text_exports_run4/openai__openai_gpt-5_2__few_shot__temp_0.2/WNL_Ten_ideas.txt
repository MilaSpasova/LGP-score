We look at some unusual ideas to make cities better places to live. City life has many good points, but living in a sustainable way is becoming harder. People in cities often face pollution, traffic, and fewer green spaces. Inventors have suggested ten strange solutions to help.

Pop-up parks
Some cities seem built more for cars than for people. A pop-up park is a simple idea. You take an empty parking space, some small items, and a few plants, and you make a small park. The PARK(ing) project started as an art experiment in San Francisco and later spread around the world. People have tried temporary city farms and ecology demonstrations. The movement has a day of action every September.

Underground bike storage
Not everyone in a city uses a car. Bikes are becoming more popular. But people need a safe place to keep them. An engineering company in Tokyo called Giken made an idea for an underground bicycle park. It is a cylinder about seven metres wide. It goes deep into the ground and can hold 204 bikes. Owners can get their bike by pressing a button. The system brings the bike back above ground in about 13 seconds.

Glow-in-the-dark trees
Many people think of Christmas lights when they think of things that glow. But Dutch designer and artist Daan Roosegaarde created a “bioluminescent” plant. This experimental technology mixes DNA from glowing marine bacteria with part of a plant to make it glow like a jellyfish. Tests are happening to make a large-scale version. Roosegaarde hopes it could one day replace normal street lights.

Energy from footsteps
In east London, many commuters and shoppers walk across a raised walkway near the West Ham underground station. Most people do not notice that the ground feels a little springy. The rubber surface bends about five millimetres. This movement helps power the streetlights above. The floor has smart tiles that take energy from footsteps and turn it into electricity. The UK company Pavegen made this system. It has also installed similar systems at London’s Heathrow Airport and other places.

Supertrees
Singapore’s Gardens by the Bay has man-made trees called “supertrees.” They are steel frames, up to 50 metres high. Flowers and ferns grow on them. Their metal tops help take in heat and spread it out. They can collect rainwater and help provide air ventilation for two climate-controlled biomes below (large conservatories). Eleven of the 18 trees also have solar panels on their branches.
