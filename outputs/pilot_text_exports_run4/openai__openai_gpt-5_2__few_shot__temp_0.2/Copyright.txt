Author Philip Pullman says illegal downloading is theft. He says it is like putting your hand in someone’s pocket and stealing a wallet. In an article for Index on Censorship, Pullman, who is president of the Society of Authors, strongly defends copyright laws. He criticizes internet users who think it is OK to download music or books without paying.

Pullman writes that new technology can look very impressive, so people do not see the bad thing they are doing. He says it is wrong that people can steal an artist’s work and not get punished.

His article came after music industry leaders met British Prime Minister David Cameron in Downing Street to talk about web piracy.

Pullman, the writer of the His Dark Materials trilogy, says authors and musicians often work in poverty and are not well known for years. They work hard to make something that gives people joy. But when they finally can earn money from it, illegal downloading can take that chance away. He says the rule is simple: if we want to enjoy someone’s work, we should pay for it.

Pullman is writing in the next issue of the group’s magazine in a dialogue with Cathy Casserly, the chief executive of Creative Commons. Creative Commons offers open content licences that let creators control copyright in a different way. Casserly says copyright has many problems because it was created in an “analogue age”. She says copyright often stops people from sharing, building on, and remixing other people’s work.

Casserly says artists need to think in new ways about how they share and make money from their work. She quotes science fiction writer Cory Doctorow, who said: “My problem is not piracy, it’s obscurity.”

Index on Censorship agrees. The editor, Rachael Jolley, said current copyright laws do not work well in the digital age and may turn consumers into criminals. She says we need new ways for artists, writers, and musicians to earn money.

The debate is strong, and illegal downloading is very large. Ofcom, the UK communications regulator, collected data. It suggests that in the UK, between November 2012 and January 2013, 280 million music tracks were pirated. Also, 52 million TV shows, 29 million films, 18 million ebooks, and 7 million software or games files were pirated.

Ofcom said 18% of internet users aged over 12 admit they recently pirated content. It also said 9% fear getting caught. Pullman writes that it is still surprising how easy and fast people can get music as MP3 downloads.

After the Downing Street meeting, Cameron appointed Conservative MP Mike Weatherley as his adviser on this subject. A spokesman for the BPI, the record industry trade body, said Weatherley strongly supports copyright and the artists and creative producers it protects. The BPI hopes his influence, and the prime minister’s support, will affect the UK’s intellectual property office.
