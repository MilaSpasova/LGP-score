It was a beautiful summer evening. I decided to go swimming near Doolin Pier in County Clare, Ireland. I moved there in 2012.

A woman was in the water with Dusty, a dolphin. Dusty often swims with a group of people, and she is close to them.

Dusty came to Doolin around 2008. Since then, hundreds of people have swum with her. This makes her seem very tame. She was even in an Irish tourist-board advertisement, where a girl says she wants to touch a dolphin.

That evening, the woman was tickling Dusty’s stomach. It looked safe and fun. About 20 tourists and local people were watching from the pier.

I got into the water. Right away, Dusty left the woman and became very wild. Later I learned that she can be very protective of her space when she is with someone. Her tail was moving fast. At first, I thought she was just showing off. Then I realized she might be angry.

I tried to swim back to the pier. But very quickly Dusty hit me hard with her nose. It was very strong and painful. I was pushed forward.

People on the pier stared at me. Dusty was still next to me, hitting the water with her tail. This scared me the most. I thought, if her tail hits me, I could go under the water.

I reached the pier, but I could not get out because I was hurt. I was very afraid. I shouted for help. A man reached down and pulled me onto the steps.

Then another man came. He said he was an orthopaedic surgeon who worked with marine injuries. He was driving into Doolin when he saw what was happening. I was very cold and very worried. My biggest fear was bleeding inside my body.

He checked me and said he could not feel signs of internal bleeding. But he said I probably had broken bones. Later I learned my injuries were serious: six fractures in my spine, three broken ribs, and a damaged lung.

I stayed in hospital for five days. I wore a back brace for several weeks. I could not work for five months. I had trouble moving, and I felt stiff and in pain.

Later, doctors said I had post-traumatic stress. After the near-death experience, I felt anxious about everything. I reacted more strongly than before. I felt that people were looking at me in the wrong way. Loud noises were hard for me. I also had memory loss.

Three months before the accident, I opened a health-food shop. But I had to give it up because I could not work.

It was the hardest year of my life, but now it is in the past. I had craniosacral therapy, osteopathy, and massage. Now I am building my own osteopathy practice. I understand patients better because I have been a patient.

I am thankful that I am healthy. I want to stop other people from getting hurt. Many people think dolphins are always kind. Who expects a dolphin to attack a person? If a dangerous animal comes at you with its teeth showing, you feel scared. But dolphins have a wide smile, so they do not look scary.

I am not angry with Dusty. I respect her. But I was in her area, and she is a wild animal that can be unpredictable. People need to understand this. Many people come to swim with her, and they do not know how dangerous it can be. My injuries were said to be one of several injuries that summer.

After the man pulled me out of the water, Dusty swam away. Then she came back. She floated upright next to me and looked at me. We looked into each other’s eyes. I felt she was sorry. She seemed like a different dolphin. The anger was gone. People on the pier were amazed. After that moment, I was not so scared. I felt at peace with her.
