Angela Erdmann never met her grandfather. He died in 1946, six years before she was born.

On Tuesday 8th April, 2014, she talked about a special moment. She got a message in a bottle. Her grandfather had thrown it into the Baltic Sea 101 years earlier. People think it may be the world’s oldest message in a bottle. A museum in Germany gave it to her, and the museum is now showing it.

Erdmann is 62. She said it was very surprising. A man came to her door and said he had mail from her grandfather. He explained that someone had found a message in a bottle. The name on the card was her grandfather’s name. The visitor was a genealogical researcher. He found her in Berlin after the letter was given to the International Maritime Museum in Hamburg.

The bottle was a brown beer bottle. It had been in the water for 101 years. A fisherman named Konrad Fischer found it in his catch in the Baltic Sea near Kiel. Holger von Neuhoff, a curator at the museum, said it was the oldest bottled message he had seen. He said the museum has older documents without a bottle. But with both the bottle and the document, this is the oldest right now. He said it is in very good condition.

Researchers think Erdmann’s grandfather, Richard Platz, threw the bottle into the sea in 1913. He was hiking with a nature appreciation group. He was 20 years old then.

Most of the postcard is hard to read. But people can read the address in Berlin on the front. They can also read his polite request: he asked the finder to send the note to his home address.

Erdmann said her grandfather also put two stamps from that time in the bottle. This way, the finder would not have to pay. But he did not think it would take 101 years.

Erdmann said she was touched by the message. She did not know her grandfather because he died at age 54.

She said she knew very little about him. Later she learned he was a writer. She said he was open-minded and believed in freedom and respect for everyone. She said he helped young people. Later he travelled with his wife and two daughters. She said this helped her understand her roots.

Erdmann said she also likes culture and travelling around the world. She said she is open-minded too. She said what he taught his two daughters, her mother taught her, and she then taught her sons.

Even though she was happy, she said she hopes people do not throw message bottles into the sea today. She said the sea is already full of bottles and rubbish.

The message and the bottle will be shown at Hamburg’s Maritime Museum until the beginning of May 2014. After that, experts will try to read the rest of the text. It is not clear what will happen to the bottle after that, but Erdmann hopes it will stay at the museum.

She said they want to add some photos with the bottle. She wants visitors to see the young man who threw it into the water.
